*Side note: this one started as two separate essay ideas. Then I realized they were the same idea wearing two different hats, scribbled into the margins of the same book a hundred pages apart. So you get the mashup.*

A while back I was re-reading my notes on Brad Stone's *[The Everything Store](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/17660462-the-everything-store)*, and I noticed I'd left two little margin notes that, at the time, I clearly thought were about two different things.

The first, scribbled around page 390, next to a passage about Amazon's cost structure: *"Being willing to accept the slimmest margins and building an organization that can operate lean enough to not need margin can be a competitive differentiation."*

The second, a hundred pages later, next to a passage about the company's brutal treatment of third-party sellers, just four words: *"Moats don't stay impenetrable forever."*

I've [written](https://investing101.substack.com/p/competitive-moats) and [re-written](https://investing101.substack.com/p/revisiting-competitive-moats) and [re-re-written](https://investing101.substack.com/p/momentum-moat) about competitive moats more than almost any other topic on here. And somehow, in all of that, I never put those two notes next to each other. When you do, they stop being two ideas and become one:

**A moat is not a wall. It's a half-life. And the most durable thing you can own isn't the moat — it's the discipline to keep digging a new one when the old one silts up.**

Thin margins, it turns out, are the cleanest example of both halves of that sentence. So let's talk about why.

# Two Kinds of Retailers

In *Competitive Moats* I argued that for most of a company's journey, [defensibility is for dummies](https://investing101.substack.com/p/competitive-moats) — that founders fall in love with a fuzzy story about why they're safe, right up until the moment they run out of cash, time, or talent and the moat becomes moot. I still believe that. But there's a version of a moat I undersold, and it's the one Bezos bet the entire company on.

The famous line is the one he repeated, in Stone's words, "ad nauseam for years":

> *"There are two kinds of retailers: there are those folks who work to figure how to charge more, and there are companies that work to figure how to charge less, and we are going to be the second, **full-stop**."*

The reflexive read on low margins is that they're a *weakness* — a sign you have no pricing power, no brand, no defensibility. Every MBA framework treats fat gross margin as the goal and thin margin as the thing you graduate out of once you've earned the right to charge more.

Bezos read it exactly backwards. Here's Stone on his actual logic:

> *"Bezos believed his company had a natural advantage in its cost structure and ability to survive in the **thin atmosphere of low-margin businesses**. Companies like IBM, Microsoft, and Google, he suspected, would hesitate to get into such markets because it would depress their overall profit margins."*

And the cleaner statement of the thesis:

> *"Bezos believed that **high margins justified rivals' investments in research and development and attracted more competition, while low margins attracted customers and were more defensible**."*

Sit with that. The thing that looks like the weakness is the wall. **Fat margins are an open invitation — they fund your competitors' R&D budgets and signal to every adjacent giant that there's room to come take a slice.** Thin margins do the opposite. They're a "do not enter" sign posted in a language only disciplined operators can read. IBM, Microsoft, and Google *could* have followed Amazon into the thin atmosphere. They structurally wouldn't, because doing so would crater the blended margins their own shareholders were paying for.

This is, I'll admit, the inverse of the usual moat story, and for a long time it looked insane from the outside. When Scott Devitt at Stifel Nicolaus upgraded Amazon to a buy in 2007, here's what happened:

> *"I was laughed out of portfolio managers' offices," Devitt says. "People were ripping apart every component of my investment thesis. At that point, **they thought Amazon was some kind of nonprofit scam**."*

A nonprofit scam. That's what a moat looks like from the outside while it's still being dug.

# A Wall vs. An Engine

So why does this kind of moat behave differently from the ones we usually obsess over?

Because thin margins aren't a *thing you have*. They're a *thing you do*. And that distinction is the whole game.

The classic moats — patents, network effects, switching costs, regulatory capture, a brand — get talked about as assets. Things you accumulate and then sit on. In *Competitive Moats* I leaned on Rick Zullo's framing that the real engine of advantage is **capabilities**: the intangible muscles a company builds that "never appear on a balance sheet" but "are the single most valuable asset of a company." Thin margins are exactly that kind of capability. Bezos didn't *find* a low-margin advantage lying around. He spent two decades building "an organization that can operate lean enough to not need margin" — my own note from page 390. That's not a wall someone handed him. That's an engine he welded together one efficiency at a time.

And it's an engine your competitor can't copy by *deciding to*, because copying it isn't a strategy decision — it's a twenty-year organizational reconstruction that their existing P&L will fight at every step. A fat-margin incumbent that announces "we're going to operate on Amazon's margins now" doesn't get a moat. It gets an investor revolt.

This is the same instinct I keep circling back to. [Elon's line](https://fatbabyfunds.com/2021/01/your-moat-doesnt-exist-but-buffetts-does/) that "moats are lame… what matters is the **pace of innovation**." My argument in [Momentum != Moat](https://investing101.substack.com/p/momentum-moat) that the whole point is building "high-quality, long-term sustainable economic engines," not heat-seeking the next ARR chart. Thin margins are what an economic engine looks like when you put it on a balance sheet and squint. **A wall is something you stand behind. An engine is something that keeps running while the wall around it erodes.** Which brings us to the erosion.

# Moats Don't Stay Impenetrable Forever

Here's the part I had filed under a totally different heading.

Granted, even the best wall decays. **But** the point isn't that moats are useless because they decay — it's that the rate of decay is the thing nobody prices, and the most "permanent" looking moats are often the ones with the shortest fuse.

Exhibit A, from the same book: eBay's auction network effect. In the late '90s and 2000s this was considered the most insurmountable moat in commerce. Stone is unequivocal about it:

> *"In the auctions category, **eBay already had an insurmountable advantage**… In the end the network effect mattered. You could say we were naïve, but we built a great product."*

Amazon — *Amazon!* — threw money and talent at auctions and got crushed, because sellers go where the buyers are and buyers go where the sellers are, and you cannot brute-force your way into the middle of that loop. As late as Amazon's 10th-anniversary year, eBay's market cap was three times larger. Eighteen of twenty-three analysts had Amazon at hold or sell. The network effect won.

Until it didn't.

> *"At the same time that Amazon's flywheel was accelerating, **eBay's was flying apart**. The appeal of online auctions had faded; a customer wanted the convenience and certainty of a quickly completed purchase, not a seven-day waiting period... Amazon had battled and mastered chaos; eBay was engulfed by it."*

The most impenetrable moat anyone could point to didn't get stormed. It got *abandoned*, because the thing it defended — patience for a seven-day auction — stopped being something customers wanted. The moat held perfectly. The castle behind it emptied out.

Permanent Equity made this point in a way I've never forgotten:

> *"Moats are routinely discussed in a static sense, as if they exist in some form or fashion for most businesses. But the facts simply don't bear this out… **Averaged across industries, a business in its 25th year has roughly the same probability of dying as it did in its 10th year**."*

Twenty-five years of "moat" and your odds of dying this year are the same as they were at year ten. That's not a wall. That's a half-life.

# The Competitors You're Worried About Today

So which moat actually saved Amazon — the thin margins, or the willingness to watch the eBay loss and keep moving?

Both. That's the synthesis. My third margin note in that book, next to the eBay legal fight, was: *"the competitors you're worried about today will not necessarily be the ones you're worried about tomorrow."* And when Amazon was being written off as a "marginally profitable online retailer," Bezos didn't go hunting for a better wall. He said the only thing he ever says:

> *"There's only one way out of this predicament, and that is to **invent our way out**."*

That's the resolution to the two margin notes. eBay *had* the moat — the textbook, insurmountable, network-effect moat. Amazon had the *moat-builder*: thin-margin discipline that competitors structurally couldn't follow, *plus* the organizational reflex to keep digging a new trench every time the old one silted up. Kindle. Marketplace. Prime. AWS. None of those were the moat Amazon started with. They were moats it dug *after* the first ones stopped mattering.

What Elon calls "pace of innovation" and what I've been clumsily circling for three years is really just this: **the only durable moat is the capacity to build the next moat faster than the current one decays.** Everything else is a wall with a countdown timer you can't see.

# What Does This Mean For Venture?

Here's where I think we get it wrong, and I include my past self in "we."

In diligence, we underwrite moats as if they're permanent. We get excited about the network effect, the switching cost, the proprietary data, the regulatory wedge — and then we model them forward in a straight line, as if a moat is a fixed asset that throws off the same cash flows in year ten that it did in year two. We are, in Permanent Equity's words, discussing moats "in a static sense." We pay a multiple today for a defensibility that has a half-life nobody wrote into the model.

So three reframes I'm trying to actually hold:

1. **Underwrite the moat-builder, not the moat.** The question isn't "does this company have a moat?" It's "when this moat decays — and it will — does this team have the engine to dig the next one before the castle empties out?" eBay had the better moat. You'd have wanted to own the better moat-*builder*.

2. **Treat thin margins as a tell, not a flaw.** When I see a founder deliberately operating in the thin atmosphere — choosing the harder, leaner path that fat-margin incumbents won't follow them into — that's not a company that failed to find pricing power. Sometimes that's a company building [a capability its competitors can't decide their way into](https://investing101.substack.com/p/competitive-moats). The "nonprofit scam" is occasionally just a moat mid-dig.

3. **Price the half-life.** A network effect and a brand are not the same maturity of risk, even if both get a tidy "moat" checkbox in the memo. Some moats are walls with thirty-year fuses; some are walls with three-year fuses dressed up as thirty. The job is to ask not just *is there a moat*, but *how fast is this one decaying, and is anybody here digging the next one?*

This is really just [Momentum != Moat](https://investing101.substack.com/p/momentum-moat) from a different angle. Momentum isn't a moat because it's the *output* of a hot moment, not an engine. Thin margins look like an anti-moat for the same reason momentum looks like a moat — and both reads are exactly backwards. **One is a wall with no engine behind it. The other is an engine with no wall in front of it. Over a long enough horizon, the engine wins every time.**

Because every moat becomes moot. That's not the bad news. That's the whole point. The companies worth owning aren't the ones with the deepest moat today — they're the ones who already know it's filling with silt, and have a shovel in their hands.

So when you find a founder operating in the thin atmosphere, getting laughed out of portfolio managers' offices, with a wall that's actively eroding behind them and a shovel already in the ground for the next one?

That's not a nonprofit scam. That's the only moat that's ever really mattered.
