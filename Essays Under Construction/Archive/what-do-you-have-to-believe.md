There is a [common bell curve meme](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/iq-bell-curve-midwit) that makes the rounds. The midwit, in the meaty middle of the curve, wails about complexity and nuance. Meanwhile, the idiot on the left tail and the gigachad on the right tail nod sagely at the exact same simple conclusion. The wojak and the chad converge.

I think about that meme a lot when it comes to investing. On one tail you have the gigachads of the moment shouting *"You can just do things."* On the other tail you have the perma-bears, the people who have been calling the top of every market since 2009. The two ends, predictably, almost meet.

But the messy middle isn't a cop-out. It is, in fact, the only place where actual investing happens. The bear and the bull are both right *and* both wrong; the certainty is the tell. **The only way to operate honestly in that middle is to ask a question that rings through investing of almost every shape and size: "what do you have to believe?"**

# Passing

Across thousands of meetings with startups over the course of my career, I've passed on dramatically more opportunities than I have chosen to invest in. As a result, I've built up a fairly broad corpus of experience telling people I've chosen *not* to invest. Lately, the framework that best articulates my pass rationale is this: **how many things have to go right, and I can't get comfortable with all of them.**

I was talking about this with a friend recently. His response was fair, and direct: *"is there any case of a startup that doesn't need a lot to go right to go big?"*

Of course not. I tried to explain that it isn't a binary. Either-this-will-be-easy-or-this-will-be-hard isn't the question. **In fact, if there is one binary in startups, it is the universality of the fact that this will be hard.**

Instead, the question is a spectrum. A weighted average of all the things that have to go right. [I've written before](https://investing101.substack.com/p/risk-management-in-the-age-of-yolo) about how Marc Andreessen calls this **the onion theory of risk**. When you look at any given investment opportunity, it isn't one risk. It's a stack of them. Each layer peels away the prior to expose another.

# The Onion Theory of Risk

When I first wrote about this topic [over three years ago](https://investing101.substack.com/p/risk-management-in-the-age-of-yolo), this is how I framed it:

> *"Building a company is a massive multivariable calculation of risk-adjusted outcomes. Think about all the checklists you've ever seen where investors tick through the aspects they look for in a potential investment. Marc Andreessen [calls this the 'onion theory of risk](https://knowen-production.s3.amazonaws.com/uploads/attachment/file/2771/pmarca-blog-ebook.pdf)'. **Each aspect of a business is another layer**:"*

Andreessen's original list still holds up. Each is a layer:

- **Founder Risk**: Does the startup have the right founding team? Is the technologist really all that? Is the business person actually capable of running the company?
- **Market Risk**: Is there a market for the product? Will anyone want it? Will they pay for it? How much? How do we know?
- **Competition Risk**: Are there too many other startups already doing this? Is this one sufficiently differentiated, both from peers and from any large incumbents?
- **Timing Risk**: Is it too early? Is it too late?
- **Financing Risk**: After this round, how many additional rounds will the company need to become profitable, and what will the dollar total be? How certain are we?
- **Marketing Risk**: Will this startup be able to cut through the noise? Do the unit economics of customer acquisition actually work?
- **Distribution Risk**: Does the startup need certain distribution partners to succeed? Will it be able to get them?
- **Technology Risk**: Can the product even be built? Does it require fundamental breakthroughs that haven't happened yet?
- **Product Risk**: Even assuming the product can in theory be built, can *this* team build it?
- **Hiring Risk**: What positions does the startup need to hire for in order to execute? Will it be able to recruit them?

This is the framework that is most often in my head when I'm evaluating a particular opportunity. **Each risk is a dial.** In some cases, certain aspects of a business are relatively derisked, so the dial sits low. In others, the entire crux of the business depends on a single, very-high-dial variable. There is no binary equation that yields a go / no-go answer. What you experience instead is a gradually deteriorating feeling of comfort.

I ended that original [Risk Management in the Age of YOLO](https://investing101.substack.com/p/risk-management-in-the-age-of-yolo) piece this way:

> *"Risks come in all shapes and sizes — personal, spiritual, physical, professional. [In the words of Andy Rachleff](https://twitter.com/marcepntoja/status/1545212014649229313): **'Human nature is not comfortable taking risk; so most venture capital firms want high returns, without risk, which doesn't exist.'** Risk is very [similar to failure](https://investing101.substack.com/p/whats-in-a-post-mortem): 'the less time people spend with it the less comfortable with it they become.'"*

The "comfort" level I feel with any given dial is not innate; it is honed. Over time, you become more comfortable with certain types of risk and dramatically less comfortable with others. Market risk is much less scary to me now because almost every market I've ever evaluated has proven to be larger than I expected. Hiring risk has become inextricably linked to marketing risk and storytelling risk; one leads to the other, so my comfort with one depends on my comfort with the other. **But some dials, no matter how many years I sit at this desk, never get less terrifying. Founder risk is one of them. There is no chance of saving the deal if you get that one wrong.**

So how do you actually get more comfortable with the onion? You think about it more. A *lot* more.

# Pre-Parade & Pre-Mortem

There's a practice that Sequoia uses in its investment memos that we adopted when I was at Index Ventures. The practice [originally came from](https://alphaideas.in/2021/11/08/pre-parade-pre-mortem/) Larry Summers: **the pre-parade** and **the pre-mortem**. Roelof Botha [explains it this way](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1oYXDK9xhb4):

> *"At the outset [of an investment], dream, for a second, if everything goes right. In the pre-parade, why should we imagine that this company could yield a 10x or 100x return for us? **And then the pre-mortem really helps you focus on the key challenges the company has to overcome.** There's a version of that the management team themselves needs to conduct, which is — forget about the fact that you have three years of runway. [What if] you only had 12 months left? What would you do? It is really clarifying to focus the mind."*

Two thought experiments. (1) One where everything has gone gloriously right. (2) One where everything has gone catastrophically wrong. Most founders I meet have done a half-version of the first one (usually labeled "vision") and almost none of the second.

## The Pre-Parade

What if everything goes right? **Dream the dream.** The bigger the opportunity, the more it offsets the risk.

![Source:Twitter](/images/what-do-you-have-to-believe-kushner.png)

The pre-parade asks: what is the forward-looking *best-case* scenario? What does this company look like in 5–10 years if everything we underwrote actually works out?

The reason this matters isn't manifestation; it's calibration. If the best-case looks like a $300M outcome, then the math of an early-stage venture investment is already broken. You can't take a 10% chance at a $300M outcome and call that a venture deal; the [expected value](https://investing101.substack.com/p/the-blackstone-of-innovation) doesn't get you to a fund return. **The pre-parade is the discipline of asking, "if I'm right about everything, am I right enough?"**

The very best founders I've met can articulate the pre-parade unprompted. They've thought through what the company looks like in a decade — not in hand-wavy terms, but in [clarity-of-thought](https://investing101.substack.com/p/clarity-of-thought) terms. They can describe the scaffolding of the future they want to build. **And critically, they want the consequences of that future.** They're not just saying it because investors like big numbers; they actually want to live in the world the pre-parade describes. That, I've [written before](https://investing101.substack.com/p/the-hardening-of-the-great-softening), is the single most important characteristic an ambitious person can have: the willingness to want the consequences of what you want.

## The Pre-Mortem

The pre-mortem asks the harder question. What is the forward-looking *worst-case* scenario? What does this company look like in 5–10 years if it goes wrong, and *what specifically went wrong*?

The pre-mortem is harder because it requires a particular kind of paranoia that most founders have been coached not to display. A founder is supposed to project conviction. **But the best founders I've met are paranoid in private and confident in public. They've already imagined every way the thing dies.**

There is a great line in [The Founders](https://www.amazon.com/Founders-Story-Paypal-Entrepreneurs-Shaped/dp/1501197266) about David Sacks's tenure at PayPal:

> *"'**One of the thought experiments I ran through was, if I was running eBay Payments, what would I do to kill PayPal?**' Sacks said. 'And I came up with lots of different things! I was always worried that one day they were gonna figure it out.'"*

That is the pre-mortem in operating mode. Sit in your competitor's chair. Imagine the smartest, best-resourced version of the entity that wants to kill you. What do they do, and how do you survive it?

The OG of this paranoid posture, of course, is Andy Grove, the former Intel CEO. The book he literally titled [Only The Paranoid Survive](https://www.amazon.com/Only-Paranoid-Survive-Exploit-Challenge/dp/0385483821) is the foundational text on this idea. Two passages have stuck with me:

> *"**Business success contains the seeds of its own destruction.** The more successful you are, the more people want a chunk of your business and then another chunk and then another until there is nothing left. I believe that the prime responsibility of a manager is to guard constantly against other people's attacks and to inculcate this guardian attitude in the people under his or her management."*

> *"You need to plan the way a fire department plans: It cannot anticipate where the next fire will be, **so it has to shape an energetic and efficient team that is capable of responding to the unanticipated as well as to any ordinary event.**"*

Notice that Grove isn't telling managers to predict the *specific* death. He's telling them to build the muscle that survives the unpredicted death. **That is the right way to do a pre-mortem. You're not trying to forecast which onion layer fails. You're trying to know that one of them will, and to be the kind of organization that responds.**

Granted, no founder can predict the future with certainty. But there is a meaningful difference between the founder who has war-gamed the death of their company in private and the founder who hasn't. The former has ten more years of survival muscle than the latter, and you can feel it in a 30-minute pitch.

# Therefore, What?

Back to the original question. *What do you have to believe?*

Every investment is a stack of beliefs about a stack of risks. The onion. The dial-by-dial accumulation of comfort and discomfort. **You cannot remove the risk. You can only increase the surface area of the things you've actually thought about.**

The pre-parade forces you to be specific about what success even means. The pre-mortem forces you to be specific about what failure even means. **Most investors and most founders skip both, and then act surprised when the outcome doesn't match the vague gut they were running on.** That isn't strategy. That's just narrative.

[I've written before](https://investing101.substack.com/p/why-bother) about how every decision is made on data and then measured by outcomes. We are wrong 90%+ of the time. But we still bother. We bother because we believe. **And the only honest version of believing is being able to articulate, with specificity, what you have to believe and why.**

So when a founder asks me, after a pass, *"what do I have to believe to be wrong?"* — that's the right question. Because the only investor worth getting a "no" from is the one who can answer it.

**And the only founder worth getting a "yes" from is the one who has already been asking it of themselves.**
