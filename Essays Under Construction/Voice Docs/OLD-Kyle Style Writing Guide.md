# The Kyle Harrison style guide: 40 rules for replicating his voice

Kyle Harrison—General Partner at Contrary, former Index Ventures, Coatue, and TCV—has written **400,000+ words across 150+ essays** on venture capital, company building, and intellectual life. His voice occupies a distinctive niche: the VC insider-critic who reads Dostoevsky, quotes scripture, references *The Last Unicorn*, and drops "Gross." as a standalone paragraph after dismantling a billion-dollar SPAC. This guide distills 16 of his essays into actionable, specific rules that capture his style.

---

## The opening is never the thesis

Harrison's most consistent structural signature is his **oblique opening**. He never begins with what the essay is actually about. Instead, he enters through a side door—a personal anecdote, a fable, a pop culture reference, a comedy bit—that seems entirely unrelated, then bridges to the thesis with a pivot sentence.

**Rule 1: Open with a story that has nothing to do with your topic—then connect it.** In "Stewards of Capital," he retells the Scorpion and the Frog fable in casual language ("But the frog is no dummy. 'Listen,' says the frog, 'I've been around scorpions before. Y'all have a tendency to sting things.'") before pivoting to capital allocation. In "Eat What You Kill," he opens with his toddler's horror at a piñata party. In "Playing Different (Stupider) Games," he starts with a Nate Bargatze comedy bit about baseball. The pivot sentence is always a bridge like: "I was reminded of this fond memory because of what I've wanted to call today's piece for a long time: 'eat what you kill.'"

**Rule 2: The opening cultural reference should come from a surprising domain.** Harrison draws from children's movies (*The Last Unicorn*), Lord of the Rings (Treebeard's speech to Merry), board games (Hungry Hungry Hippos), parenting moments, and comedy specials. The more unexpected the source for a VC essay, the better.

**Rule 3: If you reference your own prior work, use the phrase "If you've been reading my writing for a while."** This appears in multiple essays as a direct address that acknowledges repeat readers while orienting new ones: "If you've been reading my writing for a while, you'll know that I'm far from a venture capital stan."

---

## The rhythm is built on short punches after long swings

Harrison's sentence-level style alternates between analytical complexity and staccato simplicity. The pattern is: build up → snap down. A multi-clause explanatory sentence is followed by a fragment or a short declarative that delivers the verdict.

**Rule 4: Follow every complex analytical sentence with a short, punchy one.** Examples across essays: "Again, with the benefit of hindsight we know that this leads to a basket of overvalued positions, a huge drawdown from your hedge fund, a fire sale via an investment bank, and disgruntled employees making up salacious memos about your firm. **They weren't just playing different games, they were playing stupider games.**" Or: "Over $20B was invested in WeWork between equity and debt. Guess how much is left. $600M."

**Rule 5: Use standalone sentence fragments as paragraphs for maximum emphasis.** Harrison deploys one-word or very short fragment paragraphs as emotional punctuation: "Pretty rough." / "Gross." / "That's capital creation." / "Not the case." / "It IS the game." These fragments function as the writer's physical reaction, as if the reader is watching Harrison process information in real time.

**Rule 6: Start sentences with "But" and "And" constantly.** This is perhaps his most frequent tic—dozens of times per essay. "But the reality is..." / "And that's not only lazy, but it's dangerous." / "But progress is often an act of heresy." These coordinating conjunction openers create a conversational, thinking-out-loud quality that breaks formal prose conventions deliberately.

**Rule 7: Deploy the question-then-pivot structure.** Harrison frequently ends a paragraph or section with a question, then answers it immediately: "But first? I want to talk about the idea of stewardship." / "So what's the stupider game?" / "For me? I'm not a huge fan of giving people more capital after they've demonstrated a real skill at lighting that money on fire."

---

## Paragraphs breathe short, and bold text does the heavy lifting

**Rule 8: Keep most paragraphs to 2–5 sentences.** Harrison breaks up ideas aggressively. Many paragraphs are just 1–2 sentences for transitional beats or emphasis. Longer analytical blocks exist but are punctuated by block quotes, images, or embedded tweets that create white space.

**Rule 9: Bold the 1–2 most important thesis sentences in each section.** Harrison's signature formatting move is embedding bolded thesis statements within running paragraphs. A reader can skim only the bold text and reconstruct the full argument. These aren't headers—they're inline statements: "**the institutionalized belief in 'the greater fool'**" or "**Writing is a lagging indicator of observation.**"

**Rule 10: Use section headers with personality, not labels.** Headers are never dry topic labels. They're mini-hooks with wit, wordplay, or editorial attitude: "What's Hopin'in?" (followed by "*Get it? Like, 'what's happenin'?' Alright. Moving on.*"), "Old-Timey Things," "A Cornucopia of Cowards," "The Latest Magic Carpet Ride" (for a section about a rug company), "Breaking News: You're Wrong. A Lot Of The Time." Each header has a distinct voice. Avoid repetitive structural patterns like "Topic: Subtopic."

## The voice is "smart friend at a dinner party, not professor at a lectern"

Harrison's tone occupies a precise register: **conversational authority with moral earnestness**. He sounds knowledgeable but never academic, opinionated but never dogmatic, funny but never frivolous.

**Rule 11: Blend high-register vocabulary with colloquial language in the same paragraph.** This is Harrison's most distinctive vocabulary trait. He writes "bayesian algorithms" and "monkey-based hunter-gatherer brains" in the same essay. "Institutionalized belief in the greater fool" sits alongside "Ohmygawd. Talk about saying the quiet part out loud." The formal-casual blend signals: I'm educated but I'm not performing education.

**Rule 12: Position yourself as an insider-critic, never a detached observer.** Harrison always writes from inside the VC industry, not above it. He implicates himself: "I'm just as guilty of this." / "Full disclosure, I was working at TCV in 2018 when they led Peloton's Series F at a $3.5B valuation. Today? Peloton's market cap is $3.3B." He earns the right to criticize by showing he has skin in the game.

**Rule 13: Self-deprecate before asserting authority.** Nearly every essay contains a moment of strategic humility: "My wife Camden Harrison, who is smarter than me..." / "I barely knew what business was. Or photosynthesis, but that's a whole different thing; college is hard." / "I had to google it to be sure." / "(aka skimming the Wikipedia article)." This disarms readers before confident analytical claims follow.

**Rule 14: Use "I've written before about..." to build a self-referential web.** This phrase appears 8+ times in some essays. Harrison treats his corpus as an interconnected wiki, constantly linking back to prior work. This creates accumulated authority and rewards repeat readers while giving new readers a rabbit hole.

---

## Humor lives in parentheses and one-word reactions

**Rule 15: Deliver humor through parenthetical asides.** Harrison's primary comedy vehicle is the parenthetical: "(chuckles heartily.)" / "(Schwarting, cover your ears)" / "(Unless you work at SpaceX; in which case, carry on.)" / "(that's a thing people say, right?)" / "(surprise)" / "(aka skimming the Wikipedia article)." These create an aside-to-the-reader intimacy, as if Harrison is whispering a joke while maintaining his analytical composure in the main text.

**Rule 16: When a joke is bad, acknowledge it immediately.** Harrison will make a dad joke or a pun, then instantly call himself out: "What's Hopin'in? *Get it? Like, 'what's happenin'?' Alright. Moving on.*" / "Hence, my rather click-baitey title for this article." This self-awareness transforms groan-worthy wordplay into a comedic bit about the wordplay itself.

**Rule 17: Use irony against industry behavior, never against individuals (mostly).** Harrison satirizes VC culture broadly—"different flavors of axe body spray" for undifferentiated value-add, "a thousand monkeys in a room with a typewriter, except instead of typewriters they have checkbooks." He'll occasionally name someone (Chamath gets a "Gross." reaction), but the satire targets systemic behavior, not personal attacks.

**Rule 18: Deploy meme-native language sparingly but deliberately.** "Idk," "*checks notes*," "stan," "NPC," "SOL"—these internet-native terms appear once or twice per essay, never more. They signal cultural fluency without overwhelming the analytical voice.

---

## Arguments are built through escalating layers, not linear logic

**Rule 19: Structure arguments inductively—circle the idea from multiple angles until the conclusion feels inevitable.** Harrison does not write thesis → proof → conclusion. He writes: observation → context → angle 1 → angle 2 → angle 3 → counterpoint → moral close. In "Building the Global Knowledge Graph," the structure is an escalating series of analogies (Roam is Excel → Roam is GitHub → Roam is Google → Roam is the World Wide Web), each expanding the scope until the grand vision feels natural rather than hyperbolic.

**Rule 20: Always include a counterpoint or caveat section.** Harrison consistently devotes a section to steelmanning the opposing view or qualifying his own claims: "A Hesitant Case Study" in his Greater Fool essay, "The Place for Pessimists" in "The Coward's Conviction," "So what was good about 'The User Method?'" in the Lean Startup essay. He often signals these with explicit disclaimers: "I debated leaving this part out because I'm not sure I believe it. So consider this a mental exercise, not a statement of fact."

**Rule 21: Name contradictions rather than resolving them.** Harrison holds multiple positions simultaneously and names the tension explicitly: "I can simultaneously think a lot of what [Musk] does is gross, while having immense respect for his ideas and execution." / "I recognize the need for liquidity, both for founders trying to live their lives, and VCs trying to generate returns for LPs. So none of this is a blanket statement of good or bad." This intellectual honesty is a deliberate stylistic choice that builds trust.

**Rule 22: Use the dialectical structure—thesis, antithesis, synthesis—especially for concept essays.** "Is The Lean Startup a Stupid Way to Start a Company?" follows this precisely: the Lean Startup method (thesis) → the User Method's challenge (antithesis) → both are tools in a toolkit (synthesis). Harrison is a synthesizer, not a polemicist.

## Every abstract claim gets a named company with a specific number

**Rule 23: Attach at least one named company example and one specific number to every abstract point.** Harrison never lets a claim float without grounding. When he says the best companies outperform the rest, he doesn't stop there: "top 5 median at 74x vs. 16x for the rest... If you're a business expecting $500M of revenue next year that's the difference between being a $5B company or a $17B company." When discussing capital destruction: "WeWork: over $20B invested. Guess how much is left. $600M."

**Rule 24: Use the "~" tilde for approximate numbers.** This is a signature tic: ~$200B, ~80 deals, ~12.7%. The tilde signals intellectual honesty about precision—Harrison doesn't pretend to have exact figures when he's estimating.

**Rule 25: Introduce data with a dramatic reveal structure.** Rather than stating numbers flatly, Harrison builds to them: "And then, wouldn't you know it, COVID restrictions started to lift, people could travel again, and Hopin's usefulness **dropped from 15K events per month to 158.**" The number lands as a punchline. Similarly: "Guess how much is left. $600M."

## Concepts get vivid names and are defined through chains

**Rule 26: Coin vivid, memorable names for every framework.** Harrison doesn't say "large VC firms optimizing for AUM"—he calls them **"Capital Agglomerators."** He doesn't say "VC firms with disciplined fund sizes"—he calls them **"Cottage Keepers"** and **"Puritans."** Other coinages: "institutionalized belief in the greater fool," "atomic units of thought," "panic write," "short-term careerists," "the valuation stick," "talent vortex." The coinage always uses vivid physical or cultural imagery.

**Rule 27: Define concepts through sequential chain-building.** When Harrison defines "stewardship," he doesn't give a dictionary definition. He builds a chain: "First, there is capital creation. The people who actually own the money... That can be as simple as a public school teacher earning their pension. Or as massive as someone bootstrapping a company to $88M in revenue and $80M in profit. That's capital creation. Capital allocation is the act of those capital owners entrusting someone else to allocate their capital. Family offices trust fund-of-funds. Fund-of-funds trust general partners. General partners trust founders." Pattern: name it → low-end example → high-end example → chain the relationships.

**Rule 28: Introduce concepts through other people's frameworks first, then add your own.** Harrison's primary intellectual method is synthesis. Nearly every major idea enters via a named authority—Haidt's elephant and rider, Christensen's jobs-to-be-done, Elad Gil's index companies, Everett Randle's retail analogy—before Harrison adds his own interpretation. He positions himself as a curator of smart people's ideas, not as a lone oracle. Pattern: attribution → explanation → example → Harrison's commentary.

## The reference library spans from Dostoevsky to The Hunger Games

**Rule 29: Draw references from at least 3 wildly different domains per essay.** A single Harrison essay might reference: a Kauffman Foundation academic study, a *Margin Call* movie quote, a Biblical passage from Revelation, a Fred Wilson blog post, a FRED economic data chart, and a Nate Bargatze comedy special. This eclecticism is deliberate—it signals breadth of reading and creates unexpected connections.

**Rule 30: Embed tweets as evidence alongside academic citations.** Harrison treats Twitter/X as a primary source alongside books and papers. Embedded tweets serve as real-time evidence of cultural phenomena, expert opinions, and industry reactions. They also create visual variety in the essay.

**Rule 31: Quote family members as authorities alongside published intellectuals.** "My wife, per usual, explains it better" and "My Dad calls it the SALY principle (Same As Last Year)" sit alongside citations of Schwarzman, Buffett, and Munger. This grounds analytical work in lived experience and creates warmth.

**Rule 32: Use religious and moral frameworks without apology.** Harrison is openly LDS and quotes scripture, Confucius, and church leaders alongside VC metrics. In "The Puritans of Venture Capital," the entire framing is a religious metaphor, including a direct quote from Revelation: "because thou art lukewarm... I will spue thee out of my mouth." In "The Coward's Conviction," he writes: "Bit of a tangent. I'm a very religious person." This unapologetic integration of faith and finance is a distinctive part of his voice.

## Closings shift register and land on moral conviction

**Rule 33: Close with a tonal shift—from analytical to personal/aspirational.** Harrison never just summarizes. His closings change emotional register: from data-driven analysis to moral exhortation ("We could be so much better than we are."), from investigation to literary flourish ("May the odds be ever in your favor."), from combative debate to balanced synthesis ("The user method is a tool, just like lean startup is a tool."). The closing paragraph is always the most emotionally direct section of the essay.

**Rule 34: End with either a borrowed aphorism or a short declarative personal conviction.** Two modes: (1) Close with someone else's words that encapsulate his philosophy—Mark Twain's "It ain't what you don't know that gets you into trouble," Flannery O'Connor's "I write because I don't know what I think until I read what I say." (2) Close with a compact personal statement: "In business building, capital allocation isn't just a VC's game. It IS the game." / "I don't want to be a meme. I want to be a man."

**Rule 35: Thank specific people who helped shape the essay.** Most Substack essays end with acknowledgments: "Thanks to [name] for reading drafts and shaping my thinking." This creates community and signals intellectual collaboration.

---

## Pacing alternates between acceleration and deliberate deceleration

**Rule 36: Open fast with the anecdote, slow down for the analytical middle, accelerate through the evidence, then decelerate for the counterpoint before a punchy close.** The breathe-in/breathe-out rhythm is consistent across essays. In "Institutionalized Belief in the Greater Fool": fast opening (The Last Unicorn) → medium context (what's happening at Hopin) → rapid-fire capital destruction examples (WeWork, Peloton, Lyft, Quibi, Bird) → deliberate slowdown ("A Hesitant Case Study" on Stripe vs. Adyen) → punchy moral close.

**Rule 37: Use rapid-fire question-answer sequences for your strongest points.** When Harrison is most passionate, the pace accelerates into staccato Q&A: "Do I approve of his game? Hell no. Do I want to play his game? Absolutely not. But his game is not even the stupider game! Is it an awful game? Yes. Is he an absolute ass-hat for playing his game? Indeed. But is it a stupid game? If the goal of the game was to make millions of dollars, then no."

## The recurring philosophical spine underneath the VC analysis

**Rule 38: Every essay, no matter how industry-specific, should connect to a deeper human truth.** Harrison's essays about VC are never just about VC. "Stewards of Capital" is really about trust and responsibility. "The Coward's Conviction" is about intellectual courage vs. laziness. "A Tale of Two Markets" is about the illusion of ubiquitous success. "Playing Different (Stupider) Games" is about understanding other people's incentives. The VC analysis is the vehicle; the human insight is the destination.

**Rule 39: Return to the metaphor of "the stick" across essays.** Harrison's most persistent recurring metaphor is the two-ended stick—every decision, every advantage, every choice has an unavoidable flip side. "When you pick up one end of the valuation stick, you end up picking up the other end that is fraught with significant expectations of outcome." / "Choosing to be optimistic is picking up one end of the stick. So what's the other? Optimism requires responsibility." This metaphor functions as his core epistemological tool.

**Rule 40: Frame the master tension as "conviction vs. consensus" and "optimism vs. intellectual laziness."** Across the full corpus, Harrison's unified thesis is: the world rewards people who do the hard intellectual work of forming independent convictions and maintaining disciplined optimism, and punishes those who follow the herd, default to pessimism, or mistake activity for rigor. Every essay is, in some way, an exploration of this theme. As he puts it: "Pessimism will always sound smarter. And it will always play into the hand of someone desperately trying to avoid failure. But progress is often an act of heresy. And optimism is hard work."

---

## Summary of the Harrison formula

The complete architecture, distilled: **Start with a surprising story from outside your domain → Bridge to your thesis with a conversational pivot → Provide historical context or a "crash course" → Name your framework with a vivid coinage → Build evidence through layered examples from eclectic sources, each with specific numbers → Bold your thesis sentences inline → Include at least one honest counterpoint or caveat → Close with a tonal shift to moral conviction in 1–3 short, punchy sentences → Thank your collaborators.** 

Throughout, maintain the voice of an intellectually curious insider who is self-deprecating but confident, analytical but morally earnest, data-driven but story-first—someone who reads Russian novels and scrolls VC Twitter with equal enthusiasm, and who believes writing is not a display of knowledge but a tool for discovering what you actually think.
