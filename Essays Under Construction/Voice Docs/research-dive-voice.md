# The Research Dive: A Style Guide

A comprehensive pattern guide for researching and writing a long-form, deeply-sourced research synthesis in the mold of *The Anduril Thesis*, Kyle W. Harrison and Contrary Research's ~100K-word, six-part company report.

The intent of this document is to be a faithful reference for producing book-length research dives: the kind of piece that takes a single company (or idea) and earns its length by situating it inside a hundred years of history, the structure of an industry, the shape of a conflict, and a forward-looking thesis. It is the companion to [KYLES-VOICE.md](./KYLES-VOICE.md), which governs the weekly-essay voice. Where that guide is about a Saturday-morning panic-written essay, this one is about a research project that runs for weeks and synthesizes hundreds of sources into a single argument.

**Scope note:** These rules describe the long-form *research dive*, a 20K–100K-word report built on primary sources, expert-network interviews, books, and original synthesis. They borrow citation and anti-AI discipline from the [Contrary Research memo rulebook](https://research.contrary.com) but scale the ambition up from a 3–5K-word memo to a book. When the two conflict, default to the memo discipline on mechanics (sourcing, numbers, anti-AI) and to this guide on architecture (framing, flow, history).

---

## 1. Purpose & Philosophy of the Research Dive

1. **A research dive earns its length or it doesn't get the length.** *The Anduril Thesis* is ~100K words because Anduril cannot be understood without the hundred-year history of the defense industry, the structure of modern conflict, and the geopolitics that produced the company. Length is a consequence of necessity, never a goal. If a section can be cut without weakening the thesis, cut it. State the length-justification explicitly to the reader early, the way the introduction does: *"Part of the reason for that length is that Anduril as a company has been shaped by the last 100 years of the defense industry's history, an understanding of which is crucial in understanding Anduril."*

2. **The subject is the lens, not the whole picture.** The company (or idea) at the center is the reason for the dive, but most of the word count goes to the world that produced it. Roughly: spend the first half establishing the industry, the history, and the conflict, and only then turn to the company itself. The reader should arrive at the company already understanding why it had to exist.

3. **Write to understand, then write to explain.** The first draft is for the writer figuring out what the argument is. The published version is for the reader. Don't publish the thinking-out-loud draft; publish the version that knows where it's going from the first sentence.

4. **Every dive has one load-bearing thesis, stated plainly.** For Anduril it is: the US has squandered its defense-industrial lead, modern conflict has changed underneath an unprepared military, and Anduril is attempting to reboot the arsenal of democracy. Everything in the report either builds toward that claim or supports it. If a paragraph serves neither, it's a digression.

5. **The thesis is a claim about the world, not a rating.** A research dive is not a buy/sell recommendation. It is an argument about why something matters, what has to be true for it to succeed, and what could break it. Land on understanding, not a verdict.

6. **Take the subject seriously enough to argue with it.** Anduril's own framing ("rebooting the arsenal of democracy") is quoted and engaged, not parroted. The dive uses the company's self-description as a starting point and then tests it against history, competitors, and data. Steelman the company, then pressure-test it.

7. **Bias is disclosed, not hidden.** Contrary is an investor; the report says so. Disclose the relationship up front and let the reader weigh the argument knowing where the writer sits. False neutrality is less trustworthy than named interest.

8. **Depth is a form of respect, for the subject and the reader.** The reader who commits to a 100K-word report is owed primary sources, specific numbers, and original synthesis, not a longer version of the press release. Reward the commitment.

## 2. Framing & Thesis Architecture

9. **Open at the widest possible altitude, then descend.** *The Anduril Thesis* opens not with Anduril but with Herodotus and the nature of conflict itself: *"There have always been people willing to use violence to pursue their interests. Armed conflict between groups is as old as history itself."* Start with the oldest, broadest version of the problem and narrow toward the specific company over several pages.

10. **Establish the stakes before the subject.** Before Anduril is named, the introduction establishes that great-power war in a nuclear age is catastrophic, that deterrence is what prevents it, and that the US has let its deterrent decay. By the time the company appears, the reader already believes the problem is urgent. Earn the stakes first; introduce the subject as the response to them.

11. **Name the subject only after the reader wants it named.** Anduril enters the introduction with *"That's where Anduril comes in"*, after several paragraphs of problem-setting. The subject should feel inevitable by the time it arrives, like the answer to a question the reader is already asking.

12. **State the two or three reasons the subject matters, numbered and specific.** The introduction gives exactly two reasons Anduril could be among the most impactful companies of the century: the size of the market ($850B DoD, $400B from NATO allies) and the stakes of deterrence. Frame importance as an enumerated, quantified claim, not an adjective.

13. **Give the reader a map of the parts, with one sentence each.** After the thesis, *The Anduril Thesis* lays out its six parts in a numbered list, each with a one-sentence description of what it covers and why. For a book-length dive, the reader needs the table of contents in prose form near the top. This is the one place the weekly-essay rule "don't preview your structure" is reversed, at book length, the roadmap is a courtesy.

14. **Frame the subject as a counter-position.** Anduril is explained as counter-positioned against a defense industry of *"perverse incentives, regulatory capture, and special interests."* A research dive is sharper when the subject is defined against the status quo it rejects. What is this company *not*? What is it reacting to?

15. **The frame should be falsifiable.** "Anduril is attempting to transform the model of the American defense industry for the first time in almost 100 years" is a claim that could be wrong, and the report acknowledges it could fail. A frame that can't be wrong isn't a thesis, it's a slogan.

16. **Use a historical analogy as a framing spine, sparingly.** The "arsenal of democracy" framing (FDR, WWII industrial mobilization) runs through the report as a recurring reference. One well-chosen historical analogy can organize a whole dive, but pick one and earn it with history, don't sprinkle a dozen.

17. **Set up the dichotomy you'll spend the report complicating.** Exquisite legacy platforms vs. attritable autonomous systems; the old defense primes vs. the new entrants; deterrence vs. warfighting. Establish the tension early, then spend the report showing it's more complicated than a clean binary.

18. **The title should name the argument, not the company.** *The Anduril Thesis* is a thesis, not a profile. The title signals that the reader is getting an argument with a point of view, not a neutral encyclopedia entry.

## 3. Structure & Flow (the architecture of a long dive)

19. **Build in parts, then chapters, then sections.** *The Anduril Thesis* uses Parts (I–VI) → chapters → sub-sections. At book length, a flat header structure collapses; the reader needs the nested scaffolding to know where they are. Use part dividers as real visual breaks.

20. **History first, subject second, future last.** The canonical shape: (1) industry evolution and history → (2) the present state of the industry and modern conflict → (3) what changed / the reintroduction of urgency → (4) the thesis → (5) the company breakdown → (6) the future (tailwinds and risks). Context accumulates; the company is the payoff, not the premise.

21. **Each part should hand off to the next.** The history of the defense industry sets up why the industry is broken; the broken industry sets up modern conflict's new demands; the new demands set up the company's thesis. Every part ends pointing at the next. No part is an island.

22. **Write the thesis section last, even though it appears in the middle.** The argument can only be stated cleanly once all the supporting research exists. Like the KYLES-VOICE producing flow, the thesis is written after everything that grounds it, then placed where the reader needs it.

23. **Front-load the "why should I care," back-load the granular detail.** The product-by-product company breakdown (the most granular, least narrative part) comes late, after the reader is invested. Don't open with a spec sheet.

24. **Vary chapter shape deliberately.** Some chapters are sweeping historical narrative; some are dense bulleted evidence dumps (the China threat section is largely a bulleted list of escalating facts); some are close company analysis. Uneven shapes are a feature, symmetrical, same-length sections are an AI tell.

25. **Use the bulleted evidence cascade when the point is accumulation.** When the argument is "the threat is bigger than you think," a long bulleted list of escalating, individually-sourced facts (China's 900% budget increase, 536x shipbuilding capacity, 80% of the drone market) hits harder than prose. Reach for the cascade when sheer volume is the argument.

26. **Signpost transitions between altitudes.** When moving from history to the present, or from the industry to the company, tell the reader: *"Within that context, we'll unpack each of Anduril's products."* The reader of a long document needs to be told when the camera is about to move.

27. **Recapitulate the thesis at the seams.** At the start of a new part, briefly re-anchor: here's what we established, here's where we're going. In a 100K-word document the reader has forgotten the introduction; remind them without condescending.

28. **End the dive where the stakes are highest, not where the analysis is tidiest.** The final part is the future, tailwinds and risks for both the company and the country. Close on what's at stake going forward, not on a summary of what was argued.

29. **The company breakdown is itself a structured sub-dive.** Within the company part, follow a consistent internal order: vision → overview → product-by-product → funding/valuation → market → competition. Each product gets the same treatment so the reader can compare across them.

30. **Don't let any single section run past the reader's working memory without an anchor.** Long stretches need an internal signpost, a bolded claim, a sub-header, or a quote-card every page or so. A wall of undifferentiated prose at book length loses the reader.

## 4. History as Foundation

31. **Treat history as load-bearing, not decorative.** The first ~70 pages of *The Anduril Thesis* are history: radar in WWII, the Cold War golden age, post-Cold-War consolidation, the "end of history." This is not throat-clearing. The history explains why the present industry is broken and why the company had to counter-position the way it did. Research the history as rigorously as the company.

32. **Go back further than feels necessary.** The report reaches to Herodotus, the British Empire's 14.3 million square miles, Tolkien in the trenches of WWI. The deep-history opening establishes that the writer has done the reading and that the present is one chapter in a long story.

33. **Use history to explain the present, not to show off.** Every historical passage pays off in a present-day claim. WWI and WWII establish that war became negative-sum; that sets up nuclear deterrence; that sets up why the US can't afford to lose its deterrent edge. If a historical fact doesn't earn a present-day inference, cut it.

34. **Find the inflection points and name them.** The report identifies specific hinges: the World Wars as the moment war became negative-sum, the end of the Cold War as the "end of history" mirage, the rise of China as the end of unipolarity. A good historical narrative is a sequence of named turning points, each with a date and a consequence.

35. **Personalize history with a single human detail.** *"by 1918 all but one of my close friends were dead"* (Tolkien) does more than a casualty statistic. One well-chosen human quote anchors an abstract historical claim in a felt reality. Use these sparingly and let them land.

36. **Quantify the historical shifts.** The British Empire went from 14.3 million square miles (a quarter of Earth's land) to 94.5K square miles (less than 1%). History told in specific before/after numbers is more convincing than history told in adjectives.

37. **Show the industry's structure changing over time.** The defense-industry history traces a clear arc: wartime mobilization → Cold War abundance → post-Cold-War consolidation and cost controls → stagnation. The reader should understand the present industry as the end-state of a process, not a static fact.

38. **Use history to pre-empt the "this is just hype" objection.** By grounding the company in a century of industrial decline, the report makes the case that the opportunity is structural and decades in the making, not a momentary fad. History is the best defense against the charge of recency bias.

39. **Cite historians and primary-source books, not just press.** The bibliography leans on serious books: *The Kill Chain*, *Skunk Works*, *The Idea Factory*, *Freedom's Forge*, *Destined for War*, *Chip War*. Historical claims should rest on the books historians and practitioners actually wrote, cited by author and date.

40. **Let the history imply the thesis before you state it.** By the time the reader finishes the history, they should already suspect the conclusion. The thesis section then confirms what the history has been building toward, rather than introducing something new.

## 5. Research Process & Source Gathering

41. **Begin with the anchor: what does the subject actually do, to whom, for how much.** Before any history or thesis, establish in your own words what the company is. The Contrary research flow's first goal is to answer "What does it sell, to whom, for how much, how big is it?" without notes. Everything else builds on a correct anchor.

42. **Work the eight content goals in dependency order.** Anchor → founders → funding → recent events → customers/competitors → tailwinds → risks → reference material. Early goals ground the later ones; you can't write a credible risk section before you know what the company is.

43. **Exhaust primary sources before reaching for secondary.** Company filings, press releases, founder interviews, government databases (USAspending.gov, SEC EDGAR, FCC) are Tier 1. Build the spine of the report on sources where the company, a regulator, or a public market is speaking directly. Lowest interpretation distance wins.

44. **Hunt the podcast and the long-form interview, that's where the color lives.** The densest analytical material on a private company is usually a founder's 90-minute podcast appearance. Search YouTube for re-uploads with transcripts. For Anduril, Palmer Luckey's interviews carry a large share of the company's stated worldview. Mine the long-form interviews thoroughly.

45. **Use expert-network transcripts as a distinct, high-value source class.** *The Anduril Thesis* cites dozens of Tegus/AlphaSense expert calls, former Anduril PMs, former DoD CIOs, competitors at Northrop and Lockheed. These insider interviews are how a dive sees past the company's marketing. When you have access, weight them heavily; when citing them, anonymize the speaker by role and date ("Former Project Manager at Anduril Industries; September 22, 2021").

46. **Read the books, not just the summaries.** The Anduril bibliography is ~29 books deep, from Mearsheimer's *Conventional Deterrence* to Karp's *The Technological Republic*. A research dive's authority comes partly from having read the canonical books in the field. Cite them by author and publication date.

47. **Triangulate every load-bearing fact across at least two independent sources.** If a number is going to carry weight in the argument, don't trust a single source for it. The China shipbuilding figure, the DoD budget, the funding totals, each should reconcile across independent reporting before it enters the draft.

48. **When a high-value source is paywalled, name the gap explicitly, don't fake it.** If a critical interview is behind a paywall and unavailable, flag it rather than pretending to have consumed it. Never write around a source you haven't actually read as though you read it.

49. **Keep a running sources pool with a URL on every fact.** Every figure, quote, and claim carries its source URL from the moment it enters your notes. Retrofitting citations after drafting is how errors and fabrications creep in. Cite as you gather.

50. **Date every source.** *The Anduril Thesis* sources list carries a date on every entry. A defense fact from 2017 and one from 2025 mean different things. Timestamp everything so staleness is visible.

51. **Reconcile funding round-by-round against the stated total.** Don't take a headline "total raised" number on faith. Build the funding history from per-round press releases and confirm the rounds sum to the total. Discrepancies usually mean a missing round or a misreported figure.

52. **Verify founder and company credentials independently.** A credential that appears only on the company's own About page is a claim, not a fact. Cross-reference against LinkedIn, interviews, and third-party reporting before stating it as established.

53. **Distinguish what you know, what you infer, and what you're told.** Keep clear in your notes which claims are sourced facts, which are your synthesis, and which are the company's assertions. The reader should be able to tell the three apart in the final prose.

54. **Over-collect, then cut.** Gather far more than the report can hold, then keep only what serves the thesis. A dive that uses 40% of what was researched reads denser and more authoritative than one that strains to include everything found.

## 6. Source Hierarchy & Evidence Standards

55. **Rank every source by interpretation distance.** Tier 1: the company, regulators, public markets, and government data speaking directly. Tier 2: trade press and credible named analysts with editorial standards. Tier 3: mainstream press for confirmed events. Tier 4: aggregators and market-research reports, used only with explicit "estimated" framing. Know which tier each citation sits in.

56. **Build the analytical claims on Tier 1 and Tier 2, never on Tier 4.** Market-sizing aggregators (Mordor, Grand View, Statista) and revenue estimators (Sacra, GetLatka) can seed a TAM figure but cannot carry a thesis-level claim. If a Tier 4 source is the only thing available, frame the number as "one estimate of," not as fact.

57. **Never cite Statista or Wikipedia as a primary source.** They are summary layers over upstream sources. Trace the claim to the primary and cite that. Wikipedia is acceptable only for definitional/historical context where it cites traceable primaries.

58. **For market data, prefer the primary over the recycled aggregate.** Aggregated market-research reports often recycle a single primary study. Find the primary research and cite it directly with its date.

59. **Frame contested or estimated numbers as estimates.** When a figure is genuinely uncertain (a private company's revenue, a secondary-market valuation), say "reportedly," "estimated at," or "one estimate puts it at." Don't launder an estimate into a fact by stating it flatly.

60. **Calibrate investor and company sources for promotional tilt.** A16z's essay on defense tech, or Anduril's own mission document, are usable but tilted. Quote them for what the company believes, not as neutral evidence that the belief is correct.

61. **Government and public-market data are the gold standard for scale claims.** Budget figures, contract awards, market caps, and shipbuilding capacity should come from USAspending.gov, SEC filings, the Navy Secretary's own statements, and the like, not from secondary characterizations of them.

62. **When two good sources disagree, surface the disagreement.** If credible sources give different figures for China's manufacturing share or the DoD's asset base, present the range and note the dispute rather than silently picking one. The honest move is to show the reader the uncertainty.

## 7. Citation & Sourcing Mechanics

63. **Every numeric data point gets its own source.** "$850 billion agency which holds $4.1 trillion in assets, employs 3.4 million service members, operates on 4.8K sites in 160 countries." Each of those numbers needs to be traceable. When one source backs several numbers in a phrase, attach it to the connecting clause rather than repeating it on each figure.

64. **Cite the delta, not just the level.** When a change is the point ("the Chinese military budget increased by 900 percent from 1990 to 2017"), source the change, with both endpoints and the date range. A level without its trajectory undersells the argument.

65. **Quotes are reproduced exactly and attributed precisely.** Long quotes from Chris Brose, Palmer Luckey, and Shyam Sankar are block-quoted verbatim with the speaker named. Don't paraphrase a quote and present it in quotation marks; don't trim a quote in a way that changes its meaning.

66. **Attribute by name and role on first reference, then by short name.** "As Chris Brose explains," then "Brose." For insider/expert-network sources who must stay anonymous, attribute by role and date instead ("a former Anduril program manager").

67. **Put the source on the claim, not in a vague gesture.** "Some sources indicate" is weak. Either you have the source (cite it) or you don't (cut the claim or frame it as your inference). Replace hand-waving attributions with specific ones.

68. **Cite the speaker's incentive when it bears on the claim.** When Palmer Luckey describes China's threat, the reader should know he runs a defense company. When a Navy Secretary describes shipbuilding capacity, note the office. Incentive disclosure makes the citation stronger, not weaker.

69. **Keep a full, numbered, dated source list at the end.** The report closes with a numbered bibliography grouped by type (news, expert networks, books). A book-length dive needs a real bibliography, not just inline links. Group by source class and date every entry.

70. **Verify every link and citation before publishing.** Dead links and misattributed quotes destroy credibility faster than anything else. Do a final pass that confirms every source resolves and every quote matches its origin.

71. **Don't restate the source mid-sentence as filler.** "According to a report by the RAND Corporation, which is a think tank that studies defense issues, the report said…" Cite RAND, state the finding, move on. The citation carries the provenance; the prose shouldn't re-narrate it.

72. **Hyperlinks anchor to the claim phrase, never to "click here" or to a bare URL.** Anchor the link to the few words it supports, the way a memo links the specific figure or the specific assertion.

## 8. Synthesis & Weaving

73. **The value is in the synthesis, not the collection.** Anyone can gather 140 news articles. The dive's worth is in connecting the WWII radar program, Cold War procurement, and a modern drone startup into a single coherent argument. The connective tissue, the "therefore" between facts, is the actual product.

74. **Every fact should advance the argument, not just inform.** A research dive is not a Wikipedia article. Each fact is selected because it moves the thesis forward. If a fact is true but inert, it doesn't belong.

75. **Move from evidence to inference explicitly.** After a block of facts, tell the reader what they mean: the China bullet-cascade is followed by the inference that the threat needs to be "a wake up call for the US military." Don't make the reader assemble the argument; assemble it for them.

76. **Weave quotes into your own argument, don't outsource the argument to them.** Use Brose and Luckey to provide evidence and authority for a claim you are making, not to make the claim for you. Introduce the quote, drop it, then interpret it in your own words.

77. **Let sources disagree on the page and adjudicate.** When Shyam Sankar says China's manufacturing share, "while this did not equal the US' dominance at the height of its postwar power, it was still a massive amount," the report uses the quote to calibrate a claim rather than to settle it. Hold competing characterizations and reason between them.

78. **Connect across the parts, call back and call forward.** The history section should be referenced when the company's counter-positioning is explained; the conflict section should be referenced when a product's purpose is described. The parts are one argument, not six essays.

79. **Translate jargon the first time, then use it.** "Kill chain," "JADC2," "attritable," "exquisite platforms," and define each on first use in plain language, then use the term of art. The reader should never hit an undefined acronym.

80. **Find the through-line metaphor and let it recur.** "Rebooting the arsenal of democracy," "playing a different game" (Brose), "closing the kill chain." A few recurring framings give a long document coherence. Reuse them deliberately at the seams.

## 9. Numbers, Specificity & Claims

81. **Numbers are always specific and always sourced.** "$850 billion," "536 times more shipbuilding capacity," "53% of the world's shipbuilding," "0.1%", never "a lot," "vastly more," or "a tiny fraction." Specificity is the texture of credibility.

82. **Dramatize the comparison, but let the real numbers do the work.** "China has over 536 times more shipbuilding capacity than the US" and "53% … The US has 0.1%" are dramatic because the true figures are dramatic, not because of adjectives. Find the comparison that makes the number land, then state it plainly.

83. **Anchor numbers in time.** "As of March 2025," "In 2023," "by 2024." A defense statistic without a date is a liability. Every figure carries the date it was true.

84. **Use past tense for time-stamped facts and statements.** A statement made at a fixed past event is reported in the past tense ("Biden acknowledged," "the Navy Secretary stated"), not the dramatic present. The present tense makes stale facts read as current and is an AI tell.

85. **Don't over-extrapolate from a narrow figure.** A statistic about AI research papers doesn't license a claim about total Chinese technological supremacy. The scope of the source sets the scope of the claim. Widening the claim beyond what the data supports is a fact-check failure dressed as emphasis.

86. **Round honestly and show your unit.** "~33%," "94.5K square miles," "$4.1 trillion." Use tildes for approximations, keep units explicit, and don't imply false precision the source doesn't support.

87. **When a number is contested, give the range and the lower bound.** "536 times … and that estimate is actually probably low" is more honest and more powerful than a single point estimate stated as certain. Show the floor.

## 10. Voice & Register (at book length)

88. **Authoritative but not academic.** The register is that of a well-read analyst explaining something they understand deeply to an intelligent peer, not a textbook, not a term paper, not a press release. Confident on synthesis, precise on facts, plain in diction.

89. **Sentences carry weight; vary their length.** Mix long explanatory sentences with short declarative hammers ("The end of history was a mirage."). A long document of uniformly medium sentences reads flat and machine-made.

90. **Let conviction show without hype.** The writer clearly believes the thesis matters, and says so directly ("global stability (and survival) may very well depend on…"). Earnest conviction, grounded in evidence, beats both detached neutrality and breathless hype.

91. **Third person and the editorial "we."** *The Anduril Thesis* uses "we" for the authoring team ("we've organized our coverage into six parts") and otherwise stays in the third person. Avoid second-person "you" in body prose, it's a marketing-copy tell.

92. **Explain like the reader is smart but new to the topic.** Don't assume the reader knows what JADC2 is; don't condescend by over-explaining what a drone is. Pitch the explanation at an intelligent generalist encountering the specifics for the first time.

93. **Keep the moral stakes visible without moralizing.** The human cost of war, China's surveillance state, the risk of great-power conflict, these are stated soberly and let to carry their own weight. Don't editorialize on top of facts that are already damning.

## 11. Anti-AI Discipline (read this section twice)

94. **No em-dashes. Zero. Full stop.** The em-dash (`—`, including the spaced `phrase — phrase` form) is the single clearest AI tell. Replace every one with a comma, a period, a colon, parentheses, or a semicolon depending on the relationship. This rule overrides the weekly-essay guide, which permits em-dashes; in a research dive meant to read as human and rigorous, treat every em-dash as a defect. *(Note: the source draft uses en-dashes/hyphens in places like "one side won at the expense of another", keep those as commas or periods in your own prose.)*

95. **Ban the "it's not X, it's Y" inversion.** "This isn't a slight innovation, it's a paradigm shift." "Anduril isn't just a defense contractor, it's a software company." Humans rarely build analytical prose around this rhetorical flip; LLMs reach for it constantly. Rewrite every instance as a direct claim about Y: "Anduril is a software company that sells hardware."

96. **Kill the tell-phrases.** Forbidden: "delve into," "it's important to note," "in today's fast-paced world," "navigate the landscape," "leverage" (as a verb), "furthermore," "moreover," "in conclusion," "tapestry," "underscores," "plays a pivotal role," "stands as a testament," "at the end of the day," "when it comes to." Cut on sight.

97. **No vague dramatic abstractions standing in for specifics.** "Paradigm shift," "inflection point," "sea change," "watershed moment," "structural break," "fundamental shift" are only allowed when immediately operationalized with specifics ("an inflection point: the 2022 invasion of Ukraine turned attritable drones from a theory into a daily reality"). Used as a bare flourish, they're filler. The same goes for bare "structural" and for "genuine/genuinely", cut them or replace with the concrete thing.

98. **No empty transitions or self-answering colon cadence.** Don't open paragraphs with "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover," "On the other hand," unless the connection is earned. And avoid the repeated "short clause, colon, self-answer" rhythm ("The problem: data is scarce. The bet: train on reality."). One or two are fine; as a default cadence it reads as machine scaffolding. Rewrite as plain declaratives.

99. **No promotional clichés, marketing metaphors, universal-truth filler, or symmetrical structure.** Cut "natural fit," "no-brainer," "perfect storm," "best of both worlds," "win-win"; cut consumer-app metaphors ("at your fingertips," "with the tap of a button"); cut universal-truth scaffolding that would fit any topic ("As the industry evolves," "As competition intensifies," "In an increasingly complex world"). And avoid suspiciously even structure, every section the same length, every list exactly three items, every paragraph the same shape. Real research has uneven, argument-driven shapes; vary your verbs across adjacent sentences, keep paragraphs under ~5 sentences, break run-ons welded together by semicolons, and don't end sections by narrating what isn't known ("whether this proves an advantage remains to be seen"), a real risk goes in the risks section, stated as a risk.

---

## 12. Things to Avoid

100. **Avoid these failure modes, the whole list:** Don't summarize when you could synthesize, a paragraph that restates sources without adding an inference should be cut. Don't pad to length; a dive earns words by necessity, never by stretching. Don't bury the thesis under throat-clearing; the reader should know the argument by the end of the introduction. Don't smuggle estimates in as facts; frame uncertain numbers as estimates with their source and date. Don't parrot the company's marketing; quote it as belief and test it against evidence. Don't let any section run without an anchor, a bolded claim, a sub-header, or a quote every page or so. Don't fake having read a source you didn't read. Don't moralize on top of facts that already carry their weight. Don't write present-tense for past events, or undated numbers, or unsourced claims. And above all, don't let the prose read as machine-made, no em-dashes, no "not X but Y," no tell-phrases, no symmetrical filler. A research dive's entire value proposition is that a careful human did the reading, checked the numbers, and built an argument no model could assemble from a single prompt. Protect that.

## Quick Reference: Checklist for a Research Dive

Before publishing a long-form research dive, verify:

- [ ] The thesis is stated plainly in the introduction and is falsifiable.
- [ ] The opening establishes stakes and history *before* naming the subject.
- [ ] A prose roadmap of the parts appears near the top.
- [ ] History is load-bearing, every historical passage pays off in a present-day inference.
- [ ] The structure runs history → present → what changed → thesis → company → future.
- [ ] Each part hands off to the next; the thesis is recapped at the seams.
- [ ] Every load-bearing fact is triangulated across two independent sources.
- [ ] Every number is specific, dated, and individually sourced.
- [ ] Claims rest on Tier 1/Tier 2 sources; Tier 4 numbers are framed as estimates.
- [ ] No Statista or Wikipedia as a primary citation.
- [ ] Quotes are verbatim, attributed by name and role (or anonymized by role + date for expert calls).
- [ ] A numbered, dated bibliography closes the report; every link resolves.
- [ ] Synthesis dominates collection, every fact advances the argument.
- [ ] Jargon is defined on first use.
- [ ] Incentives and bias are disclosed up front.
- [ ] **Zero em-dashes.**
- [ ] **Zero "it's not X, it's Y" inversions.**
- [ ] No tell-phrases, empty transitions, marketing clichés, or universal-truth filler.
- [ ] Past tense for time-stamped facts; no dramatic present.
- [ ] Section shapes are uneven and argument-driven, not symmetrical.
- [ ] Reads like a careful human did the reading, not a model answering a prompt.
