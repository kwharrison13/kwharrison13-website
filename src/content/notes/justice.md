# Justice

In [The Broken Heart](/books/the-broken-heart), justice is the theological counterweight to mercy and the axis on which the book's account of the Atonement turns. The book defines it as a structural demand rather than punishment for its own sake: "Justice is a law of balance and order and it must be satisfied, either through our payment or his." The conditional part of the Atonement — the part covering personal sin — requires repentance as the condition of applying mercy; absent that, "we must suffer even as the Savior did to satisfy the demands of justice."

The book's key move is to recast the justice/mercy pairing as rehabilitative rather than retributive. "The doctrines of mercy and repentance are rehabilitative, not retributive, in nature." The point of satisfying justice through the Savior is not merely to balance a ledger but to induce the process of development that changes one's nature — the "natural man" remains "an enemy to God forever, even after paying for his own sins," unless he also "becometh a saint through the atonement of Christ." Justice thus sets the stakes that make mercy meaningful.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology, the tension between divine justice (sin must be answered for) and divine mercy (the repentant can be forgiven through Christ's Atonement) is a classic doctrinal pairing, expounded at length in the Book of Mormon (e.g., Alma 42).

## Where this appears

- [The Broken Heart](/books/the-broken-heart) — paired with mercy as the central theological axis; framed as a "law of balance and order" satisfied through the Atonement, and reinterpreted as rehabilitative rather than retributive.
