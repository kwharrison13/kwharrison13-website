# Fraud

In Kyle's notes, fraud sits at the far end of a spectrum that begins with optics-gaming and ends in outright deception. [How to Trick Investors and VCs](/notes/how-to-trick-investors-and-vcs) lays out three categories of investor deception — honest accounting shenanigans, operators who don't understand the basics, and "straight-up fraud" — with fraud the third and most severe, made easier by VCs who "don't dig deep" and assume the rest works out (see [Due Diligence](/notes/due-diligence)). The same piece's capstone worry: VCs "hyping up a lot of what's either blatant fraud or not real revenue numbers and attributing it to AI," turning startups into "another one of those pump-and-dump cycles."

The $1B Rorschach Test - Research is where Kyle works this out concretely against [MEDVI](/notes/medvi), the telehealth/GLP-1 company. He marks the line where "[growth hacking](/notes/move-fast-and-break-things)" crosses into fraud: 800+ fabricated doctor profiles with stock photos and fake names ("Dr. Tuckr Carlzyn"), AI-generated deepfake before/after photos, TV testimonials with fake doctors and no disclosure, **FDA Warning Letter #721455** (Feb 2026) for misbranding, and no meaningful clinical guardrails. The structural reading runs through [Regulatory Arbitrage](/notes/regulatory-arbitrage) (which "shades into outright fraud when guardrails are reduced below minimum viable compliance") and [Wrapper Companies](/notes/wrapper-companies) (where the critique lands not on the wrapper structure itself but on the fraud and lack of guardrails underneath). Kyle's larger fear is that "move fast and break laws" becoming a *respectable* playbook incentivizes more crimes, frauds, scams, and shortcuts — and makes the hard way (building a [Durable](/notes/durable) business) less likely.
