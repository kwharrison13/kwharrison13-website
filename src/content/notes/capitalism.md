# Capitalism

Across Kyle's corpus, Capitalism is examined less as a settled good than as a system with **conditional moral foundations**. [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) frames it most directly: capitalism is "predicated on the ideals of freedom and honesty" — foundations that are conditional, not given — and Lucas and Woodworth set it as one of two foils (alongside socialism) against the [United Order](/notes/united-order), insisting the United Order is a genuinely different organizing principle and "not capitalism with charity added on." A recurring critique is that "maximizing shareholder wealth cannot be the terminal goal of business," because any means is justified if the end is important enough — which makes capitalism's anti-thesis [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism) and the theme of [Financialization](/notes/financialization) central concerns.

The harder edge appears in [Evicted](/books/evicted), where Kyle reads the book's engine as **exploitation, not just deprivation** — "there is a business model at the bottom of every market," landlords and payday lenders and rent-to-own stores all running the same play of profiting excessively from people with no other option. [China in Ten Words](/books/china-in-ten-words) supplies a different register: a China "ruled by politics" transformed in thirty years into one "where money is king," growth measured as spending 100 yuan to gain 10 in GDP. The concept also anchors Kyle's own religious-economic thinking — the [Protestant Work Ethic](/notes/protestant-work-ethic) (Weber's *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*) and the [Temporarily Embarrassed Millionaires](/notes/temporarily-embarrassed-millionaires) self-image of workers who see themselves as future-rich rather than exploited.

**Context:** Capitalism is an economic system in which the means of production are privately owned and goods and services are produced for profit, coordinated chiefly through market prices. Long-running debates center on its relationship to freedom, inequality, and morality — from Adam Smith and Max Weber to twentieth-century critiques of shareholder-primacy and financialization.
