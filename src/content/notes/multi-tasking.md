# Multi Tasking

In [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child), multitasking is framed as a misnomer and a performance trap rather than a skill. The book argues that "you can't do two things at once if they require conscious thought" — what looks like multitasking is really rapid task-switching, which compromises the quality of learning and performance, multiplies errors, and slows people down. Beyond inefficiency, it is presented as actively harmful: multitasking limits deep thought, abstraction, creativity, and invention, and has been shown to elevate cortisol, putting more stress on the nervous system. The book offers meditation and mindfulness as the antidote — being present instead of "doing three things at once."

The concept connects to the book's broader case for "radical downtime": doing nothing purposeful as a corrective to the "mind-scattering and mind-numbing effects of 24/7 technology and multitasking." Kyle's note on the page links this to [Deep Work](/books/deep-work) and a Hugh Nibley story about red and blue glasses.

**Context:** The claim that multitasking is really task-switching, degrades performance, and is more accurately described as serial attention-shifting is a well-established finding in cognitive psychology and attention research.
