# Max Weber

Weber recurs across Kyle's corpus as the sociologist whose [Protestant Work Ethic](/notes/protestant-work-ethic) thesis (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*) supplies the frame for how prosperity gets read as a sign of God's favor. In the Great Softening research Kyle invokes Weber's Germany-vs-Italy contrast — "emphasis on grace makes you want to demonstrate the hand of god through prosperity" — and ties it to a Latter-day Saint analog: the [Book of Mormon](/notes/book-of-mormon) promise that "if you're righteous you'll prosper," asking "Why do so many Mormons want to be wealthy" while warning that "get-rich-quick schemes are a fake prosperity."

Weber also appears as a critic of [Franklin](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life). In Isaacson's biography, Weber charged that "all Franklin's moral attitudes are colored with [utilitarianism](/notes/utilitarianism)," accusing Franklin of believing only in "the earning of more and more money combined with the strict avoidance of all spontaneous engagement of life." Isaacson redirects the attack at the Poor Richard persona rather than the man — a distinction the book insists on. In [Restoration: God's Call To The 21st Century](/books/restoration-god-s-call-to-the-21st-century), Weber surfaces under a different concept: his idea of "disenchantment," the shift from "enchanted" traditional societies to ones organized around rational, secular, scientific, and bureaucratic norms — which Mason uses to argue that a church comfortable "canonizing meeting minutes and press releases" is fully at home in the modern, disenchanted world.

**Context:** Max Weber (1864–1920) was a German sociologist, historian, and political economist, one of the founding figures of modern sociology. His best-known work, *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905), argued that Calvinist and Puritan theology helped produce the disciplined, accumulative habits that underpinned Western capitalism.
