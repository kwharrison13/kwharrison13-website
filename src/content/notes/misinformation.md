# Misinformation

Across Kyle's corpus, misinformation is treated less as a problem of individual false facts than as a corruption of the entire **information ecology** on which collective judgment depends — the failure mode that recurs in the "is America in decline?" thread. [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) draws the sharp distinction: the danger is not that readers are "not just poorly informed, they're misinformed." [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) locates the mechanism in the medium — television "altering the meaning of being informed" by manufacturing a kind of structural disinformation. [The Assault on Reason](/books/the-assault-on-reason) frames it as a "determined disinterest in learning the truth" producing a counterfeit "consent of the governed," and [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) supplies the most weaponized version: the Kremlin's "alternative facts" and multiple competing versions of events, engineered "to create the impression that there are no reliable facts" at all. This is why [Conspiracy Theories](/notes/conspiracy-theories) read as a symptom — they exemplify the broader information-environment failure — and why misinformation sits at the center of the [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people) essay-in-progress as the corruption that undermines the coherence of public judgment.

The corpus also carries a notable counter-framing on what to *do* about it. In the January 31st, 2022 — Anson Yu (Contrary Capital) call, Anson Yu proposes treating **[Misinformation](/notes/misinformation) as Malware**: defend individual machines (people) rather than trying to kill every virus, because misinformation is the hardest thing to protect against — it requires so much context. The takeaway Kyle preserved verbatim: rather than spending energy pulling down misinformation, invest it in helping people develop **digital literacy** instead.

**Context:** "Misinformation" generally denotes false or misleading information spread regardless of intent, distinguished from "disinformation" (deliberately deceptive) and "malinformation" (true information weaponized to harm). The malware analogy — treating bad information like a security threat to be defended against at the endpoint — is a recurring frame in information-integrity and trust-and-safety circles.
