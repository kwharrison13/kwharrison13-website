# General Motors

General Motors is the anchor employer whose plant closure sets the entire narrative of [Janesville](/books/janesville) (Amy Goldstein) in motion. The book's inciting event is the day in 2008 when "General Motors handed the assembly plant its death sentence" — rumors of the shutdown had hovered over the town so long they'd become "a familiar backdrop to life in Janesville." GM functions in Kyle's notes as the case study of a single-industry town losing its economic spine: the book follows middle-class "GM'ers" tumbling out of the middle class as $28-an-hour wages, union SUB pay, 401(k) cushions, and GM health benefits disappear. Local leaders rally around the line "We are not going to wait for GM to rescue us" ([City Building](/notes/city-building) via Forward Janesville and Rock County 5.0), and the abandoned 250-acre site is later put up for sale to industrial-redevelopment buyers.

GM's closure is also the trigger for the book's central empirical finding on [Retraining](/notes/retraining): laid-off GM workers who went back to school at Blackhawk Technical College fared worse on employment and pay than those who didn't, despite the bipartisan "retraining gospel." A union instructor's advice to one worker — "If I were you guys and had an opportunity to get GM wages, I would run and not look back" — captures how irreplaceable those jobs proved.

**Context:** General Motors, founded in 1908, is one of the largest American automakers. Its Janesville, Wisconsin assembly plant — among GM's oldest — was idled in December 2008 amid the financial crisis, shortly before GM's own 2009 bankruptcy and federal bailout.
