# Alexandre Dumas

Alexandre Dumas enters Kyle's corpus as the author of [The Count of Monte Cristo](/notes/the-count-of-monte-cristo), the novel invoked in Kyle's "Great Softening" research notes. The novel is referenced there for a "whistle vs. pony" parable about contentment — paraphrased as "he was so much happier with his whistle than you were with your pony" — used to make the point that satisfaction is relative to expectation and what one paid. (Kyle's note files the parable under Monte Cristo, though the "whistle" story actually traces to [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)'s essay "The Whistle.") In Kyle's corpus, then, Dumas surfaces only by way of this single novel and its use as a literary touchstone, not as a developed biographical subject.

**Context:** Alexandre Dumas (1802–1870) was a French novelist and playwright, one of the most widely read French authors, best known for the adventure novels *The Count of Monte Cristo* (1844–46) and *The Three Musketeers* (1844). His works, frequently serialized, are landmarks of 19th-century popular fiction.
