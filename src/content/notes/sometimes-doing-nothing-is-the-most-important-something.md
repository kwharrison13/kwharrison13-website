# Sometimes doing nothing is the most important something

This is a personal maxim Kyle attributes to **[Brother Stice](/notes/brother-stice)** and uses, across the corpus, to mark moments where *apparent idleness* — pondering, reflection, letting ideas simmer — is the actual productive work. It is one of his recurring annotation phrases: he tags it as a `> **Kyle:**` note rather than developing it as prose, dropping it wherever a source touches the value of deliberate slack.

In the creative register, it sits beside [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci)'s case for productive procrastination — "Men of lofty genius sometimes accomplish the most when they work least … for their minds are occupied with their ideas" — and recurs in Kyle's [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) threads. In the spiritual register, it captures the discipline of **[Pondering](/notes/pondering)** as the precondition for revelation: in the Helaman 7–12 study, Nephi is blessed while "pondering upon the things which the Lord had shown unto him," and Kyle ties the maxim to Eyring's "when we ponder, we invite revelation by the Spirit"; in the Moroni 10 study it marks the call to let facts "sink into your soul" rather than merely review them (Alma 32:27; Enos 1:3-4). The throughline is the same: stillness, attention, and pondering are not the absence of work but its most important form.
