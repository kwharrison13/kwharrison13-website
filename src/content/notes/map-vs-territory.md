# Map vs Territory

The load-bearing distinction in [The Great Mental Models Volume 1: General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts): "the description of the thing is not the thing itself. The model is not reality. The abstraction is not the abstracted." The book attributes the idea to Korzybski, who "introduced and popularized the concept that the map is not the territory," and lays out its properties — a map may have a structure similar or dissimilar to the territory; a map is not the actual territory; and an ideal map would contain a map of the map of the map, endlessly.

The book frames this as the foundational warning behind its whole approach to mental models: "We run into problems when our knowledge becomes of the map, rather than the actual underlying territory it describes." The failure mode is treating "adherence to the map [as] more important than taking in new information about a territory." The implication Kyle drew out in his synthesis: every model degrades unless updated against real-world feedback, so feedback loops are the actual mechanism by which models stay useful. It sits alongside [Circle of Competence](/notes/circle-of-competence), [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking), and [Inversion](/notes/inversion) in the book's [Latticework of Mental Models](/notes/latticework).

**Context:** "The map is not the territory" originates with Polish-American scholar Alfred Korzybski, founder of general semantics, in the 1930s. It is a staple of the rationalist and mental-models literature (Charlie Munger, Farnam Street) used to caution that any representation — a model, a metric, a stereotype — is a lossy abstraction of the reality it stands for.
