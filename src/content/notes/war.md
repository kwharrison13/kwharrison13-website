# War

Across Kyle's notes, "war" surfaces with two very different framings. In [2034 — A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war), war's through-line is *miscalculation* — the certainty that both sides expect to win, and the maxim that empires "rot from within." The novel dramatizes American overreach and a Sino-Iranian-Russian alignment, with its warning crystallized in a character's line: "the lesson of the Maine — or even Iraq, where I fought — is that you better be goddamn sure you know what's going on before you start a war." Co-authored by a retired admiral ([James Stavridis](/notes/james-stavridis)) and a Marine veteran ([Elliot Ackerman](/notes/elliot-ackerman)), it treats war as a failure of imagination rather than of intelligence.

In [Animal Farm](/books/animal-farm), war appears in [Orwell's](/notes/propaganda) economic register: framed as the deliberate destruction of surplus labor to keep the masses too poor — and too exhausted — to think. Here war is not a clash between powers but an internal tool of control, a way for a ruling class to consume the productive capacity that might otherwise empower the governed. The two readings together cast war both as an external catastrophe born of hubris and as an instrument of domestic repression.

## Where this appears

- [2034 — A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war) — war as miscalculation and hubris; both sides certain they'll win, empires rotting from within.
- [Animal Farm](/books/animal-farm) — war framed as deliberate destruction of surplus labor to keep the masses too poor to think.
