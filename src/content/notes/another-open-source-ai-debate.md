# Another Open Source AI Debate

**Author:** John Coogan (TBPN newsletter)
**URL:** https://tbpn.substack.com/p/another-open-source-ai-debate
**One-line:** *Zhipu AI's open-weight GLM-5.2 model matching closed-source frontier models on cybersecurity benchmarks reignites the open-vs-closed-source AI debate, days after a narrative had formed that open source was falling behind.*

## Key claims

- GLM-5.2, released by China's Zhipu AI (Z.ai) on June 13, 2026, matched the latest U.S. models at finding security bugs according to the Wall Street Journal, and in some benchmarks (per cybersecurity firm Semgrep) bested Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8; it ranked among the 10 most-used models on OpenRouter.
- Prior to GLM-5.2's release, a narrative had formed that open source was slowing relative to the closed-source frontier — a dynamic John Luttig predicted in "The future of foundation models is closed-source" (May 2024), arguing open source would be relegated to smaller, less capable, configurable use cases while frontier value concentrates in closed models.
- George Hotz's counter-argument ("AI will be massively deflationary"): China benefits from giving away open-source models because it prevents any single actor (including a regulatory-captured U.S. government) from monopolizing AI in a "no longer... unipolar world."
- Dario Amodei's 2023 Senate testimony warning about the "dangerous path" of open-source model scaling (biorisk within 2-3 years, misinformation risk already present) is recirculating in light of GLM-5.2.
- A quick review (Tyler Cosgrove, quoted in the piece) argues GLM-5.2 is likely heavily distilled from Claude models (it "identifies as it often"), is the leading open-source coding model but only a mild step up from GLM 5.1 elsewhere, is token-hungry enough to erode its price advantage, and that most other benchmarking groups (e.g., Epoch AI) show a stable — not narrowing — gap between American and Chinese models. See [Distillation (AI)](/notes/distillation-ai).
- Cybersecurity firms (Crowdstrike, Palo Alto Networks) have been working with frontier models for months to harden systems against LLM-driven attacks; the piece argues the open/closed capability gap isn't currently narrowing enough to change that defensive posture.

## Notable quotes

> "Security researchers said that a new AI model released this month by China's Zhipu AI, also known as Z.ai, can match the latest U.S. models when it comes to finding security bugs, a development poised to reset the global tech race and pressure the White House in its overhaul of U.S. AI policy." (WSJ, quoted in the piece)

> "Open-source will have a home wherever smaller, less capable, and configurable models are needed – enterprise workloads, for example – but the bulk of the value creation and capture in AI will happen using frontier capabilities... open-source model providers will lose the capital expenditure war as open-source ROI continues to decline." (John Luttig, quoted)

> "This explains why the Chinese are giving the... models away for free. They love to see deflationary economics in the US. Even if you regulatory capture the US government, nobody is getting a monopoly on AI, we don't live in a unipolar world anymore." (George Hotz, quoted)

> "But I'm very concerned about where things are going... I think the path that things are going, in terms of the scaling of open source models, I think it's going down a very dangerous path." (Dario Amodei, 2023 Senate testimony, quoted)
