# Freeman Dyson

In [Range](/books/range), Freeman Dyson supplies the "birds and frogs" metaphor that frames the book's specialist/generalist argument. Quoting Dyson's 2009 formulation: "Birds fly high in the air and survey broad vistas of mathematics out to the far horizon... They delight in concepts that unify our thinking," while "Frogs live in the mud below and see only the flowers that grow nearby... They delight in the details of particular objects." Dyson — who labeled himself a frog — argued it is "stupid to claim that birds are better than frogs because they see farther, or that frogs are better than birds because they see deeper"; the world is both broad and deep, and "we need birds and frogs working together to explore it." The book carries his warning that science is "increasingly overflowing with frogs," trained in a narrow specialty and unable to change as science does, and links the metaphor to its "T-shaped" integrators and the case for [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking).

**Context:** Freeman Dyson (1923–2020) was a British-American theoretical physicist and mathematician known for work in quantum electrodynamics, the Dyson sphere concept, and a long career of wide-ranging essays.
