# Kate Walsh

In [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) (TIME's special edition), Kate Walsh of the [National Council on Teacher Quality](/notes/national-council-on-teacher-quality) is quoted criticizing a strain of romanticism in teacher education. She argues that "many teacher educators reject out of hand a method that will work with 95% of the kids because of the 5%--they fervently believe in the uniqueness of every child," and traces that attitude to "18th century Swiss Philosopher [Jean-Jacques Rousseau](/notes/jean-jacques-rousseau), a distrust of formal education for threatening children's true and good natural state." She frames it as "a kind of anti-intellectualism that almost disdains [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) as if it were Trivial Pursuit and not something that has engaged the human mind since the beginning of time."

Her voice in the piece sits on the empirical, research-grounded side of the [Education](/notes/education) debate — favoring instructional methods with proven outcomes over a pedagogy organized around the irreducible uniqueness of each child.

**Context:** Kate Walsh is an American education-policy figure who led the National Council on Teacher Quality (NCTQ), a research and advocacy organization focused on the quality and preparation of teachers in the United States.
