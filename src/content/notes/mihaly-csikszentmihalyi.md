# Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi appears across two of Kyle's book pages as the originator of "flow." In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Sönke Ahrens credits Csikszentmihalyi's 1970s work for describing flow as "the state in which being highly focused becomes effortless," which opened the door to research on forms of attention much less dependent on will and effort — the kind of momentum the slip-box workflow is meant to generate (Kyle's note: flow comes from a sense of control plus a sense of meaning). In [Essentialism](/books/essentialism), Csikszentmihalyi shows up on the receiving end of [Peter Drucker](/notes/peter-drucker)'s famous "graceful no": when the professor wrote to interview him for a book on creativity, Drucker's reply — declining, and praising the "VERY BIG waste paper basket" as a secret of productivity — was striking enough that Csikszentmihalyi quoted it verbatim, and Kyle flags it as "the greatest response I have ever seen."

So in Kyle's corpus the name carries two functions: the scholarly source for the [flow](/notes/the-flow) concept that recurs in his notes on focus and deep work, and a supporting character in the Drucker anecdote about the art of saying no.

**Context:** Mihály Csíkszentmihályi (1934–2021) was a Hungarian-American psychologist, a founder of positive psychology, best known for his concept of "flow" — the state of fully absorbed, optimal experience — laid out in his 1990 book *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*.
