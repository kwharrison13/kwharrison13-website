# Holding Company

The holding company is one of the load-bearing structures in Kyle's investing reading — the vehicle for buying good businesses and compounding them under one roof forever. [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) is the archetype: Kyle's note there describes calculating the earnings of a holding company as being "laser focused on [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) and returns regardless of where in the [Family of Companies](/notes/family-of-companies) they leave the capital" — the holdco as a capital-routing engine indifferent to which subsidiary the cash sits in.

[The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) supplies the small-business, private-markets version: [Brent Beshore](/notes/brent-beshore)'s adventur.es runs a buy-and-hold holding-company model for private operating businesses, explicitly contrasted against the fixed-life-fund schedule of [Private Equity](/notes/private-equity) that forces short-term decisions. The [Private Equity](/notes/private-equity) concept page frames this directly — a PE firm "has a fiduciary duty" to maximize return over a fixed fund life, while the holding company plus [Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital) model lets businesses compound indefinitely, with return expectations of 20–35% for smaller PE, search funds, and individuals buying at 4–5x [EBITDA](/notes/ebitda).

[On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) is the clearest statement of the thesis: it sits at the center of Kyle's Search Funds / Investing 101 2.0 / Holding Companies cluster — "build or buy one good business and hold it forever," an evergreen / "100 Year Business Plan" mindset (per Dave Whorton and the Tugboat Institute). Kyle's note there: "Better to build holding companies for your businesses and allow them to compound over time."

**Context:** A holding company is a parent entity that owns controlling stakes in operating businesses rather than producing goods itself. Berkshire Hathaway is the canonical example; the structure lets an owner allocate capital across subsidiaries and hold them indefinitely, distinct from fixed-life private-equity funds that must sell within a set window.
