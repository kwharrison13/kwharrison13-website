# Word of Wisdom

The Latter-day Saint health code (abstinence from alcohol, tobacco, coffee, and tea). Across Kyle's reading it surfaces less as devotional content than as a case study in how a practice hardens from "good advice" into binding commandment — and what blessings are promised for keeping it.

The historical thread runs through two biographies. In [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet), the early church "oscillated between strict enforcement and latitude," with the council leaving the choice to [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) (who reasoned "a forced abstainance… should be under bondage with a yoak upon our necks"); only late in his life did Young push strict obedience. [Leonard Arrington and the Writing of Mormon History](/books/leonard-arrington-and-the-writing-of-mormon-history) sharpens this with Arrington's economically-grounded reinterpretation — that Young's enforcement of the Word of Wisdom as a *binding commandment* was driven by the need to keep scarce cash in Utah Territory by proscribing imported "luxury goods." That article triggered the complaint chain Arrington made famous: *"I write an article on the Word of Wisdom. Someone complains… the dean tells me… I must write him a letter of apology. This is the way to be creative?"*

In [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest), the Word of Wisdom appears as one of the pre-baptismal "habits of faith" — David Stewart's argument that four consecutive weeks of gospel habits before the font (not a deadline imposed on top) drives convert retention, noting Seventh-Day Adventists keep an even stricter health code and retain better. And in Kyle's Ether 12–15 gospel study it carries the promise-timing lesson via [Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)'s running story: passed by people who flouted the Word of Wisdom, he learned that *"the promises of the Lord, if perhaps not always swift, are always certain."*
