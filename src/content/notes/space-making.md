# Space Making

In [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city), "space making" is treated as a deliberate discipline rather than incidental architecture — captured by [Louis Kahn](/notes/louis-kahn)'s line that "architecture is the thoughtful making of space." The book's Disney-park lens describes designing for "the quality without a name," the moment "apprehension turns into awe and delight" when you enter a space that feels right but can't quite say why (Hench: "the order here works on people… they feel more content here in a way they can't explain. You find strangers talking to each other without fear").

Practically, the discipline means engaging all five senses (down to the "Smellitzers" pumping cookie scent), designing human-scale streets, and — crucially — including places to pause and do nothing, the intimacy that big parks lose as they scale. Kyle flags it alongside the book's open question for [City Building](/notes/city-building): how do you maintain neighborhood closeness as a city grows to the largest in the world? Walt's EPCOT is the book's test case across space making, city building, [Historical Futurism](/notes/historical-futurism), and [Utopia](/notes/utopia).
