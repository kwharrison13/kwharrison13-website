# PowerPoint

In Kyle's notes PowerPoint stands less for the software than for a *type of organizational culture* — "presentation culture" — that more rigorous writing-based cultures define themselves against. [Six-Page Narratives](/notes/six-page-narratives) frames Amazon's six-page memo as the deliberate institutional alternative: per [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound), "PowerPoint presentations, with their litany of bullet points and incomplete thoughts, were banned inside the company despite being popular in the rest of corporate America," replaced by silent meeting-opening readings of data-rich written narratives. The contrast is the whole point — bullet-point slides let writers hide incomplete thinking; prose forces it into the open.

The same fault line appears in Kyle's January 2022 call with [Conor White-Sullivan](/notes/conor-white-sullivan) of [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research), where John Cutler's taxonomy of "Document Culture," "Presentation Culture (#[Powerpoint](/notes/powerpoint))," and "Knowledge Graph Culture" sorts organizations by how they think and communicate — presentation culture being the slide-driven middle that knowledge-graph tools aim to leapfrog.

**Context:** PowerPoint is Microsoft's slide-presentation software, ubiquitous in corporate settings; its bullet-point format is a recurring foil in management writing (notably Amazon's Jeff Bezos, who banned slide decks in favor of written narrative memos).
