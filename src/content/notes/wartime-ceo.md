# Wartime CEO

Ben Horowitz's framing of leadership in crisis (versus the "peacetime CEO"), which appears in The Renaissance of Rise and Grind - Research as a piece of the discourse around tech's post-2022 cultural shift. The research doc quotes a New York Times piece ("Elon Musk, Management Guru?") observing that "executives with sagging stock prices are declaring themselves 'wartime C.E.O.s,'" while "workers who could have credibly threatened to leave their jobs for cushier ones a year ago are now hanging on for dear life." In Kyle's notes the phrase is bound up with what John Ganz called "[Bossism](/notes/bossism)" — the belief that founders had "ceded too much power to the entitled, lazy, overly woke people who work for them and need to start clawing it back" — and with [Elon Musk](/notes/elon-musk)'s Twitter takeover as its emblematic case (firing workers, telling them to be "hard core," daring the rest to quit). The label thus functions in the essay research less as a neutral management term and more as the self-justifying rhetoric executives reach for when a loosening labor market lets them tighten the screws.

**Context:** "Wartime CEO vs. peacetime CEO" comes from Ben Horowitz's *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* (and an earlier a16z essay): a peacetime CEO grows an existing advantage, while a wartime CEO operates under existential threat where deviation from the mission can be fatal, demanding sharper, more directive leadership.
