# Authenticity

A recurring theme across two very different books in Kyle's library. In [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness), [Brené Brown](/notes/bren-brown) makes authenticity the precondition of true belonging: "true belonging only happens when we present our authentic, imperfect selves to the world," so one's sense of belonging "can never be greater than our level of self-acceptance." Fitting in and seeking approval are framed as hollow substitutes — people want connection, "but not at the cost of their authenticity, freedom, or power." The "wilderness" itself is where the system-buckers and risk-takers live: "the walk out there is hard, but the authenticity out there is life."

In [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning), authenticity appears as a political problem — the "let Mitt be Mitt" tension, the gap between staged personas and saying what one actually means. Kyle annotates a passage on a "least authentic profession" (politics) with the wry note that it is the "least authentic profession ever." Together the two pages frame authenticity from opposite ends: Brown as the interior discipline that makes belonging possible, [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) as the public cost of performing an inauthentic self.

## Where this appears

- [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness) — authenticity as the precondition of true belonging; presenting your imperfect self rather than fitting in.
- [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) — the "let Mitt be Mitt" gap between staged persona and genuine voice in politics.
