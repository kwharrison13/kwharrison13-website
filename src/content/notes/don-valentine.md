# Don Valentine

Don Valentine is the founder of [Sequoia](/notes/sequoia) Capital (1972) and a recurring archetype across Kyle's venture-history and investing-philosophy notes. In [VC — An American History](/books/vc) he sits in the "family tree of venture funds" as one of the figures — alongside Arthur Rock and Tom Perkins — who compounded the Bay Area's regional advantage. He is the book's exemplar of "investing in markets" rather than people.

His investing philosophy shows up most vividly in The Unbundling of Venture Capital - Research, where Kyle preserves Valentine's signature dislocation thesis: "One of our theories is to seek out opportunities where there's a major change… Wherever there's turmoil, there's indecision. And wherever there's indecision, there's opportunity… So we look for the confusion when the big companies are confused. When the other venture groups are confused. That's the time to start companies." In the Networked Conviction — Roam + Investing - Research checklist collection, Valentine surfaces twice more as a distilled discipline: his "Original Question" — *"Why should I care about this?"* — and [Sequoia](/notes/sequoia)'s original investment checklist (very large market, NorCal, advanced technology, high gross-margin potential, $100M single-investment potential, responsive to active participation).

**Context:** Don Valentine (1932–2019) founded Sequoia Capital in 1972 and is regarded as a father of Silicon Valley venture capital, with early bets on Apple, Atari, Cisco, Oracle, and others. He was known for prioritizing the size and dynamics of a market over the individual founder.
