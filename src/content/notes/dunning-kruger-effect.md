# Dunning-Kruger Effect

The Dunning-Kruger Effect recurs across Kyle's notes as the gap between confidence and competence — "the dumber you are, the more confident you are that you're not actually dumb" ([The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise)). [Tom Nichols](/notes/tom-nichols) makes it a central frame of that book, expressed as a measurable failure mode rather than a meme: "increasing confidence without increasing accuracy," and a deficit of [Metacognition](/notes/metacognition) that keeps the incompetent from recognizing their own incompetence. Nichols quotes David Dunning directly — "education, even when done skillfully, can produce illusory confidence" — and ties the dynamic to the 2016 electorate.

In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Sönke Ahrens invokes the same effect (Kruger and Dunning, 1999) to explain weak students: lacking insight into the vast knowledge they haven't encountered, they "feel more successful (until they are tested)," experience little self-doubt, and easily find confirming evidence while skipping disconfirming arguments. "Those who are not very good at something tend to be overly confident, while those who have made an effort tend to underestimate their abilities."

**Context:** The Dunning-Kruger Effect, named for psychologists David Dunning and Justin Kruger (1999), describes the finding that people with low ability in a domain tend to overestimate that ability, because the same skills needed to perform well are needed to recognize one's own incompetence.

## Where this appears

- [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) — Ahrens uses it to explain why weak students feel confident until tested and ignore disconfirming evidence
- [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) — Nichols's recurring "increasing confidence without increasing accuracy," quoting Dunning on education producing illusory confidence
