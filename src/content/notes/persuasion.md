# Persuasion

Persuasion is the end toward which [Rhetoric](/notes/rhetoric) is the means. In Kyle's corpus the [Rhetoric](/notes/rhetoric) page defines classical rhetoric as "the study of the means by which the classic authors deployed the arts of persuasion" — ethos, pathos, and logos. That framing makes persuasion the underlying capacity rhetoric trains: the craft of moving an audience by character, emotion, and argument. It anchors Kyle's essay [We Need a Renaissance of Rhetoric](/essays/we-need-a-renaissance-of-rhetoric), where the decline of these persuasive arts is treated as a lost skill that "leaves us exposed," and where language sharpened into perspective is the battleground. Persuasion sits alongside neighboring concepts in the wiki — [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) and the [Ethos, Pathos, Logos](/notes/ethos-pathos-logos) triad — as the practical aim those tools serve.

**Context:** Persuasion is the art of influencing beliefs, attitudes, or actions through communication. In the classical tradition it is the explicit object of rhetoric, formalized in Aristotle's three appeals: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic).
