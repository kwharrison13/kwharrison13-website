# Aaron Swartz

Aaron Swartz appears in Kyle's corpus through [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus), where [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari) casts him as "Dataism's first martyr." Harari's account: Swartz was "a rare genius" who at fourteen helped develop the RSS protocol and became a firm believer in the freedom of information. In 2008 he published the "Guerilla Open Access Manifesto," demanding "a free and unlimited flow of information" — "We need to take information, wherever it is stored, make our copies and share them with the world... We need to download scientific journals and upload them to file sharing networks."

Harari recounts that Swartz, "annoyed with the JSTOR digital library for charging its customers," used the MIT computer network to download hundreds of thousands of scientific papers he intended to release freely. He was arrested and put on trial; facing likely conviction and prison, he hanged himself on 11 January 2013 at twenty-six. In Harari's telling the story crystallizes the Dataist creed that "information wants to be free" and that "the greatest sin would be to block the data flow" — set against JSTOR's counter-position that creators have the right to charge for their work. Hackers reacted with petitions and attacks on the institutions that persecuted him, and JSTOR later apologized and opened much of its data. The episode is Kyle's clearest corpus link between Swartz and [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge).

**Context:** Aaron Swartz (1986–2013) was an American programmer and activist who co-authored the RSS 1.0 specification, helped build Reddit and the Markdown/web standards ecosystem, and co-founded Creative Commons infrastructure work. His federal prosecution over the JSTOR/MIT downloads — and his death during it — became a touchstone in debates over open access, computer-crime law (the CFAA), and the public ownership of knowledge.
