# Affordable Housing

The policy frame at the center of [Evicted](/books/evicted) and a recurring concern in [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis). Matthew Desmond reframes affordable housing as a [Human Capital](/notes/human-capital) investment "just like job programs or education" — families crushed by housing costs can't afford training, extra schooling, or even stay in one place long enough to hold a job. His proposed fix is a **universal housing voucher**: every family below an income line gets one, pays 30% of income toward a decent, fairly-priced home, landlords are *mandated* to participate (no discriminating against voucher holders), and rents are regulated so subsidies don't simply pad landlord profits. Economists, he notes, argue the existing program could cover every poor family in America "without additional spending" if overcharging were curbed — "if poverty persists in America, it is not for lack of resources." The wiki ties this to the cash-floor logic in [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) and [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income).

[The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) places affordable housing inside the broader cost of re-urbanization: Richard Florida argues that "providing affordable housing at the scale that is needed" is part of the expensive shift toward clustered, dense urban growth. Kyle's margin note there flags the underlying market question — what benefits (lower demand, assured tenants) would justify lower rents, and who actually serves the demand for affordable housing in cities.

## Where this appears

- [Evicted](/books/evicted) — the core policy frame ("affordable housing is a human-capital investment") and the locus of Desmond's universal housing-voucher proposal.
- [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) — affordable housing as part of the costly re-urbanization shift; the site of Kyle's note on rent, landlords, and underserved demand.
