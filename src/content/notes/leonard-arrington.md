# Leonard Arrington

The central figure of LDS academic history as Kyle reads him — economic historian, founder of the Mormon History Association, and the Church Historian whose tenure defined the so-called "Camelot" era of open Mormon scholarship. The dedicated biography [Leonard Arrington and the Writing of Mormon History](/books/leonard-arrington-and-the-writing-of-mormon-history) (by [Gregory A. Prince](/notes/gregory-a-prince)) is Kyle's main source: it frames Arrington as a model of [Institution Building](/notes/institution-building) and the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters), a leader who deflected titles ("titles were not important to him") and got the work done without making it about himself — a pairing Kyle ties to [Whatever You Are Be A Good One](/notes/whatever-you-are-be-a-good-one). The "Knopf revision episode," where Arrington's manuscript was judged "too pro-Church," becomes a case study in finding the line between hagiography and reductionism.

Arrington recurs across Kyle's Mormon-intellectual reading as the embodiment of the scholar-vs-institution tension. In [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) he is the head of the same Camelot-era Historical Department who hired (and then saw blacklisted) [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england); he organized the Mormon History Association as an independent forum, and his early *BYU Studies* article arguing the Word of Wisdom's enforcement was financially as much as spiritually motivated got that journal suspended for a year. His landmark work *Great Basin Kingdom* surfaces in [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) as the text a Manila area presidency studied to learn how LDS pioneers raised their standard of living. In [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) he's quoted on how settlement-era hardship "became more formidable with each subsequent telling" — folklore inflating over time, a note about the gap between remembered and actual history.

**Context:** Leonard J. Arrington (1917–1999) was an American economic historian, author of the seminal *Great Basin Kingdom* (1958), and the first professionally trained historian to serve as Church Historian of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (1972–1982), a period of unusual scholarly openness later curtailed. He is often called the "Dean of Mormon History."
