# Planning

In [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war), planning is the discipline of calculation done *before* the fight: "Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought," while the loser "makes but few" — "many calculations lead to victory, and few calculations to defeat." Crucially, this is not abstract dogma. Sun Tzu rejects "bookish theoric"; plans must adapt to the enemy's actual moves, which shades planning into [Game Theory](/notes/game-theory). The book illustrates this with Wellington at Waterloo, who refused to fix his plans because they depended on Bonaparte's: "as my plans will depend upon his, how can you expect me to tell you what mine are?" A countervailing note adds that for great strategists from Caesar to Napoleon, the *value of time* — being slightly ahead of the opponent — often outweighed "the nicest calculations."

The same theme surfaces in [How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital) as a horizon-collapsing maxim for execution: "Dream in Decades → Think in Years → Work in Weeks → Live in Days" — long-range planning translated down into daily action.
