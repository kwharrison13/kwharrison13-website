# 10,000 Hour Rule

The 10,000 Hour Rule — the popular claim that ~10,000 hours of practice (about 60 straight weeks) makes you an expert at anything — appears in Kyle's notes primarily as a **myth to be qualified rather than a rule to be followed**. [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) explicitly debunks it: the source grants that repetition makes "the surgeon or the mechanic who performs 10,000 hours' worth of bypasses or engine repairs" awfully good at the job, but argues "it takes more than that to become a compassionate minister, an intuitive psychotherapist, or even a good parent or a faithful friend." That piece instead frames real learning around motivation, opportunity, and sustained attention plus the habits of [Curiosity](/notes/curious) and diligence — i.e., hours alone don't produce [Expertise](/notes/expertise).

The counter-thread is practice-as-numbers-game, carried by [Kobe Bryant](/notes/kobe-bryant), who is tagged against the 10,000-hour discussion in The Science of Learning. In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store), Kyle's own note invokes Kobe's framing that "practice is a numbers game — if you're working 2-3 hours a day every day you're going to be better than the people working 2-3 days a week." The two readings sit in productive tension: deliberate daily reps compound, but raw hours are necessary-not-sufficient and don't transfer to every domain.

**Context:** The "10,000-hour rule" was popularized by Malcolm Gladwell in *Outliers* (2008), drawing on Anders Ericsson's research on deliberate practice; Ericsson himself later argued the figure was an oversimplification of his work.
