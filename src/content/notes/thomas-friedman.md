# Thomas Friedman

Author of [The World is Flat](/notes/the-world-is-flat), cited across Kyle's notes for its "Globalization 3.0" framing of how labor, education, and competitive advantage compete on a global stage. In [The Future of Work (Article)](/notes/the-future-of-work-article) (David Perell's synthesis essay), Friedman's frame supplies one of the load-bearing arguments: after a country-led wave (1.0) and a corporation-led wave (2.0), globalization's third phase is defined by complex global supply chains and individuals/developing countries reshaping the landscape — with the conclusion that "individuals who differentiate themselves will do best." That thesis sits alongside Harari's automation provocation and Alec Ross's West Virginia-vs-Estonia parable as the essay's case for creativity and adaptability as the only durable career moats.

*The World Is Flat* itself lives in Kyle's anti-library ([The World is Flat](/notes/the-world-is-flat), `books-to-read`), logged off that citation rather than as a book he has read.

**Context:** Thomas L. Friedman is a New York Times foreign-affairs columnist and three-time Pulitzer Prize winner, best known for globalization-focused books including *The World Is Flat* (2005) and *The Lexus and the Olive Tree*.
