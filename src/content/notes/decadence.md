# Decadence

Decadence is the central thesis the wiki draws from [The Time Machine](/books/the-time-machine): security and ease, taken to their endpoint, produce languor and decay. Wells's Time Traveller frames it as a law of nature — "This has ever been the fate of energy in security; it takes to art and to eroticism, and then come languor and decay." The Eloi's "too-perfect security" has not produced flourishing but "a slow movement of degeneration, to a general dwindling in size, strength, and intelligence."

The mechanism is the absence of struggle. "Strength is the outcome of need; security sets a premium on feebleness," and "intellectual versatility is the compensation for change, danger, and trouble." Where there is no change and no need of change, there is no intelligence — so the very success of civilization ("comfort and ease, a balanced society with security and permanency as its watchword") becomes its undoing: "the dream of the human intellect... had committed suicide." On the source page this connects decadence to [Historical Futurism](/essays/historical-futurism) / [Progress Studies](/notes/progress-studies) (hardship drives intelligence), [Entropy](/notes/entropy) (the far-future heat-death of activity), and the inversion of [Utopia](/notes/utopia) into civilizational decline.

**Context:** "Decadence" as a theory of civilizational decline — that affluence and comfort erode the vigor that built a society — is a recurring theme in historiography and cultural criticism, from Gibbon's Rome through later "decline of the West" arguments.
