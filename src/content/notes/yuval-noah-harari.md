# Yuval Noah Harari

**One-line:** *Historian and author of Sapiens, Homo Deus, and 21 Lessons for the 21st Century, whose central thesis is that large-scale human cooperation rests on shared fictional narratives — an argument Kyle reads with sustained, respectful disagreement.*

## What we know

- His core thesis is "imagined orders" — large-scale cooperation depends on shared stories: "there are no gods in the universe, no nations, no money, no human rights, no laws, and no justice outside the common imagination of human beings." [Sapiens](/books/sapiens)
- He argues humans think in stories, not data — "humans think in stories rather than in facts, numbers, or equations, and the simpler the story, the better." [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) (see [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling))
- On the soul: he reasons that evolution rules out an unchanging soul (evolution requires change; a soul has no parts and cannot evolve). Kyle's counter-note: that something can't be measured physically doesn't mean it doesn't change. [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus)
- On identity as fiction: "all mass identities are based on fictional stories, not on scientific facts." Kyle challenges the premise — American identity built on the actual events of the Revolution is a shared narrative, not a fabrication. [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century)
- On knowledge: "the greatest scientific discovery was the discovery of ignorance" — progress comes from admitting what we don't know. [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus)
- On meaning after God: he frames modern humanism as the worship of human feeling; Kyle reads [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation) as a live theological alternative to Harari's secular resolution of meaning-loss. [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus)

## Mentioned in

- [Sapiens](/books/sapiens) — the founding statement of the imagined-orders / shared-fictions thesis.

- [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) — the narrative frame applied to identity, education, and politics.
