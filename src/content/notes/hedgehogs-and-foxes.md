# Hedgehogs and Foxes

Isaiah Berlin's distinction between two cognitive styles — "the fox knows many things, the hedgehog knows but one" — surfaces in [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) via Philip Tetlock's *Expert Political Judgment*. Tetlock used Berlin's split to separate experts whose knowledge is "wide and inclusive" (foxes) from those whose expertise is "narrow and deep" (hedgehogs). His central finding, as Nichols relays it: while experts in general stumble when moving from explanation to prediction, **foxes generally outperform hedgehogs** at prediction. Hedgehogs tend to over-generalize their specialized knowledge into situations outside their competence; foxes integrate more information and change their minds when presented with new or better data.

In Kyle's reading the framework lines up with a broader theme about the antidote to "monocular" expertise — the fox's triangulation across many domains as a corrective to the hedgehog's single-lens overconfidence. Kyle ties it to venture investing in an adjacent highlight: "If we don't track our thinking and revisit our reasoning then we can't improve" — the fox's willingness to update beats the hedgehog's track record of confident-but-untested calls. The idea connects to [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) and the [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) threads that run through the book, where the question is how non-experts evaluate and integrate competing claims.

**Context:** The hedgehog/fox dichotomy comes from Isaiah Berlin's 1953 essay "The Hedgehog and the Fox," which borrowed the line from the Greek poet Archilochus. Philip Tetlock later operationalized it in his long-running forecasting studies, finding that "fox"-style thinkers who hold many loosely-connected ideas predict real-world events more accurately than "hedgehog" thinkers committed to one big idea.
