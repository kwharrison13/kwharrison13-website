# Wisdom vs Knowledge

A recurring theme across Kyle's corpus, anchored by T.S. Eliot's line **"Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge?"** — flagged in his notes on multiple sources. The core hypothesis, drawn from [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet), is that **when knowledge becomes too easy to acquire, it gets diluted**. Kyle pairs the Eliot line with Joseph Smith's "religion that doesn't require faith will never last" to make the point that easy access can hollow out the thing accessed — the Eliot quote bookends that read, framing its whole arc.

The same line resurfaces in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), this time applied to the information abundance the transistor enabled: knowledge expanded, but wisdom didn't track it. So the concept spans both directions of the corpus — the long history of how knowledge institutions (Alexandria, the Abbasid House of Wisdom, the Republic of Letters) accumulate and synthesize, and the modern technological glut of information that doesn't automatically produce judgment.

The religious framing comes from Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young, where Young counsels seekers to "look to the heavens and try to learn wisdom until you can organize the native elements for your benefit," and warns that "our troubles are in large measure due not so much to a lack of facts as to a want of wisdom." This grounds the distinction in a practical, almost stewardship-oriented register — wisdom as the capacity to *use* knowledge rightly, distinct from merely possessing facts.

**Context:** The T.S. Eliot quote is from the choruses of his 1934 pageant play *The Rock*: "Where is the Life we have lost in living? / Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? / Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?" — a frequently-cited lament about the gap between accumulated data and genuine understanding.

## Where this appears

- [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) — the wisdom-knowledge gap is the framing device; the Eliot line opens and closes the read, with the thesis that knowledge dilutes as it gets easier to acquire.
- [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) — the same Eliot line applied to the information abundance unleashed by the transistor; knowledge grew, wisdom didn't.
- Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young — Young's counsel to "learn wisdom until you can organize the native elements for your benefit"; troubles come "not so much from a lack of facts as a want of wisdom."
