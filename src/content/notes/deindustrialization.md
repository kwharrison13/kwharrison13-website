# Deindustrialization

Deindustrialization is the through-line of [Janesville](/books/janesville), [Amy Goldstein](/notes/amy-goldstein)'s account of what happens to a single-industry town after its anchor employer leaves. When General Motors handed the Janesville assembly plant "its death sentence" in 2008, the loss rippled outward: middle-class GM families "tumbling out of the middle class" as $28/hour wages, SUB pay, and 401(k) cushions disappeared, the town keeping up appearances over "an economic earthquake." Goldstein's central empirical finding is a [Retraining](/notes/retraining) paradox — laid-off workers who went back to school at Blackhawk Technical College fared *worse* on employment and pay than those who didn't, despite a bipartisan "retraining gospel" embraced by Paul Ryan and Obama alike.

The book treats deindustrialization not just as job loss but as a [City Building](/notes/city-building) problem — a town's attempt to reinvent its economy (Forward Janesville, Rock County 5.0, University of Michigan economic-adjustment consultants) after losing its anchor. In Kyle's reading it sits in a "community-decline cluster" alongside [Hillbilly Elegy](/books/hillbilly-elegy) and [Our Towns](/books/our-towns) documenting working-class America, with [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) supplying the industrial-decline parallel.

**Context:** Deindustrialization refers to the decline of manufacturing employment and capacity in advanced economies — especially the US Rust Belt and Midwest from the 1970s onward — as plants closed or moved offshore. The 2008–09 financial crisis accelerated a wave of auto-plant closures, of which Janesville's GM plant was an emblematic case.

## Where this appears
