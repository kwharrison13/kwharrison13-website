# Bundle

This is not a company but a strategy — the recurring verb "bundle value into platforms" that anchors [Semil Shah](/notes/semil-shah)'s [The Market Is The Greatest Critic](/notes/the-market-is-the-greatest-critic) (auto-flagged as a company during a connection sweep because the wikilink [Bundle](/notes/bundle) appears repeatedly in the piece). Shah's thesis is that after the Nov '21–Jan '22 tech crash, the public markets stopped rewarding single-product companies and began rewarding firms that diversify their product lines into platforms. "It's no longer good enough to just handle online storage, or just facilitate online meetings, or just empower consumers to freely trade securities."

Shah's exemplars of successful bundling are the firms spared in the downturn — Facebook, Microsoft, Google, and Apple — companies that acquired assets like Instagram and WhatsApp, key infrastructure like Parse and Onavo, or Slack, Tableau, Minecraft, [LinkedIn](/notes/linkedin), and Activision, and bundled them into messaging, analytics, and gaming. The closing line of the piece is the canonical statement of the idea: "those special companies and business leaders who continue to bundle value into their platforms." It is the strategic counterpart to [Unbundling](/notes/unbundling) and is fundamentally a [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) argument — public investors and quant funds reallocating capital toward diversified-growth narratives.

**Context:** *Bundling — combining multiple products or services into a single offering — is a classic platform and pricing strategy; in tech strategy it is often paired with its inverse, unbundling, in the observation (attributed to Jim Barksdale) that the only two ways to make money are to bundle and to unbundle.*
