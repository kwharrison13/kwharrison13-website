# Perfectionism

Across two of Kyle's reads, perfectionism shows up as a pull worth resisting rather than a virtue. In [The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin](/books/the-autobiography-of-benjamin-franklin) it is the lesson of [The Speckled Ax](/notes/the-speckled-ax): a man asks a smith to grind his whole ax as bright as the edge, but the work proves so fatiguing that he gives up partway and decides he "like[s] a speckled ax best." Franklin offers it as a candid meditation on the diminishing returns of perfectionism and the gravitational pull of "good enough" — he applies it to his own faltering project of moral self-amendment, nearly ready to "content myself with a faulty character."

[The Magnolia Story](/books/the-magnolia-story) reaches the same posture from a different direction. Its closing argument against perfectionism is "thankfulness and contentment," and the book frames choosing presence with family over an "empty obsession" with perfect spaces — a recurring "what's the point in worrying?" stance. In both books the move is the same: trade the unreachable polish for contentment with the speckled, faulty, good-enough version.

- [The Magnolia Story](/books/the-magnolia-story) — "thankfulness and contentment" as the closing argument against perfectionism; presence over an "empty obsession" with perfect spaces
