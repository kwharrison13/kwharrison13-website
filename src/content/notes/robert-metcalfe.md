# Robert Metcalfe

In [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), Robert Metcalfe appears as the computer engineer who later formalized the principle that the value of a networked device increases dramatically as the number of people on the network grows — the formulation "sometimes known as Metcalfe's law." The book invokes him not for his own story but to retroactively explain Bell Labs' Picturephone failure: a larger network raises the per-user value of each device, while a smaller one lowers it. The Picturephone's network was minuscule, so its value to any single user was "vanishingly small," and price cuts couldn't fix a problem that was structurally about [Network Effects](/notes/network-effects) rather than cost. The same dynamic, the book notes, explains the immense appeal of the telephone system and the Internet.

**Context:** Robert Metcalfe (b. 1946) is an American engineer who co-invented Ethernet at Xerox PARC in 1973 and co-founded 3Com. Metcalfe's Law — the claim that a network's value scales with the square of its connected users — is named after him. He received the 2022 ACM Turing Award.

## Where this appears

- [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) — named as the originator of Metcalfe's Law, used to diagnose why the Picturephone failed: too small a network for any user to find it valuable.
