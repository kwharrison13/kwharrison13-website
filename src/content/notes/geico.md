# GEICO

GEICO is the auto insurer that recurs across Kyle's Buffett corpus as a foundational Berkshire case study. In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), discovering GEICO is the moment [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) "immersed himself in insurance" — a 20-year-old Buffett showed up uninvited at GEICO's headquarters one Saturday in 1951, and Lorimer "Davy" Davidson (later the company's CEO) patiently walked him through the company and the industry, an education Buffett credited as the start of his understanding of insurance and "such a key part in Berkshire's success." Over the following years Berkshire doubled its stake to become the controlling investor, and Buffett later acquired the rest; the page notes GEICO "seemed to go through a Buffett mold." Lou Simpson, who ran GEICO's investment portfolio, is the manager on whom Buffett's "lifetime punch card" parable left a profound mark.

In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), GEICO is Buffett's marquee example of a low-cost [Competitive Moat](/notes/competitive-moat): "The difference between GEICO's costs and those of its competitors is a kind of moat that protects a valuable and much-sought-after business castle." The letters trace the "virtuous circle" — low costs permit low prices, low prices attract and retain good policyholders, and a million-plus annual referrals (more than half of new business) drive [acquisition](/notes/customer-acquisition) costs lower still. The company also exemplifies Berkshire's [Insurance Float](/notes/insurance-float) economics (Buffett expects GEICO's float to be free over time) and its incentive-compensation philosophy — bonuses tied to just two variables, growth in voluntary auto policies and underwriting profitability, with no "lottery ticket" stock options.

**Context:** GEICO (Government Employees Insurance Company), founded in 1936, is a direct-to-consumer auto insurer headquartered in Maryland. It became a wholly owned subsidiary of Berkshire Hathaway in 1996.
