# iBuyer

In Kyle's corpus, "iBuyer" (instant-buyer) is the business-model label for [Opendoor](/notes/opendoor), the real-estate startup at the center of the March 5th, 2020 — JD Rasmussen (Opendoor) diligence call. The notes describe an iBuyer as a balance-sheet-heavy operation that buys [single-family](/notes/single-family-rental) homes directly, holds them as inventory, and resells them — making it as much a capital-markets business as a real-estate one. The interesting mechanic Kyle captured is the financing: Opendoor funded its home purchases with roughly $1B of credit-hedge-fund debt at a 10–15% spread across a portfolio of homes, later transitioning to lower-cost financing as it scaled.

That ties the iBuyer model tightly to [Warehouse Debt](/notes/warehouse-debt) — the short-term, revolving credit a company draws to hold assets (here, homes) before they are sold or refinanced. In Kyle's notes the spread between the cost of that warehouse line and the yield on the homes is what drives an iBuyer's unit economics, which is why [JD Rasmussen](/notes/jd-rasmussen)'s capital-markets function was the load-bearing part of Opendoor rather than property operations alone.

**Context:** "iBuyer" is an industry term for companies that use technology and pricing algorithms to make near-instant cash offers on residential homes, buy them directly, perform light work, and relist them — Opendoor, Zillow Offers, and Offerpad were the best-known examples in the late 2010s. The model is capital-intensive and exposed to interest-rate and home-price swings, which contributed to several players (notably Zillow) exiting the business.
