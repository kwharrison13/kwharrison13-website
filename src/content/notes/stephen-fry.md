# Stephen Fry

In Kyle's notes, Stephen Fry appears as the voice of the **theodicy challenge** — the problem of suffering used as the central atheist objection Kyle's faith writing answers. The recurring reference is a Fry interview in which, asked what he would say to God, he responds: "Bone cancer in children? What's that about? How dare you create a world in which there is so much misery that is not our fault?… Why should I respect a capricious, mean-minded, stupid God?" (cited under the `#Atheism` tag in Alma 13–16 — Enter into the Rest of the Lord).

The First Principles of Faithful Pragmatism makes Fry's challenge the essay's center of gravity. Kyle grants that "if you, like Stephen Fry, see this life as the center of existence, then he's not wrong" — that without eternity the world's injustice "feels inherently evil." His answer is a **God-as-Parent** theodicy: a parent preparing a child for an 8-year life would act nothing like one preparing a child for a full life, so eternity reframes "~90 years of suffering as a single hard soccer practice." Fry thus functions in Kyle's wiki less as a biographical subject than as the sharpest articulation of the objection faithful pragmatism must meet.

**Context:** Stephen Fry is a British actor, writer, and broadcaster, and a prominent public atheist; the "bone cancer in children" remarks come from a widely circulated 2015 RTÉ interview with Gay Byrne on the program *The Meaning of Life*.
