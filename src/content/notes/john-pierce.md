# John Pierce

One of the central figures in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), John Pierce is cast as [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs)'s great "instigator" — distinct from the genius ([Claude Shannon](/notes/claude-shannon), his close friend in the mathematics department) and the skillful manager ([Mervin Kelly](/notes/mervin-kelly)), Pierce steered a course between the two, sparking ideas rather than wielding power. A dreamer and unrepentant futurist with no aspirations to manage people, he was the advocate behind communications satellites: in October 1954 he proposed orbiting unmanned spacecraft to relay communications across great distances, an idea [Arthur C. Clarke](/notes/arthur-c-clarke) had unknowingly sketched a decade earlier and which Pierce pursued through the Echo and Telstar projects.

Pierce's technologist career ran in parallel with a prolific writing career — essays, science fiction, and books, some published under the pseudonyms John Roberts and "j.j. coupling" (borrowed from physics) so he could bypass the Bell Labs publications-clearance bottleneck. He is the source of one of the book's recurring lines on [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting): "Everyone faces the future with their eyes firmly on the past, and they don't see what's going to happen next" — a warning he extended to himself, insisting that anyone in the prediction business was destined to make a humiliating false step. Confident, self-deprecating (he kept what looked like every rejection letter he ever received), and later drawn into computer-generated music with Max Mathews, Pierce counts among the "Young Turks" alongside [William Shockley](/notes/william-shockley), Shannon, and Fisk.
