# Steve Jobs

Steve Jobs appears across Kyle's book corpus less as a biographical subject than as a **recurring exemplar invoked to make a point** — about ideas, focus, margins, and the cult of the singular founder. In [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) ([Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson)), Jobs is the modern proof of the serendipitous-collision theory of creativity: "Steve Jobs liked his buildings to have a central atrium" so people with diverse interests would encounter one another — the same physical-gathering-place engine of ideas Leonardo found at Ludovico Sforza's court and [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin) built with his Friday club. Isaacson also pairs Jobs with the "Real artists ship" countermaxim to Leonardo's perfectionism, and credits him with the "crazy ones... think different" Apple ad as a portrait of genius-as-misfit.

In [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) ([Ryan Holiday](/notes/ryan-holiday)), Jobs and [Jony Ive](/notes/jony-ive) anchor the **focus-as-saying-no** discipline: Ive's "focus is not this thing you aspire to... it's something you do every minute," and Jobs constantly asking, "How many things have you said no to?" — to focus on one thing requires not focusing on less important things. [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store) uses Jobs as Jeff Bezos's recurring foil: Bezos studied Apple's control of the music business to model the Kindle and explicitly wanted to avoid "Steve Jobs's mistake" of fat iPhone margins that invited competition (Bezos preferred low, defensible margins). The same book lists Jobs with Walt Disney and Henry Ford as entrepreneurs "turning imagination into reality," and — less flatteringly — groups him with Bezos, Gates, and Ellison as figures some say "lack a certain degree of empathy."

[The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) invokes Jobs twice: as one of the modern figures people name as "bigger than life" exemplars (the way Bell Labs had its own), and obliquely through Kyle's own note that "Disney and Apple have also become super litigious" — an indicator, he wonders, of a "corporate mode of harvest vs. innovate."

**Context:** Steve Jobs (1955–2011) co-founded Apple and, after being ousted, returned in 1997 to lead its turnaround with the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad. He also founded NeXT and bought/built Pixar. He is the canonical case study in design-driven product focus and the singular-founder mythology in tech.
