# Teach for Understanding

"Teach for understanding" is one step in Kyle's [How To Begin Teaching](/notes/how-to-begin-teaching) framework, drawn from his study of Alma 17–22 — I Will Make an Instrument of Thee. The case study is Ammon teaching king Lamoni: rather than leading with his own vocabulary, Ammon meets Lamoni inside Lamoni's own frame — "Believest thou that there is a God?" / "I do not know what that meaneth." / "Believest thou that there is a Great Spirit?" / "Yea." The point Kyle draws is to "empathize and teach for understanding rather than with words you're familiar with" — start from the learner's existing belief and build, instead of assuming shared terms.

The notes contrast this with Aaron, who "jumps straight in after having already alienated his investigators," asking about "the Son of God" to people who answer "We do not believe in these foolish traditions." Kyle is careful that this is not good-teacher-vs-bad-teacher: the different outcomes owed as much to Aaron facing "a more hardened and a more stiffnecked people." Teaching for understanding sits alongside the page's related ideas of letting silence work, letting the Spirit guide questions, and asking for commitment.

## Where this appears

- Alma 17–22 — I Will Make an Instrument of Thee — Ammon meeting Lamoni inside his own understanding of "the Great Spirit" as the model for teaching toward comprehension, contrasted with Aaron's head-on approach.
