# The Family: A Proclamation to the World

In [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons), "The Family: A Proclamation to the World" is the 1995 First Presidency statement that the book treats as the text encapsulating Mormonism's "evolving tug of war over women's roles." Jana Riess describes it as upholding "a gendered division of labor as divinely ordained" — fathers "to preside over their families... and... responsible to provide the necessities of life," mothers "primarily responsible for the nurture of their children" — while also insisting husbands and wives are "equal partners." The book locates the central tension there: in Mormon usage "to 'preside' means to have charge over," so a document that assigns presiding to the husband "by divine design" yet calls the marriage an equal partnership leaves open exactly the questions ("does the husband have the right to overrule his wife?") that define the faith's ongoing negotiation of gender.

The page notes the Proclamation's cultural weight beyond doctrine: though "not included as scripture in the LDS canon, it is often quoted in General Conference and has been framed and displayed in thousands of Mormon homes." The book ties this to the broader strain it documents — a postwar church that built its identity on marriage and the nuclear family ([The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) links it to [Marriage](/notes/marriage) and [Family](/notes/family)) now facing never-married members who "feel judged or shamed" and younger members whose more liberal social values diverge from leaders' positions.

**Context:** "The Family: A Proclamation to the World" was issued in 1995 by the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve Apostles of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. It affirms marriage between a man and a woman, the family as central to the Creator's plan, and complementary parental roles. Widely cited in church teaching and in debates over same-sex marriage, it is influential but not part of the LDS scriptural canon.
