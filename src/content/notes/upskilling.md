# Upskilling

In [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), **upskilling** is the central response to digital disruption of the workforce — paired with [Retraining](/notes/retraining) as the stakes of the transition, since "few precedents exist for retraining people at this speed and scale." The book's thesis: "Companies that don't reinvent themselves, don't think about digital disruption, and don't think about retraining and upskilling their workforce will be part of that churn." Because "no one knows for sure what skills are going to be needed for the future," the book argues for building continuous learning into the work itself — "upskilling and reskilling themselves at the same rate that things are changing... learning should be built into the work that they already have, not something that they do separate from work."

Kyle also tags upskilling in his Julie Chen / Retool call notes, where the dynamic shows up bottom-up rather than top-down: BizOps teams upskill themselves "because they're excited to learn a new skill in order to replace these manual processes." This grounds the book's abstract argument in a concrete observation — workers reach for new tools out of enthusiasm, not just mandate.

**Context:** Upskilling refers to teaching existing employees new competencies to keep pace with changing job requirements (as opposed to reskilling, which retrains workers for entirely different roles). It became a prominent corporate-strategy and HR theme amid concerns about automation and AI displacing routine work.
