# Conditioning

In [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), conditioning is the World State's core mechanism of control — the "slower but infinitely surer methods of ectogenesis, neo-Pavlovian conditioning and hypnopædia" that the Controllers adopted once they "realized that force was no good." Rather than coercing obedience, the State manufactures it: babies are decanted and predestined into castes (Alphas through Epsilons), then trained to "like their un-escapable social destiny" — "that is the secret of happiness and virtue—liking what you've got to do." The neo-Pavlovian method pairs infant exposure with electric shocks and loud noises so that aversions (to books, to flowers) become "reflexes unalterably conditioned," wedded "indissolubly."

The book's synthesis frames conditioning as the engine behind a society that "runs on manufactured wanting," where people "never want what they can't get" because their wanting itself was engineered. Mustapha Mond pushes the idea to its limit: belief is conditioned too — "People believe in God because they've been conditioned to believe in God," and philosophy is just "the finding of bad reason for what one believes by instinct." Kyle's reading connects conditioning to [Growth](/notes/growth) (consumption recast as civic duty) and treats [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection) — wanting, suffering, skilled work — as the marks of the unconditioned self the World State cannot fully decant.

**Context:** "Conditioning" in Huxley's 1932 novel borrows the language of behaviorist psychology (Pavlov's classical conditioning, Watson's behaviorism) to depict a totalitarian state that secures consent through pleasure and pre-programmed preference rather than the surveillance and fear of Orwell's *1984*.
