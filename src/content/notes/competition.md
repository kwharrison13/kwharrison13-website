# Competition

A cross-corpus theme that Kyle reads less as "fighting harder" than as "choosing the right game." In [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war), supreme excellence is "breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting" and taking the country whole — competition won through positioning and deception rather than direct confrontation. [Alibaba — The House That Jack Ma Built](/books/alibaba-the-house-that-jack-ma-built) dramatizes the same idea: [Jack Ma](/notes/jack-ma)'s strategy against eBay was to choose the home battlefield rather than meet a stronger opponent head-on ("it is difficult for an elephant to trample an ant… as long as you can dodge well"), and Ma reframes rivalry itself as joy — "if it brings you more and more agony, there must be something wrong with your competition strategy." [Find Your Territory](/notes/find-your-territory) extends this to the personal level: status is a zero-sum game, and the escape is to change the game rather than out-rank others within it.

Two of Kyle's sources treat competition as an *economic force* rather than a contest. [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) makes the point that in competitive (commodity) markets, the advantages from new technology "flow through to the customers," not the owners — Kyle's gloss: competition drives down the ROI of innovation (linked to [Commoditization](/notes/commoditization)). [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care) argues the opposite failure mode in health care: "Surrogates" (insurers, Medicare) sever the consumer's disciplining role, removing the competitive pressure that elsewhere forces lower prices and higher quality, so price stops reflecting efficiency. Together these frame competition in Kyle's notes as both a strategic discipline (win by positioning) and a market mechanism whose presence or absence determines where value accrues.
