# Persecution

A core theme of Kyle's Early Mormonism study, framed through what [Richard Bushman](/notes/richard-bushman) calls the "stereotype of fanaticism": anyone who claims to speak for God is assumed to hold absolute truth and a commission to impose it, so to claim revelation is the surest way to be thought fanatical and feared. Against the Missouri slavery controversy of the early 1800s, [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s plan to build [Zion](/notes/zion) into "a new world order" guaranteed conflict, and the rapidly growing Saints (810 in Jackson County by Nov. 1832, 1,200 by summer 1833) were driven out.

The study's key insight is that what enraged the Jacksonians was not the Saints' crimes but their **obedience** and their teaching "without respect of persons" — they disregarded the people-vs-subjects hierarchy. [Alexander Majors](/notes/alexander-majors), one of the Missourians who expelled them, later admitted the trouble came "solely from the claim that they had a new revelation from the almighty." Kyle draws the lesson that the Saints should hope to be near enough the mainstream to not be feared as fanatics, but never so mainstream that people stop thinking them weird — lest they become "the salt that lost its savor."
