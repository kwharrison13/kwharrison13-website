# The Great Apostasy

In Kyle's topical study Christian Reformation, Zion, and Seekers, the Great Apostasy is the doctrinal precondition that makes the [Restoration](/notes/restoration) necessary, and it is defined through a vivid image: apostasy as the "water of filthiness." Drawing on the "salt of the earth" metaphor ([Matthew 5](/notes/matthew-5):13), the notes argue that salt loses its savor "not simply over the passage of time, but through the addition of filth." An apostate is one removed from a true course — "when the garments of the covenant are spotted with iniquity" ([Alma 5](/notes/alma-5):22; [Isaiah 24](/notes/isaiah-24):5).

Crucially, the study frames apostasy as beginning in individual hearts before it becomes general: the devil lulls people into "carnal security" so they say "all is well in Zion" ([2 Nephi 28](/notes/2-nephi-28):21), and individual drift compounds into mass apostasy as people fail to "endure sound doctrine" ([2 Timothy 4](/notes/2-timothy-4):3). A continuation note (the "leaven of internal apostasy") stresses that after Christ's resurrection it was *internal* leavening, not only external persecution, that led to destruction. The whole arc is teleological in Kyle's reading: "The Christian reformation was the purification of the apostasy to prepare the world for the Restoration."

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology, the Great Apostasy is the belief that, following the death of the original apostles, the early Christian church lost priesthood authority and saw corrupted doctrine — a falling-away that, in this framework, the Restoration through Joseph Smith was meant to reverse.

## Where this appears

- Christian Reformation, Zion, and Seekers — apostasy as the "water of filthiness" that begins in individual hearts and spreads, framed as the condition the Reformation and Restoration were meant to cure.
