# Theory of Constraints

In [Essentialism](/books/essentialism), the Theory of Constraints appears as a tool in the execution section via the "slowest hiker" / "Herbie" framing: progress is gated by the single binding bottleneck, so the highest-leverage move is to find and remove it. The book frames it as a question — "What is the 'slowest hiker' in your job or your life? What is the obstacle that is keeping you back from achieving what really matters to you? By systematically identifying and removing this 'constraint' you'll be able to significantly reduce the friction keeping you from executing what is essential." It also notes that "any improvement with Herbie, however small, will improve the pace of the whole team immediately." Kyle's note distills the lesson: "we can see significant improvements if we remove constraints; putting the slowest hiker at the front."

The same execution chapter pairs this with a related discipline of "understanding the constraints of the problem you're actually solving" — problem framing itself as a bottleneck, illustrated by stories of journalists who did or didn't grasp the real question being asked.

**Context:** The Theory of Constraints is a management methodology introduced by Eliyahu Goldratt in his 1984 business novel *The Goal*, where "Herbie" is the slow Boy Scout hiker who illustrates how a single bottleneck determines a whole system's throughput.
