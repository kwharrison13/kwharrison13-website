# Open Data

Open data — public, machine-readable government datasets — is the load-bearing thread running through [Tom Schenk](/notes/tom-schenk)'s work as deputy IT director for the City of [Chicago](/notes/chicago). Schenk expanded Chicago's Open Data Portal and built tooling on top of it: Open Grid (the public-facing interface) and Windy-Grid (its internal counterpart). His open-data agenda extended into policy — he co-wrote the legislation that legalized ride shares in Chicago, which mandated per-trip data, turning a regulatory question into a data-collection mechanism.

In Kyle's notes, open data anchors Chicago's standing as a mature municipal data operation spanning the full lifecycle from database management to publication to advanced analytics. It sits inside the broader [GovTech](/notes/govtech) and [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) threads, where municipal data is one of the levers cities pull to modernize — alongside procurement reform, mobility, and civic-engagement tooling. Notably, Schenk's enthusiasm is for *practical* analytics built on the data (food-inspector routing, lead-paint prediction) rather than open data as an end in itself or technology-for-its-own-sake.

**Context:** Open data is a movement and policy practice in which governments and institutions publish datasets in freely accessible, reusable formats. Chicago's Open Data Portal is one of the most-cited municipal examples in the US civic-tech community.
