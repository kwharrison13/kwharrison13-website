# Self-Reflection

Across Kyle's reading, self-reflection is treated as **a teachable skill, not a disposition** — and as the discipline that underwrites accurate self-knowledge. [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) makes this explicit: alongside [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s inversion and [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility), "self-reflection [is] treated as a measurable skill" — "you are not suited to strongly hold an opinion until you can make the counter-argument more effectively than the people who want to argue with you." The companion piece [Charlie Munger: A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Revisited](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom-revisited) lands it as a [Circle of Competence](/notes/circle-of-competence) claim: "you've got to know what you know and what you don't know," to which Kyle annotates, "requires intense self-reflection — early in your career it's hard to discern what you're bad or good at vs. what you just haven't learned yet."

[The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) grounds the same idea in neuroscience and parenting: self-reflection is "a teachable skill" enabled by the brain's default mode network (the "off-line" system that processes life when not on task), and the worry that technology's ubiquity leaves young people "too few opportunities for self-reflection." Its method is the "observant self" — training someone to observe their own mental state, including negative self-talk, "instead of identifying with it." Kyle's marginalia there: "self reflection requires 'self.'" [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) operationalizes it as a habit — [James Clear](/notes/james-clear)'s annual review and the three questions ("What went well? What didn't? What did I learn?") that make "long-term improvement of all habits" possible by surfacing mistakes. The concept sits adjacent to [Metacognition](/notes/metacognition) (thinking about one's own thinking) and the broader learning thread.

**Context:** Self-reflection / metacognition is a well-studied driver of learning and expertise; "deliberate practice" research (Ericsson) and reflective-practice frameworks both treat the capacity to assess one's own performance as a trainable skill rather than a fixed trait.
