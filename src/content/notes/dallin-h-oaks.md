# Dallin H. Oaks

Dallin H. Oaks is one of the most frequently-cited Latter-day Saint leaders across Kyle's gospel-study corpus, appearing in dozens of pages on a handful of recurring themes. On **prophetic fallibility and personal revelation** he anchors a key thread in [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind): "we don't believe in the infallibility of our leaders," quoted alongside Woodruff's "won't lead you astray" and Nelson's "give your leaders a little leeway" — the cluster Kyle reads as evidence that the prophet doesn't always tell us exactly what to do; sometimes he asks us to seek our own direction. The same emphasis on **agency** recurs in Obedience — Topical Study, where Oaks cautions against extending a desire to be led "to the point of wanting to be led in all things" — "Personal decision making is one of the sources of growth we are meant to experience in mortality."

He is the authoritative voice on **women and priesthood authority** in [The Priesthood Power of Women](/books/the-priesthood-power-of-women) ("when a woman is set apart to preach the gospel... she is given priesthood authority to perform a priesthood function") and on the discipline of not "putting reasons to revelation." In [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book) he supplies the missionary-purpose quotes ("a harvester, not a gleaner"; the true purpose of preaching is baptism, not membership) and his teaching that believers are saved "conditionally" until they endure to the end (Christ Defines His Gospel). His BYU address Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks frames truth and tolerance as "twin ideas" — for believers, tolerance is "a two-sided coin," respect on one side, truth always on the other. He also recurs in Faith — Topical Study ("Two Kinds of Judgment") and across many Come Follow Me pages.

**Context:** Dallin H. Oaks (b. 1932) is a member of the First Presidency of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, serving as First Counselor and President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. A former Utah Supreme Court justice and BYU president, he became an apostle in 1984.
