# Speculative Fiction

Speculative fiction is the fiction-of-possible-futures mode that anchors Kyle's [Historical Futurism](/essays/historical-futurism) thesis. In the Historical Futurism research notes it is the broader genre the thesis draws on — the catalog of "near-future / speculative works — many dystopian," kept as raw material under the essay's principle that "we can learn as much from what could be as from what we hope will never be." The research bundles it with [Science Fiction](/notes/science-fiction), [Futurology](/notes/futurology), and [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting), and cites concrete examples like the Interintellect "Power of Speculative Fiction" thread and the Guardian's "Palestine in 2048" piece — fiction imagining a real place's actual future.

[The Time Machine](/books/the-time-machine) is treated as a foundational text of the genre: H. G. Wells frames time as a navigable fourth dimension, and the book's Eloi/Morlock far-future is read as speculative fiction extrapolating "the industrial system of to-day" to its endpoint. Kyle's notes file it alongside companion dystopias [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) and [1984](/books/1984), and near-future war novels like *2034* and *2054* — all of them treated as the genre's apparatus for thinking about what could be.

**Context:** "Speculative fiction" is an umbrella term for narrative genres that depart from consensus reality — science fiction, fantasy, dystopia, alternate history, and near-future extrapolation — often used (as here) to emphasize the imaginative-extrapolation function over the technological-hardware connotations of "science fiction" specifically.
