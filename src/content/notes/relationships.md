# Relationships

"Relationships" runs through Kyle's notes in two distinct registers. In [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog) ([Bruce D. Perry](/notes/bruce-d-perry)), it is the book's central thesis about healing childhood trauma: "the research on the most effective treatments... might be accurately summed up this way: what works best is anything that increases the quality and number of relationships in the child's life." Perry frames human relationships as double-edged — "we can both create and destroy, nurture and terrorize, traumatize and heal each other" — and argues that society's "general disrespect for the importance of relationships is undermining the development of [Empathy](/notes/empathy)," which, like language, must be learned. The template of a baby's first attachment is what later allows "healthy intimate relationships as an adult," tying the concept to [Memory](/notes/memory) as a guide to behavior.

In [Building the Global Knowledge Graph](/essays/building-the-global-knowledge-graph), the word takes a knowledge-tooling sense: Kyle observes that Roam's interface "becomes their default 'thinking' interface," and that meaningful usage is "creating relationships right in the process of thinking" — i.e., the links between notes that form a personal knowledge graph. The two uses share an underlying intuition Kyle keeps circling: that value (whether in human development or in knowledge) accrues in the connections, not the isolated nodes.

## Where this appears

- [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog) — the central thesis that increasing the quality and number of a child's relationships is what most effectively heals trauma; relationships as the seedbed of empathy.
- [Building the Global Knowledge Graph](/essays/building-the-global-knowledge-graph) — "relationships" as the links a thinker creates between notes when Roam becomes their default thinking interface.
