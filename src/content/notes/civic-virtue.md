# Civic Virtue

Civic virtue is a thread Kyle traces across several books — the disposition to contribute to a community, often without recognition or personal reward. In [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) it names the tension between "conscience and oath weighed against political fortune," the workhorse-vs-performance Senate ethic that Kyle also reads as the case for [Term Limits](/notes/term-limits) (his marginal note: "We need term limits"). [The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin](/books/the-autobiography-of-benjamin-franklin) places it among Franklin's civic-mindedness and partnership-building. [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) gives it its most distilled image: the Adlerian "feeling of contribution" without visible recognition — "It's like putting away your shopping cart. You feel good because you're contributing even if no one sees you" — i.e. worth regardless of observation. And in [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) it appears as the local congregation experienced as "unchosen community that forges loyalty like a family," the ward "as inescapable" as kin.

Across these sources the common move is the same: virtue measured by contribution rather than by audience, whether the arena is a legislature, a city, a self-help framework, or a religious community.

**Context:** Civic virtue is a classical-republican idea — the cultivation of habits in citizens (public-spiritedness, self-restraint, participation) that a self-governing society depends on. It runs from Aristotle and the Roman republic through the American founders, who saw it as essential to sustaining a republic.
