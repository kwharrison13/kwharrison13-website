# Umberto Eco

Cited in [The Collector's Fallacy](/notes/the-collector-s-fallacy) (Christian Tietze, Zettelkasten.de) as a witness against the illusion of knowledge created by hoarding texts. From *How to Write a Thesis*, Eco's line: "there's a lot someone doesn't know anything about precisely because she photocopied a text; she has given herself in to the illusion of having read the text already." The article uses Eco to make its core point — that "knowing about" a thing (being certain it exists) is not the same as knowing it, and that collecting bookmarks and photocopies feels productive while producing no actual knowledge.

**Context:** Umberto Eco (1932–2016) was an Italian novelist, semiotician, and philosopher, author of the novels *The Name of the Rose* and *Foucault's Pendulum* and the academic handbook *How to Write a Thesis*. He was famous for a vast personal library and reflections on the limits of what one can actually read versus merely own.
