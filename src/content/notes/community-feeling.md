# Community Feeling

Community feeling is the endpoint of Adlerian psychology as presented in [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked): "this sense of others as comrades, this awareness of 'having one's own refuge,' is called 'community feeling.'" The book frames it as the switch "from attachment to self (self-interest) to concern for others (social interest)," and says reaching it requires three things in a self-reinforcing loop — *self-acceptance* ("one accepts one's irreplaceable 'this me' just as it is"), *confidence in others* (placing unconditional trust in people so one sees them as comrades), and *contribution to others* (acting on "I am of use to someone"). Each feeds the next: self-acceptance enables confidence in others, which enables contribution, which deepens the sense of being of use and so feeds back into self-acceptance. (source: [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked))

Crucially, the book insists the contribution "does not have to be a visible one" — all that is needed is "the subjective sense that 'I am of use to someone.'" Seeking that feeling through recognition from others is a trap, because it forces one to "walk through life in accordance with other people's wishes." This grounds the book's closing thesis, "Happiness is the feeling of contribution" (see [Happiness](/notes/happiness)) and connects to [Civic Virtue](/notes/civic-virtue) — Kyle glosses the contribution-without-recognition idea as "putting away your shopping cart. You feel good because you're contributing even if no one sees you." (source: [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked))

**Context:** "Community feeling" translates Alfred Adler's term *Gemeinschaftsgefühl* (also rendered "social interest"), the orientation toward the welfare of the broader human community that Adlerian individual psychology treats as the marker of mental health.

## Where this appears

- [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) — Adler's *Gemeinschaftsgefühl* as the book's destination: self-acceptance, confidence in others, and invisible contribution culminating in "happiness is the feeling of contribution."
