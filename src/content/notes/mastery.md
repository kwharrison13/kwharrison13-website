# Mastery

Mastery surfaces across two books as the human need for competence earned through skilled, effortful work. In [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), it is the book's "one positive image": the Savage's delight in the "mowing scene," where after "weeks of idleness in London, with nothing to do… but to press a switch or turn a handle, it was pure delight to be doing something that demanded skill and patience." Friction, difficulty, and the dignity of skilled effort are the one human need [Work](/notes/work) supplies that the World State cannot decant.

[The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) reaches the same idea from developmental psychology. Drawing on Deci and Ryan's self-determination theory, competence is one of three legs — alongside [Autonomy](/notes/autonomy) and relatedness — of healthy motivation. Crucially, the book argues competence "is more about our feeling that we can handle a situation than it is about being really great at something… an internal rather than external barometer of accomplishment." Mastery cannot be developed *for* a child; attempts to do so "undermine their own motivation." It pairs tightly with [Intrinsic Motivation](/notes/intrinsic-motivation): the progression runs "I am capable of doing this" → "I love this," and the way to build a healthy dopamine system is simply to "encourage them to work hard at what they love."

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) — the mowing scene's "skill and patience" as the one human need the engineered World State cannot satisfy.
- [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) — competence as a leg of self-determination theory; an internal sense of "I can handle this," inseparable from autonomy and intrinsic motivation.
