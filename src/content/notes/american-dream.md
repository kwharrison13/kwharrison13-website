# American Dream

The American Dream recurs across Kyle's corpus as the merit-over-birth promise that wealth and recognition should track effort and capability rather than the "fortuitous circumstances of birth or position." [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written) sources the canonical definition from James Truslow Adams's 1931 *The Epic of America* and frames the Dream — alongside "common ground" — as one of the two ideals at the heart of the Declaration; it also argues the **entrenchment of a meritocratic elite came at the expense of community and the American Dream**. The [Marie Antoinette](/notes/marie-antoinette) page makes the same contrast concrete: the queen dismisses [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin) as a former "printer's foreman," and Franklin "would have proudly agreed" — the aristocratic-birth-vs-self-made-achievement scene compressed into a single moment.

The Dream also has a darker, frayed register. In [Hillbilly Elegy](/books/hillbilly-elegy), Vance's grandparents held "an almost religious faith in hard work and the American Dream," set against a working-class drift toward [Learned Helplessness](/notes/learned-helplessness) and blaming outcomes on society. And in [It's Time To Build](/notes/it-s-time-to-build), [Marc Andreessen](/notes/marc-andreessen) casts the Dream as something to be *rebooted by building* — breaking the price curves of housing, education, and healthcare, since "the things we build in huge quantities drop rapidly in price" while the things we don't (housing, schools, hospitals) skyrocket. [John Rawls](/notes/john-rawls)'s veil-of-ignorance framing is applied to the Dream's "floor vs. ceiling" elsewhere in Kyle's research notes.

**Context:** The phrase "American Dream" was popularized by James Truslow Adams in *The Epic of America* (1931), describing a social order in which each person can attain "the fullest stature of which they are innately capable... regardless of the fortuitous circumstances of birth or position."
