# User Generated Content

In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store), user-generated content is the mechanism behind one of Amazon's earliest moats. Bezos believed that if Amazon.com had more user-generated book reviews than any other site, it would give the company a huge advantage — customers would be less inclined to go to other online bookstores. Kyle tags this passage with both #[Network Effect](/books/network-effect) and #[User Generated Content](/notes/user-generated-content): the reviews are a [network effect](/books/network-effect), because their value compounds as more customers contribute, and the accumulated content is a switching cost competitors can't easily replicate. The "unstore" framing in the same book extends this — allowing negative reviews alongside positive ones, and placing used products next to new, was a deliberate bet that customer-supplied honesty builds more trust than curated retail.

In the Open-Source Knowledge - Research notes, user-generated content sits in a cluster with [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia), influencers, and expertise — the open question being who gets to produce "notable" knowledge and how subjective that judgment is. The two uses bookend the concept: UGC as a competitive asset (Amazon reviews) and UGC as a contested epistemic commons (Wikipedia's notability fights).

**Context:** User-generated content (UGC) refers to text, images, reviews, and media produced by a platform's users rather than by the platform itself. It underpins businesses from Amazon reviews to Wikipedia to social media, and its value typically grows with network effects as contributions accumulate.
