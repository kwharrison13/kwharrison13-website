# Death of Expertise

"Death of expertise" is a recurring frame in Kyle's reading — drawn from [Tom Nichols](/notes/tom-nichols)'s book [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) — for the collapse of deference to specialized knowledge, captured in the line Kyle repeatedly cites: *"my opinions are as good as your facts."* He compresses the dynamic into a personal ladder he annotates across multiple books: *"I know nothing → I know a little → I know a lot → I know a lot about a little"* (noted in both [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) and [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet)). [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) supplies the antidote thread — [Mortimer Adler](/notes/mortimer-adler) on the relatively ignorant wrongly disagreeing with the learned, and opinion-vs-knowledge — which Kyle keeps distinct from the related but separate [Curse of Expertise](/notes/curse-of-expertise) (the specialist's *inability to communicate*, as opposed to the layperson's *refusal to defer*).

The concept connects across genres. [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) supplies the structural/mathematical version: [Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb) argues that in environments subject to Black Swans, "certain professionals, while believing they are experts, are in fact not" — better at narrating than predicting — which Kyle tags directly to the death-of-expertise thread ("Taleb supplies the math, Nichols supplies the political consequence"). [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) historicizes it through the individualizing arc of the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters), where "my opinions are as good as your facts" reads as a radicalization of post-Reformation individuality. Kyle links the idea onward to [Conspiracy Theories](/notes/conspiracy-theories) and anti-vaccine sentiment ("it's very hard to always be right but it's become much easier to jump up and down when someone is wrong"), and it recurs as a touchstone in his essay research on [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) and rhetoric.

**Context:** *The Death of Expertise: The Campaign Against Established Knowledge and Why It Matters* (2017) is a book by political scientist Tom Nichols arguing that the internet, higher education's customer-service model, and political tribalism have eroded public trust in experts.
