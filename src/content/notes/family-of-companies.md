# Family of Companies

The "Family of Companies" is Kyle's recurring tag for the Berkshire-style approach of permanently owning a collection of businesses rather than treating them as a tradeable portfolio. It is the concept Kyle attaches throughout [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) to Buffett's "'til-death-do-us-part" ownership policy — set against the "modern manager [who] refers to his 'portfolio' of businesses" as restructuring candidates "whenever such a move is dictated by Wall Street preferences." Kyle's notes frame the family-of-companies idea as the antithesis of [diversification](/notes/diversification) for its own sake ("this isn't just buying and picking stocks, it's about creating real value") and as the structure that lets a [Holding Company](/notes/holding-company) stay "laser focused on [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) and returns regardless of where in the family of companies they leave the capital."

The same idea anchors the holding-company / VC-fund-structure thread in [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds), where Berkshire is treated as the model for "a portfolio that holds and compounds rather than churns" — "funds or collections of shareholders, if assembled correctly, could much more effectively be leveraged as a collaborative body." In both sources the concept connects to [Buy and Never Sell](/notes/buy-and-never-sell) and [Compounding](/notes/compounding): keep good businesses under one roof permanently and let capital redeploy internally rather than exiting and paying the friction of trade.

## Where this appears

- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — Kyle's tag on Buffett's permanent-ownership / "'til-death-do-us-part" passages and the counterargument to a diversified, restructuring-driven portfolio.
- [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) — Berkshire's family-of-companies / holding-co model held up as the template for a fund that compounds rather than churns.
