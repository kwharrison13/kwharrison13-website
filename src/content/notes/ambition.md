# Ambition

Ambition is one of Kyle's load-bearing essay themes, and his sources hold it in deliberate tension. [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) supplies the **cautionary** reading: [Ryan Holiday](/notes/ryan-holiday) calls ambition "the most intoxicating and the hardest to control" of all addictions, "because unlike drinking, society rewards it" — yet also poses the counter-question Kyle flags, "what would the world look like if nobody tried to do anything?... If we didn't have ambition—some big goal we are after—how would we know what little things... to say no to?" [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) shows the **inherited burden** of ambition: the Adamses sought to "excite in [their children] an ambition to excel in every capacity," and the resulting "ambition his parents instilled in him was eating him up."

In Kyle's own essay research, ambition is the trait whose cultural softening he diagnoses. In The Hardening Of The Great Softening, the erosion of the "[temporarily embarrassed millionaire](/notes/temporarily-embarrassed-millionaires)" self-image — the belief that "success is not a question of if, but when" — is tied to a generational softening of ambition. Toil We Must and The Renaissance of Rise and Grind argue the opposite case ("I think we should be more ambitious than we are"), studying ambitious culture-builders like [DARPA](/notes/darpa) and [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs). The concept connects outward to [Golden Handcuffs](/notes/golden-handcuffs) (the comfort that blunts ambition), [Progressivism](/notes/progressivism) (ambition as the drive toward "more progression"), and the icon [Leslie Knope](/notes/leslie-knope) (earnest, relentless civic ambition).

- [Progressivism](/notes/progressivism) — ambition as the drive toward personal, not just economic, growth.
