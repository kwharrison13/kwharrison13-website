# Tradeoffs

In Kyle's reading of [Essentialism](/books/essentialism), tradeoffs are the *actual method* behind the discipline — not just "do less," but making explicit choices between big things. The Essentialist accepts that tradeoffs are real and inherent to life ("until we accept that reality we'll be doomed... stuck in a 'straddled strategy'"), and reframes the question from "What do I have to give up?" to "What do I want to go big on?" The book's worked examples that Kyle flagged: [Southwest Airlines](/notes/southwest-airlines) — Herb Kelleher's point-to-point, no-meals, no-assigned-seats choices made "by design, not by default"; the J&J customer-recall principle; and the "Essentialist parents" who deliberately pulled their child out of activities that weren't going to be his "big thing" in pursuit of a clear goal.

Greg McKeown contrasts this with the nonessentialist who thinks "I can do both" and asks "How can I do it all?" — versus the essentialist who asks "What is the trade-off I want to make?" The failure mode is the manager who refused to cut her eighteen "top priority" projects down to five, spreading the same effort across too many things and getting none of the results she wanted.
