# Growth Mindset

Growth Mindset is [Carol Dweck](/notes/carol-dweck)'s fixed-vs-growth distinction as it recurs across Kyle's reading on learning, hiring, and parenting. In *[The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child)* it is "a load-bearing chapter": students with a "fixed mindset" see mistakes as evidence of an unchangeable lack of ability, while those with a "growth mindset" focus on their own effort as the path to success — the book's prescription is to praise effort and strategy over innate ability, calling a growth mindset "the MVP of the self-motivated child." *[The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning)* defines it tersely as the "belief that one's ability and knowledge are capable of evolving," and groups it with cognitively rich homes, project-based and [Montessori](/notes/montessori) approaches, and a well-sequenced [Curriculum](/notes/curriculum) as things that "all measurably improve learning."

As a hiring heuristic it appears in *The Talent Vortex - Research*: when betting on potential over experience, look for "someone constantly trying to learn (books, sports, languages) outside a structured school setting." In the [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research) meetings, Conor White-Sullivan invokes it as the founding disposition of a "peer-to-peer university with the most growth-centric mindset ever," and in the Valued Empyrean discussion it sits inside the [Character Skills](/notes/character-skills) / [Practical Knowledge](/notes/practical-knowledge) framework as the "Fixed → Value standpoint" shift.

**Context:** "Growth mindset" is the term coined by Stanford psychologist Carol Dweck in her 2006 book *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*, contrasting the belief that abilities are static (fixed) with the belief that they are developable through effort, strategy, and learning.
