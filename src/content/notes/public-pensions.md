# Public Pensions

Public pensions surface in Kyle's corpus as a window onto [LP](/notes/lp) behavior — how the largest institutional allocators select managers, set performance bars, and balance returns against policy mandates. [CalPERS](/notes/calpers), the $380B fund and the largest in the U.S., is the anchor case: per [Almost $14 Billion Pulled From CalPERS Equity Managers](/notes/almost-14-billion-pulled-from-calpers-equity-managers), CEO [Marcie Frost](/notes/marcie-frost) disclosed that CalPERS terminated most of its external equity managers — cutting their allocation from $33.6B to $5.5B — on long-term-underperformance grounds, with CIO [Ben Meng](/notes/ben-meng) driving a "renewed focus" on hitting the 7% assumed rate of return. The note captures the governance tension explicitly: when CalPERS was overfunded before the financial crisis, "subpar investment performance could be tolerated or investment staff could look the other way in the name of diversity."

[Assembly Bill 2041 — UC Office of the Chief Investment Officer](/notes/assembly-bill-2041-uc-office-of-the-chief-investment-officer) is the policy companion: a 2018 California factsheet urging [UC Investments](/notes/uc-investments) (a $118B fund-of-funds LP) to encourage diversity in staff hiring and among investment partners, and to launch emerging-manager programs. Together the two reads frame the recurring public-pension theme in Kyle's notes — the friction between manager-diversity / emerging-manager mandates and raw performance accountability at state-fund scale.

**Context:** Public pension funds are government-sponsored retirement systems for public employees (teachers, civil servants, university staff). As some of the world's largest institutional investors, they act as limited partners across public equities, private equity, and venture capital, and their allocation and governance choices move large sums of capital.
