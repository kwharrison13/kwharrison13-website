# Management

A concept Kyle tracks across his investing and business reading, skeptical of management as a profession and attentive to how rarely managerial performance is actually measured. The richest source is [Managers Not MBAs](/books/managers-not-mbas) by [Henry Mintzberg](/notes/henry-mintzberg), whose central claim is that management is a *practice* learned in context — "from the bottom up," through [Apprenticeship](/notes/apprenticeship) and on-the-job mentoring — not a science or profession conferred by a degree. From the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Kyle flags [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s "supreme irony of business management": an inadequate CEO keeps his job far more easily than an inadequate subordinate, because performance standards for the top job seldom exist (or get waived), while a typist who can't hit her words-per-minute is out in no time.

The [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) long-reads sharpen two further angles. From *A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom*, Kyle pulls Munger's rule that "averaged out, betting on the quality of a business is better than betting on the quality of management" — bet on business momentum, not the brilliance of the manager, while staying alert to the rare manager good enough to follow into a mediocre business. From *Academic Economics*, he notes Ricardo's comparative advantage applied to managers: even when a manager could do every task better himself, it remains mutually advantageous to divide and delegate. The concept also threads into [Playbooks](/notes/playbooks) as one of the operational areas a company must run.
