# T.S. Eliot

In Kyle's corpus, T.S. Eliot is quoted almost entirely for two recurring epigraphs. The first, from "The Rock" — "Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?" — is the spine of his knowledge-vs-information thread. It frames the entire arc of [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) (surfacing at both the beginning and the end, tied to the hypothesis that knowledge too easily acquired gets diluted), opens [Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address) and the [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) concept page (knowledge as moral duty; the "learning machine"; real vs. [chauffeur knowledge](/notes/chauffeur-knowledge)), and recurs in [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) and the [Books 2.0](/essays/books-20) research.

The second, from *Four Quartets* ("Little Gidding") — "We shall not cease from exploration… and know the place for the first time" — anchors his appearances in religious and devotional notes. It is quoted in [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) on the return of exploration to a deepened starting point, and the "complete simplicity (costing not less than everything)" lines appear in General Conference — October 2021. He sits in [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book) alongside [C.S. Lewis](/notes/c-s-lewis) and G.K. Chesterton as a Christian literary voice folded into Kyle's largely LDS quote corpus.

**Context:** T.S. Eliot (1888–1965) was an American-born British poet, critic, and playwright — author of "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," "The Waste Land," and *Four Quartets* — and a 1948 Nobel laureate whose later work reflects his Anglican conversion.
