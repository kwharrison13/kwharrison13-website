# Continuous Learning

The idea that learning should be ongoing and built into daily life rather than crammed in episodic bursts — a recurring throughline across Kyle's reading on careers and judgment. It is the central organizing idea of [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), which argues that corporate training fails because it copies the university lecture-and-exam model (over $130B spent in the US "with little to show"); the alternative is learning woven into the work, self-directed and personalized, where there is "no such thing as an average person." A culture of continuous learning lets employees learn in their own time and way through [Videos](/notes/videos), [articles](/notes/articles), [Podcast](/notes/podcast)s, [Books](/notes/books), and events — and crucially makes learning "something that people love to do… rather than something they dread."

The same imperative shows up in two adjacent registers. [Recession Proof](/books/recession-proof) frames it as a career defense: "Always be learning… if you're not sharpening your tools, they're going to get rusty" — build skills for the job you want, not the one you have. [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in [The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment), casts it as a duty to one's better self: a wise man keeps practicing his rarely-used skills across disciplines, because narrowing them produces "man with a hammer" tendency and shrinks the latticework of theory needed to understand new experience. Munger's formulation — "continuous thinking and learning, done with joy" — is both a hedge against skill atrophy and, he allows, a way to "somewhat help delay what is inevitable."
