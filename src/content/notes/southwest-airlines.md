# Southwest Airlines

Southwest Airlines is invoked in two of Kyle's book notes as a case study, each time about a different virtue. In [Essentialism](/books/essentialism), it's the canonical example of [Tradeoffs](/notes/tradeoffs) "made by design, not by default": Greg McKeown recounts a Herb Kelleher talk where Kelleher described deliberately choosing point-to-point flights, no meals, no assigned seats, and coach-only service — each a conscious trade-off to keep costs down, accepting the risk of alienating some customers because he was totally clear about what Southwest was (a low-cost airline) and what it was not. The book contrasts this with Continental, which "was forced to sacrifice things around the margins that weren't part of a coherent strategy." Kyle's own note flags the story as also appearing in *Good to Great*.

In [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care), David Goldhill cites Southwest — alongside [Apple](/notes/apple), [Amazon](/notes/amazon), Ikea, FedEx, [Google](/notes/google), and the major credit-card companies — as a "Mainland" firm that created billions in value by simplifying something complicated, a feat Goldhill argues no health-care company in any sector even attempts.

**Context:** Southwest Airlines is a U.S. low-cost carrier founded in 1967, long known for its point-to-point route network, single-aircraft-type fleet, and no-frills operating model.

## Where this appears

- [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) — Herb Kelleher's deliberate point-to-point, no-meals, no-assigned-seats tradeoffs as Essentialist strategy by design (contrasted with Continental).
- [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care) — listed as a "Mainland" company that created value by making the complicated simple, unlike health care.
