# Celestial Kingdom

In Kyle's gospel-study notes, the Celestial Kingdom is the highest degree of glory and the destination the whole framework of discipleship points toward. The recurring theme is that entrance is earned through becoming, not merely believing: in 10 Gifts To Take Home From Your Mission, work itself "is what will gain us entrance into the Celestial Kingdom," and — citing [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley) — "you cannot get to the Celestial Kingdom alone," since teamwork is an eternal principle and your eternity is spent with your spouse. Chosen Leaders frames present affliction as preparation: our trials are "but a small moment" ([D&C 121](/notes/dandc-121):7) as we ready ourselves for celestial glory, and quotes [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) — "if you cannot endure persecution, you cannot endure the glory of the Celestial Kingdom."

The Humility notes treat baptism and [Sanctification](/notes/sanctification) ("yielding our hearts to God") as the doorway, with the meek granted entrance. The Mormon 7–9 weekly study (November 2–8, 2020. Mormon 7–9: "I Speak unto You As If Ye Were Present") ties "thinking about the Celestial Kingdom" to the [Faith](/notes/faith) / [Pascal's Wager](/notes/pascal-s-wager) thread — the long-term stakes that make present obedience rational. Across all four sources the consistent logic is that affliction, work, humility, and covenant relationships are the refining means to a celestial end.

**Context:** In the theology of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, the Celestial Kingdom is the highest of three degrees of glory in the afterlife (alongside the terrestrial and telestial), drawn from Joseph Smith's vision recorded in Doctrine and Covenants 76. Exaltation within it is associated with eternal marriage and becoming like God.
