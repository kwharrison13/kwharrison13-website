# Vertical SaaS

Industry-specific software — SaaS built for one vertical rather than sold horizontally across all of them — is a category Kyle tracks closely on the investing side, with [Bessemer Venture Partners](/notes/bessemer-venture-partners) as the canonical framework source. Two BVP pieces sit in Kyle's reading queue as the playbook: [Brian Feinstein](/notes/brian-feinstein)'s 2017 Vertical Software— A Founder's Guide to Success (market selection, winner-take-most dynamics, the economics of industry-specific SaaS) and [Christine Deakers](/notes/christine-deakers)'s 2022 update Ten Lessons from a Decade of Vertical Software Investing (choosing markets wisely, enduring growth, building industry-defining companies).

The grounded examples come from [Nina Achadjian](/notes/nina-achadjian) of [Index Ventures](/notes/index-ventures), a recurring deal-sourcing contact focused on vertical SaaS, who walked Kyle through concrete deals: [Arrow XYZ](/notes/arrow-xyz) ("vertical SaaS for car dealerships," ~40% of dealerships already running something, the product as a Trojan horse that "can sit on top of the CRM"), [Monograph](/notes/monograph) (architecture-firm practice management), and [Openspace.ai](/notes/openspace-ai) (construction-site capture). She also authored the [Seso](/notes/seso) Series A brief — vertical software for agricultural workforce management starting with H-2A migrant-worker onboarding. The throughline across these is the practical question Kyle keeps probing: switching costs, why a buyer adopts a *second* CRM, and defensibility in crowded verticals.

**Context:** Vertical SaaS targets a single industry (e.g., dental, construction, restaurants) versus horizontal SaaS (CRM, email) that serves all industries. The thesis is that deep domain fit, embedded workflows, and add-on fintech/payments can produce durable, winner-take-most companies despite smaller TAMs — popularized by firms like Bessemer and exemplified by Toast, Procore, and Veeva.
