# Academic Research

A recurring node in Kyle's [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) thinking, framed less around the research itself than around the gap between expert knowledge and the public. In [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), Kyle ties the book's argument — that experts who can't translate their findings into plain English leave "a wide open space… for amateurs, hucksters, charlatans, and conspiracy theorists" — to a [David Perell](/notes/david-perell) tweet about the opportunity to translate academic research for everyday people. His Open-Source Knowledge - Research essay notes connect academic research to [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia), [Progress Studies](/notes/progress-studies), and [Education](/notes/education) via [Matt Clancy](/notes/matt-clancy)'s argument that better access to knowledge drives more innovation.

In [The Founders](/books/the-founders), Kyle flags academic research as a contrast case: at Pinnacle, researchers "weren't publishing-or-perishing; they were producing" — real technology rather than papers, his shorthand critique of the publish-or-perish incentive. Separately, the July 18th, 2022 — Ethan Ding meeting uses "academic research" in a market sense, as a "super tough end-market to sell into" where every vendor "has sort of sucked."

**Context:** Academic research refers to the formal scholarly knowledge-production system of universities and journals; the "publish or perish" phrase Kyle invokes describes the career pressure on academics to continuously publish to secure funding and tenure.
