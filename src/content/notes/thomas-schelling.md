# Thomas Schelling

Deterrence theorist and game theorist who recurs in Kyle's reading as the voice of nuclear strategy. In [Nuclear War — a Scenario](/books/nuclear-war-a-scenario), [Annie Jacobsen](/notes/annie-jacobsen) quotes his most famous line from *Arms and Influence* — "The power to hurt is bargaining power. To exploit it is diplomacy — vicious diplomacy, but diplomacy" — as the founding logic of deterrence the whole scenario interrogates. [Game Theory](/notes/game-theory) is the strategic-interaction frame underpinning the book: deterrence, "use them or lose them," and bargaining-as-diplomacy all trace to Schelling.

In [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) (Ananyo Bhattacharya's [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann) biography), Schelling closes the nuclear-strategy thread with his 2005 reflection, delivered days before collecting his Nobel Prize, that "the most spectacular event of the past half century is one that did not occur" — sixty years without nuclear weapons "exploded in anger" — which he attributed to an unspoken taboo. The same book credits Schelling with the segregation model showing that cities can self-segregate by race even when no one minds a mixed community, and that only an active desire for diversity produces diverse neighborhoods.

**Context:** Thomas Schelling (1921–2016) was an American economist whose *The Strategy of Conflict* (1960) and *Arms and Influence* (1966) applied game theory to nuclear deterrence and bargaining. He shared the 2005 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics with Robert Aumann.
