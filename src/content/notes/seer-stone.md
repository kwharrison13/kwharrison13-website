# Seer Stone

The seer stone is central to the translation-method discussion in [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins). The book recounts that [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) had already experimented with seer stones before the Book of Mormon project, and that he used a stone to "translate" — including reading source documents that were not physically present, as when he resolved a dispute with scribe [Oliver Cowdery](/notes/oliver-cowdery) over whether John the Revelator was on earth or in heaven by translating an answer "through a stone." Court records (Justice Joel Noble's 1830 account, and affidavits from Isaac Hale, Alva Hale, and Peter Ingersoll) tie the same stone to Smith's earlier treasure-seeking.

This is the hook for Kyle's "genealogy fallacy" note. Kyle pushes back on the implicit argument — *"If he was faking stone looking to find money and he used a stone to translate then he must have been faking translation?"* — flagging it as a form-of-argument problem: methodological resemblance between the money-digging stone and the translation stone doesn't, by itself, establish equivalence of intent.

**Context:** A seer stone (or "peep stone") was a small stone used in early-American folk-magic practice for scrying — claiming to see hidden things, such as buried treasure, by gazing into it. In Latter-day Saint history, Joseph Smith is documented as having used such a stone both in his early treasure-seeking and in dictating portions of the Book of Mormon, a fact the modern LDS Church has more openly acknowledged in recent years.
