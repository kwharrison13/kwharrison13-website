# E.M. Forster

E.M. Forster appears in Kyle's Historical Futurism - Research as the author of the short story **"The Machine Stops,"** catalogued alongside other foundational speculative short fiction — [Isaac Asimov](/notes/isaac-asimov)'s "The Last Question," [Arthur C. Clarke](/notes/arthur-c-clarke)'s "The Nine Billion Names of God," [Ray Bradbury](/notes/ray-bradbury)'s "A Sound of Thunder," [Ted Chiang](/notes/ted-chiang)'s "Story of Your Life," and others. The story is gathered as source material for Kyle's work on [Historical Futurism](/essays/historical-futurism) — the study of how past visions of the future shaped, and were shaped by, their era.

He also surfaces in [Wicked](/books/wicked) through a quote [Gregory Maguire](/notes/gregory-maguire) returns to twice as the animating creed of his writing life: "My defense at any Last Judgment would be, 'I was trying to connect up and use all the fragments I was born with.'" Maguire frames it as an apologetic about the limits and range of an authorial life — a statement about making coherent art out of scattered, inherited material — and "relishes" it enough to reprise it within the same passage.

**Context:** E.M. Forster (1879–1970) was an English novelist and essayist (*A Room with a View*, *Howards End*, *A Passage to India*) whose epigraph "Only connect" echoes the fragment-connecting sentiment quoted in Wicked. His 1909 short story "The Machine Stops" is an unusually early and prescient work of dystopian science fiction, depicting a humanity living underground in isolation, mediated entirely by a global Machine.
