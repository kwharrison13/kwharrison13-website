# The Fellowship of the Ring

In Kyle's Come Follow Me study of [Helaman 7](/notes/helaman-7)–12, [J.R.R. Tolkien](/notes/j-r-r-tolkien)'s *The Fellowship of the Ring* supplies the answer to a recurring spiritual temptation he calls "the grass of righteousness is always greener in the other dispensation." The prophet Nephi laments being "consigned that these are my days" and wishes he could have lived in the easier, more faithful era of the first Nephi ([Helaman 7](/notes/helaman-7):7–9); Kyle observes that modern Saints similarly wish they could have lived in the days of [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) and the [Restoration](/notes/restoration).

Against that wish he sets the exchange between Frodo and Gandalf: "'I wish it need not have happened in my time,' said Frodo. 'So do I,' said Gandalf, 'and so do all who live to see such times. But that is not for them to decide. All we have to decide is what to do with the time that is given us.'" The note pairs this with [Alma 29](/notes/alma-29):1–3 — Alma's wish to be an angel, checked by the recognition that he should "be content with the things which the Lord hath allotted." The takeaway: every age holds both righteousness and wickedness, and the task is to find one's path in the time one is actually given.

**Context:** *The Fellowship of the Ring* (1954) is the first volume of J.R.R. Tolkien's *The Lord of the Rings*; the Frodo–Gandalf exchange about "the time that is given us" is among its most widely quoted passages.
