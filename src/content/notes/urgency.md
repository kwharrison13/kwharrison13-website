# Urgency

Urgency is the master frame of [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) in Kyle's reading — "the missing variable" and the book's most-flagged note. The whole argument runs on the claim that "you don't have the time you think you have": China's military buildup since the early 1990s (Kyle tracks it as a ~900% increase), the inversion of the kill chain, and a U.S. defense establishment whose "political, economic, and bureaucratic incentives are aligned with continuing to build and buy the military America has." Americans "are not moving with anything close to the level of urgency that is required to be successful."

Kyle's recurring annotation is the *sine wave* of urgency: existential threat plus fear produces the urgency that drives innovation, while good times produce hubris and unearned confidence that lets a power fall behind. The book's historical contrast is the early Cold War — Sputnik, the "all-encompassing sense of urgency," a "pervasive belief that America could fail" — versus the post-Soviet "hyperpower" era, when "any sense of urgency in military acquisition went with it." Kyle ties this to the "SALY principle" (same-as-last-year complacency): "lack of urgency comes before the drivers of urgency," and "good times make weak men."

## Where this appears

- [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) — the book's central thesis; Kyle tags dozens of passages "Urgency / China" and frames it as the fear-vs-hubris sine wave that determines whether a great power keeps its military edge.
