# Anthropic Principle

In [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt), Terryl and Fiona Givens invoke the anthropic principle in their chapter on science and faith as evidence that scientific honesty leaves room for wonder rather than foreclosing belief. The book notes that "even nontheist cosmologists concede the astronomically remote odds of myriad cosmological constants appearing in concert to enable the emergence of a life-sustaining universe." Its "precision and collective appearance seems to be 'astonishingly improbable,' a 'mysterious collection of pure numbers,' 'unlikely coincidences' that are nonetheless 'essential to the existence of carbon-based' life forms like ourselves, according to the classic treatment of what is called 'the anthropic principle.'"

The Givenses fold this into a broader posture toward the limits of science, quoting Freeman Dyson on science and religion as two different "bunch of tools" and Martin Rees on dark matter — that what we can see "is just the white foam on the wave-crests, not the massive waves themselves." The wiki page links the discussion to [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) as adjacent on the same science-and-religion intersection (Dyson, Rees, the anthropic principle).

**Context:** The anthropic principle is the observation that the fundamental physical constants of the universe appear finely tuned to permit the existence of observers like us. It is widely debated in cosmology and the philosophy of science, and is sometimes marshaled in fine-tuning arguments for design.
