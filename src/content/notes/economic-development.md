# Economic Development

Economic development shows up across Kyle's notes as the municipal function of recruiting, retaining, and growing local businesses and talent — and as a market for software. [CivicLift](/notes/civiclift), Evan Dobos's civic-engagement platform, aggregates events and content for cities and "surfaces data insights for economic development." The [Salt Lake](/notes/salt-lake) page is anchored entirely by the city's economic development department, which recruits and retains companies (tech and green-tech being the two target sectors) and grows local entrepreneurship — with unemployment so low the focus is matching skilled people to jobs. [Shortest Way Home](/books/shortest-way-home) (Pete Buttigieg's memoir) frames the modern shift in the discipline: economic development "is no longer just a game of luring factories... using tax incentives to buy jobs," but increasingly recruiting *people* by enhancing a community's "quality of place." The common thread Kyle draws is the link between economic development and [City Building](/notes/city-building).

**Context:** Economic development is the practice — usually run by city, regional, or national agencies — of growing an area's economy through business attraction and retention, workforce development, infrastructure, and quality-of-place investment. It spans local "smokestack chasing" to national industrial policy.

## Where this appears

- [CivicLift](/notes/civiclift) — its civic-engagement platform surfaces economic-development data insights for the cities and towns it serves.
- [Salt Lake](/notes/salt-lake) — the city's economic development department recruits/retains tech and green-tech firms and grows entrepreneurship.
- [Shortest Way Home](/books/shortest-way-home) — Buttigieg's "quality of place" reframing: recruit people, not just factories.
