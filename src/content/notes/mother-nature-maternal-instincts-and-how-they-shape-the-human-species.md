# Mother Nature: Maternal Instincts and How They Shape the Human Species

A book by Sarah Blaffer Hrdy cited inside [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog) as a key source on the importance of the extended family. Hrdy's central term, as quoted in that book, is "alloparents" — caregivers beyond the biological parents — and her finding that "for children at risk of neglect, it is amazing how much difference alloparental interventions, say, from a grandparent, can make." [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog) invokes Hrdy's work to reinforce its own thesis that healing childhood trauma depends on increasing the quality and number of relationships in a child's life, treating the dissolution of the extended family as a driver of modern developmental stress ("We have seen that throughout this book").

**Context:** *Mother Nature: Maternal Instincts and How They Shape the Human Species* (1999) is a work by anthropologist and primatologist Sarah Blaffer Hrdy examining maternal behavior, cooperative breeding, and "alloparenting" across human and primate evolution. It is referenced here as a cited source, not as a book Kyle has read.
