Kyle's 2014 business plan for **YPYS (Your Passion, Your Solution)**, a not-for-profit designed to leverage college and high school student energy to address social issues. Piloted from Provo, UT, with plans to expand state-by-state. The plan was developed in preparation for an Oxford Social Seminar.

## Key Takeaways

- **The core insight is alignment of passion with action.** YPYS's thesis: students want to contribute but lack know-how, resources, support, and social understanding. The program provides all four through a year-long experiential curriculum. "An individual's ability to change the world lies in their ability to align their passions with a focused vision."
- **Three-legged stool: college students, high schools, sponsors.** College students direct social ventures (they are the founders); high schools provide fundraising platforms and pipeline; sponsors provide prizes for high school campaigns and brand exposure. YPYS sits at the center connecting all three.
- **The incubator model covers ideation through startup — not startup through expansion.** Most business incubators focus on post-launch scaling. YPYS deliberately targets the earlier pre-incubation gap where motivated students have no infrastructure at all. The model is a mixed HUB/Venture incubator (primarily virtual, with a small physical facility for workshops and board meetings).
- **Revenue from three sources:** high school fundraisers (70% to active social ventures, up to 30% covers expenses), sponsors (40% converts to tangible prizes/incentives, 25% distributed to projects, 20% to chosen projects, 15% to YPYS operations), and incubator service fees (free for 2 years, then tiered monthly fees by service).
- **The high school pipeline is a long-game bet.** High school student committee members who graduate become the strongest college applicants for YPYS. The model is self-compounding: today's high schooler is tomorrow's college program director.
- **WOM referrals + personal networks are the growth engine.** No advertising budget for year 1-2; every management team member generates a list of 100 personal contacts for sponsor outreach. Expansion to other states requires locating a local operating team and incubator facility in each state.
- **Pilot test built into the model from day one.** 12 BYU students, 4 high schools, 12 sponsors. Full methodology with control and test groups to isolate which program elements (lectures only, workshops only, both, neither) drive social venture success.

## Connections

- Social Innovation and Social Entrepreneurship are the conceptual load-bearing ideas — YPYS is essentially a platform for teaching and practicing both simultaneously.
- The Young Entrepreneurs Academy (documented in Appendix D) is the closest comparable — YPYS studied it as a reference model. Key difference: YEA! is school-embedded; YPYS is a standalone nonprofit that partners with schools.
- The incubator model is explicitly drawn from InfoDev's Mixed-use Incubator Handbook (Appendix F), specifically the "HUB/Venture Incubator" (mixed BAM + virtual) model.
- The thesis that early engagement produces lifetime social leaders maps to what Paul Graham has written about intrinsic motivation: the sooner an identity forms around doing something meaningful, the more durable it is.

## Full Business Plan

### Executive Summary

The world around us remains saturated with both social and economical issues. The demand for necessary resources to address these social and economical challenges exceeds the supply of potential resources. Such resources include **monetary resources, physiological resources, and human resources** (OECD 2011). Along with a need for resources, many of these issues are very complex and intricate and require innovative and creative solutions.

YPYS's vision and purpose is three fold:
- To capture the potential of young and ambitious leaders (college and high school students) and help them leverage their passions to change the world
- To foster a generation were more leaders are concerned with social issues and are willing to dedicate their resources to changing the world
- To create innovative solutions that will help address the issues that our world faces

YPYS is a not-for-profit that will provide a platform for young leaders that will give them the opportunity, resources, know how, support, and network to engage in a social issue that they are passionate about and work toward creating an innovative and scalable solution.

YPYS will function as a year long developmental program for both college students and high school students, to teach them about social innovation and social entrepreneurship through experiential learning. YPYS will also function as a "HUB/Venture" business incubator that will help participating students develop their own social ventures. YPYS's incubator model will connect students to mentors, investors, partners, and professors, as well as create a collaborative community among the students, in an effort to help students develop successful social ventures/businesses.

YPYS believes that working closely with local communities will be key to the growth and development of YPYS and the success of the social ventures that are started by students. YPYS will actively seek to work with local high schools, local sponsors, and local investors. In addition to working closely with local communities, YPYS believes that establishing a network of individuals from all professions, backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives will be essential in the development of successful social ventures.

YPYS's revenue model is broken down into three key sources:
- Revenue from Sponsors
- Revenue from Collaborative Projects with High Schools
- Revenue from YPYS's Incubator Services

YPYS strongly believes that an individual's ability to change the world lies in their ability to align their passions with a focused vision.

### Problem

Our world faces a myriad of social and economic issues that are always in need of extra resources. These resources can be anything from monetary and physiological to human capital. Along with a lack of primary resources, many of these issues are very complex and intricate and require innovative and creative solutions.

As important as innovative and creative solutions are, the individuals that are creative, innovative, and passionate enough to dedicate their time to developing these solutions are just as, if not more, important. We need more leaders who will be catalysts for social innovation and other philanthropic initiatives. As the issues around us increase in size and more issues arise, a lack of human resources will greatly deter our ability to address the surmounting issues. As the problems that challenge our society continue to persist onward, we need more leaders who will seek to address the social challenges our world faces; as well as greater grassroots mobilization for a larger aggregate of individuals who are willing to volunteer their time and resources to engage in philanthropic efforts.

In order for us to increase the number of social leaders and increase the size of the aggregate who are willing to volunteer, we need to focus on introducing social innovation and social leadership development to students who are in a stage where they are most capable of making an impact.

The youth of our society remains an untapped source of potential leadership and innovation to address the social issues that our world faces, both for the short term and the long term. Many students want to contribute to society and have ideas as to how they want to help, but do not (a) have the know how, (b) have the resources, (c) have the support, or (d) have the social understanding, to actually go out into the world and translate their desires and ideas into action.

### Solution

Establish a not-for-profit organization, YPYS, which stands for Your Passion Your Solution, that is created to leverage the ambition of college students and the collective force of high schools to help address the social issues our world faces.

YPYS will serve as a developmental program to teach students about social innovation through experiential learning. Through the freedom of self-directed projects and the training students will receive from the developmental program, YPYS hopes to prepare students to become future leaders that will actively seek to change the world.

YPYS believes that if students are aware of the real impact that they can make today and understand how to go about doing so, that more students will choose to be actively engaged with social issues. YPYS believes that through **actively engaging** as many college students and high school students as soon as possible in social innovation/social leadership, that our society will **see an increase in the number of social leaders** for the future as well as **an increase in the aggregate who will dedicate resources** in the future to serving philanthropic initiatives.

As YPYS seeks to increase the number of social leaders, providing innovative and creative solutions to the issues our world faces will also be a primary focus. YPYS will function as an incubator for the social ventures that students start under YPYS. YPYS will seek to help students produce sustainable long-term solutions from the social ventures that they develop. YPYS will provide the resources, training, and network to help students create their own solutions to the issues that they are most passionate about and then YPYS will help them implement their solutions.

### Business Model

YPYS will operate as the connecting point between four different parties: College Students, Sponsors, High Schools, and Social Issues.

#### College Students

YPYS will have a very selective year round program for college students to participate in. We will have an intensive screening process to select students that will be truly motivated and willing to devote their time and talent to addressing a social issue that they are passionate about. College students that are selected for the program will create a **not-for-profit or for-profit social venture** under the wing of the parent organization (YPYS).

Through the year round program, YPYS will teach students about social innovation through select workshops and experiential learning through their projects. We will provide a platform for students to take an issue that they are passionate about and work toward alleviating that issue.

Each student that is selected to participate in the program will have the opportunity to choose their own issue/project that they would like to initially address. The year round program will consist of three phases:

**Phase I** (Fall semester): An 8-12 week program that will teach, train, and prepare students on how to launch a successful social venture. This is also where students will begin to participate in YPYS's incubator, beginning with the ideation stage.
- Meet once a week to hear from guest speakers and professors and hold discussions on social innovation and social entrepreneurship
- Meet once a week for a collaborative workshop to develop each student's social venture and help them create a business proposal
- Help each student refine their business proposal until approved by the board before moving to Phase II

**Phase II** (Winter semester): Students apply what they have learned by continuing to develop their projects and collaborating with students from local high schools. Two activities per high school:
- Go into individual classrooms for one class period to discuss social innovation and the impact students can make
- Invite students to work with a YPYS project of their choice

**Phase III** (Ongoing): YPYS representatives continue forward with their social ventures and potentially create a sustainable and independent organization. Each social venture continues to operate under YPYS with access to all incubator services. YPYS permits each social venture to design their own exit strategy.

#### YPYS's Incubator Model

YPYS will provide a unique "HUB/Venture incubator" model for all of the social ventures that are started under YPYS. The incubator will function primarily as a virtual incubator but will still have a physical facility for conferences and workshops, as well as house the operations of select members from the management team.

Services provided within the incubator:
- **Collaborative sessions:** Weekly groups of four for round table discussion and questioning of one social venture at a time (in-person or virtual conference room)
- **Business Models/Proposals:** Help each student develop their business model and write a proposal that must be board-approved before seeking investor funding
- **Network/Partners:** Ongoing development of relationships with professionals and companies from all fields; partnering social ventures with CSR programs
- **Investors:** Extensive network of investors that students can access after board approval of their business proposal
- **Human Resources:** Available to each social venture for recruiting, interviewing, hiring, etc.
- **Strategic Consultants:** External consultants from all fields (social ventures pay consultants directly)
- **Mentors:** Pair each social venture with a mentor who has experience with social ventures or the relevant social issue
- **Accounting Services:** Consolidated financials with separate reports per venture

YPYS's incubation model focuses primarily on the **ideation stage through start-up phase** — the gap before most traditional incubators engage.

#### High Schools

High schools serve two functions: (1) a platform for engaging younger students with social innovation, and (2) a fundraising platform.

Seven phases for each collaborative project:
1. Reach out to student council and key administration to gain approval
2. Present in classes to generate awareness; hold after-school workshop to form a student committee and select five officers for the student board
3. Collaborative effort with student council and YPYS student committee to determine fundraising approach
4. Assembly Presentation led by student council/student leaders
5. Carry out school-wide project
6. Concluding assembly with video and prizes
7. Student committee members who expressed desire continue working on a YPYS social venture of their choice

Value proposition for high schools:
- Students learn essential leadership skills and apply what they learn in school
- Sparks creativity and motivation
- Stronger graduating classes / stronger college applications
- Students participate in raising funds rather than just donating
- Opportunity to unify a student body
- Sponsor-donated prizes for exceptional student contributors

#### Sponsors

Sponsors provide the resources used to incentivize high school participation (in the form of prizes). **A sponsor is not an investor**: sponsors give resources with no expectations besides brand association; investors give money with clear expectations.

Value proposition for sponsors:
- Marketing opportunity and exposure through YPYS and local high schools
- Opportunity to make an impact on the local community through investing in local leaders

Revenue allocation from sponsor donations:
- **25%** distributed across all active YPYS projects
- **20%** funds a specific project or projects of their choice
- **40%** converted into tangible incentives given away during collaborative projects
- **15%** covers YPYS expenses and fuels growth

#### Social Issues

Each student identifies a social issue of their choice. YPYS and its social ventures are not limited to any type of social issue — the vision is to help foster the particular passions of each student to create sustainable solutions.

#### Long-term Strategy

The long-term strategy involves establishing functioning YPYS units in each state across the United States, with lasting relationships with surrounding high schools and local sponsors and investors. The long-term strategy also involves growing the incubation model to help foster many successful social ventures that will turn into independent organizations.

### Unique Factor / Innovation

YPYS is unique because it provides a platform for students to go from no prior experience or knowledge with social innovation to launching their own social venture. It provides the freedom to direct their own project, and the resources to make their project successful.

"There is no intellectual property involved and I would encourage imitation of my model. Imitation of my model by other individuals in different regions and states may prove easier to scale our purpose as opposed to YPYS trying to expand into areas."

### Competition

YPYS will see competition in four specific areas:
1. **Soliciting donations from sponsors** — all not-for-profits compete for the same sponsor pool
2. **Recruiting exceptional college students** — many organizations compete: Innovation Academy, Crocker Innovation Fellows, Social Venture Academy, Social Innovation Projects, Social Innovation Leadership Council (all in Provo)
3. **Working with high schools for fundraisers** — charities already use schools as platforms; schools run many fundraisers throughout the year
4. **Investors and funding institutions** — competing against 2.3 million nonprofits in the United States

**Short-term strategy:** Leverage personal networks. Management team members each create a list of 100 personal contacts for sponsor outreach. Contingency contracts with sponsors (commit to sponsor contingent on having an actionable project). WOM referrals for student recruitment.

**Long-term strategy:** Build a trusted brand name through successful collaborations. Establish lasting relationships with sponsors and high schools year after year. The high school pipeline becomes self-sustaining.

## Appendix A: Q&A

**Q: What stops a passionate student from just googling a nonprofit and helping them directly?**

Nothing. But YPYS's primary target market is bright students who have had ideas (or haven't yet) and need to discover what social innovation is and what they can personally do. For students already deeply engaged, YPYS serves as a platform to take action and further an existing project. The mission is to expose more future leaders to social innovation by actively engaging them in a philanthropic initiative they are personally passionate about.

**Q: Have you considered a Teach for America type platform (post-graduation)?**

No, for three reasons: (1) the sooner students engage, the more likely they are to carry a socially responsible mindset into their careers — waiting until graduation wastes four years of potential in-college experience; (2) students in school have immediate access to resources and networks not available post-graduation; (3) YPYS can't offer a full-time platform right now, and what students do post-program is up to them.

**Q: Do the projects have to be community specific?**

The projects do not have to be community specific in regards to the issue being addressed; however, successful ventures will draw upon resources from the community and work in collaboration with leaders within the community.

**Q: What are we going to do with social ventures that stay with YPYS past the start-up phase?**

Charge a progressive service fee dependent on which services social ventures decide to continue using.

**Q: What if graduate social ventures seek to work through high schools still?**

A contract will require that if they continue to seek high school collaboration, the pitch has to be for YPYS as a whole organization and the funds follow the original fundraiser structure.

**Q: What about revenue each venture generates outside of the high school platforms?**

If social ventures are still operating under YPYS, 5% of any funds raised from any other platform (excluding funds from investors) are given to YPYS.

**Q: What evidence do you have that your model will produce the social leaders you're hoping for?**

As of right now there is no evidence besides what other similar models have been able to produce. YPYS will be constantly measuring and gauging success, but to truly understand the impact being made will require a few years of evaluation and assessment.

**Q: Will you allow for the possibility of projects being combined together?**

Yes — encouraged if the strategy is synergistic.

## Appendix B: High School Student Committee

YPYS representatives present to select classes, then hold an after-school workshop. Students who come have the opportunity to sign up for the student committee — a group of exceptional students committed to the vision of YPYS.

Each student committee member will:
- Be assigned to work on a YPYS project
- Have weekly student-directed workshops for collaboration and developing innovative skills
- Work in conjunction with student council to hold the school-wide fundraising activity
- Attend a YPYS lecture once a month held by YPYS college representatives

Five exceptional students from the committee are selected to form the student board, which directs all weekly workshop activities and maintains dialogue with YPYS's Director of Client Success.

**Annual model per high school:**
- Year 1: Teach seniors, juniors, and sophomores; recruit from all three grades; student board selected from juniors
- Year 2+: Only teach incoming sophomores (prior classes already exposed); new student board from current juniors

## Appendix C: Pilot Test Methodology

Pilot begins with 12 college students from BYU, organized into control and test groups to isolate which program elements drive outcomes:

- Control Group (3 students): no collaborative workshops or weekly lectures
- Test Group (3 students): both workshops and lectures
- Test Group (3 students): only lectures
- Test Group (3 students): only workshops

Key questions to be tested:
- How effective are weekly collaborations for helping students develop creative and innovative social ventures?
- How effective are weekly lectures from professors and guest speakers?
- Is it essential for a student to have a mentor?
- Is an 8-12 week program necessary?
- How effective are various student fundraising platforms?
- What method is most effective to motivate an entire student body?
- Does this model really increase the number of students engaged in social initiatives in the future?
- Does this model really produce sustainable social ventures?
- Is YPYS's revenue structure with high schools and local sponsors dependent on demographics?

## Appendix D: Young Entrepreneurs Academy (Reference Model)

The Young Entrepreneurs Academy (YEA!) is a year-long class that transforms middle and high school students into entrepreneurs. Students develop business ideas, write business plans, conduct market research, pitch to investors, and launch real legal companies.

**YEA! Stats:**
- 100% of Academy graduates graduate high school on time
- 99% enroll in college
- 50% of YEA! students continue their business for more than 1 year
- 19% start second businesses
- 56% are underrepresented minorities
- Operating in 168 communities in 30 states
- 1,394 graduates have launched over 1,000 businesses

Key difference from YPYS: YEA! is a school-embedded program (offered as an after-school program or credit-bearing elective). YPYS is a standalone nonprofit that partners with schools rather than being embedded in them. YEA! also focuses on pure entrepreneurship; YPYS focuses specifically on social entrepreneurship and social ventures.

## Appendix F: Incubator Models (InfoDev)

Three operational incubator models from current literature:

- **Bricks and Mortar (BAM):** Traditional — centered on physical facilities. Interaction and partnership formation stimulated by proximity. Limited to the building's scope.
- **Virtual (Portal / "Without Walls"):** No physical building — support and services offered over the internet. Entrepreneurs from different regions can be integrated.
- **Mixed (HUB/Venture Incubator):** Offers a building for some entrepreneurs and supports development over the internet. YPYS uses this model.

Key issues for incubators: services must be adequate to area of activity and stage of development; network of relationships quality is paramount; financing alternatives must be available.

**YPYS's approach:** Mixed model — virtual incubator with a small physical facility for collaborative workshops, conference rooms for lectures and board meetings, and select management services (human resources, accounting). The mixed model enables extensive network reach beyond physical location and makes geographic expansion less costly.
