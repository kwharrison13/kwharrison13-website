# United Order

The **United Order** is the Latter-day Saint framework for cooperative economic life, and across Kyle's reading it is treated as a still-live framework rather than sealed history. [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) (James W. Lucas and Warner Woodworth) is the central text: it presents the United Order as an **unfinished experiment** — applicable today but requiring institutional infrastructure (UOPIEs, cooperative banks, the [Mondragon](/notes/mondragon) model) that the modern Church never built. The book frames it as a genuinely distinct organizing principle, set against [Capitalism](/notes/capitalism) and socialism as a third option, "not capitalism with charity added on." John A. Widstoe's standard recurs throughout: the United Order has "a practical value as an ideal by which any proposed economic system may be tested for the degree of its worthiness." The book is careful that the term is one "of holiness" and warns against coercion — "freedom is a vital part of the united order."

[Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) supplies the historical episode: the United Order movement was born in early 1874 in St. George under [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s supervision, its founding document blaming "the struggle between capitol and labor" and "monied monopolies," prescribing "self-sustaining home-manufactures" as the antidote to unfettered capitalism. The Panic of 1873 — which [Lorenzo Snow](/notes/lorenzo-snow)'s prospering Brigham City cooperatives had survived — was the trigger. The biography is candid about its failure: Young's "unwillingness to consecrate all of his own property" was "most emblematic of the struggle," and "if Young did not trust others with his property, it is hardly surprising that many church members did not trust him with theirs." [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) frames the same cooperative movement as an "institutional immune response to wealth" — Brigham's worst fear that the Saints would "wax fat, and kick themselves out of the Church."

**Context:** The United Order (or Order of Enoch) was a 19th-century Latter-day Saint economic system based on the law of consecration, in which members deeded property to the Church and received stewardships; the most fully communal implementation was at Orderville, Utah. The antipolygamy persecutions of the 1880s dismantled most of these orders.

## Where this appears

- [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) — the book's central framework: an unfinished cooperative-economic experiment tested by Widstoe's standard, contrasted with capitalism and socialism, with [Mondragon](/notes/mondragon) and Grameen as modern instances
- [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) — the 1874 St. George origin of the movement and its undoing by Young's own refusal to consecrate his property
- [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) — the United Order and cooperative movement as an institutional immune response to prosperity and the threat of wealth
