# Talent Networking

In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), this tag marks [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s recurring point that Berkshire's edge is less about Buffett and [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s own labor than about the corps of exceptional operators they have assembled. Buffett describes Berkshire as holding "an unusually large number of individuals... who are truly legends in their industries" — many of whom joined when Berkshire purchased their companies, with others identified and promoted internally, and the ranks further expanded through acquisitions like General Re and Executive Jet. The framing is that he and Munger "have the easy jobs," doing "very little except allocate capital," while the operating managers do the hard work — and what motivates those managers is not pay alone but "a vision of just how far his or her business can go and... a desire to be the one who gets it there."

The concept ties Berkshire's [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) model to the human side of the flywheel: acquiring great companies is also a way of acquiring great managers, and retaining intrinsically-motivated operators is what lets the center stay deliberately small and patient.
