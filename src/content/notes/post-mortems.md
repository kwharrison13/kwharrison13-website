# Post-Mortems

A **post-mortem** is the disciplined practice of asking "what went wrong?" — which, as Kyle's research note [What's In a Post-Mortem](/essays/whats-in-a-post-mortem) argues, first requires *acknowledging that something went wrong*. The concept migrated from autopsies in medicine into manufacturing, software engineering (SRE culture), project management, and startup investing. The real blocker isn't method but **willingness** — a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) that lets you ask "what am I wrong about?" Paired with its sibling the [Pre-Mortem](/notes/pre-mortem) ("dream the nightmare"), the discipline lets you learn as much from success as from failure, so long as you measure, record, and ask the right questions.

The building blocks are **questions** — Bezos' "five whys," Ed Catmull's "five things." Kyle flags [Blameless](/notes/blameless) post-mortems (Etsy's "Code as Craft") as the way to avoid the politics that cloud the exercise: focus on "what went wrong?" not "who did something wrong?" [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) points to the same instinct as an institutional antidote — at [Johnson & Johnson](/notes/johnson-and-johnson), "the rules make everybody revisit old acquisitions, comparing predictions with outcomes," whereas most corporations quietly forget their failed acquisitions because nobody wants to be associated with the bad outcome. [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) supplies the cognitive obstacle: we tend to see only finished products, not the losses behind tail successes, which is part of why learning from outcomes isn't intuitive.

**Context:** "Post-mortem" (Latin, "after death") originated as the medical examination of a body to determine cause of death; in business and engineering it now denotes a structured retrospective conducted after a project, incident, or deal concludes.
