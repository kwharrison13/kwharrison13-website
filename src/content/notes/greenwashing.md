# Greenwashing

Greenwashing surfaces in Kyle's notes through the Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 work as the failure mode that climate-accounting software is meant to displace. In that diligence a Watershed customer flatly observes that "today is a lot of greenwashing" — companies overstating their environmental credentials without rigorous measurement behind the claims. The proposed antidote runs through the whole thesis: **transparent public reporting is how you know a company's climate claims are real versus greenwashing.** Watershed's pitch is to turn waste bills, energy usage, fleet, and real-estate data into an auditable carbon footprint — a credible, quarterly-cadence number that rolls up to the office of the CFO — so that sustainability claims become financially-reportable facts rather than marketing.

The concept sits inside the diligence's broader frame that "winning in climate change is all about internalizing those externalities" ([Internalizing Negative Externalities](/notes/internalizing-negative-externalities)): greenwashing is what happens when externalities stay un-internalized and unmeasured, and verified reporting is the mechanism that closes the gap.

**Context:** Greenwashing is the practice of conveying a false or exaggerated impression of environmental responsibility — through vague claims, selective disclosure, or marketing imagery — without substantive emissions reduction. Regulatory scrutiny (e.g. EU and SEC climate-disclosure rules, and institutional investor pressure such as BlackRock's reporting demands) has pushed companies toward auditable, standardized carbon accounting as a defense against the charge.
