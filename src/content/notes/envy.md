# Envy

Envy appears in Kyle's notes from two angles that converge on the same verdict: it is uniquely pointless. [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) and the [Practical Thought](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought) speech, calls it "the no-fun deadly sin": "Envy is a really stupid sin because it's the only one you could never possibly have any fun at. There's a lot of pain and no fun." His prescription, attributed to [Aristotle](/notes/aristotle), is to "plainly deserve the success we get." Munger returns to it repeatedly — quoting [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) that "it is not greed that drives the world, but envy," and warning that envy of successful institutions or investors drives destructive imitation (Harvard/Yale endowment copycats; the late-stage venture losses of the high-tech bubble).

The gospel-study sources cast envy as a personal spiritual obstacle. In the Alma 30–31 study, Kyle recounts a mission moment asking what was holding him back and feeling four words: "[Anger](/notes/anger), [Pride](/notes/pride), Envy, and [lust](/notes/lust)" — paired with the two-wolves parable, where the wolf that wins is "the one you feed." The Alma 23–29 study offers the counter-virtue: Alma's freedom from envy in "O, that I were an angel" — joying not in his own success alone but in his brethren's, content with what the Lord has allotted.

**Context:** Envy — resentment or discontent at another's advantages or success — is traditionally counted among the seven deadly sins and is named in the Tenth Commandment's prohibition on coveting.

## Where this appears

- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — Munger's "no-fun deadly sin"; avoid it by plainly deserving success ([Aristotle](/notes/aristotle)); Buffett's "envy, not greed, drives the world"
- [Charlie Munger — Practical Thought About Practical Thought](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought) — the same Aristotle-based prescription against envy
- Alma 30–31 — "The Virtue of the Word of God" — envy among four things holding Kyle back; the two-wolves parable
- Alma 23–29 — "They Never Did Fall Away" — Alma's freedom from envy, joying in his brethren's success
