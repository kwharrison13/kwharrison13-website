# Newspapers

Newspapers surface across three of Kyle's books in three distinct registers — political, financial, and civic. In *[John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams)*, they appear as the engine of early-nineteenth-century public opinion: the number of American papers doubled from 400 in 1810 to 800 by 1825, and Adams himself "read thirty to forty newspapers a day." The book frames newspaper editorials as having "largely replaced the open letter or pamphlet as the chief instrument of influencing public opinion" — "outrageously partisan" journals that organized the country's factions and bound politicians and editors into mutual dependence.

In *[Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography)*, newspapers are an economic case study in monopoly. After buying the Buffalo Evening News, Buffett studied the business "from the bottom up" and concluded a dominant paper would be "like the only bridge in a small town" — anyone needing to cross had to pay the toll, and an advertiser in a one-paper city had no choice but to buy ads, giving the paper freedom to raise rates. He also liked newspapers as an inflation hedge — businesses that could raise prices in step. In *[Our Towns](/books/our-towns)*, the lens shifts to community vitality: a local paper as a self-reinforcing "cycle" of involvement — "it makes them happy and interested to be here… that motivates them to do something, and participate—which makes it more a community" — a line Kyle flagged with a "[Newspapers](/notes/newspapers)" note.

**Context:** The economics of newspapers — local-monopoly pricing power, partisan influence, and civic glue — shifted dramatically with the internet, as classified revenue collapsed and one-paper-town moats eroded.

## Where this appears

- [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) — newspapers as the rising, fiercely partisan instrument of public opinion; Adams reading 30–40 a day.
- [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) — a dominant newspaper as "the only bridge in a small town" monopoly and an inflation hedge.
