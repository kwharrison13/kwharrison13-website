# Simplify and Intensify

"Simplify and Intensify" is Kyle's tag for the discipline of taking simple ideas seriously and refusing to over-complicate. [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) states the core: "Taking simple ideas seriously" is "a distinguishing feature of extraordinary thinkers," but "grasping an idea is not the same as understanding it" (the buy-low-sell-high example) — and "if you can't say it clearly, you don't understand it yourself" ([John Searle](/notes/john-searle)). Simple ideas "can be tied together into consistent theories and build up enormous complexity," whereas complicated ones cannot; sometimes "the breakthrough… is the discovery of a simple principle behind a seemingly very complicated process."

The other two sources show the principle as both a strategy and a self-criticism. The [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) embody the simplified approach to acquisitions — "we have no master strategy, no corporate planners… Instead, we simply hope that something sensible comes along — and, when it does, we act." [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in [Practical Thought About Practical Thought](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought), turns it on himself: his failure to communicate his 1996 talk was "gross folly as an amateur teacher. I attempted too much" — a failure precisely to simplify and intensify. (Munger's "First Notion" there is to "simplify problems by deciding big 'no-brainer' questions first.")

## Where this appears

- [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) — taking simple ideas seriously as the hallmark of extraordinary thinkers; clarity as the test of understanding.
- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — Buffett's deliberately un-elaborate, opportunistic acquisition approach.
- [Charlie Munger: Practical Thought About Practical Thought?](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought) — Munger's self-diagnosis that he "attempted too much" — a failure to simplify.
