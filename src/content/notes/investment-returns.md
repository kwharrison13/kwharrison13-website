# Investment Returns

How returns actually accrue — and the recurring finding across Kyle's reading is that they are far more skewed, and far more about survival and longevity, than about cleverness. [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) makes the survival argument: there is "little correlation between investment effort and results," and the lesson from [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) is what he *didn't* do — no ruinous debt, no panic-selling through fourteen recessions, no strategy-hopping. "Survival gave him longevity… and longevity is what made compounding work wonders." Morgan Housel pairs this with the tail-event structure of returns: Correlation Ventures found 65% of 21,000 venture financings lost money while ~0.5% earned 50x or more — "that's where the majority of the industry's returns come from" — and Apple alone drove ~7% of the index's returns in 2018.

[The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) supplies the formal version of that skew: fund returns follow a [Power Law](/notes/power-law) where "the best investment in a successful fund equals or outperforms the entire rest of the fund" (Horsley Bridge's 5%/60% figure). [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) argues the long-hold case — compounding beats flipping because trade has friction (taxes, fees, idle cash, redeployment risk), and MOIC beats IRR as the metric that captures it. At the retail end, [Recession Proof](/books/recession-proof) reduces returns to three rules: buy diversified bundles via mutual funds/ETFs, buy in bust times ("buying low is the first rule of investing"), and retire at the right time.

A countervailing voice runs through the notes: in [The Unusual Ambitions Of Chamath Palihapitiya](/notes/the-unusual-ambitions-of-chamath-palihapitiya), Chamath dismisses traditional value-investing return models as broken in a zero-rate world where value sits in brand and intangibles. Together the sources frame "investment returns" less as a single number to maximize and more as a distribution shaped by survival, time, and a few outsized winners.

## Where this appears

- [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) — survival/longevity as the driver of returns, plus the tail-event distribution (VC, the index, Buffett's "what he didn't do").
- [The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) — the power-law shape of fund returns; the best deal can outperform the entire rest of the fund.
- [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) — why holding compounds and trade has friction; MOIC over IRR.
- [Recession Proof](/books/recession-proof) — the three retirement rules and "buying low is the first rule of investing."
- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — returns within the broader Munger/Buffett investing latticework.
- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — Buffett's long-run return discipline.
- [The Unusual Ambitions Of Chamath Palihapitiya](/notes/the-unusual-ambitions-of-chamath-palihapitiya) — the counter-case that legacy return models break in a zero-rate, intangibles-driven world.
