# Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial

One of [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s named human-misjudgment tendencies in [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment): when reality is too painful to bear, the mind distorts the facts until they become bearable. Munger illustrates it with a mother who refused to believe her son had died in World War II.

**Context:** In Munger's standard list of psychological tendencies, "Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial" describes how grief, loss, or threat can drive a person to deny facts rather than accept an intolerable truth — a defense Munger warns is especially destructive around chemical dependency and personal tragedy.

## Where this appears

- [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) — Munger introduces the tendency with the example of a "very sane" mother who refused to believe her son, lost flying over the Atlantic in WWII, was dead.
