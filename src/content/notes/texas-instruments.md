# Texas Instruments

In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), Texas Instruments (TI) is the early semiconductor power whose org structure couldn't keep its most consequential employee — the book's canonical [Innovator's Dilemma](/notes/innovator-s-dilemma) case study. Under Pat Haggerty, TI bet on Jack Kilby's integrated circuit and became the dominant early supplier to the U.S. military: within a year, TI's shipments to the Air Force accounted for 60% of all dollars spent on chips to date, and it supplied 100,000 integrated circuits to the Minuteman program. TI's relentless trial-and-error reliability work (Kilby pacing the hallways each Saturday) is portrayed as how American chipmakers learned mass production the Soviets never matched.

The pivotal thread is [Morris Chang](/notes/morris-chang). As early as the mid-1970s, while still at TI, Chang toyed with a company that would manufacture chips designed by customers; he pitched this "fabless/foundry" model to fellow TI executives in March 1976. They weren't convinced — TI was already making money and gambling on a market that didn't yet exist seemed risky — so "the idea was quietly binned." After being passed over for CEO and "put out to pasture," Chang left TI in the early 1980s and went on to found [TSMC](/notes/tsmc), later turning down by his former TI colleagues when he sought a technology partner. Kyle's note in the book frames the counterfactual directly: *Could TI have become TSMC if he stayed?*

TI also drove Taiwan's entry into semiconductors: Chang urged colleagues to set up a facility there, TI's board approved a Taiwan plant in 1968, and the book notes TI still has facilities on the island. Haggerty later lamented that if TI had branded its own consumer devices earlier it "would have been the Sony of consumer electronics."

**Context:** Texas Instruments is an American semiconductor company (founded 1930, Dallas) where Jack Kilby co-invented the integrated circuit in 1958; today it is a leading maker of analog and embedded processing chips.
