# Lessons From Laman and Lemuel

"Lessons From Laman and Lemuel" is a recurring lens in Kyle's Come Follow Me study of 1 Nephi 16–22: a chapter-by-chapter tracing of *what* Laman and Lemuel actually complain about each time they rebel, set as a foil to [Nephi](/notes/nephi)'s faithful initiative. Kyle catalogs the escalating grievances — being "foolishly led away from their precious things" (1 Nephi 2:11), anger that the plan with Laban failed (1 Nephi 3:28), wanting to return to Jerusalem (1 Nephi 7:6–7), complaining about hunger after the bows lose their spring (1 Nephi 16:20), and finally the plot to slay Lehi and Nephi because Nephi "has taken it upon him to be our ruler" (1 Nephi 16:36–37).

The framing carries a self-mastery moral. Where Nephi's broken bow is a natural consequence and his building a new one is a deliberate, [anxiously-engaged](/notes/anxiously-engaged) choice, Laman and Lemuel respond with murmuring — illustrating [Jeffrey R. Holland](/notes/jeffrey-r-holland)'s maxim that "no misfortune is so bad that whining about it won't make it worse." Kyle even reads their lost bowstrings as a symptom of the same laziness, citing [Truman G. Madsen](/notes/truman-g-madsen)'s note that [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) kept his bow unstrung lest it lose its spring. An open question Kyle flags: does the "he seeks to be a ruler over us" grievance only appear *after* the other complaints accumulate?

**Context:** Laman and Lemuel are the two eldest sons of the Book of Mormon prophet Lehi and the recurring antagonists of the early Nephite narrative, repeatedly rebelling against their father and younger brother Nephi.
