# Eliza Snow

Eliza Snow (Eliza R. Snow) recurs throughout [American Zion](/books/american-zion) as a central figure in early Mormon women's history and theology. In the founding of the Relief Society she argued that "as daughters of Zion, we should set an example," refusing to let the society operate "like the 'other Societies in the world,'" and she carefully noted [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s promise that participants would become a "kingdom of priests." She is most identified in the book with [Heavenly Mother](/notes/heavenly-mother) theology: finding redemption in the doctrine that "in the heav'ns parents are single" was unreasonable, she gave it enduring voice with the line "I've a mother there." The book traces a line of female autonomy "originally established by Emma Smith, expanded by Eliza R. Snow, defended by Emmeline B. Wells," and casts Snow as the figure who "synthesized" the religious imagination that originated with Joseph Smith — a platform later writers from the 1840s to the 2020s kept returning to.

**Context:** Eliza R. Snow (1804–1887) was a prominent leader, poet, and hymnwriter of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, the second general president of the Relief Society, and author of the hymn "O My Father," whose lyrics articulate the LDS doctrine of a Heavenly Mother.
