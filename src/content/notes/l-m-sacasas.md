# L.M. Sacasas

Writer behind the newsletter **The Convivial Society**, focused on technology, culture, and the human condition. His essay "Resistance Is Futile: The Myth of Tech Inevitability" supplies the pivot question and the closing argument of Kyle's essay [The Value Chain of Capital](/essays/the-value-chain-of-capital): *is technological progress inevitable?* — and its answer, no (source: The Value Chain of Capital - Research).

Sacasas's core move is to name what he calls the "rhetoric of technological determinism" — "narratives of inevitability… that frame technological development as a deterministic process to which human beings have no choice but to adapt." He argues these trends "are not merely matters of technical development… They are also functions of economic policies, legal structures, political power, and shifting cultural values," and that the appearance of inevitability "most often arise[s] from a far more banal source: self-interest, usually of the crassly commercial variety" — a trick "tech companies are prepared to exploit." In the Value Chain essay Kyle pairs this with [Thomas Misa](/notes/thomas-misa) (whom Sacasas cites) on how "the victors write the history… and the technological 'paths not taken' are often suppressed." Where [Aswath Damodaran](/notes/aswath-damodaran) gives Kyle the "capital chain is a pricing game" half, Sacasas gives the "progress is not inevitable" half — together licensing the pivot from the **capital value chain** to the **innovation value chain**: if progress isn't a guaranteed output of capital, then what you measure is what you get (source: The Value Chain of Capital - Research). The same framing seeds the [Determinism](/notes/determinism) page (its technological sense) and the [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) page (narrative and technology as twin human capacities).
