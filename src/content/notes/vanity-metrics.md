# Vanity Metrics

A metric that flatters rather than informs — one that looks like progress without measuring what actually matters. The cleanest case in Kyle's wiki is [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest), whose central provocation is a textbook vanity-vs-real metric distinction: "When individuals are baptized but do not attend church, do not identify themselves as members of the Church, and do not believe or live the teachings of the Church, has the Church grown?" Raw baptism counts are the vanity metric; *retention* — fruit that "remains" (John 15:16) — is the real one. The [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest) page ties this directly to [How Will You Measure Your Life](/books/how-will-you-measure-your-life) ("measure what matters rather than what's easy to count") and [Measure What Matters](/books/measure-what-matters) (set goals for activities you control, not results that flatter).

The term also surfaces in Kyle's own writing. In the research behind [The Renaissance of Rise and Grind](/essays/the-renaissance-of-rise-and-grind) (via [Hustle Porn](/notes/hustle-porn)), he warns of "establishing vanity metrics that act as proxy for actual output" — the same category error as over-engineering a note-taking system without producing anything noteworthy. The unifying frame across both uses: vanity metrics let the *performance* of progress substitute for the *output* of progress.

**Context:** "Vanity metrics" is a term popularized by lean-startup thinking (notably Eric Ries) for numbers that look impressive — total registered users, raw download counts, page views — but don't guide decisions or correlate with sustainable value, in contrast to "actionable metrics" like retention, conversion, and cohort behavior.
