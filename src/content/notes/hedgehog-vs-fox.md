# Hedgehog vs. Fox

This is the same breadth-vs-depth frame as [Fox vs Hedgehog](/notes/fox-vs-hedgehog) — the fox knows many things, the hedgehog knows one big thing — captured under a second filename during the connection sweep. Kyle tags it in [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) on Munger's specialization passage: "just as animals flourish in niches, similarly, people who specialize in the business world — and get very good because they specialize — frequently find good economics that they wouldn't get any other way." Kyle attaches both #[Hedgehog vs. Fox](/notes/hedgehog-vs-fox) and #[Jack of all trades, master of none](/notes/jack-of-all-trades-master-of-none) here, reading the niche argument as the hedgehog case for depth, set against Munger's broader [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) mandate for general education before specialization.

The richer treatment of the frame — including the Berkshire float example and Kyle's "Jack of all trades, master of nine" note — lives on [Fox vs Hedgehog](/notes/fox-vs-hedgehog), which draws on [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) as well.

**Context:** The distinction traces to Isaiah Berlin's "The Hedgehog and the Fox," via the Greek poet Archilochus ("the fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing"), and was later popularized in forecasting by Philip Tetlock.
