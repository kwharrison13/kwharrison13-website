# As We May Think

[Vannevar Bush](/notes/vannevar-bush)'s 1945 essay, named across two of Kyle's book pages as a hinge between the wartime laboratory and the modern internet. [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) places it in the lineage that produced the networked universal library: among those inspired by the essay were Douglas Engelbart, inventor of the computer mouse, and J.C.R. Licklider, whose *Libraries of the Future* (1965) described a digitized, networked library able to learn from and adapt to its users' queries.

[Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) reads the same essay as a "prescient" vision of a networked world where documents lived on microfilm rather than paper — a prophecy that anticipated the mid-1960s enthusiasm for computer stocks and helped shape the work that would later happen at Xerox PARC. Both framings treat the essay as a foundational forecast of associative, machine-assisted knowledge work. (The full treatment of Bush as a figure — MIT engineering dean, [Claude Shannon](/notes/claude-shannon)'s advisor, and essayist — lives on the [Vannevar Bush](/notes/vannevar-bush) page.)

**Context:** "As We May Think" was published in *The Atlantic* in July 1945, near the end of Bush's tenure directing US wartime science. It described the "memex," a hypothetical desk-based machine that would store and associatively link a user's books, records, and communications — an idea widely cited as an intellectual ancestor of hypertext and the World Wide Web.
