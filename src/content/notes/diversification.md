# Diversification

Across Kyle's investing reading, diversification is treated less as prudence and more as a confession of ignorance — a recurring contrarian thread. In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) rejects diversification outright as "an admission one cannot pick the winners," ridiculing fund managers who stuff portfolios with hundreds of stocks and paraphrasing Billy Rose's harem analogy — you cannot intelligently know so many securities any more than "a sheik could get to 'know' a harem of one hundred girls." The same Billy Rose line ("If you have a harem of forty women, you never get to know any of them very well") shows up in the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) as the canonical statement against over-diversification, where Kyle annotates the counter-argument to a diversified portfolio and ties it to the [Family of Companies](/notes/family-of-companies) idea.

The Berkshire letters complicate the picture rather than reject diversification wholesale: Buffett notes that some strategies (arbitrage, venture-style bets) *do* require wide diversification — if a single transaction carries significant risk, you reduce overall risk by making it one of many mutually-independent commitments, adopting "the outlook of the casino that owns a roulette wheel." Kyle's own notes in the letters read it as a balance: "diversify your industry exposure" and "diversify to enable level-headedness."

[The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) frames venture capital as the explicit rejection of modern-portfolio-theory diversification: Davis and Rock "ignored" the academic teaching that owning uncorrelated assets reduces volatility, instead promising concentrated bets on a dozen companies — de-risking through *control* rather than spread. [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel) extends this, certain that "a small number of huge, high-conviction bets was better than a large spread of halfhearted ones." [How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital) supplies the operator's version: the "three ventures at once / no single point of failure" rule — diversification not across a securities portfolio but across simultaneous income systems.

**Context:** Diversification is the portfolio-management practice of spreading capital across uncorrelated assets to reduce volatility, formalized by Harry Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory. Concentrated-bet investors like Buffett and venture capitalists deliberately reject it, arguing that conviction plus control beats statistical risk-spreading.
