# Thomas Piketty

French economist whose work surfaces in Kyle's notes through [Erik Torenberg](/notes/erik-torenberg)'s book notes on *Capital in the 21st Century*. Piketty's central thesis is that inequality between capital and labor grows whenever **r > g** — when the rate of return on capital (historically ~4–5% per year) exceeds the economy's growth rate (~1–1.5% per year). Because laborers' incomes track GDP per capita while *rentiers* (people who live off the interest on accumulated savings) earn the return on capital, inherited wealth compounds faster than output and income, widening the gap. The exception is wars and crises — 1914–1945 being the example — when hyperinflation, asset destruction, and wealth taxes hit rentiers hardest.

A thread Kyle has pulled out of Piketty's argument connects to [The American Dream](/notes/the-american-dream): contrary to the popular consensus, the Dream was "very real" for much of US history precisely because America, as a new country, had less time to accumulate a rentier class — the multiplication of inherited fortunes takes a couple of generations. The implication is that as r > g compounds, the mobility premise weakens. Torenberg's notes also track Piketty's observation that the rentier class shifted from the top 1% to the top 0.1%, that "super laborers" like CEOs now earn on both labor and principal, and that capital is increasingly tied up in institutions (endowments, sovereign wealth funds). Piketty's prescribed remedy is a global tax on wealth.

**Context:** Thomas Piketty is a French economist at the Paris School of Economics, best known for *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2013), an empirical study of wealth and income inequality across two centuries that became an unexpected international bestseller.
