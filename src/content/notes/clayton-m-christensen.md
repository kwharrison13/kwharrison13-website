# Clayton M. Christensen

Clayton M. Christensen appears in Kyle's notes across two registers. In business strategy, the [Apple Earnings, An Interview with Jay Goldberg About Chips and Intel](/notes/apple-earnings-an-interview-with-jay-goldberg-about-chips-and-intel) interview runs the whole conversation on "the classical Clayton M. Christensen model": new entrants integrate top-to-bottom because it's hard and integration makes the product work better, then as volume grows it makes more sense to modularize at different layers of the stack. The twist the interview flags is that [Apple](/notes/apple) appears to be going *backwards* — re-integrating all the way down to the chip — possibly because the mobile market is large enough to capture economies of scale internally. This is the [Vertical Integration](/notes/vertical-integration) / integrate-then-modularize cycle.

In Kyle's gospel study, Christensen surfaces as a faith voice rather than a strategist. In 10 Gifts To Take Home From Your Mission he is cited (with [Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)) on the "faith to act" — "the Savior asked us to forsake the rational limits of our adult minds and employ the faith of little children." In Revelation — Topical Study he is quoted that obeying when leaders ask us to do more — when "we are doing all we can" — means "miracles are the only option."

**Context:** Clayton M. Christensen (1952–2020) was a Harvard Business School professor best known for the theory of "disruptive innovation" (*The Innovator's Dilemma*) and the integrate-vs-modularize / interdependence-vs-modularity framework. A devout Latter-day Saint, he also wrote and spoke widely on faith.
