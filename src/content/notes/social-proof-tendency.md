# Social-Proof Tendency

One of the psychological tendencies [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) catalogs in [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment): the human reflex to think and act as the people around us do, especially under uncertainty. Munger uses adolescence as the canonical case — "teenagers' parents usually learn more than they would like about teenagers' cognitive errors from Social-Proof Tendency" — and ties it to a breakthrough he credits to [Judith Rich Harris](/notes/judith-rich-harris), who demonstrated that the "superrespect" young people show their peers (rather than parents or other adults) is, to a considerable extent, genetically ordained. The practical corollary Munger draws: parents are wiser to manipulate the *quality of the peer group* than to lean on exhortations to their own offspring.

Munger's single favorite self-improvement lesson from the tendency is to **learn how to ignore the examples of others when they are wrong** — a skill he rates among the most worth having. Kyle annotates this against his own framing — "[Live the Library](/notes/live-the-library), but not blindly" — and pairs it with [Joseph F. Smith](/notes/joseph-f-smith)'s line about wanting no man to follow him except so far as he is himself a consistent follower of the Master.

**Context:** "Social-proof tendency" is Munger's term, borrowed from social psychology (cf. Robert Cialdini's "social proof"): people use the behavior of others as evidence for the correct way to think or act, a heuristic that misfires badly in crowds and under stress.
