# Skills

In [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) (Kelly Palmer and David Blake), skills are framed as "the currency of the future" — the thing that should be measured and rewarded in place of pedigree, logos, and time spent learning. The book's sharpest formulation of the idea is the thought experiment: "If you could have a Harvard education OR a Harvard degree, but not both, which would you choose?" — exposing how weakly formal credentials track actual capability. The operational core is the [Skills Quotient](/notes/skills-quotient) (skills you have ÷ skills you need × 100), a way to quantify how well-equipped a workforce is.

The book argues that "no one knows for sure what skills are going to be needed for the future," so companies must build environments where employees are continuously [Upskilling](/notes/upskilling) and reskilling at the same rate that things are changing — learning built *into* the work rather than separate from it. The durable meta-skill is [Learning Agility](/notes/learning-agility): with roughly 65% of today's under-fives headed for jobs that don't yet exist, the ability to learn faster than the competition becomes the survival skill.
