# Realism

In James Traub's biography [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), realism names a school of foreign-policy thought for which Adams is the "fountainhead." Per Kyle's notes, Adams "is the fountainhead of realism not only because he distinguished so sharply between American interests and universal goods, but because he expressed such a deep skepticism about America's capacity to do good abroad." This is foreign-policy realism "before it had a name": a sharp line between national interest and universal moral goods, and wariness of founding policy upon "too advantageous an estimate of the human character."

The biography frames this stance as both prescient and politically unsellable. Those who today caution against "humanitarian intervention," regime change, or democracy promotion "have good reason to cite his words." But Adams' voice "was too skeptical to appeal to an increasingly self-confident and self-righteous people" — and as [Henry Kissinger](/notes/henry-kissinger) observed, "the American people will not accept a 'realist' president." Traub suggests Adams' warning against reckless adventure abroad "may offer more to the American people today than it ever has before." See also [Foreign Policy](/notes/foreign-policy), where JQA is positioned as the origin point of American foreign-policy realism.

**Context:** In international-relations theory, realism holds that states act primarily to advance their own interests and security in an anarchic world, treating moral or idealist aims with skepticism. It contrasts with liberal internationalism and idealism.
