# Plato

The Greek philosopher recurs across Kyle's reading as the archetypal **essentialist** and a benchmark for the Western intellectual tradition. In [The Notetaking Cold War](/notes/the-notetaking-cold-war), [Dan Shipper](/notes/dan-shipper) places Plato (with [Immanuel Kant](/notes/immanuel-kant) and [Descartes](/notes/descartes)) among the [Essentialists](/notes/essentialists) — those who believe in a universal, objective reality accessible to the intellect, where "a statement is true when it reflects something essential about the way reality is organized" — set against the [Pragmatists](/notes/pragmatists); the split maps onto two philosophies of note organization. [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) treats Plato as a master of his expository style — "no one except Plato has ever been able to handle it effectively," and the later Greeks' saying, "Everywhere I go in my head, I meet Plato coming back."

In the gospel-study material, Plato is a foil for restored doctrine. The [Truman Madsen lecture](/notes/joseph-smith-lecture-2-joseph-s-personality-and-character) ranks D&C section 93 "superior in content to Plato's *Timaeus*," arguing Joseph Smith "was more profound than Plato" as an instrument of revelation — and contrasts the Latter-day Saint view of truth as "knowledge of things as they are" against "Thinghood," rejecting the Socratic thesis that knowledge is virtue. Spiritual Gifts cites Plato directly — "The true lover of knowledge must, from childhood up, be most of all a striver after truth in every form." Plato also frames [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked), which casts itself as "a Plato for Adler" dialogue invoking the Socratic paradox that "no one desires evil," and [Why You're Christian](/notes/why-you-re-christian) notes that even the Greeks were received through a Christian lens — "[Plato](/notes/plato) through [St. Augustine](/notes/st-augustine)."

**Context:** Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher, student of Socrates and teacher of Aristotle, founder of the Academy, and author of dialogues including the *Republic* and *Timaeus*. His theory of Forms is the classic statement of metaphysical essentialism.
