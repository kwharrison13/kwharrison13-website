# James 3

James 3 is the New Testament chapter Kyle leans on in his Self-Mastery — Topical Study for its "very small helm" image: "Behold also the ships, which though they be so great, and are driven of fierce winds, yet are they turned about by a very small helm" ([James 3](/notes/james-3):2–4). He uses it to argue that self-mastery is won not by one heroic effort but by a few good decisions at a time — the small rudder that turns a great ship in a storm — pairing it with [D&C 123](/notes/dandc-123):16 and the broader paradox of denying oneself ([Matthew 16](/notes/matthew-16)) while finding the yoke easy ([Matthew 11](/notes/matthew-11)).

The same passage opens with the standard James sets for self-control: "If any man offend not in word, the same is a perfect man, and able also to bridle the whole body." In Kyle's reading this sits alongside [1 Corinthians 9](/notes/1-corinthians-9):25 ("every man that striveth for the mastery is temperate in all things") and [David O. McKay](/notes/david-o-mckay)'s framing of self-mastery as the suppression of injurious passions.

**Context:** James 3 is a chapter in the Epistle of James, best known for its teaching on taming the tongue, using the images of a horse's bridle, a ship's rudder, and a small fire to show how a small thing governs a much larger whole.
