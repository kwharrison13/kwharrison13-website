# Transcendentalism

Transcendentalism is the intellectual movement that frames [Walden](/books/walden) and the [Henry David Thoreau](/notes/henry-david-thoreau) thread in Kyle's library. The wiki names [Ralph Waldo Emerson](/notes/ralph-waldo-emerson) as the "fellow Transcendentalist whose self-reliance ethic underwrites Thoreau's insistence that 'what a man thinks of himself... determines his fate.'" That self-reliance shows up across the *Walden* highlights: *"Public opinion is a weak tyrant compared with our own private opinion"*; the call to give up prejudices because "no way of thinking or doing, however ancient, can be trusted without proof"; and the preference for "a sound conscience" over fashionable clothes. The movement's reverence for nature is there too — the line about appreciating "the vastness and strangeness of nature."

The Thoreau page identifies him as the "Transcendentalist author of *Walden*," anchoring the wiki's [Frugality](/notes/frugality) and [Solitude](/notes/solitude) threads, and the [Solitude](/notes/solitude) page notes that solitude as a subject of inquiry "runs through American transcendentalism (Thoreau, Emerson)." So in Kyle's notes Transcendentalism functions as the parent movement behind individual conscience over public opinion, living deliberately, and the cost-in-life accounting that later seeds [Cal Newport](/notes/cal-newport)'s [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism).

**Context:** Transcendentalism was a mid-nineteenth-century American philosophical and literary movement centered in New England (Emerson, Thoreau, Margaret Fuller, and others) that held that intuition and individual conscience, rather than established religion or social convention, are the surest guides to truth, and that nature reflects a deeper spiritual reality.
