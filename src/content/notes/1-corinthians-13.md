# 1 Corinthians 13

Paul's chapter on charity is a recurring anchor in Kyle's topical gospel study. In Charity & Love it supplies the hierarchy "faith, hope, charity, these three; but the greatest of these is charity" ([1 Corinthians 13](/notes/1-corinthians-13):13) — used to argue that charity is not a deed but the *prompting motive* behind great acts, the "pure love of Christ" defined in [Moroni 7](/notes/moroni-7):45–47. The same chapter's warning frames the cost of acting without that motive: without charity we become "as sounding brass or tinkling cymbal" (13:1).

In Virtue the chapter does the same work from the angle of *desire*. Kyle reads "works done without true desire 'become as sounding brass'" (13:1, 3) to make the point that what we do matters less than the heart behind it — God judges "according to the desires of [our] heart" ([Alma 41](/notes/alma-41):3) — so duty-driven action "profiteth [them] nothing." Across both notes, 1 Corinthians 13 functions as the proof-text that charity and intent, not outward performance, are what give an act spiritual weight.

**Context:** 1 Corinthians 13 is the New Testament "love chapter," Paul's discourse to the Corinthian church on charity (agapē) as the supreme and abiding Christian virtue, often read at weddings and famous for the line "the greatest of these is charity."

## Where this appears

- Charity & Love — source of "the greatest of these is charity" and the "sounding brass" warning, anchoring charity as Christ's attribute and the motive behind righteous acts
- Virtue — used to argue that works without true desire "become as sounding brass," tying virtue to inner intent rather than outward duty
