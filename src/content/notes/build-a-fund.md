# Build a Fund

"Build a Fund" is one of Kyle's recurring investing-craft tags, gathering lessons on how investment vehicles get built and run — both the *internal* decision-making and the *external* difficulty of starting one. From [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes), [Stephen A. Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-a-schwarzman) supplies the depersonalization principle: never let one person single-handedly green-light a deal — involve all senior partners, argue it out, and own outcomes collectively. Kyle ties the lesson to the Edgcomb loss, the deal that "showed the change had to start with me," after which Blackstone rebuilt its process around team-based decisions and open Monday meetings (Kyle cross-links the [Investing 101 2.0](/notes/investing-101-2-0) framing).

The tag also captures the *outside* of fund-building. In his notes on [Frederik Gieschen](/notes/frederik-gieschen)'s essay "Attempting the Impossible," Kyle records how hard it is to spin out and raise a first fund "unless you come from a prestigious shop and have initial backing" — short track records, rudimentary infrastructure, and a size mismatch where LPs don't want to be the only or largest institutional check. Gieschen's takeaway is that the edge is your ability to "build a network, tell a story, and get an introduction," echoing the [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) rule to "show, don't tell" rather than blend in with the noise allocators try to tune out.
