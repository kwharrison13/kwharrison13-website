# Lindy Principle

The Lindy Principle — that the longer a non-perishable thing (an idea, a book, a technology) has survived, the longer it is likely to keep surviving — runs through Kyle's notes as a heuristic for durability, often set against its inverse. In [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) by [Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb), Kyle pairs it with the turkey problem: "the life of a turkey, in a micro data set, goes against the Lindy principle." The turkey's existence looks safest exactly when it is most at risk, because the micro data set is a trap — pattern extrapolation breaks at the moment it matters most, the mirror image of Lindy's logic about long-attested survival.

In the The Hardening Of The Great Softening - Research notes and the [Primitivism](/notes/primitivism) page, Kyle invokes Lindy as a test of intellectual staying power: do "the thought leaders who stand the test of time (Lindy)" turn out to be [primitivists](/notes/primitivism) or [progressivists](/notes/progressivism)? Here Lindy is set opposite the "queen of hearts" / Red Queen frame — survival-by-endurance versus survival-by-constant-running.

**Context:** The Lindy effect, popularized by Nassim Nicholas Taleb (notably in *Antifragile*), holds that for non-perishable things, future life expectancy is proportional to current age — every additional year of survival implies a longer remaining lifespan. The name comes from Lindy's, a New York deli where comedians reputedly observed that a show's longevity predicted how much longer it would run.
