# George F. Johnson

**One-line:** *Co-owner and president of the Endicott-Johnson Shoe Company who built a near-utopian company town and pioneered "welfare capitalism" — his [Square Deal](/notes/the-square-deal) — believing that taking care of workers made a company more competitive.*

## What we know

- Co-owner and president of the [Endicott-Johnson Shoe Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endicott_Johnson_Corporation), which made ~52M shoes/year and supplied boots to the US military in both World Wars. (source: NPR Planet Money [Episode 618: The Square Deal](https://www.npr.org/sections/money/2015/04/17/400360768/the-square-deal))
- First in the shoe industry to introduce the 8-hour workday and 40-hour workweek with comprehensive medical care — and **remained consistently profitable despite paying some of the highest wages in the industry.** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_F._Johnson))
- Built a company town with free or near-free libraries, parks, hospitals, golf courses, pools, theaters, and 6 free carousels; gave every employee's newborn a $10 bank book; sold workers houses at a ~$1K net loss to the company. He called it **the [Square Deal](/notes/the-square-deal).**
- *"I want you to understand. Everything you have done has made everything I have done possible."* Believed it was the modern employer's responsibility to look after employee welfare. Known as "father of the community."
- During the Great Depression he cut wages rather than lay anyone off: *"There are fish in the river and dandelions on the hill. We'll have to live on that."*
- Talked workers out of unionizing "with tears in his eyes" — *"You don't need a union. They can't give you anything more than I can give you."* — but also sent spies to root out organizers. In the union vote, **80% of ~15K workers voted against the union.** *"We've proven that labor and capital can live in harmony."*
- Subject of [Gerald Zahavi](/notes/gerald-zahavi)'s book *"Workers, Managers, and Welfare Capitalism."* 40K people paid respects at his funeral; 1948 was the company's last peak before a long decline.
