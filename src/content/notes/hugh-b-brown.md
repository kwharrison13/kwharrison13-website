# Hugh B. Brown

Hugh B. Brown surfaces across Kyle's gospel sources as the [LDS](/notes/mormonism) leader who most forcefully defended freedom of the mind inside the Church. In [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins) he is quoted at length praising "the questing spirit," arguing that "thoughts and expressions compete in the marketplace of thought, and in that competition truth emerges triumphant. Only error fears freedom of expression," and warning that "neither fear of consequence or any kind of coercion should ever be used to secure uniformity of thought in the church." [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) presents the harder side of that conviction: an "apostle-heretic" who privately dissented on the priesthood ban, telling Eugene England near the end of his life, "I think all my brethren in the quorum are wrong in this decision," yet adding that he "would do nothing to destroy the unity of that quorum on which your and my salvation depends." That same book places him in the doctrinal lineage — Smith's King Follett Discourse through [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young), B. H. Roberts, John Widtsoe, [Lorenzo Snow](/notes/lorenzo-snow), to Spencer W. Kimball — that carried the teaching of divine progression.

He also appears on the devotional side of the corpus: [The Priesthood Power of Women](/books/the-priesthood-power-of-women) cites his teaching that "Jesus honored womanhood when he came to this earth... thus motherhood became akin to Godhood," and The First Principles of Faithful Pragmatism points to his BYU speech [Profile of a Prophet](/notes/profile-of-a-prophet) for the characteristics of a true prophet. [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book) preserves his quotes on law and obedience as the means of "releasing the power that is in the human soul."

**Context:** Hugh B. Brown (1883–1975) was a Canadian-born lawyer and General Authority of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints who served as a counselor in the First Presidency under David O. McKay in the 1960s. He is remembered for his liberal, intellectually open sensibility and his "Profile of a Prophet" address.
