# Edmund Burke

Invoked in Kyle's [Henry Clay](/notes/henry-clay) page as the touchstone for [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams)'s empirical, history-grounded temperament, drawn from [James Traub](/notes/james-traub)'s biography of Adams. The notes set Adams against Clay: where Clay "reasoned from principle," Adams — "like [Edmund Burke](/notes/edmund-burke)" — reasoned "from what men did, not from what one wished they did," judging Clay's more idealistic views to "smack of a dangerous unreality." Burke functions here as shorthand for the realist, precedent-respecting strain of conservatism that the biography attributes to Adams.

**Context:** Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Anglo-Irish statesman and political philosopher, widely regarded as a founder of modern conservatism, best known for *Reflections on the Revolution in France* and his emphasis on prudence, tradition, and gradual reform over abstract principle. This is general background, not stated in the source.
