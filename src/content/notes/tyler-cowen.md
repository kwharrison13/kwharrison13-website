# Tyler Cowen

Economist, blogger (Marginal Revolution), and prolific "talent" thinker who recurs across Kyle's corpus as a source on talent-spotting, stagnation, and AI's effect on education. He co-authored [Talent (Book)](/books/talent) with [Daniel Gross](/notes/daniel-gross), a book on how to identify high-potential people that Kyle read in 2023 (the title is a load-bearing reference for Kyle's hiring/talent thinking). He is also a foundational figure for [Progress Studies](/notes/progress-studies): his 2011 *The Great Stagnation* is the backdrop against which Kyle reads [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation), and the field itself was named in a 2019 Atlantic essay he co-wrote with Patrick Collison calling for a new discipline studying what actually drives progress.

In Kyle's [We Need a Renaissance of Rhetoric](/essays/we-need-a-renaissance-of-rhetoric) research, Cowen is the foil for the "skills" argument about AI in education. His Free Press piece — "Everyone's Using AI to Cheat at School. That's a Good Thing." — argues that American education was already in a "profound crisis — the result of ideological capture, political monoculture, and extreme conformism," and that AI cheating is ultimately *good news* because using AI well is precisely the skill the future demands; he proposes devoting roughly a third of the college curriculum to teaching AI use. Kyle treats this as the optimistic counterweight in his [AI Cheating in Education](/notes/ai-cheating-in-education) thread. Cowen's interests also touch Kyle's [City Building](/notes/city-building) strand.

**Context:** Tyler Cowen is a professor of economics at George Mason University, co-author of the long-running Marginal Revolution blog, host of the *Conversations with Tyler* podcast, and chairman of the Mercatus Center's Emergent Ventures grant program. He coined the influential "Great Stagnation" thesis and, with Patrick Collison, helped launch the Progress Studies movement.
