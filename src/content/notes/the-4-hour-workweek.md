# The 4-Hour Workweek

In Kyle's notes, *The 4-Hour Workweek* serves as shorthand for the idea that rightly-directed, systematized effort beats brute hours — that wealth comes from working *smart*, not from grinding the clock. The most striking pairing is in Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young, where Kyle tags Brigham Young's counsel with #[The 4-Hour Workweek](/notes/the-4-hour-workweek): *"One-third or one-fourth of the time that is spent to procure a living would be sufficient, if your labor were rightly directed. People think they are going to get rich by hard work — by working sixteen hours out of the twenty-four; but it is not so."* Kyle reads Young as anticipating Ferriss's thesis a century early.

In [How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital), the book is foundational furniture: [Tim Ferriss](/notes/tim-ferriss)'s "New Rich" frame opens the book — people who maximize time, money, and energy so it works for them, travel freely, and keep "blank calendars" with very few expenses — and the book closes its "Top Seven Books on Systems" list with *The 4-Hour Workweek* as the guide to systematizing business and life "to save time, energy, and money."

**Context:** *The 4-Hour Workweek* (2007) is a bestselling productivity/lifestyle-design book by entrepreneur and author Tim Ferriss, popularizing concepts like the "New Rich," lifestyle design, outsourcing, and escaping the deferred-life plan.
