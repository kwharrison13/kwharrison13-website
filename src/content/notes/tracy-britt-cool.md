# Tracy Britt Cool

A [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) protégé at [Berkshire Hathaway](/notes/berkshire-hathaway) who, per a WSJ piece Kyle saved on June 18, 2026, left the company to start her own firm. She appears in Kyle's notes as part of the [Berkshire Hathaway](/notes/berkshire-hathaway) orbit — listed among the company's people alongside Buffett and [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) as the "protégé who left" — and is filed under the [Investing: The Model](/notes/investing-the-model) thread, Kyle's collection of links about Berkshire-style investing and the people it produced.

In Kyle's reading of the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), she is also the recurring case study for the **[Bifurcation of Investing](/notes/bifurcation-of-investing)** thesis — the idea that as funds grow they are forced to move upmarket, leaving behind a long tail of smaller opportunities they will no longer compete for. Kyle's note frames her departure from Berkshire as the concrete illustration: "Bigger funds move up market exposing a long tail of businesses that they'll no longer compete for." He returns to the point repeatedly across the annual-letters notes — "the curse of size and scale; Tracy Britt Cool," "Tracy Britt Cool's opportunity" — treating her exit as evidence that even Berkshire's scale creates room for smaller acquirers in the small-and-medium-business tail.

**Context:** Tracy Britt Cool joined Berkshire Hathaway in 2009 as a financial assistant to [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) and went on to chair or run several Berkshire subsidiaries (including Pampered Chef, Larson-Juhl, Benjamin Moore, and Oriental Trading). In 2020 she left to co-found Kanbrick, an investment and operating partnership that takes long-term stakes in small and mid-sized companies, explicitly modeled on Berkshire's permanent-capital, hands-on-operating approach — a move that maps directly onto the "long tail Berkshire can no longer chase" framing in Kyle's notes.
