# Kaizen

Kaizen is the continual-improvement frame [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) names directly: "The Japanese word for this is kaizen. Continual improvement. Always finding something to work on, to make a little progress on. Never being satisfied, always looking to grow." The book sets it against the amateur's appetite for spectacle — "Revolution? Transformation? That's what amateurs chase. The pros are after evolution."

The book's exemplar is Tom Brady, who "isn't obsessed with winning… He's obsessed with improving the accuracy of his touchdown passes in the fourth quarter," chasing the process of getting better as "the dragon he chases." In Kyle's notes the same idea ties to President Topham's "Continuous Improvement" teaching, and the concept threads into [Compounding](/notes/compounding) on the book's page — "everything you do is cumulative," small daily increments adding up to a self over time. Kaizen is thus the discipline-of-the-daily-over-the-dramatic that underwrites the book's broader Stoic argument.

**Context:** Kaizen (改善, "change for better") is a Japanese philosophy of continuous incremental improvement, popularized in postwar manufacturing (notably the Toyota Production System) and widely adopted in business and personal-development contexts.
