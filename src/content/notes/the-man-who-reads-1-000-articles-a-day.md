# The Man Who Reads 1,000 Articles a Day

**Author:** [Dan Shipper](/notes/dan-shipper) ([Superorganizers](/notes/superorganizers))
**URL:** https://superorganizers.substack.com/p/the-man-who-reads-1000-articles-a
**One-line:** *A profile of [Robert Cottrell](/notes/robert-cottrell), who "eats" ~1,000 articles a day to find the handful with lasting value — and how he reads, abandons, and saves.*

## Key claims

- **[Robert Cottrell](/notes/robert-cottrell) reads 1,000 articles a day** — but "the verb 'to read' isn't exactly right." Ingest is closer. "No, Robert Cottrell eats articles. With gusto and verve."
- He's taken account of 3–5 million pieces of writing over 10 years — "the most read person in the world. Not the best read, mind you, but the most read."
- **What he's hunting for is pieces of lasting value** — they do three things: share really interesting ideas, deploy strong arguments, and have particularly fine writing (typically first person). By nature, most breaking-news pieces won't have lasting value. #Evergreen Notes
- **How he reads** (How to Read):
  - He puts a lot of trust in **headlines** — written by loyal allies of the writer, paid to express what's best in the piece. A weak headline strongly indicates there's not much good inside.
  - He can tell something is good if it makes him say "I hadn't thought of that," "That's interesting," or "I like that writer."
  - Then the article has to **start well**. "If a piece does not start well then the chances are vanishingly small that it's going to improve." **So if he's hooked at the first paragraph, chances are he reads to the end.**
  - It's "absurdly reductivist," but he has to allocate time efficiently — the greatest efficiency is assuming a writer leads with their strongest point. #Abandon a Book
- His morning routine: the first thing he does out of bed is read The New York Times — "for my own personal satisfaction," when he's freshest and most receptive. #[Daily Routine](/notes/daily-routine)
- **Bear Notes is the last stage of his process.** He saves the super-good stuff there (great plain-text reader, easy offline reading); toward day's end he has eight or nine full-text pieces in Bear, and selects the five that go into the newsletter.
