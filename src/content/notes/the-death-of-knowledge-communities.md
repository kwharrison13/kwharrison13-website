# The Death of Knowledge Communities

A Kyle essay theme (it has its own under-construction draft) about how generative AI threatens to kill the human knowledge communities whose content trained those very models. Its sharpest articulation comes from a 2023 call with Peter Nixey of Intentional.io, who framed it as the "death of the market for knowledge full stop": if you think of a dam or diversion, "all the tributaries lead into ChatGPT now where they used to lead elsewhere." That doesn't just kill Stack Overflow (which Nixey called "the canary in the coal mine"), it kills the demand to produce a book, a course, or any content at all — and concentrates informational power in [OpenAI](/notes/openai), so that "if they decide the earth is flat, they can make the earth flat for a time" (October 16th, 2023 — Peter Nixey (Intentional.io)). [Reddit](/notes/reddit) anchors the same question in Kyle's notes as the flagship community deciding whether to charge AI giants for its user-generated training data.

The theme also carries a second, older valence about expert authority and institutions. Kyle tags it against [Richard Feynman](/notes/richard-feynman)'s line "Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts" — which surfaces in the Techno-Optimist Manifesto material in It's Time To Build... But Build What - Research — pairing the erosion of institutional knowledge with the parallel worry, in the AI cut, that a single firm becomes the institution. The connective tissue is a concern about the corruption or capture of society's sense-making systems.
