# Don't Die With Your Music In You

A recurring tag Kyle uses to mark the imperative to fully express your gift before you die — to produce everything you are capable of producing rather than leaving it latent. It threads two registers at once: a Stoic urgency frame ("decide TODAY who you will be") and a religious urgency frame, which Kyle notes meet "on the same ground" in [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), where he tags the idea twice and stresses he means it literally.

The grounded uses cluster around legacy and bequest. In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), Kyle tags it on [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s closing meditations on passing knowledge forward — the bone-cancer doctor who spent a sabbatical year writing a definitive textbook, and Munger's Pilgrim's-Progress "my sword I leave to him who can wield it" bequest passages — and pairs it with [Whatever You Are Be A Good One](/notes/whatever-you-are-be-a-good-one) to form "a complete instruction: pick something, commit, finish." In [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) he attaches it to Beethoven, who, facing deafness, wrote that "it was only my art that held me back" from leaving the world before producing all he felt capable of. In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) it tags Jack Byrne's rare achievement of developing successors as talented as himself — extending the benefits of one's gift beyond one's own tenure.

## Where this appears

- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — tagged on Munger's bequest/legacy passages (the textbook-writing doctor, the Pilgrim's Progress sword); paired with [Whatever You Are Be A Good One](/notes/whatever-you-are-be-a-good-one)
- [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) — tagged twice and read literally; attached to Beethoven persevering through deafness to produce his art; Stoic-meets-religious urgency
- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — tagged on Jack Byrne developing successors as capable as himself, extending his stewardship beyond his tenure
