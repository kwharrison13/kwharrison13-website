# Pareto Principle

The Pareto Principle is a core idea in Kyle's [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) notes — the "Law of the Vital Few," distinguishing the "trivial many" from the "vital few." As [Greg McKeown](/notes/greg-mckeown) recounts, Vilfredo Pareto introduced (as far back as the 1790s) the observation that "20 percent of our efforts produce 80 percent of results," later expanded by Joseph Moses Juran in his 1951 *Quality-Control Handbook* into the "Law of the Vital Few" — a process Juran used to drive Japan's postwar quality revolution. Kyle flags it directly as a "Tool" in his highlight notes. In the Essentialism frame it connects to [Tradeoffs](/notes/tradeoffs) (deliberately choosing the big things) and to [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett), who "owes 90% of his wealth to just ten investments" — a concrete illustration that "sometimes what you don't do is just as important as what you do."

**Context:** The Pareto Principle, or 80/20 rule, is named after Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto, who observed that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes (originally, that 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population). It is widely applied in business, productivity, and quality management to focus effort on the highest-leverage minority of inputs.
