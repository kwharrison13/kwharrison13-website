# Tutoring

The core of the education argument in Marc Andreessen's [It's Time To Build](/notes/it-s-time-to-build): we know that one-to-one tutoring yields a *two-standard-deviation* gain — the [Bloom two-sigma effect](/notes/bloom-two-sigma-effect) — and we have the internet, so the open question is "why haven't we built systems to match every learner with a tutor?" Andreessen frames this as a build failure rather than a knowledge failure: top universities teach only a microscopic share of the world's 18-year-olds, the last major K-12 innovation was Montessori (1960s), and fifty years of education research never reached deployment.

In Kyle's wiki the thread runs straight into [Synthesis School](/notes/synthesis-school), which he tags as the worked example of the "build new education systems" call — tutoring-at-scale as the thing the internet should make possible. The concept therefore sits at the junction of the build-mandate (Andreessen) and a concrete company instantiating it (Synthesis), with the Bloom two-sigma result as the empirical warrant for why one-to-one instruction is worth re-engineering.

**Context:** Benjamin Bloom's 1984 "2 sigma problem" found that students tutored one-to-one performed about two standard deviations better than students in conventional classrooms — a benchmark frequently invoked in edtech to argue that personalized instruction can dramatically outperform mass schooling.
