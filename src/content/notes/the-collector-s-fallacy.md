# The Collector's Fallacy

**Author:** [Christian Tietze](/notes/christian-tietze) (Zettelkasten.de)
**URL:** https://zettelkasten.de/posts/collectors-fallacy/
**One-line:** *"Knowing about" something is not the same as "knowing" it — collecting texts, bookmarks, and photocopies creates an illusion of knowledge without actually increasing it.*

## Key Highlights

- **The fallacy:** We gather promising books, articles, and bookmarks and feel good about having them — but possession ≠ knowledge. "Just *knowing about* a thing is less than superficial since knowing about is merely to be certain of its existence, nothing more."
- **Why we do it:** Collecting is immediately rewarding (like Skinner's doves). A growing pile of photocopies or a folder of bookmarks feels tangible and satisfying. It conditions us to hoard.
- [Umberto Eco](/notes/umberto-eco) in *How to Write a Thesis*: "there's a lot someone doesn't know anything about precisely because she photocopied a text; she has given herself in to the illusion of having read the text already."
- **The remedy — Research, Read, Assimilate; rinse and repeat:**
  - Reading without taking notes is "a waste of time in the long run." The information stays in short-term memory and is forgotten.
  - Taking notes permanently integrates the text's information into your own knowledge system, making it available forever.
  - Quote: "I rarely consult secondary sources again. If I have to do so, it means that I did not do the job right the first time."
- **Shorter cycles beat big batches:** Research a little → read it → assimilate → repeat. Long batches accumulate material that turns out irrelevant before you process it.
- **Actionable habit:** Research for one hour, no more. Process everything collected until the stack is empty. Review the cycle. Adjust. Repeat.
