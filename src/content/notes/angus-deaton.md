# Angus Deaton

The economist whose empirical work Kyle cites on two distinct fronts. In Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young, Deaton's joint study with [Daniel Kahneman](/notes/daniel-kahneman) supplies the secular corroboration for a gospel claim about money and happiness: plotted against log income, life evaluation rises steadily, but emotional well-being shows "no further progress beyond an annual income of ~$75,000" — "high income buys life satisfaction but not happiness." Kyle uses this Kahneman–Deaton finding as outside evidence that wealth past a point doesn't deepen joy.

In [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism), Deaton appears via the "deaths of despair" framing he developed with Anne Case — the observation that working-class Americans are dying earlier from suicide, drug overdoses, alcoholism, and poor health. There the concept supplies the human cost backing the book's indictment of "Welchism" and shareholder-first financialization.

**Context:** Angus Deaton is a Scottish-American economist at Princeton, winner of the 2015 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his analysis of consumption, poverty, and welfare. With Anne Case (his wife and co-author) he coined "deaths of despair," and with Daniel Kahneman he co-authored the influential 2010 PNAS paper on income and emotional well-being.
