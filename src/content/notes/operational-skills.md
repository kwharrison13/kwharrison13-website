# Operational Skills

In Kyle's corpus, "operational skills" names the unglamorous, earned-in-context competence of actually *getting things done* — as opposed to the abstract or credentialed version of management. The through-line across the sources is that this skill is learned on the job, from the bottom up, not conferred by a degree. [Managers Not MBAs](/books/managers-not-mbas) makes the argument explicitly: management is a practice learned through [Apprenticeship](/notes/apprenticeship) — "moving, mentoring, and monitoring" — and managers should earn their stripes "from the bottom up" rather than stepping in at the top. [MBA Degrees](/notes/mba-degrees) reinforces it from the other side: the on-the-job competencies (accounting, finance, operations) are the ones interviewees said the MBA only *nominally* certified.

The exemplar in the corpus is [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley), whose biography [Gordon B. Hinckley: Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) models operational discipline as: decide and don't re-litigate, start and end meetings on time, grasp the essence of a problem fast, phone the person rather than call another meeting, and once a decision is made, don't look back — paired with [running a meeting](/notes/running-a-meeting) and [efficiency](/notes/efficiency) ("I don't know how to get anything done except by working, so let's get going"). [Turnaround](/books/turnaround) supplies the operator's version: attention to detail, strategic-audit discipline, and "rounding the flat spots" by pairing complementary strengths — sitting alongside good [Sales](/notes/sales)manship ("not fast, glib talk" but research and matching benefits to needs). [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) folds operational skill into its broader vocabulary of measurable, continuously-refreshed human capital.

**Context:** "Operational skills" generally refers to the practical, executional competencies of running a business or function day-to-day — process discipline, resource management, prioritization, and follow-through — distinguished from strategy (deciding *what* to do) and from credentialed/theoretical management training.
