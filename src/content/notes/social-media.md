# Social Media

In the corpus, Social Media appears as a corrosive force on how people process complex questions — specifically in [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind), where Bruce C. and Marie K. Hafen argue that doubters' real problem "is not that they know too much about Church history, but that they don't know nearly enough," and that "they have been conditioned by the oversimplifications of social media to expect a short answer to any question." The book pairs this with columnist David Ignatius's worry that "people don't seem to know what's true anymore," and his observation that "social media can be tools of deception as well as truth." The throughline: social media trains an appetite for short answers and manufactured certainty, which collides with questions whose honest answers are long and complex — adjacent to the book's "merchants of doubt" / tobacco-industry playbook of amplifying uncertainty. It connects to the wiki's [Doubt](/notes/doubt) thread.

**Context:** Social media refers to platforms (X/Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, etc.) built on algorithmic, short-form, engagement-driven content. A common critique — echoed by the Hafens — is that these formats reward brevity and outrage over nuance, degrading collective sense-making.

## Where this appears

- [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) — its "oversimplifications" condition people to expect a short answer to any question, and it can be "tools of deception as well as truth"; the failure mode behind manufactured doubt.
