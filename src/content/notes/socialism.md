# Socialism

In [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion), socialism is one of the three explicit organizing-principle foils — alongside [Capitalism](/notes/capitalism) and the [United Order](/notes/united-order) — that [James W. Lucas](/notes/james-w-lucas) and Woodworth use to argue the United Order is a genuinely distinct framework for ordering economic life, "not capitalism with charity added on." The book is careful to treat the three as different organizing principles rather than points on a single spectrum.

Where the book engages socialism's history directly, it does so through the "Utopian" socialists: Robert Owen, the Welsh industrialist whose humane New Lanark textile factory gave the movement its name, and the Fabians, who sought change not by "revolutionary upheaval" but by permeating the existing ruling class — aiming, in the Webbs' words, to "make thinking persons socialistic." Against both, the book sets the United Order's distinctive claim that "there should be no rich and no poor," achieved by binding "Haves and Have-nots in a mutual godly spirit" rather than through state compulsion or class antagonism — with freedom held up as "a vital part of the united order," explicitly distinguishing it from coercive collectivism.

**Context:** Socialism is a broad family of economic and political ideologies advocating social or collective ownership of the means of production; the "utopian socialists" (Owen, Fourier, Saint-Simon) and the gradualist Fabian Society are early, non-revolutionary strands distinct from later Marxist traditions.

## Where this appears

- [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) — one of the three organizing-principle foils (Capitalism / Socialism / United Order); engaged historically via Owen and the Fabians and contrasted with the United Order's freedom-based, non-coercive model.
