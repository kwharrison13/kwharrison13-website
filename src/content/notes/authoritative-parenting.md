# Authoritative Parenting

The parenting style [The Parents' Guide to Boys](/books/the-parents-guide-to-boys) argues for, framed against psychologically controlling parenting. The book's core empirical claim: "research is clear that the best adjusted and behaved children have parents who provide strict guidelines and are alert to their child's activities, but they let their son or daughter make mistakes," whereas "parents who are restrictive and psychologically controlling have children with more behavioral problems." Strict structure plus alertness plus the freedom to err is the combination the book treats as optimal.

This pairs with the book's other threads as captured on its page: that [Failure](/notes/failure) is the first step toward success (losing is what teaches a boy to become a winner), and that [Intrinsic Motivation](/notes/intrinsic-motivation) lasts where extrinsic motivation like money is temporary. Letting a child make mistakes is the practical mechanism by which both the failure-as-teacher and intrinsic-motivation arguments operate.

**Context:** "Authoritative parenting" is a term from developmental psychologist Diana Baumrind's framework, which distinguishes it from authoritarian, permissive, and uninvolved styles. It denotes high warmth and responsiveness combined with high expectations and clear limits, and is the style most consistently associated in the research literature with positive child outcomes.

## Where this appears

- [The Parents' Guide to Boys](/books/the-parents-guide-to-boys) — the book's core empirical contrast between authoritative parenting (strict guidelines + alertness + freedom to make mistakes) and restrictive, psychologically controlling parenting.
