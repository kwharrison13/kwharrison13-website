# David O. McKay

David O. McKay appears across Kyle's corpus in two registers: as a **decisive 20th-century LDS Church president** in the history books, and as a **source of quotable spiritual counsel** in the gospel-study notes. In [American Zion](/books/american-zion), Benjamin Park's history, McKay is the canonical example of prophetic intuition correctly applied — his decisiveness in telling [Ezra Taft Benson](/notes/ezra-taft-benson) "you should turn the offer down" reads as "a leadership moment where the President of the Church recognized a likely-bad outcome and resisted being swayed." [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) credits his presidency (c. 1955) with making "the prophet" standard usage for the church president. [Leonard Arrington and the Writing of Mormon History](/books/leonard-arrington-and-the-writing-of-mormon-history) adds the most consequential thread: McKay privately held that the priesthood ban "was a policy rather than a doctrine, and that it would be changed someday" — a 1954 conviction he kept largely to himself, and which the book contrasts with Spencer W. Kimball's later success in reversing the ban with a unified Quorum. The same book records McKay's refusal to burn *Dialogue* ("this Church is not about to burn any publication").

In the gospel-study notes, McKay is quoted as a moral teacher. Radiating Your Example builds on his teaching that we "radiate our personality" — that it "comes from what a person REALLY IS, not what they PRETEND to be" — paired with D&C 88:40 ("light cleaveth unto light"). Godly Characteristics of the Master and Self-Mastery — Topical Study cite his counsel on temperance and self-control ("a man who cannot control his temper… moves in daily life very close to the animal plane"). [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) uses his dual-aim formulation: "the complete ideal of Mormonism is to make upright citizens in an ideal society."

**Context:** David O. McKay (1873–1970) was the ninth President of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, serving from 1951 until his death. An exceptionally popular leader, he presided over a major period of international expansion and is widely remembered for the slogan "Every member a missionary" and for emphasizing the family ("No other success can compensate for failure in the home").
