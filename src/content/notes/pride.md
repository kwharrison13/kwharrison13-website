# Pride

Across Kyle's gospel study, pride is the recurring spiritual failure mode — the disposition that closes a person off from learning, repentance, and revelation. The clearest formulation comes from Alma 32–35: a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) makes someone open to something new, while pride makes it "nearly impossible to accept new ideas." The poor Zoramites, cast out for the "coarseness of their apparel," were poor in heart and so "in a preparation to hear the word" — affliction prepares the heart precisely by stripping away pride. The same logic drives the [pride cycle](/notes/the-pride-cycle) in Helaman 1–6 and the famine in Helaman 7–12 that "brings the people to humility," alongside the sign-seeking that pride breeds.

Two subtler treatments deepen the theme. Grace For Grace names "one of [Satan](/notes/satan)'s subtlest tools" — *pride dressed as humility*: congratulating yourself for being "worthy to be humbled," the "astounding capacity of the natural man" to be prideful about recognizing one's own humbling. And The Little Ones, The Lost Ones casts the Scribes and Pharisees as "the personification of pride," contrasting their self-righteousness with the childlike (not childish) humility Christ asks: "when you take up a prideful air of self-righteousness, you lose sight of your need to learn, correct, and progress." Throughout, pride is the antonym of teachability — the opposite pole to [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) and humility.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint teaching, pride is frequently identified as a foundational sin — most famously in Ezra Taft Benson's "Beware of Pride" — defined as enmity toward God and toward others, and dramatized in the Book of Mormon's recurring "pride cycle" (prosperity → pride → wickedness → destruction → humility → repentance).
