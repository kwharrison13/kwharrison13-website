# Alma 42

The closing chapter of [Alma the Younger](/notes/alma-the-younger)'s counsel to his wayward son [Corianton](/notes/corianton), read across Kyle's notes as the doctrinal climax of "The Great Plan of Happiness." The insight Kyle drew from the sequence: Alma spends only 14 verses on Corianton's sins but 74 verses teaching about [Jesus Christ](/notes/jesus) before relating it back to his son — and Alma 42:29 lands the turn: **"I desire that ye should let these things trouble you no more, and only let your sins trouble you, with that trouble which shall bring you down unto repentance."**

In the topical studies the chapter is cited chiefly for its theology of justice and mercy. The Excommunication — Topical Study uses Alma 42 to frame the **scales of justice**: "if the scales of justice are unbalanced, God ceases to be God — sin leans it one way, punishment balances it back," with the [Atonement](/notes/atonement) as the umbrella that shifts the punishment from the sinner to Christ. The Knowledge — Topical Study quotes Alma 42:17 ("how could a man repent except he should sin? How could he sin if there was no law? How could there be a law save there was a punishment?") on the necessity of law, sin, and punishment, and the Scripture Study — Topical Study cites Alma 42:4, 7, 14 on how the Fall makes the necessity of an Atonement obvious.

**Context:** Alma 42 is a chapter in the Book of Mormon in which the prophet Alma the Younger explains the doctrines of the Fall, justice, mercy, and the plan of redemption to his son Corianton.
