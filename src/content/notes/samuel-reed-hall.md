# Samuel Reed Hall

Samuel Reed Hall appears in Kyle's notes through [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning), TIME's special edition on how the brain learns, where he is cited as an early systematizer of teaching as a craft. The piece references his "Lectures on School Keeping" and his catalog of the impediments to good [Teaching](/notes/teaching) — lack of supplies, political factions within the school district pursued "at the expense of educational progress," poorly qualified teachers, and poor remuneration of the qualified ones. In the source's lineage of [Education](/notes/education) reform, Hall sits at the front of a chain that runs forward through [John Dewey](/notes/john-dewey), [Maria Montessori](/notes/maria-montessori), and [Jean Piaget](/notes/jean-piaget), framing teaching not as an innate gift but as a discipline with identifiable failure modes.

**Context:** Samuel Read Hall (1795–1877) was an American educator and Congregationalist minister who founded one of the first formal teacher-training schools (a "normal school") in Concord, Vermont, in 1823. His 1829 "Lectures on School-Keeping" is generally regarded as the first American textbook written specifically on the art of teaching.
