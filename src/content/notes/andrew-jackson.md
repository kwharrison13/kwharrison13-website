# Andrew Jackson

Seventh U.S. president, who surfaces in two of Kyle's book notes as the emblem of the populist, majoritarian democracy of his era. In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) (James Traub's biography), Jackson is Adams's great rival: the man whose "corrupt bargain" grievance over the 1824 election fueled a relentless campaign, who ran a Nashville "war room" — a Central Corresponding Committee of supporters working "in a time when the news cycle ran in weeks rather than hours." Adams, who had once championed Jackson as a national hero, came to call him "incompetent both by his ignorance and the fury of his passions." The notes frame the Adams–Jackson contest as a clash of political philosophies: Jackson's program "sacrifices the future and remote benefits to the present," appealing to "the prejudices and feelings of the people," while Adams's American System looked to a "longer range," and Adams argued that the simplicity Jackson prized — "the essential characteristic in all slavery" — stood against the necessary complexity of free government.

In [American Zion](/books/american-zion) (Benjamin Park), Jackson is the namesake of Jackson County, Missouri — where the early Latter-day Saints sought to build [Zion](/notes/zion) — and the personification of the Jacksonian-era democracy they collided with, what [Alexis de Tocqueville](/notes/alexis-de-tocqueville) termed "the tyranny of the majority."

**Context:** Andrew Jackson (1767–1845) was U.S. president from 1829 to 1837, a founder of the modern Democratic Party whose era gave its name to "Jacksonian democracy" — the expansion of white male suffrage and populist politics — and whose record includes the Indian Removal Act and the destruction of the Second Bank of the United States.
