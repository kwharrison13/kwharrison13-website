# Hernando de Soto

The Peruvian economist cited in [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) to ground its argument about the barriers facing microenterprise and the informal economy. The book draws on de Soto's study in which four law students took over nine months to complete the paperwork to set up a small unincorporated clothing factory — fielding 10 bribe requests, needing 11 permits, and spending 32 times the average worker's monthly salary — as evidence of the bureaucratic excess that keeps the informal sector from growing or stabilizing. His Latin American bestseller [The Other Path](/notes/the-other-path) is described as analyzing the plight of microentrepreneurs and proposing concrete remedies: simplifying business registration, reducing state regulation of small businesses, creating offices to help the poor become self-employed, training and support groups, and policies encouraging bank lending to poor business people. The book uses him to advocate institutional reforms toward "genuine, market-oriented structures," while noting skepticism over whether his ideas receive more than lip service.

**Context:** Hernando de Soto Polar (b. 1941) is a Peruvian economist, president of the Institute for Liberty and Democracy, and author of *The Other Path* and *The Mystery of Capital*, known for arguing that secure property rights and reduced legal barriers are key to unlocking the economic potential of the poor.
