# Anxiety

Anxiety appears across Kyle's notes in two distinct senses. The psychological sense is most developed in [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child), which treats parental anxiety as contagious: "our anxiety is seeping into our kids," up to 50 percent of children of anxious parents develop anxiety disorders themselves, and the book's core prescription is for the parent to be a "nonanxious presence." It points to epigenetics (experience turning predisposition genes on or off), early treatment as protective against recurrence, and concrete countermeasures — cognitive behavioral therapy to "talk back to" distorted thoughts, meditation, exercise, sleep, nature. [The Magnolia Story](/books/the-magnolia-story) surfaces the same theme as a posture rather than a disorder: "what's the point in worrying?" recurs as Chip and Joanna Gaines's stance against perfectionism, paired with [Gratitude](/notes/gratitude) and contentment.

The scriptural sense is nearly the inverse — anxiety as righteous, zealous concern for others' souls. In Kyle's Alma 13–16 — Enter into the Rest of the Lord study, Alma pleads "with great anxiety even unto pain" that his brethren repent, and the note collects parallel verses (Jacob, 2 Nephi, Mosiah) framing "the anxiety that people feel for others when they are filled with the love of God." [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) is quoted to the same end: a man filled with God's love "ranges through the whole world, anxious to bless the whole human race." Here anxiety is not a state to be calmed but the outward-facing burden of charity.

**Context:** In general usage, anxiety denotes apprehensive worry about uncertain outcomes; in scriptural English it often carries the older meaning of earnest, eager concern or solicitude.

## Where this appears

- [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) — anxiety as contagious and heritable; the parent as a "nonanxious presence"
- [The Magnolia Story](/books/the-magnolia-story) — "what's the point in worrying?" as a recurring anti-perfectionism posture
- Alma 13–16 — Enter into the Rest of the Lord — Alma's "great anxiety even unto pain" for souls; anxiety as the burden of charity
