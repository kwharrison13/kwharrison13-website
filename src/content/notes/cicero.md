# Cicero

The Roman statesman and orator who recurs across Kyle's reading as a model of two things: republican oratory and dutiful service in old age. In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), Cicero is the curricular core of "education for citizenship" — Adams' Harvard rhetoric professorship framed oratory as the connective tissue of republican self-government, modeled on Cicero — and the lifelong companion Adams revered "as the highest human achievement save for the revelation of Scripture," urging his son Charles to "make him the study of your whole life." Adams read Cicero before dawn every day and proposed reading him together with Charles as a shared intellectual project.

In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) adopts a Ciceronian view in old age: Cicero's *De Senectute* frames duty in old age, and Munger casts [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) as "a sort of modern baton carrier for a Ciceronian point of view" — staying in the arena and "communicating much that tells others what they should think and how they should behave." Munger embraces Cicero's idea that "the only life worth living is dedicated in substantial part to good outcomes one cannot possibly survive to see," and even cites Cicero to defend being lost in one's own thoughts as a virtue. In the [Harvard commencement speech](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-school-commencement-speech), Munger invokes Cicero (alongside [Demosthenes](/notes/demosthenes)) as an oratory role model via Cicero's reply when asked his favorite Demosthenes oration: "The longest one."

**Context:** Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BC) was a Roman statesman, lawyer, and orator whose speeches and treatises (including *De Senectute*, "On Old Age") shaped Western rhetoric and political philosophy for two millennia.
