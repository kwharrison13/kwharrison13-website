# Costco

Across Kyle's notes Costco is the canonical example of the low-cost-operator moat. In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Buffett names being "the lowcost producer (GEICO, Costco)" as one of the two durable forms of competitive moat (the other being a powerful worldwide brand), and the same low-cost formula echoes Berkshire's own NFM/Borsheim's playbook of lowest operating costs, shrewd high-volume buying, and gross margins far below competitors. Kyle's marginal note in that book flags Costco directly — "Look for case studies of ultra low-cost businesses e.g. Costco" — and frames it in the [Culture](/notes/culture) concept as running "at low cost / high quality" the way Costco does for simplicity and [Amazon](/notes/amazon) does for scale.

In the [Pricing](/notes/pricing) concept, Costco anchors the link between pricing and customer obsession: its across-the-board ~14% markup "even when it could charge more" is the margin-discipline model [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) explicitly absorbed at Amazon, per [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store). Costco thus functions in the wiki as the worked example for the idea that charging less — by structural choice, not by sacrificing quality — is itself a moat.

**Context:** *Costco Wholesale (NASDAQ: COST) is a US membership-warehouse retailer known for a deliberately thin product markup (capped around 14–15% on most goods), membership-fee-driven profitability, and a high-volume, limited-SKU operating model.*
