# Income Share Agreements (ISA)

**Income Share Agreements** are an education-financing model Kyle tracked closely as an Index Ventures sourcing theme: instead of a fixed loan, the student pays a share of future income, so financing is underwritten to *earning potential* rather than credit history. Tess Michaels of Stride Funding makes the underwriting case directly — "[Income Share Agreements (ISA)](/notes/income-share-agreements-isa) don't underwrite to FICO; they focus on future earning potential" — which matters because "90%+ of student loans require a co-signor and so few people have access to that." Stride's edge is its underwriting engine ("the better your underwriting engine, the more effectively you can manage your cost of capital") and capital markets brought in-house via SPVs; its university fund showed 0% delinquency and on-time graduation, and bootcamp cost-of-capital dropping from 14% to ~6%.

The model recurs as a *means to an end* across Kyle's calls. Eric Menees at TradeUp uses ISAs to fund trade-school students (welding, truck driving, dental) where "there is almost no public funding for trade schools," explicitly contrasting his 1.35–1.75x repayment cap against Lambda School's 17% monthly fees ("highway robbery") — ISAs as the *less-predatory* alternative, where "trade students aren't going to know they're getting ripped off." Moe Abbas at Acadium runs accelerator students under ISAs but flags the hard part: "hard to scale [Income Share Agreements (ISA)](/notes/income-share-agreements-isa) as a community because you have to build the entire support infrastructure." The skeptical case lives in the saved-reading cluster Kyle filed under [Student Debt](/notes/student-debt) (e.g. New America's "Income share agreements aren't the solution to student debt"). Kyle's recurring underwriting signals from these calls: future earnings potential, family connections to an industry, and supply/demand imbalances in the target field.
