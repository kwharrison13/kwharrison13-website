# Isaac Asimov

Asimov runs through Kyle's corpus in two distinct registers. As a [Science Fiction](/notes/science-fiction) futurist, he anchors the optimistic pole of the automation debate in [The AppetiZIRP](/notes/the-appetizirp) and Historical Futurism - Research: via [Packy McCormick](/notes/packy-mccormick)'s essay, his story *Whatever You Wish* is set against [Kurt Vonnegut](/notes/kurt-vonnegut)'s dystopia as the framing question — "Is automation going to lead to a depressing, meaningless future of work or a beautiful world of aristocratic abundance for all?" Asimov's answer is that machines free humans to be more human, educated in literature, arts, and philosophy, where "every field will be an elective, and the only guide will be 'whatever you wish.'" He sits alongside [Arthur C. Clarke](/notes/arthur-c-clarke) in Kyle's catalog of formative SF, including his short story *The Last Question*.

In [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound) Asimov appears as one of the science-fiction authors [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) treated as "blueprints" for conversational computing — his books, with Clarke's and Heinlein's, lined the library of Bezos's Seattle home and fed his conviction in the inevitability of voice interfaces and AI. Kyle's [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) page carries the same thread. In a separate register, [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) quotes Asimov the public intellectual on American anti-intellectualism — "the false notion that democracy means that 'my ignorance is just as good as your knowledge'" — and his observation that the words spurring the greatest scientific breakthroughs are not "Eureka" but "Gee, that's funny."

**Context:** Isaac Asimov (1920–1992) was a prolific American science-fiction author and biochemistry professor, best known for the *Foundation* series and the Three Laws of Robotics, and a leading mid-century popularizer of science.
