# Religious Intellectualism

Faith held as a serious intellectual commitment rather than a tribal or sentimental one — belief one can defend without needing to proselytize. The wiki's anchor instance is [David Perell](/notes/david-perell)'s essay [Peter Thiel's Religion](/notes/peter-thiel-s-religion), which is tagged `religious-intellectualism`. There [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel) professes Christianity-as-true while declining the missionary impulse: *"I believe Christianity to be true. I don't feel a compelling need to convince other people of that."* The essay's deeper move is to treat religion as an *explanatory* framework, not a creed: by way of [Rene Girard](/notes/rene-girard)'s [Mimetic Theory](/notes/mimetic-theory), it argues that "human culture began with a murder" and that "managing that violence is the secret reason for all religious and political institutions." Religion, in this reading, is the institution that keeps mimetic rivalry from erupting "like an angry volcano" — an intellectual claim about how civilization holds together, sourced from material modern thinkers tend to ignore (the Bible, [Marcel Proust](/notes/marcel-proust), [William Shakespeare](/notes/william-shakespeare)).

This is the thread that makes the concept "intellectualism" rather than piety: belief examined through Girardian anthropology and literary criticism, held with conviction but without evangelism.
