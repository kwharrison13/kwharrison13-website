# Obedience

Kyle's sources hold two views of obedience in tension. The traditional, devotional pole — collected in [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book) — treats obedience as the precondition of divine power: "When obedience ceases to become an irritant and becomes our quest, in that moment God will endow [us] with power" ([Ezra Taft Benson](/notes/ezra-taft-benson)); "every blessing is predicated upon obedience to law" (D&C 130:20–21); and [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s paradox that strict obedience is "the only way … to become free," not slavery. The counterpole comes from [Future Mormon](/books/future-mormon) (Adam Miller), which argues that "obedience can be sin's most successful strategy" — strict obedience practiced to put God in our debt and stay in control. For Miller, "only love can fulfill the law," and unlike obedience, love "must be freely given and cannot be commanded"; salvation "cannot be bought with the currency of obedience" ([Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)).

[The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) (Jana Riess) situates obedience sociologically: Mormonism "holds two different ideals in tension" — the "rulebook of following the prophet" (Benson's "fourteen fundamentals") against a countervailing trend acknowledging that leaders can err. Words like "authority" and "obedience" have risen in General Conference frequency, and Riess documents members like "Lisa," whose mission "culture of obedience" displaced her earlier, more personal spiritual authority and left her "disconnected with myself and with God."

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint usage, obedience to commandments and covenants is a core discipleship value; the theological debates Kyle's sources stage — obedience vs. grace, rulebook vs. personal revelation — are live tensions within contemporary Mormon thought.
