# Due Diligence

The verification work an investor or acquirer does before committing capital — a concept Kyle's sources approach from several angles. The [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) offer Buffett's distinctive version: rather than relying on plant inspections, Berkshire evaluates "the economic characteristics of the business — its competitive strengths and weaknesses — and the quality of the people we will be joining." [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) (Brent Beshore) describes it operationally as "a grueling, intentional verification process before a multi-million-dollar purchase," and notes that a buyer delaying legal engagement is "a buyer still deciding whether to move forward."

The failure mode is the subject of [How to Trick Investors and VCs](/notes/how-to-trick-investors-and-vcs), which argues many VCs "don't dig deep," focusing on topline metrics and assuming the rest works out — exactly the gap that lets founders game efficiency metrics like the Burn Multiple. On the practical tooling side, [KuppingerCole](/notes/kuppingercole) appears as an analyst firm whose research Kyle leaned on during the Salt Security diligence, illustrating how third-party analysis feeds the diligence process for cybersecurity deals.

**Context:** Due diligence is the investigation and verification a party performs before entering a transaction — examining financials, legal exposure, operations, and management — to confirm the deal is what it appears to be and to price risk accordingly.
