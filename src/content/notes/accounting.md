# Accounting

Accounting recurs across Kyle's reading as a measurement technology that is indispensable but never to be confused with reality. The [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) supply the sharpest formulations: accounting numbers are "the beginning, not the end, of business valuation"; "the accountants' job is to record, not to evaluate"; managers stressing "accounting appearance over economic substance usually achieve little of either"; and Lincoln's riddle — calling a tail a leg does not make a dog have five legs — applies even when an auditor will certify it. [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) distinguishes book value (an accounting concept, "what has been put in") from intrinsic value (an economic concept, "what can be taken out"), and warns that GAAP can be exploited to produce steadily-rising "earnings" — "many accounting-based frauds of staggering size."

[Double Entry](/books/double-entry) supplies the deep history: [Luca Pacioli](/notes/luca-pacioli)'s 1494 treatise codified Venetian double-entry bookkeeping into the foundation of modern accounting, and the book traces it forward through cost accounting (Wedgwood, Rockefeller's Standard Oil), the rise of the great London firms (Deloitte, Price/Waterhouse, Cooper), and 20th-century national-income accounting (GDP). Its argument echoes Berkshire's caution: the financial statements meant to "guarantee corporate transparency... cannot be trusted to convey the true state of a business at all." Two long-reads sharpen the fraud angle — [Charlie Munger: The Great Financial Scandal of 2003](/notes/charlie-munger-the-great-financial-scandal-of-2003) is a satirical parable of "Quant Tech" whose board optimizes for market cap through "incentive-driven accounting fictions," and [How to Trick Investors and VCs](/notes/how-to-trick-investors-and-vcs) catalogs "honest accounting shenanigans" (capitalized SaaS sales commissions, internal-use software capitalization, GAAP gross-margin optics) that flatter the numbers without changing the economics.

**Context:** Accounting is the system for recording, classifying, and reporting financial transactions; double-entry bookkeeping — each transaction recorded as offsetting debits and credits — is its foundational method, and GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) the standardized US framework these sources repeatedly probe for its limits.
