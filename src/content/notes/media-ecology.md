# Media Ecology

Media ecology is the organizing framework of [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death): the claim that each communications medium reshapes thought, discourse, and culture. As [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) puts it, "Each medium, like language itself, makes possible a unique mode of discourse by providing a new orientation for thought, for expression, for sensibility. Which, of course, is what McLuhan meant in saying the medium is the message." Postman's argument is that "as typography moves to the periphery of our culture and television takes its place at the center, the seriousness, clarity and, above all, value of public discourse dangerously declines" — print enforces argument and meaning ("there is no escape from meaning when language is the instrument"), while [Television](/notes/television) "speaks in only one persistent voice — the voice of entertainment."

The concept also anchors [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people), where it carries the political stakes directly: "the coherence of 'the people's' judgment depends on the media ecology they inhabit: a print culture trains the sustained attention and logical scrutiny that public reasoning requires." Public judgment, on this view, "is only as coherent as the medium that forms it" — making the corruption of the media ecology a corruption of democratic reasoning itself.

**Context:** "Media ecology" is the formal name of an academic field — associated with Marshall McLuhan and Neil Postman — that studies media as environments which shape how people perceive, think, and organize society.

## Where this appears

- [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) — the book's central framework: media are not neutral conduits; each medium dictates the form and seriousness of public discourse.
- [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people) — the coherence of public judgment depends on the media ecology (print vs. television) that forms it.
