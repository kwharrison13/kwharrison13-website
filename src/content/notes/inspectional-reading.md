# Inspectional Reading

Inspectional reading is the second of the four reading levels in [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) (Adler and Van Doren), and the one Kyle annotated as "surgical reading." It is defined by its emphasis on time: "the art of skimming systematically" — explicitly not the casual, random browsing the word "skimming" usually implies. The book frames it as the best, most complete reading possible given a limited time, the counterpart to analytical reading, which is the best reading possible given unlimited time. Far from passive, the book insists it is "a very active sort of reading" that demands all of one's faculties awake and working.

Its essence, per the book's "most important rule of all," is the discipline of not trying to understand every word or page of a difficult book on the first pass — to be willing "to be, or to seem to be, superficial." A quick once-over is a "threshing process" that separates chaff from kernels and answers three questions: what kind of book is it, what is it about as a whole, and what is its structural order. The book also positions it as a precursor: systematic skimming anticipates a book's structure and feeds the later analytical reading, and at the syntopical level it is how a reader first inspects an entire bibliography before reading any single book analytically.
