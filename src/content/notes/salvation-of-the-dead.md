# Salvation of the Dead

In [The King Follett Sermon](/notes/the-king-follett-sermon), [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) frames the salvation of the dead as the greatest responsibility God has laid on the living: "The greatest responsibility in this world that God has laid upon us is to seek after our dead." He grounds it in Paul ("They without us cannot be made perfect") and Elijah ("turn the heart of the fathers to the children"), and argues that the sealing power "should be in our hands to seal our children and our dead." The logic is that "all the spirits who have not obeyed the Gospel in the flesh must either obey it in the spirit or be damned" — so vicarious work is the mechanism by which "every man who has a friend in the eternal world can save him, unless he has committed the [Unpardonable Sin](/notes/unpardonable-sin)."

The doctrine is delivered at the funeral of Joseph's friend Elder King Follett and is bound up with the sermon's other expansive teachings — the [Character of God](/notes/character-of-god), [Exaltation](/notes/exaltation), the uncreated [Intelligence](/notes/intelligence) of man, and the necessity of [Baptism](/notes/baptism) by water and by fire ("necessarily and inseparably connected"). The throughline is consolation: to be comforted about the dead, one must first understand the character and being of God.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology, "salvation for the dead" refers to the belief that the deceased can receive the ordinances of salvation (such as baptism) vicariously through proxy work performed by the living, often in temples.
