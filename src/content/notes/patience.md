# Patience

Patience runs through Kyle's corpus on two tracks — investing temperament and spiritual discipline. In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) it is named "central to [Buffett's] temperament-over-IQ thesis": the biography argues that Buffett's genius "was largely a genius of character — of patience, discipline, and rationality," virtues "common enough" but "rare in the heat of financial passions." Three things made the early investments work — the bargain price, "Buffett's patience in holding on," and the turnaround. In [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war) patience is the discipline of timing: "He will win who knows when to fight and when not to fight," and the danger of a general who, "losing patience at the long delay," storms a position before he is ready.

On the spiritual track, the Patience — Topical Study treats patience as "active waiting and enduring" ([Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)) and the key to self-mastery and personal perfection — "in patience ye may possess your souls" ([D&C 101](/notes/dandc-101):38), "continue in patience until ye are perfected" ([D&C 67](/notes/dandc-67):13). At its core is desire: "God can educate our desires," and "what we insistently desire, over time, is what we will eventually become" ([Neal A. Maxwell](/notes/neal-a-maxwell)). The concept recurs across the scripture-study network as a companion virtue to Diligence, [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery), and Humility, and as a bare hashtag in the [Natural Man](/notes/natural-man) and [Enos 1](/notes/enos-1) pages.

**Context:** Patience appears here in two distinct registers — as an investing virtue (the discipline of waiting for the right opportunity and holding through volatility, a recurring theme in value-investing literature) and as a religious/character attribute studied in a Latter-day Saint scriptural framework.
