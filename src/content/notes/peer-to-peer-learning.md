# Peer-to-Peer Learning

In the notes on [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), peer-to-peer learning is what closes the four-step learning loop (gain knowledge → [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection) → [Critical Thinking](/notes/critical-thinking) → feedback) that most learning leaves incomplete by halting at "gaining knowledge." Peer learning and honest feedback are what finish the loop, and they depend on [Psychological Safety](/notes/psychological-safety) and a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) — the book cites Google's research finding psychological safety to be the key ingredient binding effective teams.

The book quotes Google education evangelist [Jaime Casap](/notes/jaime-casap), who argues peer-to-peer learning "does not come naturally to us" because "we teach kids to be individuals and to value individual accomplishments," and that giving honest feedback is hard because people default to validation over real assessment. The notes connect this to [Peter Drucker](/notes/peter-drucker)'s line: "Follow effective action with quiet reflection. From the quiet reflection will come even more effective action." Real collaboration, in this view, is "the ability to ask good questions, the ability to give good feedback and take good feedback."

**Context:** Peer-to-peer learning is a model in which people learn from and teach one another as equals — through feedback, discussion, and shared practice — rather than receiving knowledge top-down from an instructor.
