# The King Follett Sermon

[Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s discourse delivered April 7, 1844 at a general conference in Nauvoo, occasioned by the death of Elder King Follett. It is the entity Kyle's [Character of God](/notes/character-of-god), [Exaltation](/notes/exaltation), [Intelligence](/notes/intelligence), and [Salvation of the Dead](/notes/salvation-of-the-dead) concept pages all trace back to as their doctrinal source — the verbatim sermon and a section-by-section study live on the [gospel-study page](/notes/the-king-follett-sermon). Its central claims: to know God is the first principle of the gospel and eternal life itself; "God himself was once as we are now, and is an exalted man"; man may become as God ("you have got to learn how to be gods yourselves… from grace to grace, from exaltation to exaltation"); the [Intelligence](/notes/intelligence) of man is uncreated and co-eternal with God (illustrated by Joseph's ring with no beginning and no end); our greatest responsibility is the [Salvation of the Dead](/notes/salvation-of-the-dead); and the [Unpardonable Sin](/notes/unpardonable-sin) requires full knowledge of God before it can be committed.

In [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling), [Richard Lyman Bushman](/notes/richard-lyman-bushman) frames it as "the culminating statement of Joseph Smith's theology" — preserved only through the overlapping notes of four diarists, never canonized — and what the critic Harold Bloom called "one of the truly remarkable sermons ever preached in America." Bushman draws out its radical free-will and democratic implications: God is "the most intelligent of the free intelligences," each soul "radically free" and uncreated, with God acting as teacher rather than maker — "every man a god and a king." Kyle's margin note there: "*Gods in embryo. Gods in training.*"

**Context:** The King Follett Discourse is among the most-studied of Joseph Smith's sermons in Latter-day Saint thought, foundational to LDS teachings on the nature of God, eternal progression, and human deification.
