# Arius

Early Christian figure invoked in Kyle's The Godhead study as the named party in the "Arian controversy." The note characterizes his position simply: Arius "believed Christ was God's most perfect creation, separate from God." The controversy he triggered — "is Christ separate from or the same as God?" — was settled at the [Nicene](/notes/apostasy) council in favor of "begotten, not created," landing on the formula that Christ is of the *same* substance as the Father (*homoousios*) rather than merely *similar* (*homoiousios*). Kyle's study cites this as a hinge point of the [Apostasy](/notes/apostasy): Latter-day Saints reject the creedal Trinity that emerged because, in [Jeffrey R. Holland](/notes/jeffrey-r-holland)'s paraphrased framing, "it is not in the scriptures," and because the great apostate shift was from prophets who said "thus saith the Lord" to councils of men who spoke philosophy — turning simple revelation into a system "no one but philosophers can understand." Arius thus functions in the note as a marker for the moment doctrine was decided by debate rather than revelation.

**Context:** Arius (c. 256–336 AD) was a presbyter of Alexandria whose teaching that the Son was a created being subordinate to the Father precipitated the First Council of Nicaea (325 AD) and the Nicene Creed; the resulting "Arianism" was condemned as heresy by mainstream Christianity.
