# Adam Smith

Adam Smith recurs across Kyle's wiki as the canonical example of foundational thinking worth reading at the source. In [Naval Ravikant — The Angel Philosopher](/notes/naval-ravikant-the-angel-philosopher), Smith and [The Wealth of Nations](/notes/the-wealth-of-nations) head [Naval Ravikant](/notes/naval-ravikant)'s list of "old as hell" primary sources — alongside [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)'s aphorisms, [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), and [Charles Darwin](/notes/charles-darwin) — that he argues are worth reading instead of the "airport #1 bestseller." Naval's specific jab lands on "macroeconomists who've read tons of treatises but no Adam Smith," using Smith as shorthand for the original idea everyone cites but few actually read.

In [American Sphinx](/books/american-sphinx), Smith appears via the invisible-hand "natural harmony of interests" analogy — the notion that free individuals pursuing self-interest produce social order — which [Thomas Jefferson](/notes/thomas-jefferson) believed would guide a free society.

**Context:** Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and political economist, author of *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) and *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776). The latter is foundational to classical economics and the source of the "invisible hand" metaphor for how self-interested action can yield collective benefit.
