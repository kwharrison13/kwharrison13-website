# John Nash

In [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future), Nash is the young rival who extended [game theory](/notes/game-theory) beyond [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann)'s zero-sum world. As a confident graduate student — he once spent nearly an hour at Einstein's blackboard, only to be told "you had better study some more physics, young man" — Nash devised a framework for analyzing any game, zero-sum or not, with any number of participants, showing that there exist outcomes in which no player can do better by unilaterally changing strategy: the Nash equilibria. The book frames it as "a staggering accomplishment," whose usefulness even Nash did not foresee.

His rivalry with von Neumann is a full subsection. Nash himself read von Neumann's coolness toward his work as a "defensive response prompted by a Young Turk invading his turf" — "I was playing a non-cooperative game in relation to von Neumann," he later told historian Robert Leonard. The book also records skepticism about the equilibrium's real-world force: in Flood's experiment, participants "leaned towards the von Neumann-Morgenstern solution of mutual cooperation" rather than the Nash equilibrium.

**Context:** John Forbes Nash Jr. (1928–2015) was an American mathematician who won the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics for his work on game theory; his life and struggle with schizophrenia were the subject of the book and film *A Beautiful Mind*.
