# Hanlon's Razor

Hanlon's Razor is one of the general thinking concepts Kyle pulls from [The Great Mental Models Volume 1: General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts), summarized there as the principle that most badness in the world is stupidity, not malice — "when something looks wrong, look first for confusion rather than conspiracy." The book pairs it with what it calls the "Evil Genius Default" (the Devil Fallacy): the political-discourse failure mode of attributing bad outcomes to a malicious mastermind rather than ordinary incompetence. Kyle's notes trace the cognitive roots of the razor to [Daniel Kahneman](/notes/daniel-kahneman)'s Linda problem / Fallacy of Conjunction, where a vivid intentional story feels more probable than a duller, likelier explanation.

In the wider latticework the book organizes around [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), Hanlon's Razor sits next to [Map vs Territory](/notes/map-vs-territory), [Circle of Competence](/notes/circle-of-competence), [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking), and [Inversion](/notes/inversion) as a debiasing tool — a default that keeps you from over-reading intent into noise.

**Context:** Hanlon's Razor is commonly stated as "never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity" (or carelessness); it functions as a heuristic for charitable, lower-drama causal explanation.
