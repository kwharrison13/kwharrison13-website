# Leo Tolstoy

The Russian novelist who recurs across Kyle's reading less for his fiction than for two ideas that get cited as frameworks. The first is his **theory of history**: in [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) and on Kyle's [History](/notes/history) page, Tolstoy holds that to understand events we "must leave aside kings, ministers, and generals, and study the… small elements by which the masses are moved." [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) expands this — Tolstoy "was not a historian… he was a novelist," but believed "the causes of every human action… were so manifold, so complex, and so deeply hidden in unconscious motivations that it is impossible to know why anything ever happened." [Mortimer Adler](/notes/mortimer-adler) lists him among the exemplars of expository and imaginative style.

The second is the **"Tolstoy Effect,"** which [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) draws from a Tolstoy passage in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) (and in [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment)): the worst criminals don't appraise themselves as bad — they either deny the crime or decide it was understandable and forgivable. Munger's second half of the effect — making excuses for "fixable poor performance" instead of providing the fix — becomes, for Kyle, a note on humility and parenting (#[The Tolstoy Effect](/notes/the-tolstoy-effect), #[Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility)). Tolstoy appears once more in [Double Entry](/books/double-entry), where (through the character Levin) he voices literary unease at the "rationalisation" of life that double-entry bookkeeping embodied.

**Context:** Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910) was a Russian author of *War and Peace* and *Anna Karenina*; the history-theory Kyle cites runs through the second epilogue of *War and Peace*, and Levin is the autobiographical farmer-philosopher of *Anna Karenina*.

## Where this appears

- [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) — Tolstoy's theory of history: study "the small elements by which the masses are moved."
- [History](/notes/history) — the same theory, used to frame how to read events (and Church history) honestly.
- [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) — Tolstoy the novelist-as-historian; causes of action too complex to fully know; an exemplar of style.
- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — Munger's "Tolstoy Effect" on self-regard and making excuses for fixable poor performance.
- [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) — the same Tolstoy-effect passage in Munger's talk.
- [Double Entry](/books/double-entry) — Levin voices Tolstoy's unease at the "rationalisation" of life that double entry embodied.
- [Mortimer Adler](/notes/mortimer-adler) — lists Tolstoy among his style/literature exemplars.
