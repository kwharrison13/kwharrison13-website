# Enlightenment

Across Kyle's library the Enlightenment recurs as the intellectual milieu that produced the American founding and, especially, the figure of [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin). In [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life), Henry May's *The Enlightenment in America* is quoted to the effect that the Constitution reflected "all the virtues of the moderate Enlightenment, and also one of its faults: the belief that everything can be settled by [Compromise](/notes/compromise)" — a "fault" the book argues was in fact a virtue in Franklin's hands, who "embodied the Enlightenment and its spirit of Compromise."

[The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written) places the same era at the center of the Declaration: deism "was the creed of most thinkers of the Enlightenment, the eighteenth-century Age of Reason," whose leaders included Locke, Hume, Voltaire, Rousseau, and in America Franklin and Jefferson — the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) network. Franklin and Jefferson "understood balance… part of an Enlightenment era that embraced the scientific method of testing and revising beliefs based on evidence," with the goal of equilibrium rather than triumph. [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) echoes this, describing Lincoln-and-Douglas-era audiences as "the grandsons and granddaughters of the Enlightenment (American version)… the inheritors of the Empire of Reason" — a print-based rational culture that the photograph and telegraph would later displace.

**Context:** The Enlightenment was the 17th–18th-century European intellectual movement that prized reason, empirical science, individual liberty, and natural rights over inherited authority and dogma. Its ideas (Locke on government, Montesquieu on separation of powers) directly shaped the American and French revolutions.

## Where this appears

- [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life) — quotes Henry May framing the Constitution as a product of "the moderate Enlightenment" and its compromise ethos.
- [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written) — locates the Declaration's deism and "balance" in the Enlightenment / Age of Reason and its founders.
- [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) — calls the print-literate founding-era public heirs of the Enlightenment's "Empire of Reason."
