# Alexander Majors

In Early Mormonism, Alexander Majors is cited as one of the Missourians who took part in driving the Saints out of Jackson County during the persecutions of the 1830s. Kyle uses him as a hostile-witness corroboration of the study's central argument — that what enraged the Jacksonians was not the Saints' crimes but their claim to revelation. Later in his life, Majors admitted that the difficulty in Missouri "did not come from the crimes of the Mormons, but solely from the claim that they had a new revelation from the almighty." His confession anchors the lesson Kyle draws: to claim revelation is the surest way to be thought fanatical, and the Saints should hope to stay near enough the mainstream to not be feared, but never so mainstream that people stop thinking them weird.

**Context:** Alexander Majors (1814–1900) was a Missouri freighting magnate, co-founder of the firm Russell, Majors and Waddell, and a backer of the Pony Express. In his memoir *Seventy Years on the Frontier* he reflected on the early Mormon conflicts in Missouri.
