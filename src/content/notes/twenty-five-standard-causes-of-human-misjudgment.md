# Twenty-Five Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment

The Twenty-Five Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment is [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s catalog of recurring psychological biases — described in Kyle's notes as "his famous Twenty-Five Standard Causes of Human Misjudgment." In the [Charlie Munger — Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address) (Talk 5 of [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack)), it appears under the "Psychology-Based Tendencies" heading of the closing additions, sitting alongside Munger's other problem-solving apparatus: the Two-Track Analysis, the "Ultra-Simple, General Problem-Solving Notions" (decide the big no-brainer questions first, apply numerical fluency, invert, watch for the Lollapalooza effect), and the use of [Checklists](/notes/checklists) as "a permanent part of the knowledge system."

Within that talk it functions as the psychology leg of Munger's broader argument for [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) — the idea that a person with "a vast set of skills over multiple disciplines... carries multiple tools and, therefore, will limit bad cognitive effects from man-with-a-hammer tendency." The list itself is the worked inventory of those "bad cognitive effects."

**Context:** "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" is Munger's best-known standalone talk (Talk Eleven in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*), enumerating roughly twenty-five cognitive biases — among them reward/punishment superresponse, liking/loving and disliking/hating tendencies, doubt-avoidance, inconsistency-avoidance, social proof, authority-misinfluence, and the "Lollapalooza" effect of several biases compounding at once. It is a foundational text in the behavioral-finance and mental-models tradition Munger championed.
