# Ricky Gervais

In Kyle's notes Gervais surfaces as a witness for the opinions-vs-facts distinction that runs through [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise). A Gervais clip (a TikTok bit) is cited for the line that social media made "popular" more important than "right," and for the escalation he names in the post-truth era: "We've always had, 'my opinion is worth as much as your opinion.' But now we've got 'my opinion is worth as much as your facts.' You can have your own opinions but you can't have your own facts." On the [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) page Kyle flags a note to "connect to Ricky Gervais video about opinions vs facts," tying the comedian's riff to [Tom Nichols](/notes/tom-nichols)'s argument about the collapse of expertise.

The same clip reappears in The Meme Economy - Research, where Gervais's distinction — *people don't examine the argument, they ask who is making it* — is used as raw material for how status and identity, rather than truth, drive what spreads online.

**Context:** Ricky Gervais is a British comedian, writer, and actor known for creating *The Office* (UK) and *Extras*, and for his stand-up specials. He is also a prominent public atheist and frequent commentator on free speech and social media culture.

## Where this appears

- [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) — cited as the "opinions vs facts" video; Kyle flags it to connect to [Tom Nichols](/notes/tom-nichols)'s thesis on the erosion of expertise.
- The Meme Economy - Research — his post-truth riff ("they say 'who's saying the argument?'") used as evidence that identity, not argument, drives what spreads.
