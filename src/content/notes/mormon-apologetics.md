# Mormon Apologetics

A concept Kyle uses for the defensible, evidence-backed side of religious belief — knowledge as opinion you can argue for, not merely feel. In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), he tags Adler's line that knowledge "consists in those opinions that can be defended… if we really know something, we must believe that we can convince others of what we know" with #[Mormon Apologetics](/notes/mormon-apologetics), holding it next to Why I Believe (objective evidence "that other reasonable men are likely to accept"). The pairing frames apologetics as the move from private testimony to publicly arguable claim.

In practice, Kyle's example of the genre is the [FairMormon](/notes/fairmormon) response to the Helaman 3 "houses of cement" objection — a once-supposed anachronism now answered by archaeological evidence of sophisticated pre-Columbian cement technology, which he tags `#[[FairMormon]] #[[Mormon Apologetics]]` in the August 17–23, 2020. Helaman 1–6: "The Rock of Our Redeemer" week. The concept also sits at the center of [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) by [Bruce C. Hafen](/notes/bruce-c-hafen), the book's argument that a true testimony has nothing to fear from honest analysis — faith that runs *through* complexity rather than around it — linking apologetics tightly to [Doubt](/notes/doubt) and [Faith and Reason](/notes/faith-and-reason).
