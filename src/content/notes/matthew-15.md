# Matthew 15

A chapter in the Gospel of Matthew that Kyle's gospel study draws on for its account of the Gentile (Canaanite) woman — the mother who pleads with Christ to heal her daughter, and whom Christ answers after first saying he was sent "to the lost sheep of the house of Israel." In the Olive Tree Allegory study, Matthew 15 is cited as one of the proof-texts that Christ labored not only among the Jews (the tame olive tree) but also among non-Israelites (wild trees): the Samaritans ([John 4](/notes/john-4)), the Gentile woman ([Matthew 15](/notes/matthew-15)), and the Gentile centurion ([Matthew 8](/notes/matthew-8)). It maps onto the [Jacob 5](/notes/jacob-5) history of the Lord reaching beyond Israel toward the eventual gathering of the Gentiles.

In Miscellaneous Scriptures it appears in a chained reference (2 Nephi 26:33 > Matthew 10:6 > Matthew 15:28 > Mark 7:27–28 > D&C 50:30), grouping the verse with the broader theme of the gospel going first to Israel and then to all people.

**Context:** Matthew 15 (KJV) includes Jesus' teaching that what truly defiles a person comes from the heart rather than from unwashed hands, the healing of the Canaanite woman's daughter (who answers that "even the dogs eat of the crumbs"), and the feeding of the four thousand.

## Where this appears

- Olive Tree Allegory — cited as the Gentile woman, evidence that Christ labored among non-Israelites as well as the house of Israel
- Miscellaneous Scriptures — part of a chained reference (Matthew 15:28) on the gospel going to Israel first, then the Gentiles
