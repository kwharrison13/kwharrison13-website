# Curse of Expertise

The "curse of expertise" is the gap that opens up between a specialist and a general audience once knowledge becomes deep and institutionalized — the more you know, the harder it becomes to communicate it to anyone outside the field. In the [Valued Empyrean](/notes/valued-empyrean) discussion (August 18th, 2021 — Valued Empyrean (Conor White-Sullivan & Darwin Harrison)) Kyle pairs the term directly with "Inferential Distance," summarizing the dynamic as: *"The more you think about something the harder it is to communicate it."*

Kyle's other anchor for the idea is [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book). He tags Adler's observation — *"Today, science tends to be written by experts for experts. A serious communication on a scientific subject assumes so much specialized knowledge on the part of the reader that it usually cannot be read at all by anyone not learned in the field"* — with the note *"Curse of expertise."* Adler frames this as a fall from "the happy childhood of science," when Galileo, Newton, and Darwin still wrote for a lay audience and there were "no boundaries that could not be crossed." The advantage is faster progress (experts reach the frontier quickly); the cost is that the ordinary intelligent reader is "left quite out of the picture." Kyle keeps this distinct from the related [Death of Expertise](/notes/death-of-expertise) (Adler on the relatively ignorant wrongly disagreeing with the learned).
