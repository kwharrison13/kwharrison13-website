# CAC War

Kyle's shorthand for the competitive dynamic in which rival companies bid up [customer-acquisition cost](/notes/customer-acquisition-cost) chasing the same customers — a "land grab" that burns cash without producing durable economics. It functions as a risk flag in his diligence: in the Point Card pre-brief, he tags the open question "Will Patrick find a way to efficiently scale growth and margins at a later stage after a cash burn heavy land grab?" with #[CAC War](/notes/cac-war) — the worry being whether a consumer fintech can win an expensive customer-acquisition fight and still reach healthy margins on the other side.

The concept becomes a thesis tool in The $1B Rorschach Test - Research, where Kyle positions a CAC war as the opposite of a [Durable](/notes/durable) business. Price-conscious customers churn between intro offers, so brands overspend on [CAC](/notes/customer-acquisition-cost) for LTV that never materializes — "huge revenue, few profitable companies." In the GLP-1 telehealth case study he diagrams the mechanism: a commoditized market produces "ungodly CAC," constant churn to the latest discount, and back-of-envelope unit economics ($500–700 CAC against ~$200/mo LTV) that only pencil because overhead is near zero. A CAC war is thus his label for revenue bought rather than earned — a billion of *revenue*, not value.
