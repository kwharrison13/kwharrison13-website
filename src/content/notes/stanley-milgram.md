# Stanley Milgram

Stanley Milgram anchors the [Authority-Misinfluence Tendency](/notes/authority-misinfluence-tendency) section of [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment). Munger cites Milgram, a psychology professor, who — in the wake of Nazi Germany leading ordinary believing Lutherans and Catholics "into orgies of genocide" — designed an experiment to measure exactly how far authority figures could lead ordinary people into gross misbehavior. In the experiment a man posing as an authority figure (a professor running a respectable experiment) tricked many ordinary people into delivering what they believed were massive, torturous electric shocks to innocent fellow citizens. Munger reads the result two ways: it demonstrates the real power of authority-misinfluence, but it also exposed "extreme ignorance in the psychology professoriate" of the era, who he argues misattributed the phenomenon.

**Context:** Stanley Milgram (1933–1984) was a social psychologist at Yale and CUNY, best known for the 1961 obedience-to-authority experiments and for the "small-world" / six-degrees-of-separation studies.
