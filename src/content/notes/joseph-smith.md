# Joseph Smith

Founding prophet of [The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints](/notes/the-church-of-jesus-christ-of-latter-day-saints) and the central figure Kyle reads across his Mormon-history shelf. The richest portrait comes from [Richard Lyman Bushman](/notes/richard-lyman-bushman)'s [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling), which Kyle organizes around three pillars — [Profile of a Prophet](/notes/profile-of-a-prophet), [City Building](/notes/city-building), and [Gods in Embryo](/notes/gods-in-embryo) — and reads with one tension held in view: prophets are fallible humans operating in fallen systems, and the institution they build has to be evaluated on that basis rather than against a perfect ideal. The book's notes register Joseph as a man who "could have built a railroad or governed a state," whose storytelling was "oracular rather than argumentative" ("thus saith the Lord" rather than making the case — [Truman G. Madsen](/notes/truman-g-madsen)), who equipped followers to seek their own revelation rather than demand blind obedience, and who was not protected from failure ("not twenty men in Kirtland believed Joseph was a prophet" by June 1837).

[City Building](/notes/city-building) is the through-line Kyle returns to most: "we are a city-building people," Joseph building cities "after the order of heaven," and the prophet-vs-democracy tension — using authority "to return power to illiterate men" while warning that an unjust majority calls for "the wisest & best Laws" rather than raw vox populi (Mosiah 29). [Gods in Embryo](/notes/gods-in-embryo) supplies the metaphysics: divine potential, salvation paced by [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) ("a man is saved no faster than he gets knowledge"), and the imperative to act, not merely know. [American Zion](/books/american-zion) extends the institutional story past his death — his lack of a clear succession plan, [Sidney Rigdon](/notes/sidney-rigdon)'s failed claim, and [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s rise — while noting Smith "offered an anchor to people who felt themselves at sea," even as his original vision later splintered into hundreds of movements. [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins) supplies the skeptical counter-reading ([Grant H. Palmer](/notes/grant-h-palmer) reconstructing his translation method and shifting godhead theology), and [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) supplies the devotional frame — "by proving contraries, truth is made manifest," the grove, and acting with "real intent."

**Context:** Joseph Smith (1805–1844) founded the Latter Day Saint movement, dictated the Book of Mormon, organized the church in 1830, and led settlements culminating in Nauvoo, Illinois, before being killed by a mob in Carthage Jail at age 38.
