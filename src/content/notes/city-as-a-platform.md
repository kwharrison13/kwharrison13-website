# City as a Platform

"City as a Platform" enters Kyle's notes through his May 2018 networking call with [Michael Mulquin](/notes/michael-mulquin) of [IS Communications](/notes/is-communications), a smart-city standards expert active across ISO, IEC, ITU, and the TM Forum. Mulquin pointed to the TM Forum's "City as a Platform" manifesto as one of his two key frameworks (alongside the Smart City Maturity Model). As he described it, the manifesto is about how a city can adopt the platform model that businesses like Airbnb and Uber use — bringing together people who need something with people who have something to offer. Applied to cities, the central asset is data: city governments increasingly recognize that the thing that moves a city forward is the gathering and utilization of data, and the platform's job is to democratize that data so that people who need it can find it, find companies with expertise in aggregating/analyzing/cleansing it and generating insights, and exchange it while keeping sensitive data secure and only available to those allowed to use it.

The concept sits inside Mulquin's larger argument that the smart-city market must move away from highly tailored per-city solutions toward massively customizable, standards-conformant ones — a city platform only works if cities can describe themselves consistently and solution providers can describe their offerings to fit. He noted the TM Forum was running catalyst projects to have 5–7 cities use a "platform canvas" to map their ecosystems, requirements, and data infrastructure in parallel, so they could learn from one another. In Kyle's wiki the idea threads into the broader [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) / [City Building](/notes/city-building) interest in how cities modernize, procure, and use data.

**Context:** The TM Forum (a telecom-industry standards body) published a "City as a Platform" manifesto promoting the idea that municipalities should operate like digital platforms — using open, interoperable data infrastructure to connect citizens, businesses, and service providers — rather than buying siloed, vendor-specific smart-city systems.
