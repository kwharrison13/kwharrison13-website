# Propaganda

Propaganda is one of the densest recurring threads in Kyle's library — the mechanism of mass control that runs from twentieth-century totalitarianism into the modern media economy. [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) makes it the book's central subject: [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) distinguishes rational propaganda (logical argument from the best available evidence) from the kind that "offers false, garbled or incomplete evidence… by the mere repetition of catchwords, by the furious denunciation of foreign or domestic scapegoats." He traces this from Hitler's demagoguery — "all effective propaganda must be confined to a few bare necessities… expressed in a few stereotyped formulas," constantly repeated — to Madison Avenue's "motivation research," the commercial "Dr. Hyde" who studies human weakness "to take advantage of their ignorance." Intellectuals, Huxley notes, are the demographic most resistant to it because they "demand evidence and are shocked by logical inconsistencies."

[The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) modernizes the idea into the television age, with [Arkady Ostrovsky](/notes/arkady-ostrovsky) describing TV propaganda as a drug — a "psychoactive agent" pumped into the nation's bloodstream that "works… that enough people want to believe it." Almost half of Russians know the Kremlin is lying about troops in Ukraine "but approves of these lies and sees them as a sign of strength." [The Assault on Reason](/books/the-assault-on-reason) gives the same diagnosis an American frame: [Al Gore](/notes/al-gore) argues that the fusion of psychologically driven public relations ([Edward Bernays](/notes/edward-bernays)'s engineering of "consent") and electronic broadcasting "led to modern propaganda," displacing print reason. [Animal Farm](/books/animal-farm) supplies the literary archetype — Squealer's revisionism and the Party slogans as instruments of control — and [Alexander Solzhenitsyn](/notes/alexander-solzhenitsyn) the moral one: the daily lie "incorporated into the state system as the vital link holding everything together."

**Context:** Propaganda — information, often biased or misleading, deployed systematically to shape mass opinion — became a field of formal study after WWI; Edward Bernays's 1928 book *Propaganda* is a foundational text linking public relations to the engineering of public consent.
