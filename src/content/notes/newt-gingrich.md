# Newt Gingrich

Across Kyle's notes, Newt Gingrich is the recurring archetype of language-as-control politics. In [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) he is "the architect of language-as-control politics," a former history professor and architect of the "Republican Revolution" who "pioneered a ruthlessly effective style of politics that fed on conspiracy theories" and sent cassette tapes to candidates who wanted to "speak like Newt." His memo "Language: A Key Mechanism of Control" supplied a vocabulary for describing Democrats — *sick, pathetic, anti-flag, traitors, radical, corrupt*. The book also frames the contrast with Romney: Gingrich "understood something visceral about Tea Party voters that Romney wouldn't realize until later," that they "weren't interested in policy" but "just wanted someone who was… going to blow everything up."

In [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) he reappears as the failure-mode marker for an electorate that can no longer evaluate expertise — Kyle tags the "I'll go with how people feel" quote as the moment where feeling displaces fact in the social contract between citizens and knowledge. Kyle's annotations explicitly cross-link the two books ("Connect to Newt Gingrich: 'I'll go with how people feel'"), treating "politics will always fetter language" as a single pattern that surfaces in both — Tom Nichols's argument about democratic epistemology made concrete in Gingrich's playbook.

**Context:** Newt Gingrich is a former U.S. Representative from Georgia who served as Speaker of the House (1995–1999) after leading Republicans to a House majority in 1994, and ran for the 2012 Republican presidential nomination against Mitt Romney.
