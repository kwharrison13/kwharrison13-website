# Print Culture

"Print culture" is the load-bearing concept in [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death), and in Kyle's notes it is the structural condition that made an [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) possible. [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) argues that eighteenth- and nineteenth-century America was "perhaps the most print-oriented culture ever to have existed," where public business "was channeled into and expressed through print, which became the model, the metaphor and the measure of all discourse." The defining property is that print demands sustained attention and logical reasoning: "in a culture dominated by print, public discourse tends to be characterized by a coherent, orderly arrangement of facts and ideas," and writers "make mistakes when they lie, contradict themselves, fail to support their generalizations." Quoting Lewis Mumford, Postman notes that "more than any other device, the printed book released people from the domination of the immediate and the local."

Kyle reads this as the historical baseline against which media decline is measured. Postman is explicit that he is "no relativist" — the epistemology created by television "is inferior to a print-based epistemology" and "dangerous and absurdist" — and that as typography moves to the periphery and television takes the center, "the seriousness, clarity and, above all, value of public discourse dangerously declines." Kyle extends the frame to the present: print culture vs. social-media culture mapped onto the original print-vs-television argument, where social media is "structured around loudness and conflict" so that lying and self-contradiction become *ideal* because agreement and disagreement both amplify reach. Print culture is also tied to its enabling technology, the [Printing Press](/notes/printing-press), and to the hope that typography "kindled" — "that the world and its manifold mysteries could at least be comprehended."

## Where this appears

- [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) — the book's core argument: print as a coherence-enforcing epistemology, its erosion by entertainment media, and Kyle's update to the social-media era.
- [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) — print culture framed as what trained citizens to manage public discourse — the structural precondition for self-government.
