# J. Robert Oppenheimer

In [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future), Oppenheimer figures throughout the Manhattan Project section as the scientific leader of the atom-bomb effort. In October 1942 — a month after Leslie Groves was appointed to run the project — Groves chose Oppenheimer to head the top-secret laboratory (Project Y) that would develop the bomb. Groves later called him "a genius… a real genius. Why, Oppenheimer knows about everything," recognizing in him a tireless leader who would get things done.

The book frames his arc as both triumph and humiliation. At the Trinity test, with the blast measured at roughly 20,000–22,000 tons, Oppenheimer reached for the Bhagavad Gita — which he had read in the original Sanskrit — and said "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." It also recounts the 1954 security-clearance saga: his closest associates (girlfriend, wife, brother, sister-in-law) had been or perhaps still were members of the Communist Party, and these facts were used to strip his clearance after a four-week hearing that began 12 April and ended 29 June 1954 — "a public act of humiliation that effectively ended his work for the government." Groves himself testified against him in the end. The book notes that in 2009, historians with access to KGB archives found Soviet intelligence had repeatedly tried to recruit Oppenheimer but failed.

**Context:** J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904–1967) was an American theoretical physicist who directed the Los Alamos Laboratory during World War II and is often called the "father of the atomic bomb."
