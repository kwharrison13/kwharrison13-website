# William Shakespeare

Shakespeare appears across the corpus less as a literary subject than as a **moral and psychological authority** the other sources lean on. In [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), he is the load-bearing absence: the Savage's entire moral vocabulary (Othello, Hamlet's "slings and arrows") "supplies the language the World State has erased" — Shakespeare stands for the depth of feeling and tragedy that a frictionless utopia must abolish. In Kyle's gospel-study That All Men May Come Unto Him, Shakespeare is cited for the *mechanism* of self-mastery: each refusal makes the next easier, because "use almost can change the stamp of nature" — "Refrain tonight; and that shall lend a kind of easiness / To the next abstinence, the next more easy." The bridle/passions metaphor in that study uses the line as evidence that habit can retrain the natural man.

In [Peter Thiel's Religion](/notes/peter-thiel-s-religion), Shakespeare is one of the three pillars of [Rene Girard](/notes/rene-girard)'s [Mimetic Theory](/notes/mimetic-theory) evidence base — alongside the Bible and novelists like [Marcel Proust](/notes/marcel-proust) — "the kinds of books and people that modern people tend to ignore." Girard reads the plays as theatres of envy and imitated desire. So across the wiki, Shakespeare functions as a touchstone for moral depth (Huxley), the trainability of human nature (the gospel study), and the imitative structure of human desire (Girard/Thiel).

**Context:** William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright and poet, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language, author of works including Hamlet, Othello, and Macbeth.

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) — the Savage's Shakespearean vocabulary as the moral language the World State has erased.
- That All Men May Come Unto Him — "use almost can change the stamp of nature" cited for the mechanism of self-mastery and habit.
- [Peter Thiel's Religion](/notes/peter-thiel-s-religion) — Shakespeare as a pillar of Girard's mimetic-theory evidence base (envy, imitated desire).
