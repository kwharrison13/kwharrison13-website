# Shared Mobility

Shared mobility enters Kyle's corpus through [Adam Cohen](/notes/adam-cohen), described as a "shared-mobility researcher and consultant" at [UC Berkeley](/notes/uc-berkeley) whose time is largely spent on shared mobility and the future of mobility (June 1st, 2018 — Adam Cohen (UC Berkeley)). The grounding case is Cohen's work leading the independent national evaluation of the USDOT [Mobility-on-Demand](/notes/mobility-on-demand) "sandbox" — an $8M federal grant program testing eleven distinct new-mobility projects — and the policy framework his team developed to help communities think about these issues.

The throughline in Kyle's notes is that the decisive variable for shared mobility is not the vehicles or the technology layered on top but [Land Use](/notes/land-use) — walkable communities, street-grid design, and the future of parking. Cohen is contrarian on [Autonomous Vehicles](/notes/autonomous-vehicles) hype on exactly these grounds, arguing that built environment and policy (not the cars) drive mobility outcomes. The concept sits inside the broader [Transportation](/notes/transportation) cluster of people Kyle has met around mobility, and inside the #[The New City](/notes/the-new-city) thread on how cities modernize.

**Context:** "Shared mobility" is the umbrella term for transportation services shared among users — carsharing, bikesharing, ridehailing/ridesourcing, microtransit, scooters, and pooled trips — as opposed to privately owned single-occupancy vehicles. It is often discussed alongside Mobility-on-Demand and Mobility-as-a-Service as part of the post-2010s reshaping of urban transportation.
