# Philippe Laffont

Philippe Laffont is the founder of [Coatue](/notes/coatue), the technology-focused crossover firm where Kyle worked. In Kyle's notes he is the central figure in the [Inside the Rise and Fall of Coatue's Quant Fund](/notes/inside-the-rise-and-fall-of-coatue-s-quant-fund) postmortem, where Business Insider frames Coatue's failed quant fund (>$350M raised in early 2019, wound down within 15 months) as less a model failure than a buy-in failure at the top. Laffont — "a stellar stock-picker with no formal quant training" — second-guessed signals, scaled strategies up and down on his own wishes rather than a quantitative rationale, and fixated on individual losing positions even when the portfolio netted positive. The article's thesis is his refusal "to let a quant fund be a quant fund": "He wanted to make it more and more like a hedge fund." Some sources suggested what he actually wanted was a "robo-advisor… but with Laffont's investing DNA as the central algorithm" — to automate the fundamental process that made him rich.

He called the data effort "existential" ("if we don't get this bet right, we won't be around in five years," May 2019), but the breadwinner — a dollar-neutral credit-card data strategy run by 23-year-old data-science head [Alex Izydorczyk](/notes/alex-izydorczyk) — produced double-digit returns in 2018 only to be arbitraged away to ~2% in 2019 as larger funds caught on. Kyle files Laffont's resistance as the cautionary anchor of [Data in Investing](/notes/data-in-investing): data infrastructure fails without genuine buy-in at the top, and a stock-picker's conviction-driven allocation instincts clash with a systematic mandate.

**Context:** Philippe Laffont founded Coatue Management in 1999, a "Tiger Cub" hedge fund (Laffont worked at Julian Robertson's Tiger Management) that later expanded into venture and growth investing. (General background; Kyle's notes attest the quant-fund account specifically.)
