# Asynchronous Communication

The central operational shift in [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required): moving "from synchronous to asynchronous collaboration." As the book puts it, with a distributed workforce "not only do we not have to be in the same spot to work together, we also don't have to work at the same time to work together." Relaxing fixed workday hours accommodates early birds, night owls, and parents alike; the authors urge readers to "release yourself from the 9am-to-5pm mentality" because "it's the work — not the clock — that matters."

The book ties async work tightly to [Writing](/notes/writing): because so much coordination happens in text rather than in real-time conversation, written discipline becomes the core skill of [Remote Work](/notes/remote-work) — "few people are born with an innate talent for writing; most get better through practice." This is the same thread that the [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) page picks up under the heading [Async Communication](/notes/async-communication), reframing written, asynchronous work as a form of open knowledge-sharing.

**Context:** Asynchronous communication is the practice of collaborating without requiring participants to be present at the same time — exchanging written messages, documents, and recordings that recipients act on when convenient. It is a defining feature of distributed and remote-first organizations, especially those spanning multiple time zones.
