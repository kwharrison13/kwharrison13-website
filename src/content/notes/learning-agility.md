# Learning Agility

The ability to learn new things quickly, framed in [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) as the durable survival skill of the modern workforce. The book's central statistic — roughly 65% of today's under-fives are headed for jobs and careers that don't yet exist — drives the argument that the lasting advantage is learning faster than the competition, not mastering any fixed job description. [Hoffer's](/notes/eric-hoffer) line crystallizes the thesis: "In times of change learners inherit the earth; while the learned find themselves beautifully equipped for a world that no longer exists."

In the book's framing, learning agility sits alongside [Continuous Learning](/notes/continuous-learning), collaboration, perseverance, curiosity, and self-reflection as the vital skill set for success — "skills are currency in [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy)." It connects to [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection) (reflecting on one's own work, in [Peter Drucker](/notes/peter-drucker)'s "follow effective action with quiet reflection" sense) and to [Self-Directed Learning](/notes/self-directed-learning) as the practice that keeps a person competitive in a rapidly changing world.

**Context:** "Learning agility" is an established talent-management term — popularized in leadership and HR research — for the capacity and willingness to learn from experience and apply those lessons to new, unfamiliar situations. It is widely used as a predictor of leadership potential.
