# Work Ethic

A trait Kyle tracks across very different figures. In [Elon Musk — Tesla, SpaceX, and the Quest for a Fantastic Future](/books/elon-musk-tesla-spacex-and-the-quest-for-a-fantastic-future), [Ashlee Vance](/notes/ashlee-vance) renders Musk's ethic as near-total: "you should be working as hard as you can" because "life is short," and an early-life quote that struck the author as one of the most unusual things he'd heard — "If there was a way that I could not eat, so I could work more, I would not eat." Vance's sources ([JB Straubel](/notes/jb-straubel) among them) repeatedly cite it alongside Musk's [Conviction](/notes/conviction) and [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking).

[Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) frames the same trait in a religious key — "get on your knees… then get on your feet and go to work." Hinckley admired work ethic and was described by some as a workaholic (though he rejected the label); his daughter Kathy traces it to duty rather than ambition: "you do the job you're asked to do, and when that job is over, you do the next job," explicitly decoupled from power or position.

Against these two celebrations, [Leslie Knope](/notes/leslie-knope) marks a cultural reversal: in the [Great Softening](/essays/the-hardening-of-the-great-softening) research, "Gen Z hates Leslie Knope" — the shift from celebrating her earnest overwork to recoiling from it is read as a generational change in attitudes toward effort, possibly a symptom of the "great softening" itself.

## Where this appears

- [Elon Musk — Tesla, SpaceX, and the Quest for a Fantastic Future](/books/elon-musk-tesla-spacex-and-the-quest-for-a-fantastic-future) — Musk's extreme, life-is-short ethic, attested by Vance's sources.
- [Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) — duty-framed work ethic ("get on your feet and go to work"); admired, not equated with ambition.
- [Leslie Knope](/notes/leslie-knope) — invoked as a cultural barometer where the celebration of overwork flips into rejection.
