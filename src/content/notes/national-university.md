# National University

In Kyle's notes on the [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) biography, "national university" refers not to a present-day institution but to a flagship proposal in Adams's 1825 inaugural message to Congress. Adams listed the establishment of a national university first among his proposals for federally fostered cultural and scientific institutions, alongside academies "for the propagation of knowledge, especially in 'geographical and astronomical science.'" The idea expressed his conviction that national greatness was measured "not merely in wealth and military power but in enlightenment and understanding," and that "God himself had enjoined upon society the obligation to improve man's 'moral, political, intellectual' condition."

The notes use the national-university proposal as an emblem of Adams's far-sighted, ahead-of-his-time governing temperament. He knew the Jeffersonian- and Jacksonian-dominated Congress "would never endorse a national university," yet insisted on including it, telling Henry Clay he was looking "to a practicability of a longer range than a simple session of Congress" — making the strongest possible case for what the nation needed and waiting for public opinion to catch up. As such it reads less as a company entity than as a policy idea, tightly bound to themes of [Education](/notes/education) on the [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) page.

**Context:** A federally chartered "national university" in Washington was a recurring early-republic proposal — championed by George Washington and later John Quincy Adams — that was never enacted by Congress.
