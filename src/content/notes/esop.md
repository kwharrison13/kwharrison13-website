# ESOP

An **Employee Stock Ownership Plan** — the recurring structural device in Kyle's notes for sharing the gains of an enterprise with the people who do its work. It shows up in two registers. The **practitioner** register comes through [John Hoffmire](/notes/john-hoffmire), the Saïd Business School (Oxford) faculty member and former ESOP investment banker who ran his own firm helping employees buy and manage ~$2.2B of ESOP stock and led the first sale of microfinance-bank shares to an ESOP (K-REP, Kenya). On the [Investment Banking](/notes/investment-banking) page Hoffmire is "the one practitioner among the sources," a use of the trade aimed at employee ownership rather than dealmaking for its own sake. He sits inside the broader [Employee Ownership](/notes/employee-ownership) thread, which pairs ESOPs with worker cooperatives and startup option pools as ways workers can hold equity in the firm.

The **idea** register comes through Kyle's book reading, where the ESOP becomes a template he scales *up*. In [The War on Normal People](/books/the-war-on-normal-people), [Andrew Yang](/notes/andrew-yang)'s Freedom Dividend is read as "an ESOP for the country" — a VAT-funded ownership stake giving citizens "an honest stake in society," and Kyle's own margin note pushes it further: "What if there was a way to structure an ESOP but for towns? Could citizens yield the benefits of the success of their towns?" The same instinct surfaces in [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis), where his "purpose-driven employment" what-if — selecting employees by character and commitment, paying more in exchange for more buy-in — explicitly invokes "ESOPs and the old concept of 'company families'." The concept was originally seeded from a June 29th, 2021 Roam Quick Capture reading log, where Kyle saved an NCEO ESOP primer alongside Axios/HBR pieces on retail labor and Main Street.

**Context:** An ESOP is a U.S. qualified retirement plan that buys and holds company stock on behalf of employees, giving the workforce a beneficial ownership stake (often used to facilitate owner succession in closely held firms). Advocates argue it aligns incentives, builds worker wealth, and distributes the gains of enterprise more broadly than absentee shareholding.
