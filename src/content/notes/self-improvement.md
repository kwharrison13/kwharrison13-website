# Self Improvement

In [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life), self-improvement reads as deliberate practice rather than aspiration — the method, not the pose. The page flags Franklin's self-taught writing method as the actual mechanism behind his prose: he read an essay, took brief notes, set them aside for days, then tried to recreate the essay in his own words and compared his version to the original (sometimes jumbling the notes so he had to reconstruct the argument's order himself). The [Thirteen Virtues](/notes/thirteen-virtues) are framed the same way — a tracked system worked "one of the beds at a time," like weeding a garden, rather than a moral aspiration held all at once. Franklin's [Junto](/notes/junto) extended the same impulse socially: a club where members "devised schemes for self-improvement" and built a network for their careers. The throughline is that betterment is a repeatable, instrumented process.

## Where this appears

- [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life) — Franklin's self-taught read-note-rebuild-compare writing method and the bed-by-bed [Thirteen Virtues](/notes/thirteen-virtues) system as the patron saints of deliberate self-betterment.
