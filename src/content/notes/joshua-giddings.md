# Joshua Giddings

Antislavery congressman who appears in Kyle's notes on the [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) biography (by [James Traub](/notes/james-traub)) as Adams's ally and successor in the congressional fight against slavery. The book records Adams overwhelming Southern confidence — Giddings overheard a colleague say "I would rather die a thousand deaths than again to encounter that old man" — and Adams writing Giddings a short poem ("I sought, and found at last — in thee"). Giddings, the notes record, would remain in office until 1859, taking over from the aging Adams the leadership of the anti-slavery movement in Congress.

**Context:** Joshua Reed Giddings (1795–1864) was a U.S. Representative from Ohio and one of the most outspoken abolitionists in antebellum Congress, closely associated with John Quincy Adams's later antislavery efforts.
