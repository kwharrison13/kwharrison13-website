# Samuel Johnson

Samuel Johnson recurs in Kyle's notes as a moral authority on knowledge and ignorance, channeled mostly through [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger). The load-bearing reference is Johnson's "ethical rule," which Munger invokes in both [Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics) and [Charlie Munger — Philanthropy Roundtable](/notes/charlie-munger-philanthropy-roundtable): that "maintenance of easily removable ignorance by a responsible officeholder was treacherous malfeasance" — Johnson's own word was "treachery." Munger extends it to thinking tools (via the Warner & Swasey machine-tool ad: "the man who needs a new machine tool and hasn't bought it is already paying for it"), an argument Kyle connects to [Personal Knowledge Management](/notes/personal-knowledge-management) and [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research). Munger also deploys Johnson for brevity in his [Charlie Munger — Harvard School Commencement Speech](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-school-commencement-speech), quoting his line on [John Milton](/notes/john-milton)'s [Paradise Lost](/notes/paradise-lost): "No one ever wished it longer."

The same sensibility shows up on the gospel side: Kyle's Topical Study page quotes Johnson on the intrinsic value of knowledge — "All knowledge is of itself of some value. There is nothing so minute or inconsiderable that I would not rather know it than not." Together the citations make Johnson a patron figure for Kyle's case that knowing is a duty and curiosity has no floor.

**Context:** Samuel Johnson (1709–1784) was an English writer, lexicographer, and critic best known for *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755) and as the subject of James Boswell's *Life of Samuel Johnson*. He is among the most quoted figures in English letters.
