# Land Use

The recurring argument across Kyle's mobility and city-building notes that land use — how land is zoned, built, and laid out — is the real lever on urban outcomes, often more than the vehicles or technology layered on top. [Adam Cohen](/notes/adam-cohen) is contrarian on autonomous-vehicle hype precisely on these grounds: he argues that "land use, built environment, walkable communities" and the policy that shapes them (not the cars) are what drive mobility outcomes, and that there are surprisingly few good policy models for laying out streets and grids well. The same lens reframes parking — a parking structure has a ~60-year lifespan, so cities should be planning adaptable, repurposable structures now even if AVs are decades away (June 1st, 2018 — Adam Cohen (UC Berkeley)).

In [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis), land use shows up as the binding constraint on housing and growth: if the housing and land-use restrictions that constrain development were removed so everyone who wanted to work in San Francisco could afford to live there, the city would see a 500% increase in jobs (New York, 800%). That book's central fix — a [Land Value Tax](/notes/land-value-tax) in place of the property tax — is itself a land-use intervention, meant to push high-priced urban land toward its "most efficient and productive use," increasing density and clustering.

## Where this appears

- [Adam Cohen](/notes/adam-cohen) — argues land use, walkable communities, and policy (not the vehicles) are the real levers on mobility
- June 1st, 2018 — Adam Cohen (UC Berkeley) — networking call on AVs, street layout, and adaptable parking as land-use problems
- [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) — land-use restrictions as the constraint on jobs/housing; land value tax as the proposed fix
