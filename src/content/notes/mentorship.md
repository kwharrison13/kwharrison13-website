# Mentorship

Mentorship recurs across Kyle's reading as the *social* mechanism by which hard-to-codify skills — management, history, leadership, character — actually transfer. [Managers Not MBAs](/books/managers-not-mbas) makes the strongest theoretical case: management is a practice, so "moving alone leaves the learning to the individual, whereas moving with mentoring turns it into a social process." Mintzberg holds up "Red Cross style" mentoring — pairing each experienced delegate with a local "counterpart" who works alongside them — as more powerful than solo stretch assignments, and laments the decline of internal mentoring as "coaches and mentors for hire" exploded. [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime) supplies the lived version: Bob Iger names Roone Arledge, Tom Murphy, and Dan Burke as the formative mentors without whom "I would not be telling this story."

[Becoming](/books/becoming) reframes mentorship as a relay and a duty: "always have three friends — one who walks ahead… one who walks beside us… and one who we reach back for," with genuine interest in someone's development mattering most "for women, for minorities, for anyone society is quick to overlook." In the Mormon-history strand, [Leonard Arrington and the Writing of Mormon History](/books/leonard-arrington-and-the-writing-of-mormon-history) presents Arrington as "the entrepreneur of history" who mentored a whole generation of scholars through letters, fellowships, "Bravo" notes, and unprompted checks from the Mormon History Trust Fund. Two grounded skeptical and operational notes round it out: [John Hoffmire](/notes/john-hoffmire) is "skeptical of mentorship at scale," arguing it mostly fails without a quid-pro-quo; and Kyle's own [Your Passion, Your Solution (YPYS)](/notes/your-passion-your-solution-ypys) venture treated mentorship as a named, testable incubator service — pairing each social venture with an experienced mentor to test whether mentorship is essential to success.
