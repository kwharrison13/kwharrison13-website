# Community

A recurring theme spanning Kyle's civic, social, and company-building reading, where "community" carries two distinct senses. In the **place-based** sense, [What I Found in a Thousand Towns](/books/what-i-found-in-a-thousand-towns) treats community as something that "grows out of positive proximity and bridging social capital rather than being created from scratch" — small-town vitality built event by event, with affordable housing and welcoming third places (a café "at the community crossroads") as levers against displacement. [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness) reads it as fracturing: Brené Brown's "big sort" of self-sorting into like-minded groups versus genuine connection to a larger shared humanity — the loneliness of having "no ideological home or community" on a divisive issue.

In the **company / network** sense, [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era) frames developer communities as a growth and insight engine ("well-built developer communities offer software companies unique insight into the minds of the people who are creating the code"), earned through content that contributes "to the greater good of engineering." [Bernard Liang](/notes/bernard-liang)'s Community Building Thesis pushes furthest — a draft thesis on building a *global, independent talent community* beyond any single firm's incentives, naming a "Focus Paradox": community leaders are incentivized to optimize for *their* community's success, so only an independent agent can optimize across the whole ecosystem. [Dan McMurtrie](/notes/dan-mcmurtrie)'s memetic model (Content → Reference → Advocacy → [Community](/notes/community)) places community as the terminal stage of audience-building, where "markets are now metagames."

## Expert Calls — Community-Management Space

Primary research from Kyle's market-mapping of the enterprise community-management and virtual-events space (LCS/JF/LS expert calls). Preserved from the Roam capture.

### Ashley Lengen — former Head of Social Media at Slack (on Bevy)

LS/JF met with Ashley Lengen, former Head of Social Media at Slack, to discuss views on Bevy and the broader community management space. Bevy served a valuable role across sales, customer success, and developer engagement by 1) coordinating with "Slack Admins" (Slack users within companies that championed the tool and drove their bottoms-up sales motion), 2) maintaining satisfaction of Slack super users at key accounts, and 3) building a thriving developer ecosystem. Lengen had a very positive experience leveraging Bevy to scale the number of events the Slack team hosted without scaling headcount in tandem, given much of their event planning was previously done manually. Slack spent >$115K on Bevy when Lengen was on the team and measured ROI primarily through forgone headcount from replacing manual event planning tasks with Bevy's tools. Lengen views the virtual events adjacency as a better fit for smaller trainings because much of the value of larger events comes from networking rather than the speakers themselves. This is part of our work to better grasp the event management space and we'll continue getting smart on names like Discourse, Commsor, and Common Room.

- **Bevy value proposition:**
    - Previously teams were very siloed and community management efforts were mostly manual. The field marketing team used Cvent and SplashThat to coordinate invites to public events.
    - Three-pronged demographic focus for Bevy:
        - "Slack Admins" — employees at companies that Slack assigned as champions, tasked to carry out the bottoms-up sales motion (onboarded new members, ensured configurations were correct, training)
        - Current Slack super users at companies — main purpose here was not sales but rather customer success and ensuring they tended to any complaints
        - Developer ecosystem — especially important for software companies building a platform like [Salesforce ($CRM)](/notes/salesforce) and [Atlassian](/notes/atlassian)
    - Bevy was used to scale the number of community chapters and grow the number of community representatives (e.g. served as a communication/collaboration tool for the core community team to work with a Slack community leader in India to hold events and build the developer community overseas)
    - ROI was viewed primarily in terms of forgone headcount in growing the community and events team — Bevy allowed Slack to scale the number of events planned without scaling the team in tandem. As usage matures, they may transition toward measuring engagement from their target "Slack Admins" and the success of their bottoms-up motion.
- **Product:** Two-pronged — 1) a tool for hosting company events and standardizing details like date and location, and 2) a repository and toolkit for growing non-company-managed events (e.g. starting a community initiative overseas led by a local advocate).
- **Post-COVID potential:** Lengen questions the value of virtual user conferences since much of the value comes from the networking and serendipity of meeting others in the same role rather than the speakers — likely better attuned for smaller trainings.
- **Competition:** Previously used Khoros, SplashThat, and Cvent, but noted none of these were designed from the start to be community-management platforms — the function was more an extension of their core business. Lithium also came up in RFPs (more forum-based). What drew them to Bevy was that it was designed with the intent in mind.
- **Pricing:** Contract of $115K–$250K. Big driver was cost of headcount and cost of sales — staff time vs. ability to scale the platform. For a platform-type software model (Salesforce, Atlassian, Slack), having a degree of community / dev buy-in / admin buy-in is a huge value prop and decider in success or failure.
- **Feature wishlist:** Tighter integration with social media tools like Sprinklr and Khoros.
- **Structured Q&A responses:**
    - *What was your use case?* — First wave of programs on Bevy focused on establishing a broader developer community to support the creation of apps for Slack. The tool was then expanded to support programs designed to engage Slack administrators and super fans on a global level.
    - *What did you do before?* — All programs were run by headquarters staff; Bevy allowed them to scale developer and other community programs much more aggressively.
    - *Most relevant alternatives?* — Cvent and Splash for similar events prior to Bevy, but they were limited in their ability to scale.
    - *Decision maker?* — On the committee that reviewed the contract; final decisions made by developer relations and IT.
    - *Measuring ROI?* — Initially viewed ROI in terms of cost savings on staff time / headcount required to run the program manually; Bevy allowed them to scale community programs much faster with far fewer staff.
    - *Missing features?* — Tighter integration with social media tools like Sprinklr and Khoros.

### Philippe Theriault — CRO of MayaData (on Commsor)

LCS/JF met with Philippe Theriault, CRO of MayaData — an open-source Kubernetes company. This is part of a series of calls mapping out the enterprise community management and virtual events space. MayaData uses [HubSpot](/notes/hubspot) as their CRM and Commsor (Bevy competitor) as their community analytics engine. The main use cases for Commsor are: 1) aggregate data from community channels (GitHub, Stack Overflow, Twitter, Slack, etc.) and correlate to identify user profiles, 2) score user profiles and feed the key users into HubSpot to enroll in marketing campaigns. Community engagement comprised a large portion of the open-source company's top of funnel. MayaData targeted active contributors within large enterprises that had already deployed the open-source software and needed support. They viewed ROI in the form of saving on headcount and could justify paying $100K+ for a community engagement tool as they scaled. Philippe views virtual events as here to stay with events becoming hybrid going forward. However he noted that in-person events tend to generate higher-quality leads given attendees have already committed time and money.

- **Community stack:** HubSpot was primary CRM; Commsor was selected over Bevy and Influitive as their enterprise analytics tool. Commsor connected with all community mediums and correlated activity to identify the same users across different accounts on GitHub, Stack Overflow, Twitter, etc.
- **Use case:** User profiles were scored based on activity, then bucketed into passive vs. active contact.
    - Notable activity: posting questions on Slack and Stack Overflow; posting comments and forks on GitHub. Also bucketed users by interest (e.g. databases).
    - Key user contacts were fed into HubSpot to enroll them into a marketing campaign. Target contacts are users within large enterprises that deployed the open-source software and need support.
    - Meetup is commonly used among the open-source community vs. Bevy across enterprises.
    - End goal is to re-engage dormant users by identifying activity and attribution (mapping contracts back to particular users or activities).
- **Pricing:** Based on community size of active users (similar to Slack). Difficulty with some pricing models (e.g. Influitive) is that not all active users have commercial potential. ROI was saving on 2 headcount — could see MayaData spend $100K+ on a community-management tool if they continued to scale.
- **Virtual events:** Open-source world is used to working async. Commonly uses Slack, Stack Overflow, and Google Lists. Sees the future of events as hybrid, though in-person generally garners higher-quality leads. Noted On24 is very legacy and an awful tool for webinars. Uses Monday.com and [Airtable](/notes/airtable) to manage projects/action items for in-person events — very inefficient.
- **Chat platforms:** Slack, [Intercom](/notes/intercom), Spectrum.chat, and Discourse — most people are already on Slack, so that is easiest.
- **Forums:** Reddit, Stack Overflow, FB, Twitter. Analytics space here is very crowded; MayaData used [Zendesk](/notes/zendesk) to aggregate forum feeds and float up the notable ones to respond to.

### Jacob Peters — Founder of Commsor

LCS/JF met with Jacob Peters, Founder of Commsor, as part of our market mapping of the enterprise community management space. Community management was historically a function reserved for only large, well-capitalized businesses that had the capacity to hire large community teams. There's been a surge in community management tools in recent years, but there are still several factors holding the category back from becoming big: 1) lack of education about how to go from 0 to 1 (CEOs recognize the importance, but community is still very amorphous to define), 2) lack of an established playbook to maintain a community (vs. B2B sales which is more formulaic), and 3) difficulty tying community back to the business and attributing direct ROI of interactions (community can flow into product decisions, top of funnel, customer success, and recruiting). The category is still very nascent and will continue to grow in total spend and size as companies start getting crisper on how to best approach community.

- **Old world:** Very few large enterprises with the ability to hire large community teams could leverage community ([Salesforce ($CRM)](/notes/salesforce), [Gainsight](/notes/gainsight)).
- **New world:** Bottoms-up, community-led adoption is the gold standard for enterprise businesses ([Twilio](/notes/twilio), [Stripe](/notes/stripe), [Lattice](/notes/lattice)). Peters views the future of community as parallel to that of data science within organizations — once siloed, then recognized as something that should sit at the center of the org, driving decision-making and flowing into every function.
- **Inertia holding back an explosion in the community-management space:**
    - Lack of education about how to go from 0 to 1 — CEOs and founders recognize the importance of community but it has an amorphous, nebulous definition.
    - Lack of a playbook to maintain a community once it starts going (vs. building a B2B sales org, which is formulaic) — not as systematic since it's constantly free-flowing.
    - Difficulty tying community back to the business and attributing direct ROI of interactions (community can flow into product decisions, top of funnel, customer success, and recruiting) — the hardest to surmount since it requires a full revamp of company culture.
- **How companies evaluate ROI:** Typically by workflow automations that reduce headcount, freeing up time for community managers to engage in more personal interactions. Though no one knows how to value community, so it's hard to quantify and is bespoke to each org.
