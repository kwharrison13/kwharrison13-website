# The Square Deal

**One-line definition:** *[George F. Johnson](/notes/george-f-johnson)'s name for the Endicott-Johnson Shoe Company's brand of "welfare capitalism" — provide enough for workers' welfare (free healthcare, cheap housing, parks, an 8-hour day) that they feel like partners and don't need a union, while the company stays consistently profitable.*

## How sources describe it

- A near-utopian company town: free or near-free libraries, parks, hospitals, golf courses, pools, theaters, 6 free carousels, $10 bank books for newborns, houses sold at a loss, and the most extensive medical plan in the country. (source: NPR [The Square Deal](https://www.npr.org/sections/money/2015/04/17/400360768/the-square-deal))
- From the EJ pamphlet: *"If you are faithful, loyal, and reliable you will earn a good living under fair conditions. You are, indeed, a part of the company. You are cared for when sick."*
- The wager: *"if you took care of your workers, you could be more competitive"* — and EJ stayed profitable "despite paying some of the highest wages in the industry."
- The other side of the coin: critics noted the benefits were engineered to make workers feel **grateful** rather than fairly compensated — *"We're making money for them."*
- [Gerald Zahavi](/notes/gerald-zahavi)'s later verdict: *"the Square Deal is an anachronism today. People don't expect much from corporations."* The essay Unions, Unions Everywhere - Research argues against accepting that as inevitable.

## Where it shows up

- Unions, Unions Everywhere - Research — the central case study; the "if every employer were a George F., we wouldn't need unions" argument.
