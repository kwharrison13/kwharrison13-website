# Remote Work

A recurring theme in Kyle's reading on cities, work, and autonomy. In [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) it does structural work in the argument: [Richard Florida](/notes/richard-florida) says the affluent return to the urban core for high-paying jobs, short commutes, and amenities — and remote work "dissolves the proximity constraint," removing the first two and leaving only amenities, which in a [COVID](/notes/covid) world (backyards, space, the outdoors) point *away* from the dense core. [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) frames it as a question of autonomy and trust: "if managers don't trust their employees to work remotely, why hire them at all?" — managers should focus on *what* gets done, not *how*. [Hillbilly Elegy](/books/hillbilly-elegy) gets Kyle's margin note that "the internet changes that," pushing against the assumption that one's local environment fully bounds aspiration.

On the company side, the Coder - Diligence thesis treats the shift to remote as one of three tailwinds (with [Developer Productivity](/notes/developer-productivity) and a security shift-left) driving development into the cloud — cloud dev environments solve the setup-and-consistency pain that remote engineers feel acutely. The theme also collects under [Future of Work](/notes/future-of-work), where Kyle filed sources like Google's 100,000-employee remote-work study.

**Context:** "Remote work" refers to employees performing their jobs outside a central office, often distributed across locations — a practice that expanded sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic and reshaped debates about cities, commuting, real estate, and management.
