# Platform Risk

The danger of building a business that depends on a platform you don't control — the load-bearing example in [The Founders](/books/the-founders) is PayPal's total dependence on eBay. Most of PayPal's payment volume flowed through eBay's marketplace, leaving the company "completely dependent on our enemy," as Amy Rowe Klement put it: "We were completely dependent on our enemy." [Reid Hoffman](/notes/reid-hoffman) captured the precariousness with a memorable line Kyle flags: "Just because someone shoots five bullets at you and misses… does not preclude the sixth one from killing you." Kyle tags this passage and the "Operation Overlord" episode directly as `#Platform Risk`.

The team's response shows how acute platform risk distorts strategy. Fearing eBay would simply "shut PayPal down, consequences be damned," Levchin, Hoffman, [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel), and Nosek seriously discussed building their own rival auction network — "Operation Overlord," named for D-Day — luring eBay's PowerSellers away using PayPal's own data. [David Sacks](/notes/david-sacks) ran the mirror-image [Pre-Mortem](/notes/pre-mortem): "If I was running eBay Payments, what would I do to kill PayPal?" The grim background data — "the majority of its payments still came from eBay" — made international expansion and, ultimately, the eBay acquisition strategic necessities rather than choices.

**Context:** "Platform risk" is a standard term in technology strategy for a company's exposure to a dominant platform (an app store, marketplace, ad network, or API) that can unilaterally change terms, fees, or access — Zynga-on-Facebook and countless App Store-dependent apps being canonical later examples of the same dynamic PayPal faced with eBay.
