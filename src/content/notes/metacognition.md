# Metacognition

**One-line definition:** *"Thinking about thinking" — the self-monitoring and regulation skills (knowing what questions to ask, running your own feedback loops, understanding your own cognition) that readers must supply to actually learn from a static text.*

## How sources describe it

- [Andy Matuschak](/notes/andy-matuschak) in [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work): the skills that distinguish readers who *do* absorb books — "What questions should I be asking? … Did I understand that? Should I re-read it? … What does it feel like to understand something? Where are my blind spots?"
- "The experimental evidence suggests that it's challenging to learn these types of skills, and that many adults lack them." Even when readers have them, "the process is quite taxing" — they must juggle the content *and* the meta-questions, which is hardest when the material is unfamiliar.
- It is **"an inherently dynamic process, evolving continuously as readers' own conceptions evolve."** Because books are static, prose can't respond to thoughts as they unfold; the reader must plan and steer their own feedback loops. Courses exist partly to shoulder this burden (scope/sequence, feedback, office hours).
  - Kyle's gloss: *knowing what questions to ask is itself a metacognitive skillset, and it's not easy for most people.*

## Where it shows up

- [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work) — Matuschak's core diagnosis of *why* books fail: they offload all the metacognitive work onto the reader.
