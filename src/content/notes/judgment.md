# Judgment

A concept that recurs across Kyle's corpus in three registers — moral, managerial, and predictive — all circling the same tension: judgment as an irreducible human faculty that measurement and rules cannot replace. In Sermon on the Mount, the study unpacks [Dallin H. Oaks](/notes/dallin-h-oaks)'s distinction between *final* judgments (forbidden) and *intermediate* judgments (directed, but only on righteous principles): avoid "never," judge guided by the Spirit, within your stewardship, with adequate information, judging situations rather than people where possible, with forgiveness as a companion. The point of Matthew 7 is "not 'do not judge' but use your agency" righteously.

In [Managers Not MBAs](/books/managers-not-mbas), [Henry Mintzberg](/notes/henry-mintzberg) argues that rankings and numerical scores "get rid of judgment" — substituting measurement for the very judgment that management requires, and trading "science over art and craft." His critique extends to corporate governance: the chief executives who reduced their duty to shareholder value "lost their sense of judgment." In [Range](/books/range), [David Epstein](/notes/david-epstein) frames good forecasters as good belief-updaters who hold "hunches lightly," and juxtaposes [Daniel Kahneman](/notes/daniel-kahneman)'s "heuristics and biases" lens on flawed expert judgment against the finding that experience often "bred confidence but not skill" in domains where patterns don't repeat.

## Where this appears

- Sermon on the Mount — Oaks's final vs. intermediate judgment framework; "use your agency" righteously rather than refusing to judge.
- [Managers Not MBAs](/books/managers-not-mbas) — Mintzberg's claim that numerical rankings strip out the judgment management depends on.
- [Range](/books/range) — judgment as belief-updating; the limits of expert judgment in "wicked" domains (Kahneman).
