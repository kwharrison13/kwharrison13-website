# Latticework

"Latticework" is [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s frame for how usable knowledge is organized: not isolated facts but a framework of theory on which a thinking person hangs experience, both direct and vicarious. As Munger puts it in *[The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment)*, "you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts… if the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in a usable form." Gaps in that latticework are what drive "man with a hammer" error, which is why a wise man keeps practicing rarely used skills outside his own discipline — to avoid creating gaps in the framework he needs for understanding new experience.

The concept is load-bearing in [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), which threads it to memory and [Expertise](/notes/expertise): memory is about retrieval strength, not storage, so the slip-box works by transferring ideas into "our latticework of theories, concepts and mental models" where they connect durably to existing knowledge. Kyle links the same idea to [Elder Klebingat](/notes/elder-klebingat)'s "mind palace" — maintaining a latticework of as much relevant information as possible while filtering out irrelevant detail. The Books 2.0 - Research notes restate the cognitive mechanism: the more connected information we already have, the easier it is to learn, because new information "docks" onto the network rather than being stored in isolation.

**Context:** The latticework-of-mental-models idea comes from Charlie Munger's widely circulated 1994 USC Business School talk "A Lesson on Elementary, Worldly Wisdom," later collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. It argues for acquiring the big models from many disciplines and arraying experience across them.
