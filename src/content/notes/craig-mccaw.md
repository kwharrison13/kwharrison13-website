# Craig McCaw

Telecom entrepreneur (McCaw Cellular) who appears in Kyle's notes as the historical ancestor of investor "storytelling" and proxy valuation. In [Bill Gurley](/notes/bill-gurley)'s 1999 essay *The Rising Importance of the Great Art of Storytelling* (cited throughout The Storytelling of Investing - Research), Bill Gates credits McCaw with convincing investors in two industries *"to pay attention to proxy valuations as opposed to traditional valuation metrics"* — arguing that price-to-earnings and price-to-cash-flow were the wrong lens for early-stage infrastructure, and offering *"homes passed"* and *"POPs"* instead. The move Kyle highlights: *"Don't look at this, look at that."*

That reframing is, in Kyle's reading, the deeper game of storytelling — not bilateral data-gathering but *changing the investor's consideration process*. McCaw is named as the 1990s predecessor of the [Community-Adjusted EBITDA](/notes/community-adjusted-ebitda) of 1999 and WeWork: the same maneuver of substituting a flattering proxy metric for conventional valuation. He sits on the cautionary, "hype"-adjacent side of Gurley's storytelling spectrum.

**Context:** Craig McCaw (b. 1949) built McCaw Cellular Communications into the largest U.S. cellular operator, sold it to AT&T in 1994 for ~$11.5 billion, and later founded Nextel and Clearwire — a pioneer of valuing wireless networks on coverage proxies ("POPs," "homes passed") before they were profitable.
