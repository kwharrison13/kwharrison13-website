# Uber

Rideshare company that recurs across Kyle's notes mostly as a reference point for venture dynamics, gig-economy labor structure, and marketplace operations rather than as an active investment. In [The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future), Uber is a recurring portfolio case the book uses to trace VC's evolving founder-friendliness and "late-stage unicorn distortions" — [Bill Gurley](/notes/bill-gurley)'s anti-SoftBank governance critiques, liquidation preferences, and limited partners exposed to "the Uber paper-gain swings." Cash—Kingmaker or Killer - Research uses Uber as a case in the fundraising-as-weapon thesis ("Why did Uber win?"), while noting the cleaner apples-to-apples comparison in food delivery is DoorDash vs. Postmates, since Uber Eats had an existing distribution network to lean on.

In Kyle's labor-economics notes, [Fissured Workplace](/notes/fissured-workplace) cites Uber (alongside FedEx, hotels, and tech companies) as a propagator of the model that pushes work out to arm's-length, non-employee contractors. On the operational side, [Josh Curry](/notes/josh-curry) — interviewed in March 30th, 2022 — Josh Curry (Uber Eats) — built out new markets for [Uber Eats](/notes/uber-eats) at Uber, and his marketplace-ops background is the lens through which Kyle tracks the company's go-to-market machinery. Uber also surfaces as a comp inside several Kyle diligence dossiers (Pave Diligence — 2021, Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021, where it's noted for fleet electrification).

**Context:** Uber Technologies is a global ride-hailing and delivery company founded in 2009; its food-delivery arm Uber Eats launched in 2014, and the company went public in 2019 ($UBER). It is a frequent reference case in debates over gig-economy labor classification and late-stage venture-capital dynamics.
