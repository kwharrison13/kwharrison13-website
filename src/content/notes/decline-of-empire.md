# Decline of Empire

"Decline of Empire" names the recurring thesis the character Lin Bao voices throughout [2034 — A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war): empires are undone not by external conquest but by internal rot. Teaching the fall of Greece's golden age, he tells his students that no invader "accomplished what the Persian fleet could not at Salamis" — instead, "the end came—as it always does—from within." Generalizing it: "Look over the ages, from Britain, to Rome, to Greece: the empire always rots from within."

In the novel this becomes a diagnosis of America specifically. The qualities Lin Bao admires in Americans — "their moral certitude, their single-minded determination, their blithe optimism" — are the very things that undermine them in crisis: "Our strengths become our weaknesses... Always." The idea overlaps with [Decadence](/notes/decadence) (success breeding the conditions of decline) and runs alongside the book's quotation of [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war) as Lin Bao's strategic frame.

**Context:** The "decline from within" reading of empire — that Rome, Britain, and others fell more to internal decay than to outside force — is a long-running thread in historiography; *2034* (by Elliot Ackerman and Admiral James Stavridis) deploys it as near-future strategic fiction.
