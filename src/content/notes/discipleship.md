# Discipleship

Across Kyle's gospel study, discipleship is defined less as belief than as enduring, whole-souled commitment. In "We Are Responsible for Our Own Learning", the rich young ruler who kept the commandments yet "went away sorrowful" supplies the model: "being a disciple means giving our whole souls to Heavenly Father and Jesus Christ. We progress in our discipleship as we identify what we lack, change, and seek to more fully follow Them." It sits alongside [Agency](/notes/agency) and [Agents Unto Themselves](/notes/agents-unto-themselves) — disciples learn by faith rather than waiting to be acted upon.

The books sharpen two facets. [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest) recasts the John 15:16 standard ("bring forth fruit... that your fruit should remain") as discipleship that endures, set against quick-baptize missionary tactics that generate inactive members rather than living disciples. [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) frames discipleship as measuring oneself by the canon — "the scriptures... are given for 'correction' and 'instruction in righteousness'" — while warning against "the call to discipleship with the desire to unload responsibility for our spiritual direction onto another." The Good Shepherd adds the following-then-leading pattern: Christ invites us first to be followers, then to be leaders, because the best way to follow better is to lead and see how you wish people would follow.

[Feed My Sheep](/notes/feed-my-sheep) ties the theme to the first and great commandment, "Do you love Him?" — framing a mission and the whole life after it as discipleship, where "you've left your nets, and you're going to feed sheep... disciples of the Lord Jesus Christ, for time and eternity."
