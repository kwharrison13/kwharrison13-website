# Muhammad Yunus

Bangladeshi economist and founder of the [Grameen](/notes/grameen) Bank, cited across Kyle's corpus as the archetype of micro-lending and social enterprise. In [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion), Yunus's village-banking model is held up — alongside [Mondragon](/notes/mondragon) and Value Added Ventures — as a contemporary instance of an economic framework genuinely distinct from both capitalism and socialism. The book recounts how Yunus, returning to Bangladesh after a U.S. Ph.D., found conventional economic theory inconsistent with the Third World's "painful struggle for survival" and responded by offering micro-loans to the poor; the Grameen model used small peer-liability lending groups, a set of 16 principles emphasizing frugality and savings, and achieved a 98% repayment record, inspiring imitators like the Lakota Fund and Chicago's Full Circle Fund.

In [MBA Degrees](/notes/mba-degrees), Yunus's Grameen Bank (together with [Wendy Kopp](/notes/wendy-kopp)'s Teach for America) is named as the inspiration for a counter-current of MBAs using the degree as a springboard into the social sector — the "Social MBA" thread.

**Context:** Muhammad Yunus (b. 1940) is a Bangladeshi economist who founded the Grameen Bank in 1983 to provide microcredit to the rural poor; he and the bank shared the 2006 Nobel Peace Prize for pioneering microfinance and "economic and social development from below."
