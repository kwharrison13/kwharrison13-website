# Paul Polman

In [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism), Polman is the lead exemplar of the counter-model to [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch)'s short-termism — a CEO who openly rejected the quarterly-earnings playbook. The book recounts that, to signal he was serious about change, he announced Unilever would stop issuing quarterly guidance, putting Wall Street on notice that he didn't intend to be measured by short-term results: "You cannot solve issues like poverty or climate change or food security with the myopic focus on quarterly reporting."

Polman is also the book's vehicle for indicting Welch directly. He dismisses Welch's celebrated returns as "accounting games" — "If he was that wonderful, he should have been in Las Vegas" — casting him as "little more than a gambler." After Unilever rebuffed a Kraft Heinz takeover, Polman later gloated that Kraft Heinz's share price was down 70 percent while Unilever's was up about 50 percent: "Some people think greed is good. But over and over it's proven that ultimately generosity is better." He embodies the [Stakeholder Capitalism](/notes/stakeholder-capitalism) alternative the book champions against Welch's shareholder-primacy legacy.

**Context:** Paul Polman is a Dutch businessman who served as CEO of Unilever from 2009 to 2019 and is a prominent advocate of sustainable, stakeholder-oriented capitalism.
