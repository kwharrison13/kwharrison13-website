# Harry Stonecipher

The central agent of cultural change in [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind)'s account of [Boeing](/notes/boeing)'s decline. A self-described protégé of [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch), Stonecipher was hired by John McDonnell as the first outsider to run [McDonnell Douglas](/notes/mcdonnell-douglas), then carried his bottom-line mindset into Boeing through the 1997 merger — what the book frames as a "reverse takeover," with the defense-contracting ethos overrunning Boeing's engineering culture. He was explicit about his intent: "When people say I changed the culture of Boeing, that was the intent, so that it's run like a business rather than a great engineering firm... people invest in a company because they want to make money."

Per the book, Stonecipher pressed Boeing to think in financial terms it had previously kept away from engineers — wanting them to make design decisions "with the cold eye of a Jack Welch or a Harry Stonecipher," and shifting incentives toward return on net assets (RONA) and free cash flow. He sold off parts-making operations Boeing had owned for decades, including a 75-year-old Wichita factory employing 7,200 people — moves that improved the financial metrics but turned in-house collaboration into outside negotiations with lawyers and procurement chains. The book ties Boeing's later hollowing-out directly to this period: total equity fell from $13 billion in 1997, before "Stonecipher and his successors started hollowing out the company," to $410 million by the end of 2018. He pushed a "team, not a family" rhetoric, and the book notes the irony that the $178 million loss he once shamed employees over in 1997 was modest against the $11.9 billion Boeing lost in 2020.

**Context:** Harry Stonecipher (1936–2025) was an American executive who ran Sundstrand and McDonnell Douglas before becoming Boeing's president and COO after the 1997 merger; he later served as Boeing CEO from 2003 to 2005, when he resigned over a relationship with a Boeing executive.
