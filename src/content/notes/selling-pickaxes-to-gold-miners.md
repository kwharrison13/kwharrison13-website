# Selling Pickaxes to Gold Miners

This is the signature framework of [How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital): rather than chase a hot trend directly, profit from the infrastructure the trend relies on. In the author's image, the gold mine is the visible part of the iceberg that everyone wants; the pickax is the part below the waterline that the trend needs to function. "You let the gold miners do all the work and then siphon profits off the market they've created" — sell the Fidget Spinner Sticker Kit while everyone sells fidget spinners; build inventory-tracking software for Amazon third-party sellers rather than competing as a seller. The book's worked example is Tucker Max's Book in a Box (later Scribe Writing): aspiring thought-leaders are the gold miners, and a $25K-minimum ghostwriting service is the pickax — a business Max found more lucrative than his own bestselling books. The page ties the framework to [Trends](/notes/trends) as one of the book's organizing concepts.

**Context:** "Selling pickaxes to gold miners" is a long-standing business adage rooted in the 1849 California Gold Rush, when merchants who sold mining supplies often profited more reliably than the prospectors. It is a common heuristic for picking "arms-dealer" or infrastructure plays into a speculative boom.
