# Anti-Platform

The core thesis Kyle drew out of [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) by [Christian Brose](/notes/christian-brose): a shift away from a military built around small numbers of large, expensive, exquisite, heavily-manned, hard-to-replace platforms (carriers, bombers, the F-35) toward large numbers of smaller, lower-cost, expendable, highly autonomous machines networked into a single battle network. In Brose's framing the right shape for the next conflict is many cheap autonomous nodes wired into a [Lattice](/notes/lattice) — treated in the book as the obvious endpoint of the argument and as [Anduril](/notes/anduril)'s whole thesis. Kyle tagged this idea repeatedly in his highlights ("Anti-platform based warfare," "Status quo: centralized power but siloed / decentralized systems").

The same logic appears from the adversary's side: China's "Assassin's Mace" and "systems destruction warfare" — the carrier-killer DF-21 anti-ship ballistic missile, antisatellite weapons, and attacks on GPS and communications — are designed to render large US platforms deaf, dumb, and blind rather than to beat them head-on (David vs. Goliath). Kyle flagged these passages as "Anti-platform" too, reading China's buildup as an applied case of the same shift the US has been slow to make. The book pairs the idea with the [Consumerization of the Enterprise](/notes/consumerization-of-the-enterprise) thread — that cheaper, more autonomous nodes are exactly where commercial tech has outpaced defense procurement.

**Context:** "Anti-platform" reflects the broader debate over the U.S. military's "revolution in military affairs" and the move from few exquisite weapons systems toward attritable, mass, autonomous, networked forces — a thesis associated with defense reformers including Christian Brose, a former staff director of the Senate Armed Services Committee and later an executive at Anduril.
