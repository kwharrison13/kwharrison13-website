# The Precariat

In Kyle's notes, "the precariat" is the term coined by British economist [Guy Standing](/notes/guy-standing) for "a growing class in low-wage, temporary work with no political voice." It enters the corpus through [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), where [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman) quotes Standing's prediction of "the emergence of a new, dangerous 'precariat' — a surging social class of people in low-wage, temporary jobs and with no political voice."

The concept sits inside the book's broader argument cluster — [Automation](/notes/automation) and the [Second Machine Age](/notes/second-machine-age) displacing workers ([Wassily Leontief](/notes/wassily-leontief)'s "humans as horses displaced by tractors"), [Bullshit Jobs](/notes/bullshit-jobs) ([David Graeber](/notes/david-graeber)), and [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) as the policy response. The precariat names the human cost that this argument is built to address: the population stranded by precarious, low-status work that the post-scarcity and basic-income proposals aim to make obsolete.

**Context:** Guy Standing's 2011 book *The Precariat: The New Dangerous Class* popularized the term, a portmanteau of "precarious" and "proletariat," describing workers lacking stable employment, occupational identity, and labor protections.
