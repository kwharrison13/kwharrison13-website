# The Fairness Principle: How the Veil of Ignorance Helps Test Fairness

**Author:** [Shane Parrish](/notes/shane-parrish)
**URL:** https://fs.blog/veil-ignorance/
**Recommended by:** [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)
**One-line:** *[John Rawls](/notes/john-rawls)'s [Veil of Ignorance](/notes/veil-of-ignorance) is a thought experiment for stress-testing any rule for fairness — if you had to design society without knowing who you'd be in it, you'd design a fair one.*

## Key claims

- **The [Veil of Ignorance](/notes/veil-of-ignorance) is a fairness test, not a literal proposal.** A component of Social Contract Theory, it asks you to design a society without knowing your own future place in it — class, sex, race, ability, personality all unknown. Because any choice could harm or benefit you, the rational choice is a fair, egalitarian one.
- **Behind the veil, cognitive biases melt away.** The hypothetical designers are rational, probabilistic thinkers with no group to stereotype (they might be in it) and no prior self to defend. Their only incentive — self-preservation — is identical to preserving the whole group.
- **They aren't blank slates.** Rawls grants them general knowledge of science, psychology, politics, and economics (comparative advantage, supply and demand) — the "minimum viable knowledge" for rational decision-making.
- **Rawls's two components of justice:** everyone gets the best possible life that harms no one else, and inequalities are permitted only when they benefit everyone.
- **Ignorance can build robust structures.** Illustrated by queen ants who band into stronger colonies before knowing which will survive, and by [Empathy](/notes/empathy) as the everyday version of the same move — imagine being the person on the receiving end.
- **The practical tool: "you split, I pick."** When contemplating a moral action, imagine you don't know whether you'll be the doer or the receiver — Michael Shermer's "Fairness Principle." Applies to writing laws (abortion, benefit cuts, offshoring) and dividing the last cookie.
- **It rhymes with the Tragedy of the Commons** ([Garrett Hardin](/notes/garrett-hardin)): behind the veil, the logical choice for a shared resource is equal parts and a cap on overuse.

## Notable quotes

> "Because people behind the Veil of Ignorance do not know who they will be in this new society, **any choice they make in structuring that society could either harm them or benefit them.** … No one wants to be part of a disadvantaged group, so the logical belief is that the Veil of Ignorance would produce a fair, egalitarian society."

> "The Fairness Principle: When contemplating a moral action, imagine that you do not know if you will be the moral doer or receiver, and when in doubt err on the side of the other person." — Michael Shermer, *The Moral Arc*

> "Behind such a veil of ignorance all individuals are simply specified as rational, free, and morally equal beings." — Spencer J. Maxcy, *Ethical School Leadership*

> "Justice will not be served until those who are unaffected are as outraged as those who are." — Benjamin Franklin

> "What is common to many is taken least care of, for all men have greater regard for what is their own than for what they possess in common with others." — Aristotle, via N. Gregory Mankiw's *Principles of Microeconomics*

> "Few will have the greatness to bend history itself, but each of us can work to change a small portion of events… each time a man stands up for an ideal… he sends forth a tiny ripple of hope." — Robert F. Kennedy

## How it connects

- [Veil of Ignorance](/notes/veil-of-ignorance) — this is the canonical explainer for the concept; the article is its primary source in the corpus.
- [John Rawls](/notes/john-rawls) — the originator (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971; *Justice as Fairness: A Restatement*).
- [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) — recommended the piece; the veil is a worldly-wisdom-style [mental model](/notes/mental-models) for inverting a decision.
- [Empathy](/notes/empathy) — Kyle's read: the veil is systematized empathy. Ties to [Fred Rogers](/notes/fred-rogers)'s "I can't imagine eating anything that had a mother" — you can't act harshly toward someone you picture with their mother.
- Tragedy of the Commons / [Garrett Hardin](/notes/garrett-hardin) — the shared-resource problem the veil would solve by mandating equal shares.
- Social Contract Theory — the tradition the veil sits in, traced back through Kant, Plato's *Crito* (Socrates), and Arthur Miller's *The Crucible* (John Proctor).
- [Alexis de Tocqueville](/notes/alexis-de-tocqueville) — opening epigraph on the self "sufficiently veiled to leave much impenetrable darkness."
