# WeWork

The office-rental giant founded by [Adam Neumann](/notes/adam-neumann) is Kyle's recurring cautionary tale about capital distorting company-building. [The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) traces the arc: [Benchmark](/notes/benchmark) invested $17M in 2012 at a sub-$100M valuation and watched the company leap from $1.5B to $5B to $10B by 2015, then go "off the rails" — a company with "nothing particularly digital about an office-rental company" and weak-at-best network effects (adding tenants on Park Avenue didn't improve the experience for tenants on Fifth). The book uses WeWork to argue the villain wasn't standard VC but nonstandard late-stage money: Benchmark provided ~1% of the $1.7B raised before Masayoshi Son's "monster check," with capital coming "overwhelmingly from nonstandard players: banks, mutual funds, and then Masayoshi Son, acting as a conduit for Arab Gulf money." ([The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future))

In Kyle's own reading and research, WeWork is the anchor case for two essays. His [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) notes invoke it directly — imagining "some high flying Silicon Valley companies (e.g. WeWork) if compensation had been tied to return on incremental cash burn vs. 'growth' without a [Cost of Capital](/notes/cost-of-capital) context." Cash—Kingmaker or Killer - Research sets WeWork opposite [Fast](/notes/fast) in its "paranoid abundance" section, and The Hits Business - Research quotes a WeWork competitor on how investors didn't want "something that just made a healthy living — they all wanted to find the next Zuckerberg." [Adam Neumann](/notes/adam-neumann) himself is treated as the archetype of "big, soulless, monolithic" company-building. ([Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Cash—Kingmaker or Killer - Research, The Hits Business - Research, [Adam Neumann](/notes/adam-neumann))

**Context:** WeWork is a co-working / flexible-office company founded in 2010 that became one of the most-valued U.S. startups before a 2019 IPO attempt collapsed amid scrutiny of its finances and governance, ousting founder Adam Neumann; it later went public via SPAC and filed for bankruptcy in 2023.
