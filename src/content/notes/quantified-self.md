# Quantified Self

In [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus), the Quantified Self movement is the everyday, consumer-facing face of the [Dataist](/notes/dataism) worldview Yuval Noah Harari critiques. Its motto — "Self-knowledge through numbers" — captures the claim that the self is "nothing but mathematical patterns," patterns so complex the human mind has no chance of understanding them. So if you want to know thyself, you should not waste time on philosophy, meditation, or psychoanalysis, but systematically collect biometric data and let algorithms analyze it and tell you who you are and what you should do.

Harari ties this to corporate data projects that make the movement infrastructural rather than a hobby. The [Google](/notes/google) Baseline Study aims to build a "perfect health" profile and flag the smallest deviations from baseline, fed by a line of wearables (Google Fit clothes, bracelets, shoes, glasses) collecting a "never-ending stream of biological data." This is Dataism's practical commandment — maximize data flow, link everything to the system — applied to the human body itself. The deeper move Harari flags is authority: outsourcing self-knowledge to algorithms is the same shift that lets Dataism "sideline humans" by moving from a homo-centric to a data-centric worldview.

**Context:** The Quantified Self is a real movement and community founded around 2007 by Wired editors Gary Wolf and Kevin Kelly, organized around tracking personal metrics (sleep, heart rate, mood, activity) through devices and apps. Its tagline is indeed "self-knowledge through numbers."
