# George Orwell

The recurring authorial reference point across Kyle's dystopia and media-criticism reading. As author of [Animal Farm](/books/animal-farm), Orwell anchors the allegory's argument that revolutions devolve into the tyranny they overthrew ("ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS"), and his *1984* supplies the "Who controls the past controls the future" machinery that surfaces throughout. In [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) he is the book's recurring lens — quoted on his 1940 review of *Mein Kampf* ("human beings… also want struggle, self-sacrifice… drums, flags, and loyalty parades") and on "truth is lies, war is peace"; Kyle annotates a passage simply "a la Orwell," and the page notes that *1984* topped bestseller lists in both Putin's Russia and Trump's America. In [The Assault on Reason](/books/the-assault-on-reason), [Al Gore](/notes/al-gore) invokes Orwell's "impudently twisting the facts" line, which Kyle reads as being about the discipline of admitting error.

The defining frame across these books is the **Orwell-vs-Huxley contrast**. [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) (via [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman)) argues Orwell was the *wrong* prophet for the West: where Orwell feared externally imposed oppression — banned books, concealed truth, a captive culture — [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) feared a trivial culture drowned in irrelevance, controlled by pleasure rather than pain. Huxley makes the same comparison himself in [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) ("In *1984* the lust for power is satisfied by inflicting pain; in *Brave New World*, by inflicting a hardly less humiliating pleasure"). Orwell also sits in the broader dystopia cluster alongside [Anthem](/books/anthem), [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), and [Metamorphosis](/books/metamorphosis), and is cited by [Mortimer Adler](/notes/mortimer-adler) in [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) as an exemplar of imaginative literature's grip on the mind.

**Context:** George Orwell (1903–1950), pen name of Eric Arthur Blair, was a British writer and journalist best known for the anti-totalitarian works *Animal Farm* (1945) and *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949), and for essays such as "Politics and the English Language."
