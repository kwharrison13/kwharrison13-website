# Planned Obsolescence

Planned obsolescence — building replacement into products to drive recurring sales — is Kyle's exhibit for how craftsmanship got traded for convenience and a building capability got lost. In the research behind It's Time To Build... But Build What - Research it anchors the "Barely Building" / "Practiced Pondering" sections: the Phoebus cartel's deliberately shortened ~1,000-hour lightbulb, set against the still-burning [Centennial Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centennial_Light), plus the "Billy Crystal sweater" and modern echoes like proprietary Apple ports and non-replaceable AirPods batteries. It's marshaled as evidence for [Marc Andreessen](/notes/marc-andreessen)'s claim that the failure to build is partly a "failure of imagination" and partly a failure to *do*.

The concept gets its positive contrast in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), Jon Gertner's history of [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs), where it's "contrasted against Bell's design-to-last-30-years ethos" — Kyle's margin note on the Labs' durable engineering reads "a far cry from today's planned obsolescence." Together the two sources frame planned obsolescence as a declension story: from institutions that engineered for permanence to a consumer economy that engineers for replacement.

**Context:** "Planned obsolescence" is the practice of designing products with an artificially limited useful life so they must be repurchased; the canonical historical example is the Phoebus cartel (1924–1939), in which major lightbulb makers colluded to cap bulb lifespans at roughly 1,000 hours.
