# Claude Shannon

The resident genius of [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs) in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), cast there as one of "a select and representative few" — alongside Mervin Kelly, Jim Fisk, William Shockley, [John Pierce](/notes/john-pierce), and William Baker — through whom Gertner tells the Labs' story. His master's thesis (~25 pages) is described as "the most influential master's thesis in history," and his 1948 "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," published in the Bell System Technical Journal, is called "the magna carta of the information age." It founded information theory on the tenet that "information can be treated very much like a physical quantity, such as mass or energy," and introduced the "bit" (a term he borrowed from colleague John Tukey for "binary digits"). Colleagues described it as "one of the great intellectual achievements of the twentieth century," and mathematicians later debated not whether Shannon was ahead of his contemporaries but whether he was twenty, thirty, or fifty years ahead.

The book paints him as temperamentally an artist inside a scientist's role: by 1955 a man who "rarely showed up on time for work," played chess, juggled and pogoed down the halls, and worked behind a closed door — a breach of Bell Labs norms tolerated only for him. His MIT advisor [Vannevar Bush](/notes/vannevar-bush) had called him "a decidedly unconventional type of youngster." In the [John Pierce](/notes/john-pierce) page he is framed as the "genius" pole of a trio — Shannon the genius, Kelly the manager, and Pierce the "instigator" steering between them — and as Pierce's close friend in the mathematics department, with Pierce remarking he wished he, rather than Shannon, had thought of information theory. Shannon is counted among the Bell Labs "Young Turks" alongside Shockley, Pierce, and Fisk.

**Context:** Claude Elwood Shannon (1916–2001) was an American mathematician and electrical engineer widely regarded as the father of information theory; his 1948 paper laid the mathematical foundations for digital communication, data compression, and error-correcting codes.
