# Knowledge

A throughline across Kyle's reading is a distinction between two kinds of knowledge — rational fact-gathering versus skill development. [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) (the Hafens) draws it sharply: "There are two very different kinds of knowledge. One involves such rational processes as gathering information and memorizing. The other kind … we might call skill development." Their key claim is that "the process of becoming Christlike is more about acquiring skills than it is about learning facts and figures" — and that "simply knowing something will not sanctify us." They lean on [Michael Polanyi](/notes/michael-polanyi)'s tacit-knowledge argument that "to learn by example is to submit to authority," picking up rules "not explicitly known to the master himself."

[Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address) frames the acquisition of knowledge as a moral duty and prizes the "learning machine" who goes "to bed every night a little wiser." Munger distinguishes meaningful, deep knowledge from "[chauffeur knowledge](/notes/chauffeur-knowledge)," and quotes [T.S. Eliot](/notes/t-s-eliot): "Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?" [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) adds the religious-intellectual angle — "an honest seeking after knowledge of all things of heaven and earth" — while warning (via the example of Aquinas weaving "very bad mechanics" into proofs) that systematizing all knowledge prematurely produces "a philosophy shot through with palpable error"; better to "start on it right now."
