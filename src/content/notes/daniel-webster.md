# Daniel Webster

The nineteenth-century American statesman and orator, who surfaces in Kyle's corpus across three very different registers. In [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death), [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) lists Webster among the great figures of American jurisprudence — alongside John Marshall, Joseph Story, James Kent, David Hoffman, and William Wirt — as "models of intellectual elegance and devotion to rationality and scholarship" whose aspiration was to "save civilization in America by 'creating a rationality for the law.'" Kyle tags that passage [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people), reading it as part of the book's print-culture argument that a literate public discourse once disciplined democracy's "undisciplined individualism."

In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) (the James Traub biography), Webster appears as a Senate contemporary, delivering the "majestic rebuttal" to Hayne over states' rights that ended "Liberty and union, now and forever, one and inseparable!" — the constitutional argument that the Union was a pact among people, not among states. Separately, Kyle's Scripture Study — Topical Study notes lean on Webster's teaching-as-engraving aphorism: "If we work upon marble, it will perish; but if we work upon immortal minds, we engrave upon those tablets something that will brighten through eternity" — used to frame the sacred accountability of teaching.

**Context:** Daniel Webster (1782–1852) was a U.S. Senator, Secretary of State, and renowned orator and constitutional lawyer, celebrated for his Senate "Second Reply to Hayne" defending the Union and for his Supreme Court advocacy in landmark cases.
