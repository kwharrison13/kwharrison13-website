# Plutarch

In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), Plutarch appears as one of the ancient historians at the curricular core of the early American "education for citizenship." The book frames the study of ancient history — [Cicero](/notes/cicero), [Tacitus](/notes/tacitus), Plutarch, [Sallust](/notes/sallust), and [Thucydides](/notes/thucydides) — not as a discrete subject like geography but as "a lens through which to examine and understand the life around you." For Adams's generation, the classics were a way of seeing, not a body of facts to memorize.

**Context:** Plutarch (c. 46–120 CE) was a Greek-born essayist and biographer whose *Parallel Lives* pairs Greek and Roman statesmen and generals to draw out moral character. The *Lives* were a foundational text for the Enlightenment and the American founders, prized for teaching virtue and statesmanship through biography.
