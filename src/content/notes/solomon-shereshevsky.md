# Solomon Shereshevsky

Russian reporter (Lurija 1987) profiled at length in *How to Take Smart Notes* as a famous case in the history of psychology: he could recount entire meetings verbatim from memory and never took notes. Psychologist Aleksandr Luria tested him extensively and found none of the usual memory limits — but the book uses him as a cautionary example: Shereshevsky's near-total recall came at the cost of being unable to forget, so he struggled to extract the *gist* of things (like the meaning of *Romeo and Juliet*) from the flood of particulars, and was often overwhelmed by involuntary associations triggered by ordinary cues. The book's takeaway is that forgetting via "active inhibition" (cf. MacLeod, 2007) is a needed filter, not just a failure of memory.
