# Speech Act Theory

In [Ted Chiang](/notes/ted-chiang)'s [Story of Your Life](/books/story-of-your-life), speech act theory is the linguistic frame that lets the heptapods' language make sense: statements like "You're under arrest," "I christen this vessel," or "I promise" are *performative* — the speaker performs the action only by uttering the words, so **saying equals doing**. For such acts, knowing in advance what will be said changes nothing; everyone at a wedding anticipates "I now pronounce you husband and wife," but the ceremony doesn't count until the minister actually says it.

Chiang's move is to make *all* heptapod language performative: instead of using language to *inform*, the heptapods use it to *actualize*. They already know what will be said in any conversation, but the conversation must still take place for that knowledge to be true — which is why every linguistic event, like every physical one in the story, parses two ways: as a transmission of information and as the realization of a plan. The narrator notes that a morphological relative of "performative" is "performance" — conversing when you already know the lines feels like performing in a play. The concept threads into the story's larger meditation on [Determinism](/notes/determinism), [Free Will](/notes/free-will), and [Teleology](/notes/teleology).

**Context:** Speech act theory, developed by philosophers J. L. Austin (*How to Do Things with Words*) and John Searle, distinguishes performative utterances — which *do* something by being said (promising, christening, declaring) — from merely descriptive ones, and is a foundational framework in the philosophy of language.
