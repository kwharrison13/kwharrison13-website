# Extraordinary Person Philosophy

A frame Kyle tags onto [Wicked](/books/wicked): the idea that moral seriousness is a quality of the unusual mind rather than a default disposition. He attaches it to the Witch's line — "is it only in youth that we can have the nerve to ask ourselves such serious questions?" — at the dinner-table argument over the nature of evil, where Avaric counters that "evil isn't doing bad things, it's feeling bad about them afterward. There's no absolute value to behavior." The Witch's posture, in contrast, is that of the figure who keeps asking the serious questions everyone else finds indigestible.

In Maguire's telling this connects to a wider pattern in the book: the most-themselves are the victims ("when the times are a crucible… those who are the most themselves are the victims"), and the people to be wary of are those "who claim that they're good, or anyway better than the rest of us." The "extraordinary person" here is less a hero than someone whose refusal to default into easy moral postures sets them apart — and exposes them.
