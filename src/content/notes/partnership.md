# Partnership

Across Kyle's notes, partnership shows up as a recurring lesson about who you bind yourself to in business and on what terms. In [The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin](/books/the-autobiography-of-benjamin-franklin), [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin) cautions that even partners with high esteem and confidence at the time of contract can fall into "little jealousies and disgusts… with ideas of inequality in the care and burden of the business," ending in "breach of friendship and of the connection, perhaps with lawsuits." The [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin) page distills the same point: Franklin was wary of disputation and of partnerships without clear, explicit terms.

The [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) supply the positive counterpart — partnership as a multiplier when you work with people you respect. [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) describes [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) as "my partner in management" with whom he is "interchangeable in business decisions," and the notes frame the Buffett–Munger partnership as "the spine" running across the Berkshire reads. The same letters extend the lesson beyond business to choosing a spouse: "Find people who will excel with or without you" (tagged [Two Complete People](/notes/two-complete-people)). The two sources together stake out the concept's poles — clear terms and shared burden prevent the failure Franklin warned of, while genuine mutual respect makes a partnership compound.

## Where this appears

- [The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin](/books/the-autobiography-of-benjamin-franklin) — Franklin's caution to "all who enter into partnerships" about jealousies, inequality of burden, and the value of clear terms
- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — the Buffett–Munger partnership as a multiplier; "Charlie Munger, my partner in management… interchangeable in business decisions"
