# LP

A **limited partner** is the capital source that funds a venture or private-equity fund — the endowments, foundations, and pension systems whose money the general partner deploys. Across Kyle's corpus, the LP shows up less as a passive check-writer than as a force whose *timeline* and *incentives* shape how venture works. [VC — An American History](/books/vc) traces the limited-partnership *structure* back to the first Silicon Valley venture LP, Draper, Gaither & Anderson (1959), and notes that its poor returns "underscored the difficulty of realizing payoffs… within the timeline of a limited partnership." [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) makes the same point human: Benchmark's partners lay awake over a 13% Uber stake because their LPs — "endowment investment officers" — had already booked the paper gains, taken bonuses, and distributed proceeds to universities and foundations.

The most pointed critique comes via [The Unusual Ambitions Of Chamath Palihapitiya](/notes/the-unusual-ambitions-of-chamath-palihapitiya), where Chamath argues LPs are "the fuel" to a broken cycle: because funds must raise every two or three years, GPs "pump up" portfolio companies with "fake IRR" and mark-to-market gains, and "LPs are the ones that are torching their money on fire, feeding this dynamic." [We're Selling Entrepreneurship Short](/notes/we-re-selling-entrepreneurship-short) shows the institutional side of that incentive — Stanford, a major LP, responded to capital imbalance by *concentrating* into existing large-fund relationships rather than diversifying. The same manager-selection and underperformance dynamics drive [Almost $14 Billion Pulled From CalPERS Equity Managers](/notes/almost-14-billion-pulled-from-calpers-equity-managers), one of the largest LPs in the world pulling money from active managers. The thread connects to Kyle's [Fees and Carry](/notes/fees-and-carry) and [Direct Investments](/notes/direct-investments) notes — the structural levers (fee load, carry, going direct) that LPs pull when the standard fund relationship disappoints.

**Context:** In a venture/PE fund's limited-partnership structure, limited partners contribute the capital and bear liability only up to their committed amount, while the general partner (the VC firm) manages the fund, makes investments, and earns management fees plus carried interest. Typical LPs include university endowments, public pension funds, foundations, sovereign wealth funds, and family offices.
