# Extremistan / Mediocristan

[Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb)'s central dichotomy in [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan), separating two kinds of randomness. In Mediocristan, no single observation can meaningfully move the total — physical quantities like weight, height, and calorie consumption live here; it is "the tyranny of the collective, the routine, the obvious, and the predicted." In Extremistan, "inequalities are such that one single observation can disproportionately impact the aggregate, or the total" — wealth, and "almost all social matters," because social quantities are informational rather than physical and "can take any value without necessitating the expenditure of energy." The practical hazard the book hammers is that people import Mediocristan tools (R-squared, Gaussian statistics) into Extremistan — likened to landing at La Guardia using a map of Atlanta "because there is nothing else." It also drives Taleb's career advice to avoid scalable professions (which "produce a large cemetery" of starving winners) and his survival rule that in Extremistan "nobody is safe" but "nobody is threatened with complete extinction either" — so your only job is to not get knocked out of the game. The dichotomy is what makes [Black Swan](/notes/black-swan) events either trivial (Mediocristan) or catastrophic (Extremistan).

**Context:** Taleb coined Extremistan and Mediocristan as intuitive labels for fat-tailed (power-law / scalable) versus thin-tailed (Gaussian / bounded) distributions, a recurring theme across his Incerto series.
