# Net Revenue Retention

Net revenue retention (NRR / net dollar retention, NDR) is the share of recurring revenue retained from existing customers — expansion net of [Churn](/notes/churn) and contraction — and across Kyle's notes it functions as the board-level SaaS metric that justifies investment in retention and customer success. [Product Led Growth: The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era) supplies the benchmarks Kyle leans on (via OpenView): the fastest-growing SaaS companies hit ~89% logo and ~109% net-dollar retention vs. 82%/90% for slower-growing peers, most churn is decided in the first week (making [Onboarding](/notes/onboarding) the key lever), and [Net Promoter Score](/notes/net-promoter-score) turns out to be barely predictive of NDR. The book also carries a sharp org lesson Kyle flags: making NRR one team's North Star let every other team off the hook — better to be retention's "champion" than its "owner."

In diligence, Planhat - Diligence treats rising attention to NRR as a category tailwind for customer-success tooling ("as SaaS matures, renewal revenue exceeds new-bookings growth; NRR is a board-level metric"), while noting the tension of low switching costs that make retention earned, not locked in. In Natural Selection Among Startups - Research Kyle generalizes it: retention is the strongest determinant of a long future because acquisition can swing 1000x but retention maybe 2x at best — the "leaky bucket" problem. It also appears in the mParticle — Diligence (2018) notes and connects to [Land and Expand](/notes/land-and-expand) as the expansion engine behind net-positive retention.

**Context:** NRR/NDR is a standard SaaS metric: revenue from a cohort of existing customers at period end (including upsell/expansion, minus downgrades and churn) divided by that cohort's revenue at period start. Above 100% means the existing base is growing even before new sales.
