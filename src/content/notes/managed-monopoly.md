# Managed Monopoly

A frame Kyle develops while reading [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) for the defense industry's failure mode: **anti-Darwinian competitive behavior** where a "big-egos, big-platforms, low-risk" equilibrium blocks lower-cost, higher-volume innovation. Where founder-led, missionary-mentality engineering (Kelly Johnson's Skunk Works) "builds for the mission," the managed-monopoly mode "builds for the buck" and is, in Kyle's gloss, "more focused on dishing out favors than achieving efficiency" — closer to a cartel than a meritocracy.

The book's marquee illustration is the B-2 award: despite Lockheed beating Northrop "across the board" on the radar range at a lower quoted price, Northrop won "on the basis of technical merit," and the program ballooned from ~$480M to $2.2B per aircraft as Air Force auditors and "official kibitzers" swarmed the project (the GE-inspector vs. warranty and bureaucracy contrasts run through Kyle's notes). Kyle reads this against [Built to Mission vs Spec](/notes/built-to-mission-vs-spec) and [Founders Mentality](/notes/founders-mentality), and flags the live risk for [Anduril](/notes/anduril): even with self-funded R&D, a defense innovator remains "at the mercy of the interplay between DOD, politics, and budget."

## Where this appears

- [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) — founder-led innovation vs. managed monopoly as the book's central tension; the B-2 award and ballooning costs as the case study; carried forward to [Anduril](/notes/anduril) and the modern defense-procurement risk.
