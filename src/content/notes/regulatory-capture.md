# Regulatory Capture

When the regulator ends up serving the entity it is supposed to police rather than the public. [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) is the wiki's anchor case: the book argues [Boeing](/notes/boeing) "co-opted regulators with heaps of money," and that for the 737 MAX the FAA was "just one more constituency to satisfy" — not a particularly forceful one. The mechanism is vivid. Early org charts for the certification system "mandated by Congress" put the FAA manager's headshot *underneath* his Boeing counterparts, with the implication that "the regulators were there in service of Boeing, not to police them." A critic in the book reduces it to a sentence: "It's basically putting the regulatory decisions into the hands of those who are being regulated." Boeing engineers even had a name for working the agency — the "drawer full of paper" technique: "If you can just inundate them with information it makes them go away."

[Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) is insistent that capture is downstream of money: "It's impossible to divorce these regulatory failings from the financial imperatives underlying them." A company "ruled by engineers who thumbed their noses at Wall Street" reinvented itself into "one of the most shareholder-friendly creatures of the market," and the regulatory relationship bent to that. The page's nearest cousin in the wiki is [Regulatory Arbitrage](/notes/regulatory-arbitrage) — but the two differ in mechanism: arbitrage profits from gaps and ambiguity *in* the rules, while capture is about hollowing out the *enforcer*. Both end in the same place when guardrails fall below minimum viable compliance.

**Context:** "Regulatory capture" is a term from economics (associated with George Stigler and the Chicago school) for the systematic tendency of regulatory agencies to come to advance the commercial interests of the industries they oversee, through revolving-door staffing, information asymmetry, and concentrated industry lobbying against diffuse public interest.

## Where this appears

- [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) — the FAA-and-Boeing 737 MAX case: regulators co-opted "with heaps of money," placed organizationally beneath Boeing, reduced to "one more constituency to satisfy"
- [Regulatory Arbitrage](/notes/regulatory-arbitrage) — sibling concept; arbitrage exploits gaps in the rules, capture neutralizes the enforcer
