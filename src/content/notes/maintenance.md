# Maintenance

Maintenance is one of Kyle's load-bearing concepts about knowledge work and open systems: the unglamorous, ongoing upkeep that everything requires but almost no one is intrinsically motivated to do. The framing comes from [Nadia Eghbal](/notes/nadia-eghbal)'s [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), where the structural tension is stated directly — "creation is an intrinsic motivator, maintenance usually requires extrinsic motivation." Software, once written, "is never really finished"; it might be feature-complete but still needs ongoing maintenance to keep running, which is why open-source projects suffer maintainer burnout and free-rider problems ([Open Source](/notes/open-source)). Kyle repeatedly pairs this with the [Kurt Vonnegut](/notes/kurt-vonnegut) line, "everybody wants to build and nobody wants to do maintenance" — the builders-vs-caretakers distinction.

The idea extends from code to knowledge. In Open-Source Knowledge - Research and [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge), maintenance is the reason open knowledge decays without active tending: we assume content has no marginal cost because platforms absorb distribution, but "it's the maintenance of content that incurs hidden costs with time and use." It also surfaces in the Historical Futurism - Research notes as the counterweight to the "builders and imagineers" — everyone wants to build, nobody wants to maintain.

**Context:** In software and infrastructure, maintenance refers to the continuous work of fixing bugs, updating dependencies, patching security, and keeping a system running after its initial creation — distinct from the more visible and rewarded work of building something new. Eghbal's *Working in Public* (2020) is a widely cited treatment of the maintenance-and-curation burden in open-source software.
