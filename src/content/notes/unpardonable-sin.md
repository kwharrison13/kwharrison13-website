# Unpardonable Sin

The Unpardonable Sin is one of the doctrines [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) expounds in The King Follett Sermon (April 7, 1844, Nauvoo). In Kyle's reading of the sermon, every other sin and blasphemy can be forgiven "either in this world or the world to come" — God has made provision so that "every spirit in the eternal world can be ferreted out and saved unless he has committed that unpardonable sin." This is the doctrinal hinge of Smith's expansive view of [Salvation of the Dead](/notes/salvation-of-the-dead): "every man who has a friend in the eternal world can save him, unless he has committed the unpardonable sin."

The sermon is precise about what the sin actually requires: **it can only be committed after full knowledge**. "He must receive the Holy Ghost, have the heavens opened unto him, and know God, and then sin against him" — to "deny Jesus Christ when the heavens have been opened unto him, and to deny the plan of salvation with his eyes open to the truth of it." It cannot be committed after the dissolution of the body, nor before one has received the Holy Ghost. Its punishment is self-inflicted: "a man is his own tormentor and his own condemner." Smith identifies it with the sin against the Holy Ghost and the fate of the sons of perdition.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint doctrine the unpardonable sin is the sin against the Holy Ghost, equated with becoming a "son of perdition" — a state reserved for those who, having received a perfect knowledge of the truth, deliberately deny and war against it. It is held to be exceedingly rare precisely because of the level of certain knowledge it presupposes.

## Where this appears

- The King Follett Sermon — Joseph Smith's teaching that all sins but one can be forgiven in this world or the next; the unpardonable sin requires full knowledge first and is self-inflicted torment
