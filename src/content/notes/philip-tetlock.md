# Philip Tetlock

Philip Tetlock is the forecasting researcher whose work anchors the [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting) and [Expertise](/notes/expertise) chapters of [Range](/books/range) and is leaned on heavily in [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise). Both books draw on his twenty-year study and on Isaiah Berlin's [Hedgehogs and Foxes](/notes/hedgehogs-and-foxes) distinction: broad, self-doubting "foxes" out-predict narrow, confident "hedgehogs." In [Range](/books/range) his findings supply some of the book's sharpest lines — "there is often a curiously inverse relationship between how well forecasters thought they were doing and how well they did," and the "perverse inverse relationship" between fame and accuracy where the most TV-featured experts are "roughly as accurate as a dart-throwing chimpanzee" (from his book *Superforecasting*). Tetlock's prescription, as both books quote it, is that "good judges are good belief updaters" — foxes adjust when surprised, hedgehogs barely budge and sometimes double down.

In [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), Tom Nichols leans on Tetlock's *Expert Political Judgment* for the same foxes-vs-hedgehogs result and, beyond it, for accountability reforms: experts maintaining a public record of how often they were right or wrong, with journals, universities, and other gatekeepers holding peers responsible for mistakes. Nichols calls Tetlock's study "one of the most important works ever written on how experts think."

**Context:** Philip E. Tetlock is a political-science and psychology professor at the University of Pennsylvania, author of *Expert Political Judgment* and co-author (with Dan Gardner) of *Superforecasting*, and a leader of the IARPA-sponsored Good Judgment Project on geopolitical forecasting.
