# Service

In Kyle's notes, **service** recurs as the idea that setting aside personal striving for the sake of others has "its own sanctifying effect" — that "a purpose greater than self is what motivates now." In [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning), Mitt Romney's French mission becomes his first taste of this: in a letter home he describes "living for others, dependent only upon God" and finding "joy when you hear of others' successes," glimpsing a future of "service to the Church… service to the world and my country."

The theme is developed most fully in What I Want My Son to Know before He Leaves on His Mission, where [James E. Faust](/notes/james-e-faust) frames missionary service as "one of life's most fulfilling experiences" precisely because it is hard — the counsel to **"forget yourself and work"** and to **love the people you serve**. The throughline across both sources is the paradox of losing the self: "If you will lose yourself in missionary service, you will find indescribable joy," and "the ultimate measure of success is not in achieving goals alone but in the service you render and the progress of others" ([Preach My Gospel](/notes/preach-my-gospel)).

## Where this appears

- [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) — Romney's French mission as the discovery that sacrifice and "living for others" carry a sanctifying, self-transcending purpose.
- What I Want My Son to Know before He Leaves on His Mission — [James E. Faust](/notes/james-e-faust)'s charge to "forget yourself in His service" and love the people, the measure of missionary success.
