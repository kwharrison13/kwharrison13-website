# Business-in-a-Box

A recurring investment thesis in Kyle's sourcing notes: a platform that packages the operating infrastructure of an entire business — front-office, mid-office, and back-office functions — so an individual operator can run their own practice or storefront without rebuilding the whole stack. In the [Behavioral Health](/notes/behavioral-health) sourcing work with [Darwin Harrison](/notes/darwin-harrison), the model is framed as outsourcing the functions of a practice (patient acquisition and insurance contracting up front; treatment delivery in the middle; billing, EHR, practice management, legal/compliance, and HR in the back) so that "people typically start with what they can wrap their arms around (e.g. EHR, CRM)" and build out from there. It is the modern, software-mediated cousin of the [Franchises](/notes/franchises) model.

The pattern shows up repeatedly across Kyle's library and deal flow. In [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound), Kyle tags Amazon's 2018 delivery-service-provider program — discounted Amazon-branded vans, uniforms, fuel, and insurance offered to small operators who still run independently — as a business-in-a-box: Amazon supplies the box, the operator supplies the labor and takes the liability. In the June 24th, 2021 — Brainstorm with Gokul Rajaram, Eric Tarczynski & Saanya Ojha sourcing session, Wonderschool is described as a "business-in-a-box for infant and toddler preschools." The June 29th, 2021 reading log pairs the concept with franchise-investing articles (the rise of the "business in a box" platform program), reinforcing the through-line: a platform absorbs the hard, repeatable infrastructure so the operator can focus on the customer-facing work.
