# Larry Fink

CEO of [BlackRock](/notes/blackrock), cited across two books as a marker of the rise of stakeholder capitalism. In [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism), Fink is the counterweight to the [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch) / "Welchism" shareholder-primacy model the book indicts: starting in 2014 he "began embracing the rhetoric of stakeholder capitalism," warning in an open letter to corporate America that too many companies had cut capital expenditure and increased debt to fund buybacks and dividends. He developed the argument in subsequent annual letters, declaring in 2018 that "society is demanding that companies, both public and private, serve a social purpose" and must benefit all stakeholders — shareholders, employees, customers, and communities — to earn BlackRock's support. (The same book notes the gap between such pledges and behavior: signatories to the Business Roundtable stakeholder statement were *more* likely to announce pandemic layoffs.)

In [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes), [Stephen Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-schwarzman)'s memoir, Fink appears as "Larry Fink of BlackRock" on the roster Schwarzman assembled for the President's Strategic and Policy Forum — alongside Jack Welch, Jamie Dimon, Mary Barra, Bob Iger, Elon Musk, and others — positioning him among the top tier of American CEOs.

**Context:** Laurence "Larry" Fink (b. 1952) co-founded BlackRock in 1988 and built it into the world's largest asset manager. His annual letters to CEOs, especially on ESG and long-term value, became influential and contentious in corporate-governance debates.

## Where this appears

- [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism) — held up as the leading corporate voice for stakeholder capitalism via his BlackRock annual letters, a counter to Welchism.
- [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes) — listed among the all-star CEO roster Schwarzman assembled for the President's Strategic and Policy Forum.
