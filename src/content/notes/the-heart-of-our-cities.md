# The Heart of Our Cities

*The Heart of Our Cities* is [Victor Gruen](/notes/victor-gruen)'s 1964 book on city-building, and in Kyle's reading it is most notable for one fact: it was the **only urban-planning book in Walt Disney's office** at the time of his death (per the Disney Archives' Dave Smith, quoted in [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city)). Sam Gennawey uses this detail to argue that Gruen's cellular theory of the city was the direct conceptual model for EPCOT — the Experimental Prototype Community of Tomorrow Walt intended to build. Walt "resonated with its message, especially with regard to solving the mobility issues," then improved on Gruen's designs with monorails, PeopleMovers, and electric vehicles — "synthesize the best of other people's ideas and create something not only better, but wholly unique."

In Kyle's [Open-Source Knowledge](/essays/open-source-knowledge) research the book anchors a thesis about what cities are *for*: Gruen's line that "one of the primary purposes for a city is to bring together many people so that, through direct communication with each other, they may exchange goods and ideas without undue loss of energy and time." That framing — cities as engines of shared ideas and sociality — connects in Kyle's notes to [Great Artists Steal](/notes/great-artists-steal) and [Idea Sex](/notes/idea-sex).

**Context:** *The Heart of Our Cities: The Urban Crisis — Diagnosis and Cure* (1964) is by Austrian-American architect Victor Gruen, who is also widely credited as the inventor of the modern enclosed shopping mall.
