# Tolerance

Across the corpus, tolerance appears as a contested virtue rather than a settled good. The fullest treatment is [Dallin H. Oaks](/notes/dallin-h-oaks)'s BYU address Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks, which pairs tolerance with truth as "twin ideas" and defines it as "a friendly and fair attitude toward unfamiliar opinions and practices." Oaks's central, counterintuitive claim is that **tolerance is harder for believers** than for relativists: belief in [Absolute Truth](/notes/absolute-truth) creates more occasions where another's ideas confront you with the challenge to tolerate, whereas [Moral Relativism](/notes/moral-relativism) makes tolerance cheap because "almost anything goes." He frames it as a "two-sided coin" — respect on one side, truth always on the other — modeled on Christ with the woman taken in adultery ("Neither do I condemn thee" *and* "Go, and sin no more"). Crucially, one tolerates persons and their *beliefs* but is "not required to respect and tolerate wrong *behavior*," and never abandons covenants: "There is no middle ground."

A very different framing comes from [Sapiens](/books/sapiens), where [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari) calls money "the apogee of human tolerance" — "more open-minded than language, state laws, cultural codes, religious beliefs and social habits," the one trust system that bridges any cultural gap "without discriminating on the basis of religion, gender, race, age or sexual orientation." So the wiki holds two poles: tolerance as a demanding moral discipline that must be held in tension with truth (Oaks), and tolerance as the impersonal, frictionless universality of money (Harari).

**Context:** Tolerance — forbearance toward beliefs or practices one disagrees with — has been a core theme in Western liberal political philosophy since the Enlightenment (Locke, Voltaire, Mill), debated as both a civic necessity and a contested limit (the "paradox of tolerance").
