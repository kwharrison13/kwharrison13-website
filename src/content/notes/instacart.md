# Instacart

In Kyle's corpus Instacart is the central worked example for the "capital dependency" critique of venture returns. Its September 2023 IPO — a $9.3B valuation, down from a 2021 private peak of $39B — anchors The Value Chain of Capital - Research via [Aswath Damodaran](/notes/aswath-damodaran)'s round-by-round return breakdown. The arithmetic: funding through the Series B (the last round to beat the S&P 500) was only ~$55.7M, after which the company burned ~$1.8B more. So the spectacular early-investor returns of [Sequoia](/notes/sequoia) and [a16z](/notes/a16z) are *dependent* on later, largely unprofitable capital — a company's terminal value rests on downstream, often money-losing rounds, and any round's "worth" is just what the next investor will pay.

That reading feeds Kyle's broader [Venture Returns](/notes/venture-returns) thesis. Damodaran's framing — "most successful VCs share more with successful traders than they do successful investors... they play the pricing game" — uses Instacart to reframe venture returns as a function of entry/exit timing and reflexive pricing rather than fundamental business judgment. On the [Sequoia](/notes/sequoia) page the same example carries the capital-dependency point: early winners' returns were structurally reliant on the unprofitable rounds that followed them.

**Context:** Instacart (legal name Maplebear Inc.) is a US grocery-delivery and pickup company founded in 2012 by Apoorva Mehta; it scaled sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic and went public on Nasdaq in September 2023.

## Notes

The capital-value-chain math Kyle ran (from the research notes):

- Total capital raised: **$2.9B**; total liabilities **$774M**; ending cash **$1.8B**; free cash flow **$387M** (FCF-positive for the first time in 2022).
- Funding through the Series B (the last round to beat the S&P 500) was only ~$55.7M; the company went on to burn ~$1.8B more. So the spectacular early-investor returns ([Sequoia](/notes/sequoia), [a16z](/notes/a16z)) are *dependent* on later, largely unprofitable capital — the core "capital dependency" point.
