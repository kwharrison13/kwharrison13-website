# Gods in Embryo

"Gods in embryo" is the third of the three pillars around which Kyle organizes his reading of [Joseph Smith: Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling) (alongside [Profile of a Prophet](/notes/profile-of-a-prophet) and [City Building](/notes/city-building)). The phrase captures Joseph Smith's King Follett doctrine that human beings carry divine potential — "gods in embryo; gods in training" — and that subjects subordinate themselves to God "in preparation for assuming that power themselves." Bushman frames it as the doctrine that "every man a god and a king fulfilled democratic aspirations to a degree unknown in any other religion," with Joseph's assertion that "all mind is susceptible of improvement" opening the possibility of limitless growth.

Kyle's notes pull two practical commitments out of the doctrine. First, you become more like God by gaining [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) — "Knowledge is power… Whatever principle of intelligence we obtain in this life will rise with us in the resurrection" ([D&C 130](/notes/dandc-130):18), so "a man is saved no faster than he gets knowledge." Second, knowing is not enough: "To know is not enough. One must do" ([Spencer W. Kimball](/notes/spencer-w-kimball)) — intelligence grows through obedience and action, the Nehemiah "arise and build" frame Kyle tags as [Be Up and Doing](/notes/be-up-and-doing).

**Context:** "Gods in embryo" is a common LDS gloss on the King Follett Sermon, Joseph Smith's 1844 funeral discourse articulating the doctrine of eternal progression — that God was once as man is, and that humans may progress toward godhood (theosis/exaltation).
