# Utopias

The hopeful, visionary end of the imagined-future spectrum that Kyle's [Historical Futurism](/essays/historical-futurism) thesis prizes. The argument running through the Historical Futurism - Research notes is that science fiction is overwhelmingly dystopian, and that this pessimism is upstream of poor public perception of technology and of slower innovation — *"to innovate, we need to be inspired to do so."* The [David Perell](/notes/david-perell) thesis Kyle quotes captures the calibrated version: *"We don't need utopias, but we do need hope and enthusiasm. That's where science fiction helps — it injects people with imagination."*

So in Kyle's usage "utopias" is less a demand for perfect imagined worlds than a marker for optimistic, aspirational futures — the "best utopian universes so far" he catalogs ([Gerard K. O'Neill](/notes/gerard-k-o-neill)'s space habitats via [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos), among others), and the counterweight to "social-science fiction" that only dramatizes failure. It connects to [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) as a cousin idea — historical futurism as "open-source imagination" — and sits in the foundational-futures → dreaming → forecasting → building feedback loop.

**Context:** A utopia (coined by Thomas More, 1516, punning Greek "no-place"/"good-place") is an imagined society of ideal arrangement; its inverse, the dystopia, dominates modern speculative fiction — the imbalance Kyle's thesis pushes against.
