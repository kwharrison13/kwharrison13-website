# Fox vs Hedgehog

The fox/hedgehog distinction is one of the mental models Kyle annotates most heavily while reading [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack). The frame — the fox knows many things, the hedgehog knows one big thing — gets used in two directions in Kyle's [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) notes. On the **hedgehog** side, Kyle tags Munger's description of Berkshire's float-and-invest engine ("If you can generate float at three percent and invest it in businesses that generate thirteen percent, that's a pretty good business") with the note *"Fox vs. Hedgehog"* — the one big, repeatable idea Berkshire executes relentlessly. On the **fox** side, Kyle pairs it with *"Jack of all trades, master of nine"*, reading Munger's [Latticework of Mental Models](/notes/latticework) / [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) mandate as the case for breadth: "you can't really say 'I'm not interested in math, physics, etc.'"

In [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) the same tension shows up around specialization: Munger argues that, just as animals flourish in narrow niches, people who specialize "and get very good because they specialize frequently find good economics they wouldn't get any other way" — a hedgehog-style argument for depth that Kyle tags alongside the [Jack of all trades, master of none](/notes/jack-of-all-trades-master-of-none) counterweight. Across the corpus the frame functions less as a binary verdict than as a recurring lens on the breadth-vs-depth question in investing and learning.

**Context:** The fox-and-hedgehog distinction comes from Isaiah Berlin's essay "The Hedgehog and the Fox," which borrows the line from the Greek poet Archilochus: "the fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing." Berlin used it to sort thinkers; it was later popularized in forecasting by Philip Tetlock, whose research found "fox"-style breadth tends to predict better than single-framework "hedgehog" conviction.

## Where this appears

- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — Kyle tags both Berkshire's float engine (hedgehog: one big repeatable idea) and Munger's multidisciplinary mandate (fox: jack of all trades) with this frame
- [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) — applied to Munger's niche-specialization argument, paired with [Jack of all trades, master of none](/notes/jack-of-all-trades-master-of-none)
