# Untruth Machines

**Author:** [Matt Levine](/notes/matt-levine)
**URL:** https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2026-07-06/untruth-machines
**One-line:** *A short item on the economics of fake Spotify streaming fraud — bots can generate roughly 1,000 fake streams for about $1, while artists earn a few dollars per 1,000 real streams, making the fraud arithmetically profitable despite being illegal.*

## Key claims

- Artists earn on the order of a few dollars per 1,000 legitimate Spotify streams, while bots can generate 1,000 fake streams for roughly $1 — meaning fraudulent streaming can return something like a 100% profit margin before accounting for legal risk.
- Spotify and U.S. federal prosecutors both actively pursue this kind of fraud; Levine notes a prior case where someone was arrested for allegedly running a fake-streaming operation.
- Levine references his own earlier column on the same topic ("the economics of that business," discussed in a 2024 piece) as background.

*Note: the captured clipping is a short excerpt (the newsletter's opening item on streaming fraud) rather than the full "Untruth Machines" newsletter, which per its own description also covers botting, memecoins, puts, private credit, emerging markets, and bowling. Only the streaming-fraud section was present in the source; the rest was not captured.*

## Notable quotes

> "The order of magnitude is that artists get a few dollars for every 1,000 times their songs are streamed on Spotify, but bots can generate fake Spotify streams at a cost of something like $1 per 1,000 streams."

> "If you can generate 1,000 streams for $1, and those 1,000 streams pay you $2, then you're in business, sort of."
