# Personal Knowledge Management

Personal Knowledge Management recurs in Kyle's notes as the practical answer to a moral argument, tagged consistently alongside [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s case for acquiring the right thinking tools. In both [Charlie Munger — Philanthropy Roundtable](/notes/charlie-munger-philanthropy-roundtable) and [Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics), Munger invokes [Samuel Johnson](/notes/samuel-johnson)'s ethical rule — that "maintenance of easily removable ignorance by a responsible officeholder was treacherous malfeasance" — and pairs it with the Warner & Swasey machine-tool ad: "The man who needs a new machine tool and hasn't bought it is already paying for it." Munger extends the rule to *thinking* tools: "If you don't have the right thinking tools, you, and the people you seek to help, are already suffering from your easily removable ignorance." Kyle files this point under both #[Personal Knowledge Management](/notes/personal-knowledge-management) and #[Roam Research](/notes/roam-research) — reading PKM as the discipline of acquiring those thinking tools, with Roam as the concrete instrument.

The concept sits in Kyle's broader cluster with [Knowledge Management](/notes/knowledge-management), [Note Taking](/notes/note-taking), and the [Zettelkasten](/notes/zettelkasten) — the conceptual ancestry of his own LLM wiki — but its distinct framing here is moral rather than mechanical: PKM as a duty to eradicate one's own easily-removable ignorance.

**Context:** "Personal Knowledge Management" (PKM) is the set of practices an individual uses to capture, organize, connect, and retrieve information for their own thinking and work — popularized by networked-note tools like Roam Research, Obsidian, and Notion and methods like the Zettelkasten.
