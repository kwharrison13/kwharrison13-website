# Public-Private Partnerships

In [Our Towns](/books/our-towns), public-private partnerships are explicitly named as a common mechanism behind comeback towns. James and Deborah Fallows observe that despite "deep dissimilarities on most issues of national politics," the leaders and voters of both Greenville (SC) and Burlington (VT) "relied steadily on public-private partnerships, in which state and city governments have taken active steering roles for corporate and philanthropic efforts." The book folds this into its "traits of a town on the rise" — occupied storefronts, river walks, public-private partnerships — under the [City Building](/notes/city-building) heading. The Greenville case shows the texture: the local business community pushed the school district toward a STEAM curriculum to nurture a future workforce, an example of the private side steering a public institution.

[David Pickeral](/notes/david-pickeral) is the corpus's practitioner of the idea: an advisor focused on "New Mobility" whose core work is "developing institutional frameworks and partnerships around smart-city / transportation efforts" — public-private partnerships as the institutional scaffolding for transportation and smart cities. For the narrower, company-specific use of the term as a [DigitalTown](/notes/digitaltown) business model, see [Public-Private Partnership](/notes/public-private-partnership).

**Context:** Public-private partnerships (PPPs / P3s) are cooperative arrangements in which government bodies and private firms (and often philanthropic actors) jointly fund, build, or steer projects — common in urban redevelopment, infrastructure, and smart-city efforts.
