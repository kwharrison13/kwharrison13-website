# Carbon Capture

Carbon Capture — capturing CO2 at the source of emission — appears in Kyle's notes as a real but **insufficient** lever in the decarbonization toolkit. In the Watershed diligence, [BloombergNEF](/notes/bloombergnef)'s [Kyle Harrison (Bloomberg)](/notes/kyle-harrison-bloomberg) frames it through a project-pipeline outlook: BloombergNEF estimates **~200M metric tons of CO2 avoided by 2030 from all carbon-capture projects** — which sounds large until you note it's "enough to avoid a third of Shell's emissions," i.e., "just not enough" against global totals. The notes distinguish carbon capture (avoiding emissions at a direct source) from **direct air capture** (removing CO2 already in the atmosphere — the first major plant came online in Iceland but is small); even bullish scenarios don't make it a silver bullet. Watershed itself maintains a carbon-capture outlook as one of the offset/removal categories its platform points customers toward.

[How To Avoid a Climate Disaster](/books/how-to-avoid-a-climate-disaster) places carbon capture (point capture and direct air capture) among the technologies on the path to net zero, consistent with [Bill Gates](/notes/bill-gates)'s broader argument that getting to zero requires deploying existing tools faster *and* inventing breakthrough ones — capture being part of the latter set, expensive and small-scale today.

**Context:** Carbon capture (CCS / point-source capture) traps CO2 at industrial emission sources before it reaches the atmosphere; it is distinct from direct air capture (DAC), which extracts CO2 already in ambient air.

## Where this appears

- Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 — BloombergNEF's ~200M-tons-by-2030 estimate, framed as too small to be a silver bullet; contrasted with direct air capture.
- [How To Avoid a Climate Disaster](/books/how-to-avoid-a-climate-disaster) — point capture and direct air capture named as part of the path to zero in Bill Gates's framework.
