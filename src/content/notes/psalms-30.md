# Psalms 30

Psalms 30 recurs in Kyle's topical gospel study through a single verse — 30:5, "weeping may endure for a night, but joy cometh in the morning" — which he uses to frame the relationship between present suffering and eventual joy. In his Diligence notes (drawn from Topical Journal #2) he reads it against the question of why God allows his children to suffer: He "will allow weeping to endure for a night in order to allow joy to come in the morning," the night of affliction being preferable to being "cast off into eternal damnation" by ease. In the Patience — Topical Study notes the same verse anchors a [Robert D. Hales](/notes/robert-d-hales) passage on waiting on the Lord through loss — the death of a spouse, financial hardship, wandering children — where "weeping may endure for a night" gives way to the dawn of "increased faith and understanding" and the choice to say "Thy will be done."

Across both pages the verse functions as Kyle's shorthand for the temporal structure of trial: suffering is bounded ("a night"), joy is promised ("the morning"), and the virtue being cultivated — whether diligence or patience — is endurance through the dark interval.

**Context:** Psalm 30 is a psalm of David, a thanksgiving for deliverance; verse 5 ("weeping may endure for a night, but joy cometh in the morning") is among its most-quoted lines on the transience of sorrow.

## Where this appears

- Diligence — 30:5 read on why God permits suffering: weeping endures for a night so that joy may come in the morning, rather than ease leading to damnation.
- Patience — Topical Study — 30:5 anchoring a [Robert D. Hales](/notes/robert-d-hales) passage on waiting upon the Lord through loss until "the dawn of our increased faith."
