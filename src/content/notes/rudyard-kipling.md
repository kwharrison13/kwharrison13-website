# Rudyard Kipling

In Kyle's notes on the [Charlie Munger — Harvard School Commencement Speech](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-school-commencement-speech), Kipling supplies the closing frame for "avoiding life's pitfalls." [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) — who the notes say has always admired the poem — excerpts Kipling's "If," whose lines about keeping your head when others lose theirs, trusting yourself amid doubt, and filling "the unforgiving minute / With sixty seconds' worth of distance run" stand as Munger's guide to temperament and resilience. The poem sits alongside the speech's other quoted touchstones (Newton on standing on the shoulders of giants, Darwin's habit of seeking disconfirming evidence) as a model for avoiding self-inflicted misery.

**Context:** Rudyard Kipling (1865–1936) was a British author and poet, winner of the 1907 Nobel Prize in Literature; his inspirational poem "If—" (1910) is among the most widely quoted poems in the English language.
