# Charles Darwin

Across Kyle's library Darwin is almost always invoked for **one idea: the discipline of actively seeking out disconfirming evidence** — "thinking in reverse." [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) is the locus: [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) holds up Darwin's working method, which "always gave priority attention to evidence tending to disconfirm whatever cherished and hard-won theory he already had," against most people who "process new and disconfirming information so that any original conclusion remains intact." [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) quotes Darwin's own "golden rule" — to make a memorandum of any fact opposed to his results "without fail and at once," because such facts "were far more apt to escape from the memory than favorable ones" — making him the book's model of [Self-Criticism](/notes/self-criticism). [Range](/books/range) frames the same trait as "actively open-minded thinking": Darwin "copied into his notes any fact or observation… contrary to a theory he was working on," relentlessly attacking his own ideas through model after model until one fit the evidence.

The motif radiates outward. [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) (Isaacson) rhymes Leonardo's willingness to abandon a flawed theory with Darwin "thinking in reverse." [The Great Mental Models Volume 1 — General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) pairs the journaling-as-self-feedback habit with Darwin and quotes him on "ignorance more often begets confidence than knowledge." [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) cites the *Origin of Species* recapitulation chapter and Darwin's "doctrines felt as facts" warning, which Kyle reads against confronting one's own assumptions ("understand what you believe to be facts and how that is viewed by those who disagree with you") — directly feeding the [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias) thread.

**Context:** Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was the English naturalist whose *On the Origin of Species* (1859) established evolution by natural selection; his notebooks and decades-long marshaling of evidence are a recurring exemplar of disciplined, self-skeptical reasoning.
