# Quorum of the Twelve

In [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand), the Quorum of the Twelve is the governance body Kyle pulls out as a case study in a distinction he calls "authority to decide vs. authority to execute." The Lord revealed in 1835 that the Twelve were to make decisions "unanimously or not at all," acting "in all righteousness, in holiness, and lowliness of heart" — so the quorum could only act as a quorum if it came together in unity. Brigham Young frames the binding force of that unanimity in the book: "If the Quorum of the Twelve do a thing, it is not in the power of two of them to rip it up... When we got the machine moving, it was not your business to stick your hands among the cogs to stop the wheel." Kyle's marginal note crystallizes the takeaway — there is authority to decide, and then there is authority to execute. The book also presents the president of the Twelve as set apart with authority to receive revelation for the quorum and everyone within its stewardship, yet unable to act alone. Kyle connects this to [Henry B. Eyring](/notes/henry-b-eyring) studying decision-making bodies as an academic and finding the Quorum genuinely abnormal, and links the theme to [Institution Building](/notes/institution-building) and [Stewardship](/notes/stewardship).

[M. Russell Ballard](/notes/m-russell-ballard) appears in the corpus as a member of the Quorum of the Twelve — the apostolic office that gives weight to his BYU devotional counsel to anchor on the [Doctrine of Christ](/notes/doctrine-of-christ).

**Context:** The Quorum of the Twelve Apostles is the second-highest governing body of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, after the First Presidency. Its members are sustained as prophets, seers, and revelators, and it leads the Church between the death of one Church President and the reorganization of the First Presidency.
