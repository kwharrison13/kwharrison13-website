# Morgan Housel

Morgan Housel is the author of [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), one of the load-bearing investing books in Kyle's corpus, and a recurring authority on the behavioral side of finance. Two of his ideas get their own concept pages. [Enough](/notes/enough) is, in Housel's telling, "the hardest financial skill" — getting the goalpost to stop moving — anchored by the [John Bogle](/notes/john-bogle) anecdote where Joseph Heller tells a hedge-fund billionaire that he has "something he will never have… enough"; the danger "gets dangerous when the taste of having more increases ambition faster than satisfaction," and the rule is "there is no reason to risk what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need." [Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital) captures Housel's flywheel: "content → trust → permanent capital → durable advantage," with [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) and [Howard Marks](/notes/howard-marks) as the worked examples — "they used content to instill trust, trust gave them permanent capital, and permanent capital gave them a massive financial advantage."

Beyond the book, Housel anchors Kyle's "storytelling of investing" thread. The [Naked Brands](/notes/naked-brands) entry includes a "Morgan Housel: Naked Brands Interview Series" where Housel argues that because information now flows freely "you can't hide misbehavior anymore," and content becomes the "Chef's Table" of asset management — building subconscious trust a legacy brand can't. In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) itself, his connections run to [The Intelligent Investor](/notes/the-intelligent-investor) (praised as wisdom but questionable as a how-to), [Thinking, Fast and Slow](/notes/thinking-fast-and-slow) (the [Jason Zweig](/notes/jason-zweig) "I have no sunk costs" anecdote), and a [Sapiens](/books/sapiens)-style argument that shared narratives are the most powerful force in the economy, underwriting [Financial Independence](/notes/financial-independence) as cash being "the oxygen of independence." Kyle cites Housel directly in his [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) margins ("connect to Morgan Housel talking about Buffett and Marks").

**Context:** Morgan Housel is a former columnist for The Motley Fool and The Wall Street Journal and a partner at the Collaborative Fund. His 2020 book *The Psychology of Money* became a bestseller for its short-essay treatment of behavior, luck, risk, and "enough" over technical financial analysis.
