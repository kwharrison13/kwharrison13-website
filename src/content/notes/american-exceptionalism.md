# American Exceptionalism

American exceptionalism — the idea that the United States holds a distinctive, exemplary role in the world — recurs across Kyle's corpus as a contested theme, sitting on the opposite pole from [American Decline](/notes/american-decline). It surfaces in the highlights of [2054](/books/2054) (where exceptionalism is described as having "devolved into an exceptional capacity to divorce themselves from reality," and "America's promise" framed around forgetting) and in Mitt Romney's [No Apology](/books/no-apology), whose central argument is a defense of America's exceptional role. Through the [Jamie Raskin](/notes/jamie-raskin) page it attaches to the broader decline-versus-exceptionalism thread, where Kyle gathers evidence on both sides of whether the United States is genuinely exceptional or hollowing out.

**Context:** American exceptionalism is the belief that the United States is qualitatively different from other nations — in its founding ideals, democratic institutions, or global mission. It is invoked both as a celebratory claim (a force for good, a model republic) and critically (as a source of self-deception or overreach), and is a frequent fault line in U.S. political debate.
