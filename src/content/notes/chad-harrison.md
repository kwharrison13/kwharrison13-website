# Chad Harrison

A source of horse-training notes that Kyle leans on to interpret Alma 38:12's command to "bridle ALL [our] passions." Across two gospel-study pages, Chad Harrison's observations supply the controlling image for the scriptural metaphor of self-mastery: that to *bridle* a passion is to guide it, not to imprison, excise, or destroy it. In Life's Gentle Helm he notes that a bridle uses a noseband exerting gentle pressure on sensitive areas of a horse's face to provide direction and control — "the point is not to become the tyrant of the horse, but to guide it" — and that a horse is a useful tool only when the rider controls where it goes. He adds that halter-breaking takes at least a year, and that the younger a horse is the more susceptible to training, though an older horse can still be trained.

In That All Men May Come Unto Him, his note extends the metaphor to the inner life: passions — love and devotion, but also hate, anger, the desire for revenge, the pull of the natural man — are tools to be "broken and trained," not given "free rein." Giving them free rein would let them carry us wherever they want, in direct opposition to the bridle's purpose. The horse-and-bridle image becomes Kyle's working frame for [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery) and discipleship throughout these reflections.

## Where this appears

- Life's Gentle Helm — his bridle/halter-breaking note is the controlling image for Alma's "bridle your passions"; bridling guides rather than tyrannizes the useful "tool."
- That All Men May Come Unto Him — his note extends the bridle metaphor to passions as tools to be broken and trained, never given "free rein."
