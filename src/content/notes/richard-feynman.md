# Richard Feynman

The Nobel-Prize physicist recurs across Kyle's corpus as a portable authority for several distinct themes. He is the load-bearing anecdote in [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) for the thesis that **[Writing](/notes/writing) is thinking, not a record of it**: when a historian called his notebooks "a record of my thinking process," Feynman protested — "No, it's not a record, not really. It's working. You have to work on paper, and this is the paper." Sönke Ahrens uses him (alongside [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)) to argue real thinking requires externalization. In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) he anchors [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s self-criticism discipline — "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself, and you're the easiest person to fool" — and in [The Great Mental Models Volume 1 — General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) he supplies the understanding-vs-rote line, "their knowledge is so fragile."

Feynman also threads through Kyle's innovation reading. In [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) he is invoked for "things on a small scale behave nothing like things on a large scale" and noted as a former MIT colleague of [William Shockley](/notes/william-shockley); in [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) he is the critic of the Institute for Advanced Study's isolation ("these poor bastards could now sit and think clearly all by themselves… nothing happens") and the source of the "There's Plenty of Room at the Bottom" talk that seeded nanotech. Finally, his line "**Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts**" appears in the Techno-Optimist Manifesto material in It's Time To Build... But Build What - Research, tagged to Kyle's [The Death of Knowledge Communities](/notes/the-death-of-knowledge-communities) theme.

**Context:** Richard Feynman (1918–1988) was an American theoretical physicist who shared the 1965 Nobel Prize for quantum electrodynamics, worked on the Manhattan Project, and is widely remembered for his gift for explanation and his investigation of the Challenger disaster.
