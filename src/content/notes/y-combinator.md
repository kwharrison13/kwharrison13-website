# Y Combinator

Y Combinator is the batch-based seed accelerator founded by [Paul Graham](/notes/paul-graham) that recurs across Kyle's reading as both a model institution and a marker of an era. [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) singles out YC's batch-based seed investing as "genuinely novel" — one of the rare cases where a firm's special *approach* (rather than its partners' status) explains most of its success — and tells the origin story of Graham's hacker-founder youth revolt against traditional VC. [Talent](/books/talent) holds up YC's hiring instincts as a case study: Jessica Livingston's "powerfully accurate gut instinct for talent" and YC's deliberate practice of always having at least one woman on its three-partner interview panel (women partners proved better at detecting deceit in founders), alongside Sam Altman's writing on founder idiosyncrasies.

In [A Message to Every Entrepreneur](/notes/a-message-to-every-entrepreneur), YC is cited as the model of the "incubator-as-university" — alongside Betaworks and Techstars, it gives founders support, wisdom, and connections rather than trivial knowledge, and the piece holds it up as what universities *should* be. Kyle's own Lowercarbon research leans on YC to mark a structural shift in startup economics: the rise of open-source tooling, cloud hosting, and the collapse of legal/financing friction "made YCombinator and its companies possible," letting tiny ragtag teams launch in weeks — the same dynamic he argues is now repeating in climate tech. YC also surfaces as a plain reference point in deal diligence (Pave Diligence — 2021, the Deepnote pre-brief) and as the publisher of the YC Startup Library in a saved tweet.

**Context:** Y Combinator is a startup accelerator founded in 2005 by [Paul Graham](/notes/paul-graham), Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell. It funds early-stage startups in batches and has backed companies including Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, and Coinbase.
