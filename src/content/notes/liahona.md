# Liahona

In Kyle's [Come, Follow Me](/notes/come-follow-me) study of 1 Nephi 16–22 — I Will Prepare the Way before You, the Liahona — the "round ball of curious workmanship" Lehi finds at his tent door (1 Nephi 16:9–10) — anchors a lesson on [Hope](/notes/hope) and [agency](/notes/anxiously-engaged): it works "according to the faith and diligence and heed which we did give unto it." A key pattern Kyle draws is *commandments then guidance* — Lehi is first commanded to take his journey, and only after he rises to obey does the Liahona appear, which Kyle compares to the "study it out in your mind, then ask" model of D&C 9:7–9. He contrasts it with Nephi's broken bow: unlike the Liahona, the bow "wasn't just going to show up — he had to go make it."

The Preach My Gospel Missionary topical study uses the Liahona as a *type and shadow* of heeding God's direction, citing Alma 37:45–46 — "the words of Christ, if we follow their course, [shall] carry us beyond this vale of sorrow… the way is prepared, and if we will look we may live forever." There it sits within the broader theme of doing the Lord's work in The Lord's Way.

**Context:** In the Book of Mormon the Liahona is a brass compass-like instrument given to the prophet Lehi to guide his family's journey through the wilderness to the promised land; it functioned only according to the faith and diligence of those who used it, and is frequently cited in Latter-day Saint teaching as a symbol of personal revelation.
