# Self-Determination Theory

Self-Determination Theory — the autonomy / competence / relatedness model from psychologists Deci and Ryan — is the organizing psychological framework of [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child). The book's central claim is that a child's sense of control is the developmental mechanism: kids who feel they can impact the world around them do better regardless of background, and a child who decides on his own whether to do his homework ends up happier, less stressed, and more capable. The book pairs this with a progression of [Intrinsic Motivation](/notes/intrinsic-motivation) over extrinsic rewards — "I am capable of doing this" → "I love this" — and warns that reversing the order produces strivers who don't actually want the thing.

In Kyle's notes the theory anchors the book's parenting thesis: [Autonomy](/notes/autonomy) takes practice, so the move is to *collaborate on decisions rather than command obedience* — "building a muscle, not checking a box." The book is read as the explicit anti-thesis to [Battle Hymn of The Tiger Mother](/books/battle-hymn-of-the-tiger-mother), which removes autonomy; here autonomy *is* the mechanism. Kyle also notes a religious overlay, where [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s "let them govern themselves" rhymes with the autonomy thesis and the LDS framing of agency.

**Context:** Self-Determination Theory (SDT) is a major framework in motivation psychology developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, proposing that humans have three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — and that intrinsic motivation and well-being flourish when those needs are met, while controlling, reward-driven environments tend to undermine them.

## Where this appears

- [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) — Deci and Ryan's three-needs model (autonomy, competence, relatedness) is the book's core organizing framework, set against the Tiger Mother approach as its anti-thesis.
