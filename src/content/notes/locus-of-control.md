# Locus of Control

Locus of control is the empirical hook of [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child): the distinction between an **internal locus of control** ("the belief that they can control their own destiny") and an **external locus of control** ("the belief that their destiny is determined by external forces"). The book reports that from 1960 to 2002, high school and college students "steadily reported lower and lower levels of internal locus of control and higher levels of external locus of control" — a generational drift the authors treat as a root cause of rising stress and declining wellbeing.

The book's prescription is to restore kids' Sense of Control: "Without a sense of control, regardless of their background, inner turmoil will take its toll. We all do better when we feel like we can impact the world around us." It cites the landmark 1970s nursing-home study in which residents given responsibility over their own lives lived longer, and argues that the kid who decides "on his own to do his homework (or not) will be happier, less stressed, and ultimately more capable." An ideal school, it adds, "provides a nearly perfect model of an internal locus of control" — teacher autonomy and student choice — yet the authors note a student's sense of control "lowers with every year they attend" as grades, test scores, and admissions push toward external validation.

**Context:** Locus of control is a concept from psychologist Julian Rotter's social-learning theory: the degree to which people believe outcomes in their lives are the result of their own actions (internal) versus outside forces like luck, fate, or powerful others (external). It is widely studied as a correlate of motivation, resilience, and mental health.
