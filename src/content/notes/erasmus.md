# Erasmus

Across Kyle's wiki, Erasmus appears as the archetype of the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) — the trans-national scholarly network that, per [Reinventing Knowledge: From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet), emerged from the Reformation, the printing press, and Europe's political fragmentation, showing that "knowledge can flourish without a fixed campus or state patron." McNeely & Wolverton credit him as "Europe's first celebrity intellectual," who built his public image in print "by carefully editing and publishing his own letters."

That same line is one of the seven core pulls Kyle drew into The State of Startup Media - Research, where Erasmus serves as the historical precedent for an individual building authority through self-published correspondence — the early-modern analog to the modern creator/operator who cultivates a public voice directly. The through-line across both uses is the same: distribution technology (print, then the internet) lets a single thinker assemble influence and a peer network without institutional sponsorship.

**Context:** Desiderius Erasmus (c. 1466–1536) was a Dutch Renaissance humanist, Catholic priest, and theologian — author of *In Praise of Folly* and a landmark Greek New Testament — and one of the most widely published authors of the early print era, famous for his vast Latin correspondence with scholars across Europe.
