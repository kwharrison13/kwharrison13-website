# Manager Selection

How a [LP](/notes/lp) chooses, retains, and terminates the external managers it allocates to — and the underperformance dynamics that govern those decisions. The grounding case is [Almost $14 Billion Pulled From CalPERS Equity Managers](/notes/almost-14-billion-pulled-from-calpers-equity-managers): [CalPERS](/notes/calpers), the largest U.S. pension at ~$380B, terminated most of its external equity managers, cutting their allocation to $5.5B from $33.6B on long-term-underperformance grounds. CIO Ben Meng framed the moves as a "renewed focus on performance and our ability to achieve our 7% assumed rate." Per the disclosure memo, over the prior five years "traditional managers have underperformed their benchmarks by 48 bps and emerging managers by 126 bps."

The episode surfaces a recurring tension in manager selection at large LPs: the trade-off between performance discipline and other mandates (here, the long-running political pressure to include more diverse money managers), and how that tolerance shifts with funding status — when CalPERS was overfunded before the financial crisis, subpar performance "could be tolerated or investment staff could look the other way."
