# Adolf Hitler

Adolf Hitler appears in Kyle's corpus primarily through [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), where [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) treats him as the case study in mass [Propaganda](/notes/propaganda) and demagoguery. Huxley reads Hitler's own writing as "remarkably clear and astute" precisely on the subject of manipulating crowds: "When he writes about the German masses and the methods he used for dominating and directing them... Nonsense gives place to sense." Citing biographer Alan Bullock's line that "Hitler was the greatest demagogue in history," Huxley argues his method was to "first move the masses and then, having pried them loose from their traditional loyalties and moralities, to impose upon them... a new authoritarian order of his own devising."

Huxley uses Hitler to make a structural point that recurs across the book: crowds suspend individual reason ("assembled in a crowd, people lose their powers of reasoning and their capacity for moral choice"), and propaganda exploits the unconscious. He notes Otto Strasser's description of Hitler as "a loud-speaker, proclaiming the most secret desires... of a whole nation," and stresses that "twenty years before Madison Avenue embarked upon 'Motivational Research,' Hitler was systematically exploring and exploiting the secret fears and hopes... of the German masses." In Kyle's notes Hitler is therefore a negative anchor for the book's central warning: democracy survives only on an informed, reason-responsive citizenry, while dictatorship "maintains itself by... appealing, not to reason... but to passion and prejudice."

**Context:** Adolf Hitler (1889–1945) was the dictator of Nazi Germany from 1933 to 1945, leader of the Nazi Party, instigator of World War II in Europe, and architect of the Holocaust. His writings and Joseph Goebbels's propaganda apparatus are widely studied as a paradigm of mass manipulation and totalitarian control.
