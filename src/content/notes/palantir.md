# Palantir

Palantir runs through Kyle's corpus in two very different registers. In the defense/American-dynamism thread it is a hero of selling cutting-edge technology to a resistant government. [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) tells the story of Palantir and SpaceX as "two California-based start-ups" whose experiences were "nearly identical": Palantir built software that could analyze vast quantities of data and surface patterns to help thwart terrorist attacks — a capability the US Army had spent years and billions failing to build — yet the Army wasn't eager to alter the status quo, so Palantir had to wage a multiyear fight and ultimately *sue its own customer* to get a fair hearing (it won, and became a multi-billion-dollar company). [Building American Dynamism](/notes/building-american-dynamism) lists Palantir among the companies "entrenched in the work and mission of government," as the intelligence example alongside SpaceX/Blue Origin (aerospace) and Anduril/Shield/Skydio (defense). Its founders sit across two wiki people pages: co-founder and CEO [Alex Karp](/notes/alex-karp) and co-founder [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel).

In the venture-critique thread, Palantir is the cautionary case. The VC Contagion - Research notes treat Palantir's "roundtripping" — booking revenue from non-government SPAC customers that Palantir had itself invested in — as the cleanest public illustration of the essay's "VC dollars all the way down" / "self-licking ice cream cone" thesis. The fragility: the SPACs Palantir invested in fell ~80% on average, so far less money came back when its "customers" were dying investments.

**Context:** Palantir Technologies (NYSE: PLTR) is a data-analytics software company founded in 2003 by Peter Thiel, Alex Karp, and others, known for its Gotham and Foundry platforms serving government/intelligence and commercial customers; it went public via direct listing in 2020.
