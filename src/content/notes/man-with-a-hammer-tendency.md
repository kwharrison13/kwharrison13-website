# Man-with-a-Hammer Tendency

The man-with-a-hammer tendency is [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s name for the cognitive bias captured by the proverb "to a man with a hammer, every problem looks like a nail" — the disposition to force every situation into the one or few tools you happen to possess. Munger names it directly in his [Charlie Munger — Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address) and offers the cure: **"If a man has a vast set of skills over multiple disciplines, he, by definition, carries multiple tools and, therefore, will limit bad cognitive effects from man-with-a-hammer tendency."** It is the bias that makes [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) not a luxury but a defense — the whole point of building a latticework of mental models is to stop reaching for the same hammer.

Within the wiki it is the misjudgment that [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias) most closely approximates: both describe a mind that distorts incoming reality to fit a prior commitment (in confirmation bias, a belief; in the hammer tendency, a tool or discipline). It belongs to Munger's catalog of the standard causes of human misjudgment.

**Context:** Man-with-a-hammer tendency appears in Munger's psychology-of-human-misjudgment framework (collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*) as a driver of single-discipline over-reach; his prescribed antidote is broad, fluency-level multidisciplinary knowledge.
