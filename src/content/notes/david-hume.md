# David Hume

David Hume appears across two of Kyle's sources as an Enlightenment touchstone. In [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written), "Hume's fork" grounds the book's reading of "self-evident" in the title sentence: Hume distinguished *synthetic* truths (contingent on empirical observation, e.g. "London is bigger than Philadelphia") from *analytic* truths (true by reason and definition, e.g. "all bachelors are unmarried"). Analytic truths are "discoverable by the mere operation of thought" — their truth is self-evident. Hume is also named among the leaders of Deism and the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) milieu of the Age of Reason (Locke, Hume, Voltaire, Rousseau, Franklin, Jefferson).

In [Why You're Christian](/notes/why-you-re-christian), Hume supplies the is/ought distinction: "science can tell us how the world *is*, not how it *ought* to be," used to argue that science cannot ground values.

**Context:** David Hume (1711–1776) was a Scottish Enlightenment philosopher, historian, and economist, central to empiricism. He is best known for his skeptical empiricism, his analysis of causation, and the "is–ought problem" (Hume's guillotine).
