# Jonathan Haidt

Haidt is one of the recurring moral-psychology anchors in Kyle's reading. [The Righteous Mind](/books/the-righteous-mind) is his book — read with explicit cross-reference to Kyle's narrative-and-cohesion thread — where Haidt develops Moral Foundations Theory and the social-intuitionist "elephant-and-rider" model, frames reasoning as an "inner lawyer" that asks "Can I believe it?" rather than "Must I believe it?" ([Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias)), and names empathy "an antidote to righteousness." [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) cites him again on the same mechanism applied to facts: "when facts conflict with our values, almost everyone finds a way to stick with their values and reject the evidence."

His co-authored [The Coddling of the American Mind](/notes/the-coddling-of-the-american-mind) (with [Greg Lukianoff](/notes/greg-lukianoff)) sits in Kyle's anti-library — its argument that three "Great Untruths" (fragility, emotional reasoning, us-vs-them thinking) are miseducating a generation. That book was recommended to Kyle by [Zach Edwards](/notes/zach-edwards) in the September 2021 call. Haidt also appears in We Need a Renaissance of Rhetoric - Research, cited for his argument on the importance of children learning to "do hard things" and the value of structured adversity that technology makes too easy to avoid.

**Context:** Jonathan Haidt is an American social psychologist (NYU Stern), author of *The Righteous Mind*, co-author of *The Coddling of the American Mind* and author of *The Anxious Generation*, known for Moral Foundations Theory and his work on moral psychology, free speech, and youth mental health.
