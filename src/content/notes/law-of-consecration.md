# Law of Consecration

A recurring doctrine in Kyle's gospel study tying personal discipleship to one's relationship with wealth and time. In the Alma 39–42 Come, Follow Me week he frames it as giving the Lord one's whole self — "time, talents, energy; might, mind, and strength" — alongside the charge to turn to the Lord with [might, mind, and strength](/notes/might-mind-and-strength).

The fullest treatment is in Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young, where [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s core claim sits underneath the doctrine: wealth is a *stewardship, not a possession* — "it all belongs to Him," lent "to see what they will do with it," to be disposed of "whether it is to give all, one-tenth, or the surplus." Stepping "to the right or to the left" of that is "an illegitimate train of business." The law thus pairs tightly with [Stewardship](/notes/stewardship) (we manage, we don't own). [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) connects it to the 19th-century economic project — the United Order, cooperation, and retrenchment — that Brigham's anti-gold, anti-extravagance sermons set in motion, with [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) as the economic counterpart volume.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology, the law of consecration is the principle of dedicating one's time, talents, and material possessions to building the kingdom of God, historically practiced through the United Order and today expressed in temple covenants and Church service.
