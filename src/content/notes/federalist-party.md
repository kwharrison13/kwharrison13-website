# Federalist Party

The early American political party referenced in the [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) biography as the party Adams broke from. New England factionalism, in the book's telling, "had reduced the Federalist Party to a disgruntled rump of secessionists" — and Adams, writing to his father, said he "no longer felt like much of a Massachusetts man" because "my system of politics more and more inclines to strengthen the Union and its government." His nationalism put him at odds with a party that had narrowed into sectional, secessionist grievance.

The biography also uses the Federalists to make a point about the founders' anti-party ideal: America's Founding Fathers were "unified in the belief that political parties were inimical to republican government," viewing them as "vehicles for selfish or sectional interests as opposed to national and collective ones." Party spirit nonetheless arose in the struggle between Federalists and Republicans — but "once the Federalists shrank into insignificance, the nation had returned to a one-party, or in effect no-party, rule" (the so-called Era of Good Feelings), a thread Kyle connects to [Two Party System](/notes/two-party-system).

**Context:** The Federalist Party (c. 1789–1820s), associated with Alexander Hamilton and John Adams, favored a strong central government and commercial/financial interests; it collapsed after the War of 1812, partly discredited by the secession-flirting Hartford Convention of 1814–15.
