# IBM

IBM appears across Kyle's notes as a stand-in for incumbent enterprise IT — both the safe default and the legacy-vendor critique. In the Starburst - Diligence, IBM shows up twice: literally, as one of the analytics "key players" in the data-platform market (alongside SAP, [Microsoft](/notes/microsoft), [Databricks](/notes/databricks), [Snowflake ($SNOW)](/notes/snowflake-snow)); and figuratively, as the GTM benchmark a young middleware company has to overcome — "how do you become the 'IBM' choice that no one gets fired for?" That second usage invokes the classic "nobody ever got fired for buying IBM" trust archetype as the bar [Starburst](/notes/starburst) must clear to win conservative enterprise buyers.

It also appears in [David Pickeral](/notes/david-pickeral)'s smart-city critique, named alongside [Microsoft](/notes/microsoft) and [Cisco](/notes/cisco) as big-tech firms he sees as wanting to *sell to* cities rather than genuinely partner with or invest in them.

**Context:** IBM (International Business Machines) is a US multinational technology company, historically dominant in enterprise hardware, software, and services, and the source of the proverbial "nobody ever got fired for buying IBM."

## Where this appears

- Starburst - Diligence — listed among data-platform key players, and invoked as the "IBM choice no one gets fired for" trust bar Starburst must clear in enterprise GTM.
- [David Pickeral](/notes/david-pickeral) — named with Microsoft and Cisco among big-tech firms he critiques for a sell-to-cities (rather than partner-with-cities) posture in smart cities.
