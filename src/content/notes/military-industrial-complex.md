# Military-Industrial Complex

The military-industrial complex runs as a connective thread between two of Kyle's defense-technology reads. In [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works), Ben Rich invokes the term to mark a singular achievement: refunding ~15% of the U-2's production cost back to the CIA was, in his telling, "probably the only instance of a cost underrun in the history of the military-industrial complex" — a wry indictment of the system's normal cost-overrun behavior.

[The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) makes it a central object of critique. Christian Brokaw traces the term to Eisenhower's 1961 Farewell Address and its expansion (via McCain) into the "military-industrial-congressional complex" — the iron triangle of the Department of Defense, Congress, and the defense industry. The book argues the complex began under Eisenhower's risk-tolerant, pick-winners approach (e.g. backing General Bernard Schriever on the ICBM), then bureaucratized in the 1960s into "cumbersome, stultifying central planning." Its sharpest claim: the biggest problem with the complex is not that it threatens liberty (Eisenhower's warning) but that it has "failed at the one job it had" — getting the nation the best technology, fast. When the Soviet threat vanished, distorted incentives drove apart the worlds of national defense and Silicon Valley.

**Context:** The "military-industrial complex" is the term President Dwight D. Eisenhower coined in his 1961 Farewell Address to warn of the entrenched relationship between the armed forces, defense contractors, and the policymakers who fund them.

## Where this appears

- [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) — Rich cites the U-2 cost underrun as the lone such instance in the complex's history.
- [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) — extended critique of the complex's bureaucratization and its failure to deliver technology fast, tracing the term to Eisenhower.
