# Apollo Program

The central historical case study of [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation), where Apollo is read as the high-water mark of [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel)'s "definite optimism" — the belief that the future will be better for specific, concrete reasons — and its aftermath as the moment that optimism died. The book leans on Thiel's observation that immediately after the 1969 Moon landing came Woodstock, and "the Space Age gave way to the New Age": the Technological Sublime that Apollo embodied was replaced by meditation, yoga, and individualistic spirituality, marking a broader turn toward escapism and diminished ambition.

The book treats Apollo as a model of productive organization. Larger than the Manhattan Project (with a price tag about twelve times higher), it was simultaneously decentralized and centralized — von Braun's "Monday notes" system made him a hub for surfacing problems while solutions stayed decentralized, and a NASA researcher credits the program's "common goal" and "sense of purpose" with preventing "bureaucratic sclerosis." Gene Kranz, an Apollo 13 flight director, called "irrational exuberance" a prerequisite to space exploration. Boom contrasts this with the rigid, hierarchical, data-bound NASA culture implicated in the 1986 Challenger disaster.

Apollo also anchors the book's argument that bubbles and moonshots subsidize transformative technology: its "most transformative technological contribution was the integrated circuit." NASA bought 60 percent of all US-manufactured integrated circuits by the end of 1963, accelerating their development and kickstarting the recursive chip-improvement cycle behind the computer revolution — and, the book half-jokes, any future Singularity would be "traceable back to the Eagle," the lunar module's onboard computer. von Braun's own admission that what to do once on Mars was a "weak point" leads the book to its closing claim that manned spaceflight should be judged by "spiritual effectiveness," not economic efficiency.

**Context:** The Apollo program was NASA's crewed spaceflight effort (1961–1972) that landed twelve astronauts on the Moon, beginning with Apollo 11 in July 1969. It was launched under President Kennedy and reached peak funding in the mid-1960s.
