# Palmer Luckey

Palmer Luckey is the founder of [Oculus](/notes/oculus) (VR) and later [Anduril](/notes/anduril) (defense), and across Kyle's corpus he functions as the recurring case study for conviction holding firm against social disapproval. In [Conviction-Led Contrarianism](/essays/conviction-led-contrarianism), Kyle frames Luckey as someone who "has never really struggled with Layer 1 [Dependencies](/notes/dependencies)" — indifferent to broad social approval from the start — and who persevered through "Layer 2" naysayers twice: investors who said VR couldn't produce a large company, then those who said building defense technology was immoral. On the unavoidable "Layer 3" dependencies, Kyle cites Luckey's line that ["you need to care about that 1% of the world that is going to be your ride or die,"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Pnc5G0RzTLw) noting that Anduril deliberately solicits true believers.

Luckey also anchors the [Anduril](/notes/anduril) thesis that runs through Kyle's defense reading. [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) treats Anduril — Luckey and [Brian Schimpf](/notes/brian-schimpf)'s company — as the obvious endpoint of its [Anti-Platform](/notes/anti-platform) argument: the future of military power belongs to many cheaper, autonomous, networked nodes rather than a handful of exquisite legacy platforms. Anduril is read against the hollowed-out industrial culture of [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works)-era engineering as the modern attempt to revive founder-led defense innovation, positioned against the [Robert McNamara](/notes/robert-mcnamara) consolidation era. Kyle's marginal notes flag recurring Luckey themes (comparing him to Richard Branson, his dismissal of quantum computing) throughout the book.

**Context:** Palmer Luckey founded Oculus VR (acquired by Facebook in 2014) and, after departing, founded the defense-technology company Anduril Industries in 2017, known for its Lattice software platform and autonomous systems.
