# John D. Rockefeller

Rockefeller surfaces across two books as an archetype of the great capitalist. In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), he heads the closing comparison of history's great capitalists — Rockefeller "the oil cartelist," Andrew Carnegie "the philanthropic steel baron," Sam Walton "the humble retailer," and Bill Gates "the software nerd" — all of whom owe their fortunes to a single product or innovation. The point is a foil for [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett), who is set apart because he made his money as a pure investor picking diverse businesses and stocks rather than building one dominant enterprise.

In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), Rockefeller appears twice. He is the case study for how thin the line is between "genius" and "criminal": Morgan Housel asks how little the narrative would have to shift to turn "Rockefeller was a genius, try to learn from his successes" into "Rockefeller was a criminal, try to learn from his business failures." He also appears as a model of disciplined silence — a recluse who "lets everybody else talk, while he sits back and says nothing," reciting the wise-old-owl poem about how the less he spoke, the more he heard.

**Context:** John D. Rockefeller (1839–1937) founded Standard Oil, building it into a near-monopoly over American oil refining; he became history's first known dollar billionaire and later one of its most consequential philanthropists.

## Where this appears

- [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) — heads the lineup of single-product great capitalists, used as a foil for Buffett the pure investor
- [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) — case study in the thin genius/criminal narrative line, and a model of disciplined silence
