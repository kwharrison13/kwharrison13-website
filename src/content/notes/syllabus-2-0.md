# Syllabus 2.0

Kyle's tag for the "courses do what books and lectures can't" thread in [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work). The essay's argument is that books are *static* — prose can frame or stimulate a reader's thoughts but can't respond to them as they unfold — so the reader is forced to carry the full [Metacognition](/notes/metacognition) burden (deciding which exercises to do, running their own feedback loops, noticing when they solved a problem but missed the insight). Courses shoulder some of that cognitive load by supplying the scaffolding a lone reader lacks.

Under this tag Kyle collects the concrete affordances a course provides that a book cannot: **scope and sequence**, **feedback on exercises** (individual and class-wide), **office hours** for targeted support, **review sessions**, **social learning** (students understand more deeply by grappling with peers' understandings), **access to an expert** as a conduit into the discipline's culture, an **accountability structure** that supports willpower, and **emotional salience** — the passion of an instructor or the prose itself can inspire. Kyle's annotation ties the last point to [Sapiens](/books/sapiens), which "didn't say anything new" but spoke with an "authorial voice" that struck people's passion. The framing sits alongside the essay's adjacent "Books 2.0" / [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) thread.
