# Search Funds

A vehicle in which an entrepreneur raises capital to *search for* and acquire a single small business to run, rather than starting one from scratch. In [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) the search fund appears as a distinct buyer type in the lower-middle-market acquisition landscape, sitting alongside smaller private-equity funds, fundless sponsors, and wealthy individuals — a cohort the book pegs as expecting roughly a **20–35% return** depending on strategy and opportunity cost. The book also flags the financing tells a seller should verify: a fundless sponsor or search fund should produce "written proof of investor interest in providing the capital for the deal under the agreed upon terms," and an individual operator seeking **SBA financing** should furnish a bank statement certifying capacity. The page therefore reads search funds as a financing structure with its own return math and its own diligence checklist, not just a generic acquirer.

[On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) places search funds at the center of a cluster with [holding companies](/notes/holding-company) and "[Investing 101 2.0](/notes/investing-101-2-0)" — the "build or buy one good business and hold it forever" / evergreen mindset associated with Dave Whorton and the Tugboat Institute. That source cautions against the common student framing of acquiring a business with a five-year exit in mind; it cites Will Thorndike (author of [The Outsiders](/notes/the-outsiders)) as a thought leader in the search-fund world, and David Dodson's mixed record (a 40% IRR on Smith Alarm alongside later losses) as evidence that even talented searchers face uncertain outcomes.

**Context:** The search fund model was formalized at Stanford and Harvard business schools in the 1980s; a "searcher" raises a small fund to cover a 1–2 year search, then a larger pool of the same investors funds the eventual acquisition of an established, cash-flowing small company (often via SBA-backed debt) which the searcher operates as CEO.

## Where this appears

- [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) — search funds as a buyer type with 20–35% return expectations and SBA-financing / proof-of-capital diligence requirements
- [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) — search funds at the center of the holding-company / long-hold cluster; Thorndike and Dodson as cautionary exemplars against exit-first thinking
