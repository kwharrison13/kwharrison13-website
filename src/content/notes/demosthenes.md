# Demosthenes

The ancient Greek orator surfaces in Kyle's corpus entirely through [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in two distinct ways. First, Munger repeatedly quotes Demosthenes' line *"What a man wishes, that also will he believe"* as a crisp statement of [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias) — it appears in both [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Revisited](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom-revisited) and [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment). Second, in his [Charlie Munger — Harvard School Commencement Speech](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-school-commencement-speech), Munger names Demosthenes (alongside [Cicero](/notes/cicero)) as an oratory role model, recalling [Cicero](/notes/cicero)'s reply when asked which was his favorite among Demosthenes' orations: "The longest one." On the [Public Speaking](/notes/public-speaking) page this same Cicero/Demosthenes pairing anchors Munger's brevity theme — judging speeches by whether one wished them longer.

**Context:** Demosthenes (384–322 BC) was an Athenian statesman and the most celebrated orator of ancient Greece, famous for his *Philippics* against Philip II of Macedon and as a model of classical rhetoric.
