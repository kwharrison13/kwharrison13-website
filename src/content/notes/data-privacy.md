# Data Privacy

**Data Privacy** is the policy/economics pole of a three-way concept cluster with [Privacy](/notes/privacy) (the human-stakes pole) and [Personal User Data](/notes/personal-user-data) (the asset pole), all built from the same June 29th, 2021 Roam "Quick Capture" reading log on surveillance, data ownership, and algorithmic trust scoring. Where [Privacy](/notes/privacy) carries the synthesized argument — [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari) in [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus) framing personal data as "the most valuable resource most humans still have to offer" — this page holds the regulatory and market-design strand of the same debate: the saved links lean toward Shoshana Zuboff's *Age of Surveillance Capitalism*, the WSJ "secret trust scores companies use to judge us all" piece, the Economist's "we need to own our data as a human right and be compensated for it," MIT Tech Review's "let's make private data into a public good," and Tim Cook on data privacy. [Amazon ($AMZN)](/notes/amazon) sits in this thread as a node tagged off the "constant consumer" reading.

The through-line across the saved sources is the tension between data-as-an-owned-asset (compensated, a public good, a human right) and data-as-a-vector-of-surveillance-and-discrimination (trust scores, algorithmic bias, the Xinjiang DNA story, "a tax on the poor: surrendering privacy for survival"). These are unsynthesized reading pointers Kyle was tracking, kept verbatim below.

**Context:** "Data privacy" in regulatory usage refers to the rules governing how personal data is collected, stored, used, and shared — the domain of frameworks like the EU's GDPR and California's CCPA — as distinct from "data security" (protecting data from breach). The sources here trace the late-2010s policy debate over whether individuals should own and be compensated for their data.
