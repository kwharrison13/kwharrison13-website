# Power Law

The power law — the idea that returns are extremely concentrated, with a tiny number of outcomes dwarfing the rest — is one of the organizing concepts of Kyle's investing thought, running through both his venture reading and his essays on the entertainment business. [VC — An American History](/books/vc) frames it as the book's central claim, that exceptional VC payoffs are "sporadic and infrequent, concentrated in specific firms and time periods," while its sibling [The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) makes the namesake argument that one big winner must cover the losses of every other investment in a fund. In [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting), the power law connects to the venture critique that disruptions creating wealth "cannot be predicted based on extrapolations of past data."

Kyle's most developed application is to the entertainment industry. In The Hits Business - Research, he argues that both Hollywood and [Venture Capital](/notes/venture-capital) have "collapsed into a hits business — a reflection of the [Power Law](/notes/power-law)," with the gravitational pull toward big outcomes reshaping each industry — but insists "bigger does not mean better, and good does not mean small," with [A24](/notes/a24) as proof that scale and taste need not be mutually exclusive. The "your size is your strategy" VC saying maps directly onto film (why majors gravitate to sequels), and the power law shows up in public markets too (FAANG/big-tech names making up nearly 40% of the S&P 500). [Legendary Entertainment](/notes/legendary-entertainment) and [Thomas Tull](/notes/thomas-tull) are the scale-over-taste contrast case. [Intellectual Property](/notes/intellectual-property) notes the power law as "the hit-driven economics underlying why owning a proven property is the dominant business model." Even outside finance, [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) uses it as a metaphor for nonlinear leverage — a single chine dropping the radar cross-section 90% — "small shape changes producing order-of-magnitude effects." The Puritans of Venture Capital - Research raises the quantitative version: how many generational ($10B+) outcomes per vintage actually exist, and whether they support even one $3B+ fund.

**Context:** A power-law distribution is one where a small number of events account for the majority of total magnitude (the "80/20" or "winner-take-most" shape), in contrast to a normal distribution. In venture capital it is the standard framing for why a handful of investments must return the entire fund.
