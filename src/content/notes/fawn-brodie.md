# Fawn Brodie

Fawn McKay Brodie (1915–1981), author of *No Man Knows My History: The Life of Joseph Smith the Mormon Prophet* (1945), the landmark psychobiography that reframed [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) as a conscious fabricator. In Kyle's First Vision — Topical Study she is the foil for a methodological principle Kyle keeps returning to: **"Never set a historical argument on the LACK of evidence."** Brodie argued that Joseph's later First Vision accounts (1835, 1838) were embellished and invented — written only to manufacture authority — but Kyle treats arguments from absent evidence as fragile, distinguishing *interpretive* memory ("seems") from *factual* memory and noting that memories shift without being false.

In [Leonard Arrington and the Writing of Mormon History](/books/leonard-arrington-and-the-writing-of-mormon-history) Brodie is the "anti-Mormon scholar whose reception colored the archives debate." Her book drew an enormous 160-column-inch unattributed rebuttal in the *Church News* ("a composite of all anti-Mormon books that have gone before"). But [Leonard Arrington](/notes/leonard-arrington) himself set the record straight on a common myth: Brodie's poor reception did NOT poison the well and close the Church Archives to later scholars — Brodie had used anti-Mormon collections at the New York Public Library, not the archives, and the restriction Arrington experienced came in 1951–52 with the flood of BYU graduate students, not in 1946–47 after Brodie's book. She sits in the same generation of mid-century Mormon-studies authors Arrington tracked (alongside Wallace Stegner, Bernard DeVoto, Dale Morgan, and Juanita Brooks), and a 1981 tribute to her — "A New Climate of Liberation: A Tribute to Fawn McKay Brodie" — was among the scholarship [Boyd K. Packer](/notes/boyd-k-packer) targeted in his "The Mantle Is Far, Far Greater Than the Intellect" address.

**Context:** Fawn McKay Brodie was a niece of LDS Church president David O. McKay who left the faith and became a professor of history at UCLA. Beyond the Joseph Smith biography she wrote controversial psychobiographies of Thaddeus Stevens, Richard Nixon, and Thomas Jefferson (the last advancing the Sally Hemings relationship decades before DNA evidence).
