# Desire for Recognition

In [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked), the desire for recognition is the antagonist concept: the Adlerian claim that seeking approval from others is precisely what robs a person of freedom, and that genuine freedom requires the willingness to be disliked. The book — a Plato-style dialogue expounding [Alfred Adler](/notes/alfred-adler)'s individual psychology — frames recognition-seeking against [Dale Carnegie](/notes/dale-carnegie)-style influence (the text cites Carnegie praising Adler) and treats the craving for external validation as a trap rather than a motive to be optimized.

Kyle's notes translate the book's positive alternative into other vocabularies. Adler's idea of contribution "without visible recognition" — feeling "of use to someone" — glosses as "It's like putting away your shopping cart. You feel good because you're contributing even if no one sees you," i.e. civic virtue as worth regardless of observation. A parallel note reframes Adler's critique of reward/punishment pedagogy as "Learning to be righteous in the dark," a religiously inflected version of internalization over external reward. Read together, the notes treat the book as a translator's exercise — mapping the recognition critique onto tiger-mother concepts, religious internalization, and civic virtue.

**Context:** *The Courage to Be Disliked* (Ichiro Kishimi and Fumitake Koga, 2013) popularizes Alfred Adler's individual psychology in dialogue form. Its core argument — that the "desire for recognition" and the resulting fear of others' judgment are the chief obstacles to freedom and happiness — is a deliberate counter to approval-driven, reward-and-punishment models of motivation.
