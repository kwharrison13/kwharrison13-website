# Circle of Competence

The idea that what matters is not the size of your competence but knowing where its perimeter lies. In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) tells the apocryphal Max Planck story — the chauffeur who memorized Planck's lecture and could recite it flawlessly, but could not answer a real question — to dramatize the distinction between true mastery and surface fluency. [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s gloss runs alongside it: "If we have a strength, it is in recognizing when we are operating well within our circle of competence and when we are approaching the perimeter." Munger frames building one as an attainable discipline — you may never win a chess tournament, but "the best plumbing contractor in Bemidji" is reachable through work — and pairs it with intellectual humility: "stay within a well-defined circle of competence" and "never fool yourself, and remember that you are the easiest person to fool."

[The Great Mental Models Volume 1: General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) turns the concept into a worked model: the difference between the "Lifer's" detailed web of knowledge and the "Stranger's" surface knowledge is the difference between being inside the circle and outside its perimeter — and "true knowledge of a complex territory cannot be faked." The book adds the operating mechanics: building a circle takes years of mistakes and three practices (curiosity, monitoring, feedback); journaling is the easiest private form of self-feedback; and operating *outside* the circle means knowing you're a Stranger, consulting Lifers, and probing the limits of their circles. It also surfaces the deeper hazard Kyle flags — incentive distortion: "the problem of incentives can really skew how much you can rely on someone else's circle of competence," which is why [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) is the prerequisite skill.

In [Buffett: The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), the principle is operationalized as managerial restraint: Buffett "didn't need a big circle of competence — but it was important to know where the perimeter was," restricting himself to businesses he could understand and reserving only a few big decisions for himself. Kyle's [Value Investing](/notes/value-investing) page wires the concept directly to its supporting principles ([Margin of Safety](/notes/margin-of-safety), a non-forecasting posture), positioning the circle as one of the load-bearing ideas of the Munger/Buffett method.

**Context:** "Circle of competence" is a term popularized by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger to describe the set of subjects a person genuinely understands well enough to make sound judgments within; the discipline is to act only inside it and to honestly mark where its edges are.
