# Pete Peterson

In [Stephen Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-schwarzman)'s memoir [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes), Pete is Schwarzman's co-founder and partner in building [Blackstone](/notes/blackstone). The two had worked together at Lehman; when Schwarzman, at thirty-eight, decided he wanted to start his own business, the draw was less the idea than the partner — "I just want to work with Pete again," he says. Pete was the cautious one of the pair: when they set out to raise their first fund, he worried, "Why would somebody give us money when we've never done anything?" — to which Schwarzman answered, "Because it's us. And because it's a moment."

The memoir credits a founding principle the two agreed on from the start of Blackstone: they would never do hostile deals, because businesses are made up of people who deserve to be treated with respect. Rather than slash costs and strip a company until it collapsed, they would invest in improving the businesses they bought — a "friendly" approach they believed protected their reputation and earned higher long-term returns.

**Context:** Peter G. "Pete" Peterson (1926–2018) was U.S. Secretary of Commerce under Nixon and chairman/CEO of Lehman Brothers before co-founding the Blackstone Group with Stephen Schwarzman in 1985. He later became a prominent philanthropist focused on U.S. fiscal policy through the Peter G. Peterson Foundation.
