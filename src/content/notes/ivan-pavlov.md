# Ivan Pavlov

Pavlov appears in [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) in Munger's discussion of stress and its effects on cognition. Munger notes that "light stress can slightly improve performance" while heavy stress causes dysfunction, then credits Pavlov for going further than most scientists: he "spent the rest of his long life giving stress-induced nervous breakdowns to dogs," reversing them, and keeping careful experimental records. Munger draws four findings from this work — that dogs could be classified to predict how easily they would break down, that the hardest to break were the hardest to restore, that any dog could be broken down, and that a breakdown could only be reversed by reimposing stress. The passage sits within Munger's broader latticework of psychological tendencies and their standard antidotes.

**Context:** Ivan Pavlov (1849–1936) was a Russian physiologist who won the 1904 Nobel Prize and is best known for classical conditioning — his experiments on stimulus-response learning in dogs. His later work studied experimental "neurosis" induced by stress, which is the strand Munger draws on here.
