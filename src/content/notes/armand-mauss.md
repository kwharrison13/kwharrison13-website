# Armand Mauss

Armand Mauss is the sociologist whose 1994 book *The Angel and the Beehive* supplies the central interpretive frame in [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons). Mauss argued that Mormons always exist in a state of tension between a desire to **accommodate** their host culture and a simultaneous need to **stand apart** as a peculiar people. The "angel" is the other-worldly, charismatic strain that embraces exclusive truth claims and distinctive theology; the "beehive" is the industrious Mormon culture in its quest to assimilate into American society. In the notes, this assimilation-vs-retrenchment pendulum is treated as the best available sociological theory for where Mormonism now stands — better than O'Dea's pessimism about secularization or Stark's buoyant assurances — and it is used to explain how the church could jettison [Polygamy](/notes/polygamy) (1890), theocracy, and the racial [Priesthood](/notes/priesthood)/temple ban (1978) while still maintaining a distinctive edge.

**Context:** Armand L. Mauss (1928–2020) was an American sociologist of religion and a leading scholar of Latter-day Saint studies, best known for *The Angel and the Beehive: The Mormon Struggle with Assimilation* (1994).
