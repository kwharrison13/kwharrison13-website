# Kobe Bryant

Across Kyle's reading, Kobe Bryant is invoked both as an exemplar of relentless practice and as a cautionary foil. In [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), [Ryan Holiday](/notes/ryan-holiday) presents him as "one of the most exacting and dedicated players to ever step on a basketball court" who nonetheless "had trouble accepting that his teammates 'couldn't all be Kobes'" — driving them as hard as he drove himself, often "into the dirt," and even driving away talent like Shaquille O'Neal, costing himself "another ring or two." He is the cautionary case against the lift-your-supporting-players frame.

The other thread is practice as a numbers game. In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store), Kyle's own note connects a Bezos passage to "Kobe Bryant talking about practice being a numbers game. If you're working 2-3 hours a day every day you're going to be better than the people working 2-3 days a week." In [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning), Bryant is tagged against the **10,000-hour myth** discussion — the long read pushes back on the idea that fixed hours alone produce expertise.

**Context:** Kobe Bryant (1978–2020) was an American professional basketball player who spent his entire 20-season NBA career with the Los Angeles Lakers, winning five championships; he was famous for his obsessive work ethic ("Mamba mentality") and died in a helicopter crash in 2020.

## Where this appears

- [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) — the cautionary foil: exacting and dedicated, but couldn't accept that teammates "couldn't all be Kobes," driving talent away.
- [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store) — Kyle's note connecting Bezos to Kobe's "practice is a numbers game" ethos (daily reps compound).
- [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) — tagged against the 10,000-hour-rule discussion of what actually builds expertise.
