# Long Term Thinking

The throughline Kyle tags most heavily across the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — long-term thinking and [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) as the core discipline. [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s operating premise is that "every investment should be approached as if it were a Buy and Never Sell investment," and Kyle flags the maxim repeatedly: "We prefer good long term ideas," "Lethargy bordering on sloth remains the cornerstone of our investment style." Buffett selects equity holdings "on a long-term basis," weighing favorable long-term economic characteristics, honest management, and an attractive price; the goal is to attract long-term owners "who, at the time of purchase, have no timetable or price target for sale but plan instead to stay with us indefinitely." A single business that doubles 20 times beats 20 separately-taxed doubles — "we would rather stay put, even if that means slightly lower returns" — which ties the discipline to [Buy and Never Sell](/notes/buy-and-never-sell) and "look-through earnings" thinking a decade out.

The same posture surfaces in [How Will You Measure Your Life?](/books/how-will-you-measure-your-life), where [Clayton Christensen](/notes/clayton-christensen) diagnoses the opposite failure mode: companies' "decision-making systems are designed to steer investments to initiatives that offer the most tangible and immediate returns," shortchanging the initiatives crucial to long-term strategy. Kyle reads this as the ancestor of [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos)'s long-term discipline ("this is exactly why Amazon ignores short term metrics"). In [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city), it takes a city-building form: Walt's advice was "Think beyond your lifetime if you want to accomplish something truly worthwhile" — the Florida Project meant to keep his Imagineers busy for decades, with the eventual Disney World development pattern mirroring his very first sketch.
