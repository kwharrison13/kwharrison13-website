# Great Artists Steal

One of Kyle's most-tagged recurring frames — the Picasso-attributed maxim that "good artists copy, great artists steal," which Kyle uses to mean **case-study mining as a learning discipline**: reading biographies and operating histories as raw material for thesis-building, not entertainment. In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store) it is flagged 4+ times and surfaces as a named Key Takeaway: Bezos "went to school on everybody" — Sam Walton (who Bezos noted "described borrowing the best ideas of his competitors"), Jim Sinegal of Costco ("I've always had the opinion that we have shamelessly stolen any good ideas"), and the airline yield-management trick that became Amazon Prime's free-shipping tiers. Kyle's note on the Apple-aspiration passage captures the nuance: *"As important as it is to not be focused on competition it's just as important to have role models. Sometimes the same company can be both."*

The same frame appears in Kyle's Open-Source Knowledge - Research, where [Walt Disney](/notes/walt-disney)'s habit of synthesizing the best of others' ideas — *"Walt would synthesize the best of other people's ideas and create something not only better, but wholly unique"* — is tagged to Great Artists Steal alongside the companion concept [Idea Sex](/notes/idea-sex).

**Context:** The phrase is popularly attributed to Pablo Picasso ("good artists copy, great artists steal") and was further popularized by Steve Jobs; it captures the idea that originality often comes from transformative synthesis of borrowed ideas rather than invention from scratch.
