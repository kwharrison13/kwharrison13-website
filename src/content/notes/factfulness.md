# Factfulness

[Hans Rosling](/notes/hans-rosling)'s *Factfulness* surfaces in Kyle's reading as the canonical statement of the **fact-based, cautiously-optimistic worldview** — the idea that the world is less frightening and less hopeless than it feels, and that a clear-eyed look at the data shows problems are solvable. In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel) cites it on how people systematically misjudge risk: "Every group of people I ask thinks the world is more frightening, more violent, and more hopeless—in short, more dramatic—than it really is." In [How To Avoid a Climate Disaster](/books/how-to-avoid-a-climate-disaster), [Bill Gates](/notes/bill-gates) closes the book quoting his friend Rosling to exactly the opposite of fatalism: "When we have a fact-based worldview, we can see that the world is not as bad as it seems—and we can see what we have to do to keep making it better." Both authors deploy Factfulness as a corrective to the human tendency toward drama and despair — Housel for investing psychology, Gates for climate solvability.

**Context:** *Factfulness: Ten Reasons We're Wrong About the World — and Why Things Are Better Than You Think* (2018), by Swedish physician and global-health educator Hans Rosling (with Ola Rosling and Anna Rosling Rönnlund), argues that systematic cognitive instincts make people misperceive global progress, and offers rules of thumb for a more accurate, data-grounded worldview.
