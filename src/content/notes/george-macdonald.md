# George MacDonald

In [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt), George MacDonald is quoted twice at length as a voice on belief and the limits of scripture. The first quotation argues that "to the man who would live throughout the whole divine form of his being... a thousand questions will arise to which the Bible does not even allude," and that it would be sad indeed "if the Bible told us everything God meant us to believe" — a counter to scriptural literalism that fits the book's argument about doubt and incomplete knowledge. The second is MacDonald's declaration that he "would live my time believing in a grand thing that ought to be true if it is not," choosing to "hold by the better than the actual" and, if necessary, "fall into nothingness off the same precipice with Jesus and Paul" rather than live denying them. The Givenses use these passages to frame faith as a deliberate, hopeful choice rather than a conclusion forced by certainty.

**Context:** George MacDonald (1824–1905) was a Scottish author, poet, and Christian minister whose fantasy and theological writing deeply influenced later figures including C.S. Lewis, who called MacDonald his "master."

## Where this appears

- [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) — quoted twice on the limits of the Bible and on choosing to believe "the better than the actual," supporting the book's treatment of doubt and faith.
