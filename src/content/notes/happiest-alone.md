# Happiest Alone

"Happiest alone" is the phrase Kyle attaches to the portrait of [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) in the biography of the same name — Adams as "that rare politician who is happiest alone." The book describes a man constitutionally unsuited to the warmth politics demands: bearish with friends, withdrawn from his own wife and children, who "knew this and perpetually rebuked himself for his bearish manner, but… did not really wish to be otherwise." Yet his compensations — discipline, principle, and inexhaustible drudgery — let him outlast every contemporary. Kyle files the highlight under [Solitude](/notes/solitude) as one of the most sustained portraits in his reading of a politician who genuinely preferred his own company.

The trait connects to Adams's theory of [Leadership](/notes/leadership) elsewhere in the book — his willingness to defend constituents' interests "against their inclinations," a posture that requires the kind of self-sufficiency a more crowd-pleasing temperament could not sustain.
