# Eric Hoffer

Eric Hoffer appears in Kyle's notes as the source of the line that crystallizes the central thesis of [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — that [Learning Agility](/notes/learning-agility) is the durable survival skill in a world where ~65% of today's under-fives are headed for jobs that don't yet exist:

> "In times of change learners inherit the earth; while the learned find themselves beautifully equipped for a world that no longer exists."

In the book Hoffer's closing line is set against the related warning that "bored and complacent people don't innovate; they get disrupted," anchoring the argument for [Continuous Learning](/notes/continuous-learning) built into the work rather than episodic training.

**Context:** Eric Hoffer (1898–1983) was an American moral and social philosopher, largely self-taught and known as "the longshoreman philosopher" for working the San Francisco docks while writing. His best-known book is _The True Believer_ (1951), a study of mass movements; he received the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1983.
