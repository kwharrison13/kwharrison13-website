# Grace

Grace is one of the central organizing themes of Kyle's gospel study, and his notes work hard to hold its tension. Christ Defines His Gospel frames it through "two kinds of grace" — **saving grace** (what Christ did *for* us) and **enabling grace** (what Christ allows us *to do*) — and warns against the "pendulum of grace," the two equally-wrong extremes [Satan](/notes/satan) pushes people toward: pride ("step back, I'll save myself") and hopelessness ("save me," doing nothing). Grace and works are not rivals there: "we are saved by grace, but faith without works is dead." The note quotes [Dietrich Bonhoeffer](/notes/dietrich-bonhoeffer)'s distinction between "cheap grace" (grace without discipleship) and "costly grace" (the call that costs a man his life). [The Broken Heart](/books/the-broken-heart) makes the same conditional/unconditional cut: the unconditional parts of the [Atonement](/notes/atonement) (resurrection, paying for Adam's transgression) are "free gifts of unmerited divine grace," while the conditional part "requires our repentance—part of 'all we can do.'"

The books push the idea further toward a grace-first theology. [Future Mormon](/books/future-mormon) reads Mormon theology through "a single, inverting move: grace comes first" — creation itself is grace, the fall is a *response* to it, and sin is "the suppression of grace"; "we don't have to work our way into grace; we have to stop working so hard to pretend we aren't already in it." [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) records [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england)'s resolution of the old faith-vs-works argument: "salvation as a condition of being… not a mere reward for works or irresistible infusion of grace," and Joseph Smith's "from grace to grace" theosis. A Mighty Change of Heart — Ezra Taft Benson anchors grace in repentance: "no flesh… can dwell in the presence of God, save it be through the merits, and mercy, and grace of the Holy Messiah," and "His grace is sufficient." The Self-Mastery — Topical Study threads grace into discipleship — becoming like God "grace for grace," overcoming the natural man by small, simple decisions.

**Context:** In Christian theology, grace is the unmerited divine favor and help given to humanity, central to debates over the relationship between God's gift and human effort in salvation. Latter-day Saint theology generally frames grace and works as complementary rather than opposed.
