# Mary Helen Immordino-Yang

Quoted across Kyle's learning notes for one line that frames the whole information-to-knowledge problem: "The task of learning is to transform some of that information into knowledge that can be used and acted upon." In both [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) and Books 2.0 - Research, Kyle pairs it with [T.S. Eliot](/notes/t-s-eliot)'s "Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?" — using Immordino-Yang to name the active step (turning inert information into usable knowledge) that the Eliot lines lament losing. In [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) it sits within a broader argument about retrieval and the necessary ingredients of learning (motivation as the brain noticing a discrepancy between what it has and what it wants).

**Context:** Mary Helen Immordino-Yang is a neuroscientist and education professor at USC whose research connects emotion, social cognition, and learning — arguing that deep learning and meaning-making are inseparable from emotional engagement.

## Where this appears

- [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) — quoted on transforming information into knowledge "that can be used and acted upon"
- Books 2.0 - Research — the same quote, paired with T.S. Eliot on the information/knowledge/wisdom hierarchy
