# Marshall McLuhan

Marshall McLuhan is the most-cited theorist across Kyle's media notes, almost always for the single line "the medium is the message" (from *Understanding Media*, 1964). [David Perell](/notes/david-perell)'s [Naked Brands](/notes/naked-brands) series is built on him: McLuhan argued societies are shaped more by the *nature* of their media than by the content communicated, and that print bred conformity while electric/digital media decentralize influence and produce diversity — a "global village" of more varied creativity, personality, and style. Perell applies this across fashion, basketball, music, and finance to argue audiences now "follow people, not brands."

The same lens runs through Kyle's politics and news reading. [News in the Age of Abundance](/notes/news-in-the-age-of-abundance) cites McLuhan for how shifting from text to images makes societies "worship glamour over truth, emotion over rationality, and youth over wisdom." The [FeelTheBern](/notes/do-you-feelthebern-behind-bernie-sanders-quest-to-take-over-the-white-house) read (and the [Politics](/notes/politics) page) quote him directly — "societies have always been shaped more by the nature of the media by which men communicate than by the content of the communication" — to argue social platforms, not the debate stage, are now the arena of presidential politics. He is also flagged in the [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) notes, where [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) invokes "the medium is the message" as the book's intellectual foundation.

**Context:** Marshall McLuhan (1911–1980) was a Canadian media theorist whose *The Mechanical Bride*, *The Gutenberg Galaxy*, and *Understanding Media* coined "the medium is the message" and "the global village," anticipating much of how electronic and digital media reshape culture.
