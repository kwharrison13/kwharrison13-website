# Parenting

Parenting is one of the more developed personal threads in Kyle's reading, and the books that carry it largely converge on a single thesis: autonomy and relationship, not control, are the developmental mechanism. [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) is read "primarily as a parent," and its throughline is that "parenting is less about producing outcomes and more about building the psychology behind being good at things" — controlled, age-appropriate stress builds resilience, while yelling and tight control are "ego-driven attempts to regain leverage." Kyle overlays an LDS frame: [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s "let them govern themselves" rhymes with the autonomy thesis, mapping onto [Agency](/notes/agency) ("agency takes practice"). [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog) supplies the neurodevelopmental floor — "what most effectively heals child trauma is increasing the quality and number of relationships in a child's life," and "we tend to parent as we were parented," with the dissolution of the extended family ("alloparents") as a driver of modern developmental stress. [The Parents' Guide to Boys](/books/the-parents-guide-to-boys) sits adjacent on boy-specific development and motivation.

A second cluster reframes praise and discipline. [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) brings Adler's critique of reward-and-punishment pedagogy — "separation of tasks," horizontal vs. vertical relationships with children, and treating a child "like a human being," not like an adult or a child — which Kyle glosses as "righteous in the dark." [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) adds teaching children to "deal productively with complexity" rather than around it. The historical case study is [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams): John and Abigail Adams's conviction that "intellect taught without morality was pointless," the classical curriculum imposed on JQA, and "the most painful thread" — "the same thresher that forged JQA destroyed his brothers and eventually his own sons." [Becoming](/books/becoming) contributes Michelle Obama's rejection of "what do you want to be when you grow up?" as "one of the most useless questions an adult can ask a child."

**Context:** "Parenting" here is a cross-corpus reading theme — Kyle's standing interest in child development, autonomy, and moral formation — drawn from developmental-psychology books, an LDS framework, and biography, rather than from one source.
