# Small Towns

**Small Towns** is the through-line of Kyle's "geography of opportunity" reading — the question of which towns thrive when manufacturing or extraction leaves, and whether prosperity still has to concentrate in superstar cities. [Dar Williams](/notes/dar-williams)'s [What I Found in a Thousand Towns](/books/what-i-found-in-a-thousand-towns) supplies the optimistic mechanism: small towns thrive through **positive proximity** ("a state of being where living side by side with other people is experienced as beneficial"), main-street "fabric" (mixed architecture "unified without uniformity"), history, and natural amenities — but face displacement via [Gentrification](/notes/gentrification), which can "squash vitality" and abandon the "soul" residents built event by event. Kyle threads the same idea into [Prosperable](/notes/prosperable), his own city-building thesis that talent no longer has to cluster in big cities and smaller towns can be deliberately revived — coupled to [Remote Work](/notes/remote-work) and post-COVID dispersion (Bend, Greenville, Sioux Falls). [MainStreet](/notes/mainstreet) is the company he saved against that thesis: a startup bringing "Silicon-Valley-style tax-credit help to small towns."

The Walmart material supplies the harder, contested view. [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart) notes that "to Walton's surprise, there was far more business in small-town America than anybody — including Walton — had ever dreamed of" — the under-served small-town market was the whole opportunity. [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) presses on the cost: Walton "basically broke other small town merchants," yet Munger argues "you shouldn't get too idealistic" — small-town nostalgia is "nothing more than nostalgia and delusion," and "the world is better for having Wal-Mart." So across the corpus, small towns sit between a romance of bottom-up civic vitality (Williams, Prosperable) and a clear-eyed account of creative destruction (Munger, Walton).

## Where this appears

- [What I Found in a Thousand Towns](/books/what-i-found-in-a-thousand-towns) — positive proximity, main-street fabric, and natural amenities as the levers that determine which towns thrive; gentrification as the displacement risk.
- [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart) — the under-served small-town market as Walton's central (and surprising) opportunity.
- [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom) — Walmart "breaking" small-town merchants; Munger on small-town nostalgia as "delusion."
- [Prosperable](/notes/prosperable) — Kyle's city-building thesis that smaller towns can capture dispersed talent (remote work, post-COVID).
- [MainStreet](/notes/mainstreet) — the tax-credit-for-small-towns company saved against the Prosperable / Small Towns thesis.
- [Dar Williams](/notes/dar-williams) — author whose small-town tour stops supply the "positive proximity" case studies.
