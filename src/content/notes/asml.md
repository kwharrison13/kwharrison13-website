# ASML

In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), the Dutch company ASML is the book's central illustration of a single-point chokehold in the global semiconductor supply chain: it builds **100 percent of the world's extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines, "without which cutting-edge chips are simply impossible to make"** — a concentration the book notes makes OPEC's 40% share of world oil production "look unimpressive by comparison." ASML's EUV scanner is described as "the most expensive mass-produced machine tool in history," so complex it can't be used without ASML personnel on-site for the tool's entire lifespan.

The book uses ASML to make a deeper point about how modern hard-tech monopolies are actually orchestrated networks. ASML itself produced only ~15% of an EUV tool's components, buying the rest from a far-flung web of optics, laser, and software suppliers — crucial parts came from Cymer in California and Zeiss and Trumpf in Germany — so its real edge was the ability to coordinate that network, backed by "constant surveillance" of suppliers ("If you don't behave, we're going to buy you," CEO Peter Wennink told one; ASML did end up buying Cymer). The technology was pioneered in America's National Labs and largely funded by [Intel](/notes/intel), yet commercialized by a Dutch firm — a fact the book says would have "rankled America's economic nationalists." Chip War also leans on ASML to argue why this chokehold is nearly impossible to replicate: replicating just the laser in an EUV system requires assembling 457,329 parts, and the machines took nearly three decades to develop — knowledge that "can't simply be copied and pasted like a stolen file." A failed Chinese fab (Wuhan Hongxin / HSMC) is cited as a cautionary tale of acquiring an ASML deep-ultraviolet machine and still going bust.

**Context:** ASML Holding N.V. is a Dutch company headquartered in Veldhoven, Netherlands, and the world's leading supplier of photolithography systems for chip manufacturing. Its monopoly on EUV lithography has made it one of Europe's most valuable companies and a focal point of US-China export-control policy.
