# Henry Eyring

Chemist and Latter-day Saint thinker; the author of [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist), a collection arguing that true science and true religion are in harmony. Eyring's central framing in that book is the image of "two men in each one of us" — the scientist and the believer (a device he ties to [Louis Pasteur](/notes/louis-pasteur)) — and his answer to the recurring question "how can you accept revealed religion as a scientist?" is that "the Gospel commits us only to the truth," subject to the same pragmatic tests as science ("Does it work? Try it."). He treated mathematics as a tool rather than an end, never letting the physical or chemical nature of a problem be submerged in mathematical obscurity, and turned to LDS thinkers like [Orson Pratt](/notes/orson-pratt), [James E. Talmage](/notes/james-e-talmage), and [John A. Widtsoe](/notes/john-widtsoe) for the right "slant on the Gospel for scientific friends."

Eyring surfaces a second time in [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) — not in [Ananyo Bhattacharya](/notes/ananyo-bhattacharya)'s text, but in a Kyle note on the discipline of thinking: the line "Preach a sermon to yourself on the subject," which Kyle attributes to Eyring.

**Context:** Henry Eyring (1901–1981) was a prominent theoretical chemist best known for absolute-rate / transition-state theory, with a career spanning Princeton and the University of Utah. A devout member of the LDS Church, he was widely cited as a model for reconciling rigorous science with religious faith. (His son, Henry B. Eyring, became an LDS apostle — a distinct person.)

## Where this appears
