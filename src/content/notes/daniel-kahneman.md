# Daniel Kahneman

Daniel Kahneman recurs across Kyle's reading as the founding figure of the **heuristics-and-biases** view of human judgment, cited by six different sources. In [Range](/books/range) his "heuristics and biases" lens is contrasted against Gary Klein's naturalistic decision-making to frame the book's central argument: experience breeds skill only in "kind" learning environments, and when Kahneman probed highly trained experts he often found experience "had not helped at all… it frequently bred confidence but not skill." [The Great Mental Models Volume 1 — General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) uses his and Amos Tversky's **Linda problem** (the Fallacy of Conjunction) to motivate Hanlon's Razor. [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) cites him on risk-taking out of "ignorance and blindness to probability" and on "anticipated utility" / affective forecasting — that we fail to learn recursively from past experience.

In [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) Kahneman is both admirer and critic of [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann): he called *Theory of Games* "the most important theory in the social sciences," then challenged its rationality assumption with the **prospect theory** he and Tversky built. [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) draws on him for the hindsight illusion, his "I have no sunk costs" self-revision habit (relayed via [Jason Zweig](/notes/jason-zweig)), and founder overconfidence. Finally, Temporal Wealth and the Kingdom of God — Brigham Young reaches outside finance to cite the **Kahneman & Deaton** PNAS finding — that high income buys *life satisfaction but not happiness* past ~$75,000 — as secular corroboration of a gospel point about wealth.

**Context:** Daniel Kahneman (1934–2024) was an Israeli-American psychologist who won the 2002 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for the prospect theory he developed with Amos Tversky; he popularized the "two systems" model of cognition in his 2011 book *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.
