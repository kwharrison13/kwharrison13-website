# Honesty

In Kyle's notes, honesty surfaces where secular and scriptural framings of integrity meet. In [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Revisited](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom-revisited), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) argues for building systems that don't reward cheating — "it's much better to let some things go uncompensated... than to create systems that are easy to cheat" — and Kyle amplifies the point with a line from the LDS apostle [Mark E. Petersen](/notes/mark-e-petersen): "Honesty is a principle of salvation in the kingdom of God. Just as no man or woman can be saved without baptism, so no one can be saved without honesty." The juxtaposition treats honesty both as a practical defense against [incentive-caused bias](/notes/incentives) and as a first-order moral principle.

Munger's broader [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) thinking reinforces this: he flags self-honesty as the antidote to excuse-making (the Tolstoy effect) and tags his own practice of "stealing" psychology from its discoverers with explicit new attributions as a small act of intellectual honesty. Across these notes the concept functions less as a standalone framework than as a value Kyle keeps surfacing — the trustworthiness that underwrites both good [systems](/notes/capital-allocation) and good character.
