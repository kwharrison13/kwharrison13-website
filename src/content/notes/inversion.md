# Inversion

A core mental model in Kyle's [Latticework of Mental Models](/notes/latticework) reading: approaching a problem "from the opposite end of the natural starting point." [The Great Mental Models Volume 1: General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) defines it from the root word *invert* — "to upend or turn upside down" — and gives it as a tool to "identify and remove obstacles to success": instead of aiming directly at a goal, "think deeply about what you want to avoid and then see what options are left over." The book illustrates it with Bernays's "appeals of indirection" (don't sell more cigarettes within the existing social structure; imagine a world where women smoke anywhere and make it real) and Kurt Lewin's force-field analysis (identify and reduce the forces impeding change rather than only adding forces that push for it).

The idea is most associated in Kyle's corpus with [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), who borrowed the algebraist Carl Jacobi's refrain **"Invert, always invert."** Across the Munger talks Kyle annotated, inversion shows up as a working method: in [Charlie Munger: The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) he "sought good judgment mostly by collecting instances of bad judgment, then pondering ways to avoid such outcomes"; in [Charlie Munger: Practical Thought About Practical Thought?](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought) it is the "Third Notion" of his problem-solving checklist; in [Charlie Munger: Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address) it is the "forward and reverse thinking technique" borrowed from algebra. [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) folds it into the analytic-rigor list — "Think forwards and backwards — Invert, always invert" — and pairs it with [Charles Darwin](/notes/charles-darwin)'s discipline of writing down disconfirming evidence and "thinking in reverse as he tried to disprove his own hardest-won and best-loved ideas."

Kyle reads inversion as a tool for *strengthening* conviction, not weakening it. On the [Charlie Munger: Practical Thought About Practical Thought?](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought) page he notes: "Not 'killing your babies.' Thinking through the inverse of your beliefs doesn't mean you believe them less. It means you're more prepared to believe them strongly." He applies the same move to faith in [Why I Believe](/notes/why-i-believe), where inverting "Why I believe" into "Why wouldn't I believe?" reframes the question. Inversion sits alongside [Map vs Territory](/notes/map-vs-territory), [Circle of Competence](/notes/circle-of-competence), and [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking) as a general thinking concept, and connects to [Self-Criticism](/notes/self-criticism) and [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) as the habits that make it work.

**Context:** "Invert, always invert" traces to the 19th-century mathematician Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi, who advised attacking hard problems by reformulating them in inverse terms. Charlie Munger popularized it as a general decision-making heuristic; it is a staple of the Farnam Street / mental-models literature alongside the map-territory and circle-of-competence ideas.

- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — "Invert, always invert" listed among the analytic-rigor disciplines; paired with Darwin's habit of recording disconfirming evidence.
- [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) — relevant to the same latticework Kyle was building, on the brittleness of forward-only reasoning under tail events.
- [Charlie Munger: The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) — Munger "sought good judgment mostly by collecting instances of bad judgment"; calls for psychology professors to make "heavy use of inversion."
