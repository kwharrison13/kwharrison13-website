# Self-Reliance

The transcendentalist doctrine — most associated with [Ralph Waldo Emerson](/notes/ralph-waldo-emerson)'s essay of the same name — that one should trust private conviction over public opinion and conformity. In the wiki it shows up as the philosophical spine running through [Walden](/books/walden) and into a modern application by [Alex Karp](/notes/alex-karp). [Walden](/books/walden) carries the purest line: *"Public opinion is a weak tyrant compared with our own private opinion. What a man thinks of himself, that it is which determines, or rather indicates, his fate."* The wiki's [Ralph Waldo Emerson](/notes/ralph-waldo-emerson) page treats this as Emerson's self-reliance ethic underwriting [Henry David Thoreau](/notes/henry-david-thoreau) — the two transcendentalists paired as a single source.

[Alex Karp](/notes/alex-karp) is the live instance of the doctrine. In the wiki's conviction-led contrarianism thread, Karp invokes Emerson's *Self-Reliance* directly — *"for nonconformity, the world whips you with its displeasure"* — and extends Emerson's "Why drag about this corpse of your memory… leave your theory" against the internet's permanent record, which he argues confines us in "a straitjacket of our former selves." So the concept links a 19th-century essay to a present-day argument about thinking for yourself in a world that never forgets.

**Context:** "Self-Reliance" (1841) is Emerson's best-known essay and a foundational text of American individualism, urging readers to trust their own intuition over inherited opinion and social pressure ("Whoso would be a man must be a nonconformist"). It deeply influenced Thoreau and the broader Transcendentalist movement.
