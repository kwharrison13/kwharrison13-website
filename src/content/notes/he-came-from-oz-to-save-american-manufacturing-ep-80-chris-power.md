# He Came From Oz To Save American Manufacturing — EP 80 Chris Power

**Author:** [Ashlee Vance](/notes/ashlee-vance) (host, *Core Memory* podcast)
**URL:** https://www.corememory.com/p/he-came-from-oz-to-save-american-manufacturing-hadrian-chris-power
**One-line:** *[Chris Power](/notes/chris-power), an Australian-born founder, built [Hadrian](/notes/hadrian) into an automated-manufacturing company for aerospace and defense parts after realizing that America's aging, family-run machine shops were about to lose an irreplaceable generation of manufacturing skill.*

## Key claims

- **The founding insight: America's small aerospace/defense suppliers are family-run shops on the verge of a generational skills collapse.** [Chris Power](/notes/chris-power) identified mom-and-pop machine shops supplying automotive and aerospace industries whose aging owner-operators had no successors — their children weren't taking over — threatening a "massive manufacturing crisis."
- **[Hadrian](/notes/hadrian) automates the craft knowledge those shops hold**, building software and machine-driven automation to absorb decades of specialized manufacturing skill, aiming to make parts faster and better than the legacy shops could.
- **America has offshored ~90%+ of manufacturing since the 1980s**, per Power, through CCP subsidies, policy mistakes, and globalization — leaving an industrial base of small suppliers (each under $10M revenue, often owned by ~65-year-olds) that feed prime contractors like [SpaceX](/books/elon-musk-tesla-spacex-and-the-quest-for-a-fantastic-future), Anduril, Lockheed Martin, and Northrop Grumman, with all of their technical staff aging out simultaneously.
- **Power estimates SpaceX spends roughly as much on contract manufacturing as Lockheed** — i.e. even a vertically-integrated "prime" like SpaceX still depends heavily on this fragile small-supplier base, and Power estimates ~85% of defense productive effort sits in that fragile supply chain.
- **The Ukraine war exposed the re-ramp problem**: the U.S. shipped roughly three years of Stinger/Javelin-class munitions inventory to Ukraine in about six weeks, then found it would take years to re-ramp production because the people who knew how were retired.
- **Hadrian's "Opus" platform has six components**: (1) extracting/standardizing data from manufacturing-spec PDFs via AI, (2) automated production-inspection programming per material/alloy, (3) taking over "driving" the machine itself (the human isn't the bottleneck on loading/unloading — they're piloting the machine like an F1 car with no autopilot), (4) coordination/scheduling/inventory/compliance software, and (5)–(6) workforce development that trains people with no manufacturing background (ex-Marines, bus drivers, paralegals) to be productive in ~30 days rather than the ~20 years a traditional welder apprenticeship takes.
- **Hadrian now operates three facilities** — Factory 2 (CNC, at max capacity, scaled in 18 months), Factory X (R&D — welding, additive, sheet metal, foundry/large-format castings), and a new Arizona facility (four times the size of Factory 2, leased July of the prior year, ramped in six months) — with another facility (~2 million square feet, described as the biggest US factory build-out outside Tesla's Gigafactory) announced later that year. Power estimates the company is on a billion-plus-revenue run rate heading into the next year.
- **Power frames reindustrialization as a "fourth turning" problem**: great powers that get complacent offshore heavy industry, and the reckoning arrives generations later in a great-power conflict; he compares the psychological difficulty of the undertaking to a "Manhattan-level project" requiring tens of billions of dollars and a small number of teams capable of both serious engineering and serious capital-raising simultaneously.

## Notable quotes

> "It's kind of like a legacy software company. Hey, we're super successful because we have a great product and sales are good. And then maybe five years in, you've stopped innovating on product and it comes around and bites you. This is just like a multi-generational version of this."

> "It's not systemized, it's like one guy or gal that knows how to do the thing and when they retire you're screwed."

> "You're in a late stage fourth turning trap where you're so successful for such a long time that you've forgotten that like at some point someone needs to pave the roads."

> "You need to have the best people and you have to have like a ludicrous amount of capital to go even make a dent."

> "I don't think the American people really quite grasp how far behind we are. But this is like Manhattan level project complexity. It really is."
