# Tristan Harris

A central figure in the attention-economy critique that runs through Kyle's reading. In [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism), Cal Newport tells Harris's origin story: after a "mind-opening experience at Burning Man," Harris wrote a 144-slide manifesto, "A Call to Minimize Distraction & Respect Users' Attention," and circulated it inside Google, where it spread to thousands — including co-CEO Larry Page, who created the invented title of "product philosopher" for him. But nothing much changed; Newport argues the real friction was simple economics — minimizing distraction and respecting attention would reduce ad revenue. Harris then quit and founded the nonprofit Time Well Spent, demanding technology that "serves us, not advertising," and went public warning about how tech companies "hijack" our minds.

Harris recurs in [The War on Normal People](/books/the-war-on-normal-people) as one of the voices Andrew Yang marshals on technology's costs, quoted with the line: "the best minds of my generation are thinking about how to make people click ads." Across both sources he functions as the same archetype — the insider-turned-critic who names the conflict between user attention and the advertising business model.

**Context:** Tristan Harris is a former Google design ethicist who co-founded the Center for Humane Technology and was prominently featured in the 2020 Netflix documentary *The Social Dilemma*. He is widely associated with popularizing concerns about persuasive design and the "attention economy."
