# Ben Graham

The founding figure of value investing across Kyle's investing corpus, and the intellectual source of several recurring concepts. His book [The Intelligent Investor](/notes/the-intelligent-investor) is the canonical text Kyle's notes return to — [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) calls it "still the best book on investing," crediting it with "the only three ideas you really need": Mr. Market, treating a stock as a piece of a business, and [Margin of Safety](/notes/margin-of-safety). [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) (by [Roger Lowenstein](/notes/roger-lowenstein)) records that "Ben Graham opened the door" for Buffett — giving him "the tools to explore the market's manifold possibilities" plus "an approach that fit his student's temper," the margin-of-safety principle, and detachment from market gyrations. Graham's dictum "You are neither right nor wrong because the crowd disagrees with you" anchors Buffett's facts-over-crowd discipline.

Graham is the origin point of [Value Investing](/notes/value-investing) as a school: buying securities below a conservatively estimated intrinsic value with a margin of safety, a tradition Buffett and [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) later evolved from "cigar butt" deep-bargains toward paying a fair price for great businesses. He is also the source of Kyle's favorite [Margin of Safety](/notes/margin-of-safety) framing — via [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), Graham's line that "the purpose of the margin of safety is to render the forecast unnecessary." [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel) adds a notable qualification: Graham's book is full of wisdom but "questionable" as a how-to guide, because Graham himself kept discarding his own published formulas edition to edition ("these are the formulas that seem to work better now"), making the enduring value the principles and temperament rather than the mechanical screens. Graham's price-vs-value lens is also the foil Buffett uses against [Efficient Market Theory](/notes/efficient-market-theory), lamenting that business schools teach "betas" instead of Graham's texts.

**Context:** Benjamin Graham (1894–1976) was an economist and investor who, with David Dodd, developed value investing at Columbia Business School (*Security Analysis*, 1934; *The Intelligent Investor*, 1949). Warren Buffett was his student and employee; Graham is widely regarded as the "father of value investing."
