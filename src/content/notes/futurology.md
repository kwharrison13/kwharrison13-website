# Futurology

The formal discipline (also "futures studies" / "futurism") of systematically studying possible, probable, and preferable futures — the academic cousin of Kyle's [Historical Futurism](/notes/historical-futurism) thesis. In the Historical Futurism - Research dossier, Kyle dates his first exposure to futurology to the **Institute for the Future (IFTF)**, a 1968 spin-off from the [RAND Corporation](/notes/rand-corporation) that has run a ten-year forecast for fifty years; he credits [Sahil Bloom](/notes/sahil-bloom) with later pointing him to the Futures Studies subreddit. The field became "one of Kyle's portfolio ideas." Where academic futurology emphasizes forecasting and institutional foresight, Kyle's [Historical Futurism](/notes/historical-futurism) reframes it by looking *backward* — at how the past imagined its future — and pairs it with the argument that optimistic [Science Fiction](/notes/science-fiction) is the literature that seeds buildable futures.

The research collects the surrounding ecosystem: futurist organizations (Futurist Foundation, Forum for the Future, r/Futurology), the BBC's futurology coverage, and reference points like Howard P. Segal's *Technological Utopianism in American Culture* (cataloguing futurist fiction 1883–1933).

**Context:** Futurology / futures studies emerged as a recognized field in the mid-20th century around figures like Herman Kahn (Hudson Institute), Alvin Toffler (*Future Shock*), and RAND-derived forecasting methods such as the Delphi technique. It studies long-range scenarios rather than predicting a single future.
