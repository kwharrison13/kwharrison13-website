# Latticework of Mental Models

[Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s central thesis: worldly wisdom comes not from a single discipline but from "a **latticework of mental models** in your head" — onto which you hang your actual and vicarious (reading-derived) experience until "things gradually get to fit together in a way that enhances cognition." The phrasing is verbatim from [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Revisited](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom-revisited), and the same thesis dominates [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), where Kyle's notes flag the fox-vs-hedgehog framing and the explicit mandate "you can't really say 'I'm not interested in math, physics, etc.'" The model is inseparable from [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking): Munger argues that academia's "balkanization" cripples thinkers who can't integrate across departments, since "there are a relatively small number of disciplines and a relatively small number of truly big ideas."

The latticework is also the organizing premise of [The Great Mental Models Volume 1: General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts), which Kyle reads as [Shane Parrish](/notes/shane-parrish)'s "explicit attempt to organize the latticework Munger argues for" — turning the thesis into a curriculum built around [Map vs Territory](/notes/map-vs-territory), [Circle of Competence](/notes/circle-of-competence), [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking), and [Inversion](/notes/inversion). The load-bearing caution there is that every model degrades unless updated against the real-world territory it describes. The concept connects outward to [Polymath](/notes/polymath), [Compound Knowledge](/notes/compound-knowledge), and [Reading](/notes/reading) as the practices that build and maintain the lattice.

**Context:** "Latticework of mental models" is Charlie Munger's term, most associated with his USC and Harvard-Westlake talks collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*. The idea is that broad, cross-disciplinary fluency in a few dozen big ideas (from physics, biology, psychology, economics, math) lets you avoid the "man with a hammer" tendency to force every problem into one familiar tool.
