# Smart Cities

A recurring theme across Kyle's [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) networking calls (mostly a June 2018 sourcing run): using data, sensors, and software to make urban infrastructure more responsive, and the question of how vendors actually sell into and monetize cities. Two of those calls map the supply side cleanly. [Jesse Berst](/notes/jesse-berst) of the [Smart Cities Council](/notes/smart-cities-council) frames the council as a vendor coalition organized around a "triple bottom line" and a framework of 15 verticals, argues "the gold is in the data," and maps the mindshare leaders (Cisco, Microsoft, AT&T, with Intel "priming the pump") plus the "integration gap" — while staying skeptical of procurement platforms, which he says have "never succeeded" (June 28th, 2018 — Jesse Berst (Smart Cities Council)). [Scott Shephard](/notes/scott-shephard) of the Dutch [ARS Traffic & Transport Technology](/notes/ars-traffic-and-transport-technology) comes at it from mobility: traffic management built on a big-data model of the Dutch transportation system, with the conviction that city data should be treated as interconnected nodes rather than silos, and that winning the market requires bottom-up engagement with subject-matter experts even though that stretches the sales cycle (June 28th, 2018 — Scott Shephard (ARS Traffic & Transport Technology)).

Across these notes the through-line is less "smart" technology than the commercial and procurement reality of cities as buyers: opening new revenue channels via public-private partnerships, the structural mismatch between top-down platform sales and the bottom-up expertise that actually closes deals, and data aggregation as the durable advantage. The motivating scale, per Berst, is that cities are ~1% of land but 55% of population, 70% of energy use, and 80% of greenhouse emissions.

**Context:** "Smart cities" broadly refers to using IoT sensors, connected infrastructure, and data analytics to manage urban services — traffic, energy, water, public safety — more efficiently. Major IT and telecom vendors (Cisco, IBM, Microsoft, Siemens) have pursued it as a market for over a decade.
