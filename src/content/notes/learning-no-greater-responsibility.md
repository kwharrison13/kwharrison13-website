# Learning, No Greater Responsibility

"Learning, No Greater Responsibility" is one of Kyle's **essays-in-progress** — a thesis about self-directed, lifelong learning as a personal and moral duty — and it functions in the wiki as the spine that his reading repeatedly substantiates. [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) is the most explicit anchor: Kyle reads Adler and Van Doren "with constant religious-text overlay and explicit cross-reference" to this essay, calling it "the essay-in-progress this read repeatedly substantiates." The thread spans the practical and the philosophical: [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required) connects it to the read-read-read discipline of becoming a better writer ("few people are born with an innate talent for writing; most get better through practice"), while [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) places it alongside [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) and the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) as the responsible counter to a culture that confuses opinion with knowledge.

Two pillars supply the moral and aspirational charge. The **gospel pillar** comes from the 2019 New Testament Come Follow Me intro week, "We Are Responsible for Our Own Learning", built on [David A. Bednar](/notes/david-a-bednar)'s charge to be "doers of the word and not simply hearers who are only acted upon" — learning by faith, not passive reception, as an act of [Agency](/notes/agency). The **secular pillar** is [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address)'s "be a learning machine" — "I constantly see people rise in life who are not the smartest… but they are learning machines"; "you can progress only when you learn the method of learning." [Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) models the ideal in a single life: an "insatiable appetite for learning," reading widely and grilling every expert he met — "the learning process is an endless process… I believe in growth."
