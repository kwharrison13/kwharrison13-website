# Sunzi

Sunzi (Sun Tzu) is the author of [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war), the ancient Chinese military treatise in Kyle's library. The book's notes frame him not as an abstract theorist but as "a practical soldier" who "will have none of the 'bookish theoric.'" His recurring counsel is to reject fixed principles in favor of reading the actual situation — "you must be guided by the actions of the enemy" — illustrated by Wellington at Waterloo refusing to fix his plans until he saw Bonaparte's. Other ideas the notes attach to him: winning whole rather than destroying ("the best thing of all is to take the enemy's country whole and intact"), controlling a large force being the same problem as controlling a few ("merely a question of dividing up their numbers"), and resolving domestic troubles before facing an external foe. These thread into Kyle's tags on [Planning](/notes/planning), [Competition](/notes/competition), [Game Theory](/notes/game-theory), [Leadership](/notes/leadership), [Incentives](/notes/incentives), and [Patience](/notes/patience).

**Context:** Sun Tzu (traditionally 6th–5th century BCE) is the named author of *The Art of War*; modern scholarship treats the text as a compiled work of the Warring States period. "Sunzi" is the pinyin romanization of the same name.
