# The Fall of Adam of Eve

In Kyle's Plan of Salvation — Topical Study, the Fall of Adam and Eve is one of the "three pillars" of the plan, standing between [Creation](/notes/creation) and the [Atonement](/notes/atonement). The study captures the Latter-day Saint reading of the Fall as a necessary, two-directional event rather than a pure catastrophe: quoting Cowley & Whitney, "The fall had a two-fold direction: downward, yet forward. It brought man into the world and set his feet upon progression's highway." The framing question in the notes — "Were Adam and Eve created to fail?" — points to the doctrine that the two commandments in Eden (multiply and replenish the earth; do not eat the fruit of the tree of knowledge) were deliberately in tension.

The Fall is also the source of the mortal "natural man" who, per [Mosiah 3](/notes/mosiah-3):19, "has been an enemy to God... from the fall of Adam" ([2 Nephi 9](/notes/2-nephi-9):28–29). Because of the Fall, the study notes, we stand today as fallen man — "carnal, sensual, devilish" ([Mosiah 16](/notes/mosiah-16):3) — which is precisely the condition the Atonement is meant to redeem.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology the Fall of Adam and Eve is treated as a foreordained and ultimately positive step (a "fortunate fall") that made mortality, agency, and human progression possible — a notably more optimistic reading than the traditional Christian doctrine of original sin.

## Where this appears

- Plan of Salvation — Topical Study — one of the three pillars (Creation · Fall · Atonement); framed as "downward, yet forward" and as the origin of the fallen "natural man."
- Genesis 3 — the scriptural account of the Fall: the serpent, the fruit, the curse, and the expulsion from Eden.
