# Alan Mulally

Alan Mulally appears in Kyle's corpus through [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), where he is the recurring positive counterexample to [Boeing](/notes/boeing)'s drift toward financial engineering. As the leader of the 777 program — "Boeing's Camelot," a roughly $12B bet that paid off as orders topped two thousand — Mulally embodied an information-first engineering culture. The book records his maxims "no secrets" and "the data will set you free," and an engineer's recollection that "the only thing that will make me rip off your head and shit down your neck is withholding information." The 777 program institutionalized this by welcoming bad news ("every airplane development program has twenty thousand surprises") and pulling early customers (the "Gang of Eight") into design decisions.

In Kyle's notes Mulally functions as the road not taken. He resisted stack-ranking layoffs ("if you hire a team that you think is really good... why in the hell would you eliminate 10 percent every year?"), but watched the culture shift toward "shareholder value" — "the idealism just went out." Condit fired the higher-ranking Woodard to install him, then Mulally himself was ultimately pushed out; a colleague calls letting him go "a big mistake," noting "he brought technical excellence to Ford." His old rule about surfacing problems is explicitly inverted later in the book, when a leader instead "encouraged the pilots to give her good news, not bad news."

**Context:** Alan Mulally (b. 1945) is an American engineer and executive who led Boeing Commercial Airplanes before becoming CEO of Ford Motor Company (2006–2014), where he is widely credited with engineering Ford's turnaround through the financial crisis without a government bailout, partly via his "Working Together" management system and weekly color-coded business reviews.
