# Desirable Difficulties

Desirable difficulties is the learning-science thesis at the core of [Range](/books/range): the idea that making learning *harder* in specific ways produces knowledge that is more durable and more transferable, even though it slows you down and hurts performance in the short term. The book attributes the phrase to psychologist Robert Bjork (coined in 1994) and lists the key techniques — spacing, interleaving, testing, and "making-connections" problems. As Range puts it: "Desirable difficulties like testing and spacing make knowledge stick. It becomes durable. Desirable difficulties like making connections and interleaving make knowledge flexible, useful for problems that never appeared in training. All slow down learning and make performance suffer, in the short term."

The central warning is to "avoid interpreting current performance as learning" — fast, fluent performance during study often indexes "fast but fleeting progress" rather than mastery. Range generalizes this into an argument against early hyperspecialization: a head start "comes fast, but deep learning is slow," and "the slowest growth occurs for the most complex skills." The concept ties into the book's wider cluster — [Spaced Repetition](/notes/spaced-repetition) (spacing as a desirable difficulty), [Learning](/notes/learning), and [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) (breadth and analogical reasoning beating narrow early specialization).

**Context:** "Desirable difficulties" is a term from cognitive psychologist Robert A. Bjork (UCLA), describing conditions that impede short-term performance during learning but improve long-term retention and transfer — spacing, interleaving, varied practice, and retrieval (testing). David Epstein's *Range* (2019) builds much of its case for generalists on this research.
