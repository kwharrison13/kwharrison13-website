# Mobility-on-Demand

Mobility-on-Demand (MOD) enters Kyle's corpus through his June 2018 call with Adam Cohen, a shared-mobility researcher at [UC Berkeley](/notes/uc-berkeley). Cohen was leading the **independent national evaluation of the USDOT Mobility-on-Demand "sandbox"** — an $8M federal grant program testing eleven distinct new-mobility projects, ranging from public-private partnerships to integrated trip/travel planners. His team was producing impact assessments, lessons learned, and best practices to be published the following year.

The call situates MOD inside a broader skepticism: Cohen is less bullish on autonomous vehicles than the industry (citing legal and institutional drag) and argues the more decisive variable is **land use** — walkable communities, street-grid design, and the future of parking. His stated end-state for on-demand mobility is a fully integrated system where a connected vehicle knows where you need to be and dispatches itself to pick you up, drop you off, and park or recharge automatically.

**Context:** "Mobility on Demand" was a U.S. Department of Transportation / Federal Transit Administration initiative (the FY2016 MOD Sandbox) funding pilot projects that integrate on-demand, traveler-centric transportation services — a public-sector cousin of the [Mobility-as-a-Service](/notes/mobility-as-a-service) concept.
