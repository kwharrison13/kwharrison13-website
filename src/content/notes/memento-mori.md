# Memento Mori

In [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), Ryan Holiday uses *memento mori* as the reminder that "you've got to know the time precisely because you will never know when it's going to run out on you." Time is framed as "the only truly nonrenewable resource we have" — no one is making more of it, and once lost it cannot be recovered. The book's hardest line drives the point home: "time that is wasted is also wasting us. When we kill time, we are killing ourselves."

The wiki page links this directly to [Compounding](/notes/compounding): the same way small amounts of interest applied over a long enough period become large, the tiny increments of how we spend time accumulate into a self over time ("everything you do is cumulative" / kaizen). It is the Stoic backbone of the book's case for [Self-discipline](/notes/self-discipline) in little things — the discipline of time, in W. H. Auden's framing, being the surest way to discipline passion.

**Context:** *Memento mori* ("remember that you must die") is an ancient Stoic and later Christian meditative practice — a deliberate reminder of mortality meant to sharpen one's sense of what matters and how to spend finite time.

## Where this appears

- [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) — explicit passage on time as the only nonrenewable resource ("when we kill time, we are killing ourselves"), tied to compounding and daily self-discipline.
