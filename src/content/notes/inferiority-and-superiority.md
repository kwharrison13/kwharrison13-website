# Inferiority and Superiority

In the Adlerian psychology of [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked), the **feeling of inferiority** is not a disease to be cured but a stimulant to healthy striving and growth. The book traces the term to [Alfred Adler](/notes/alfred-adler)'s German *Minderwertigkeitsgefühl* — literally a feeling of having less worth — and stresses that it is always *subjective*: inferiority arises only through comparison with others (the philosopher's example of feeling short only because someone taller exists). Because we inhabit a subjective world, the interpretation can be altered at will. A *healthy* feeling of inferiority comes not from comparing oneself to others but from comparison with one's own ideal self.

The book sharply distinguishes the feeling of inferiority from the **inferiority complex** — using that feeling as an excuse ("I'm not educated, so I can't succeed") — and from the **pursuit of superiority**, which Adler frames not as climbing over others but as "taking a single step forward on one's own feet" along a flat plane where everyone walks at their own pace. Competition is the trap: once you cast life as victory and defeat, "you start to see each and every person, everyone in the whole world, as your enemy," and even constant winners "never have a moment's peace." Kyle's own note maps this pairing onto [Amy Chua](/notes/amy-chua)'s tiger-mother dynamic — the experience of simultaneously feeling chosen (superiority lifted) and rejected (inferiority inflamed) — reading the two as a single mechanism of identity formation rather than opposites.

**Context:** Inferiority and the "striving for superiority" are foundational constructs in Alfred Adler's individual psychology; *The Courage To Be Disliked* (Ichiro Kishimi and Fumitake Koga, 2013) popularized them through a Socratic dialogue between a philosopher and a youth.
