# Howard Marks

Across Kyle's investing research, Howard Marks appears as one half of a recurring pair with [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) — the two "forerunners" who turned investor communication into a durable edge. In [The Storytelling of Investing](/essays/the-storytelling-of-investing) research and on the [Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital) page, [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel)'s flywheel runs through them: "Buffett and Marks used content to instill trust, trust gave them permanent capital, and permanent capital gave them a massive financial advantage over other investors." They "were using their ability to communicate as a bridge towards trust" — a thread Kyle Roam-tags to [Live the Library](/notes/live-the-library) (learning through others' written records).

Marks also surfaces as a source of hard performance discipline. In [The Death of a Venture Fund](/essays/death) research, Kyle leans on Marks's 2006 memo "You Can't Eat IRR" — the argument that IRR misleads on dollar size, time frame, and relation to actual cash returned, so that in a long up-market IRR becomes "a vanity metric" and the only return that matters is cash in LPs' pockets. He is listed among the people threaded through the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) and the [Morgan Housel: Naked Brands Interview Series](/notes/morgan-housel-naked-brands-interview-series) notes as part of this same school of trust-through-writing investors.

**Context:** Howard Marks (b. 1946) is co-founder and co-chairman of Oaktree Capital Management, a distressed-debt and credit investment firm. He is widely known for his investor memos and the book *The Most Important Thing*, and is frequently cited alongside Warren Buffett as a model of the long-form letter as an investing communication discipline.
