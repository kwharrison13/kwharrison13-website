# Daily Routine

In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) the daily routine is treated as an instrument of self-improvement rather than mere habit. The biography quotes two detailed schedules verbatim — one from Adams's diplomatic years, one from the White House. The young Adams resolved to be "diligent, disciplined, scholarly," waking at six to read "books of instruction," translating Dutch state papers, walking, dining, and reading "works of amusement" before bed by eleven. He kept *monthly accounts* of this schedule "for much of the rest of his life — a spur to self-improvement and a reminder of discarded resolutions."

The routine is bound up with [Solitude](/notes/solitude) and [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery): as president he "awoke in darkness almost every day," rising as early as four in summer to take a long solitary walk, a habit cultivated for years. After the presidency he "awoke before dawn every day, read the Bible and Cicero, went swimming and riding," and wrote down "absolutely everything in his journal" — yet still found it "wasn't enough" ("Shall I never do better?"). In this corpus, the daily routine is the visible discipline behind a polymath's lifelong output, tightly linked to his [Journals](/notes/journals).
