# Civic Engagement

In Kyle's notes, civic engagement is most concretely embodied by [CivicLift](/notes/civiclift), [Evan Dobos](/notes/evan-dobos)'s platform built to "manage their civic engagement efforts" for cities and towns. The May 2018 networking call frames the problem the category addresses: in most towns it is "nobody's job to aggregate all the events going on" and package them for residents, and many New England cities struggle to keep millennial citizens. CivicLift's value proposition is to aggregate content from any source — town sites, social media, libraries, small businesses, city halls — into one place, and to surface economic-development data insights for municipal staff (e.g. telling an economic-development department that 70 people searched for coffee at a given intersection, a signal for where to seed and support businesses). Experiments like a "cashmob" voting contest doubled as both engagement mechanics and a way to learn why residents favor particular businesses.

The notes also expose civic engagement's hard edge as a market: selling engagement tooling to towns means long municipal sales cycles (Danbury required eight separate presentations, and re-election turnover forced re-selling to new officials), pushing CivicLift toward a pivot away from towns as the direct customer. In the broader wiki, civic engagement is one of the threads in [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) — alongside city procurement and municipal data — and overlaps with [GovTech](/notes/govtech) as software sold into local government.

**Context:** Civic engagement broadly refers to the ways individuals participate in their communities and local government — attending events, supporting local businesses, and interacting with civic institutions. A wave of "GovTech" / civic-tech startups in the 2010s built software to digitize and aggregate this participation at the city and town level.
