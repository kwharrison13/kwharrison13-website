# Learning in Public

Learning in Public is one of Kyle's own recurring theses: doing all of one's intake (reading, listening, tweeting) with the purpose of feeding *public* thinking, which changes how you do all of it. [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) supplies the strongest articulation — "Studying, done properly, is [Research](/notes/research)," and "an idea kept private is as good as one you never had... There is no such thing as a history of unwritten ideas." Kyle's own note records the payoff directly: "by asserting something you invited the world to respond with what is right or wrong about your idea. You invite dialogue." The book threads this through [Tim Berners-Lee](/notes/tim-berners-lee) (the lament that the human race's knowledge sits unshared on private databases) and [Ward Cunningham](/notes/ward-cunningham)'s maxim that "the best way to get the right answer on the internet is not to ask a question; it's to post the wrong answer."

The concept has a sharp negative case study in [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci): his "great failing was the inverse of his great gift." He pursued knowledge "for its own sake, and for his own personal joy," with "no real understanding of the way in which the growth of knowledge was a cumulative and collaborative process" — so his unpublished treatises diminished his historical impact for want of a disciplined partner who could help him ship. On Kyle's [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) page, Learning in Public is named as the pedagogy-and-learning side of that umbrella concept (distinct from software licensing), tied to his own practice in [Investing 101 2.0](/notes/investing-101-2-0) and named directly by [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public). A live tension surfaces in Kyle's [Munger](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) note: "when is it okay to speak declaratively on topics in which you're not an expert?"
