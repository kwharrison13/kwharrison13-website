# David Gelles

Author of [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism), the book in which [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch) is cast as the central antagonist — the executive whose "Welchism" of downsizing, dealmaking, and [Financialization](/notes/financialization) reshaped corporate America. Gelles's thesis, as captured on the book page, treats the financialization of the economy as the diagnosis: a "line of demarcation" around 1981 (when Welch took over GE) after which wages, manufacturing jobs, and union representation went "off the rails" while executive pay and buybacks soared. The book draws on figures like William Lazonick on share buybacks and Roger Martin (former Rotman dean) on Welch's mindset, and extends the indictment to [Boeing](/notes/boeing) as the marquee case of Welchism imported by GE alumni. Gelles also frames [Milton Friedman](/notes/milton-friedman)'s 1970 "social responsibility of business is to increase its profits" essay as Welchism's intellectual charter. Kyle flagged the book as a quake book despite a sparse in-text annotation pass — the single note being a research prompt ("What is it today?") on Gelles's data point that financial services rose from under 5% of GDP in 1980 to nearly twice that.

**Context:** David Gelles is a New York Times reporter and columnist (the "Corner Office" column) who covers business and climate; *The Man Who Broke Capitalism: How Jack Welch Gutted the Heartland and Crushed the Soul of Corporate America—and How to Undo His Legacy* was published in 2022.
