# Boeing

In Kyle's corpus, Boeing is the case-in-chief for what happens when an engineering-driven industrial culture is hollowed out by financial engineering — the central subject of [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) and a recurring anti-pattern Kyle reads against his [Anduril](/notes/anduril) thesis. Peter Robison's account, as Kyle synthesizes it, has three threads: a **Reagan-era shareholder-primacy diagnosis** (the same financialization story [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism) tells about [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch)'s GE, here imported into Boeing via the 1997 [McDonnell Douglas](/notes/mcdonnell-douglas) merger — what one executive called "McDonnell Douglas bought Boeing with Boeing's money"); **outsourced profits as a systematic failure mode** ([John Hart-Smith](/notes/john-hart-smith)'s "Out-Sourced Profits" paper, on how parceling out construction impoverished the manufacturer while enriching suppliers); and the standout line Kyle flags — *"People didn't spontaneously grow less loyal. Companies systematically stopped being loyal to employees."* The book traces $30B+ poured into buybacks during 737 MAX development while R&D was cut, culminating in two crashes, a two-year grounding, and CEO [Dennis Muilenburg](/notes/dennis-muilenburg)'s ouster.

Within the [Anduril](/notes/anduril) page, Boeing is explicitly named the contrast company — the hollowed-out incumbent against which Anduril's founder-led, speed-of-delivery model is positioned. Kyle's marginal notes throughout *Flying Blind* repeatedly tag passages `#Anduril`, reading Boeing's decline (and the parallel McNamara-era defense bloat in [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) / [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) / [Chip War](/books/chip-war)) as a single argument about American industrial decay.

**Context:** Boeing is one of the world's largest aerospace manufacturers and defense contractors, maker of commercial jetliners (737, 747, 777, 787) and major military programs.
