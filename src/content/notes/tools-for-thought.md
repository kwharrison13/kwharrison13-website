# Tools For Thought

The intellectual and product lineage Kyle returns to whenever he thinks about *designing new mediums for understanding* — software and systems built "out of" how people actually learn and think, rather than inheriting the [transmissionist](/notes/transmissionism) assumptions of books and lectures. The canonical text in Kyle's notes is [Andy Matuschak](/notes/andy-matuschak)'s [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work), which argues that books and lectures both fail because neither has any explicit cognitive model; the proposed fix is to invent mediums (like [Quantum Country](/notes/quantum-country), which Matuschak built with [Michael Nielsen](/notes/michael-nielsen)) that interleave prose with [Spaced Repetition](/notes/spaced-repetition) so that "reading" becomes the same as "understanding."

For Kyle the theme is also a venture-sourcing lens. He met repeatedly with [Conor White-Sullivan](/notes/conor-white-sullivan) of [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research) during 2021–2022 while sourcing and diligencing tools-for-thought companies, and the August 18th, 2021 Valued Empyrean conversation traces the program's deeper roots: Conor pointed Kyle to [Douglas Engelbart](/notes/douglas-engelbart) (the "Mother of All Demos," the invention of the mouse and hypertext, the Engelbart Hypothesis on improving improvement processes) and [Ted Nelson](/notes/ted-nelson)'s eulogy for Engelbart as the founding figures of the field. The January 2022 call with Michael Truell (then at MIT working on tools for thought) and the 1x1 with Luke Piette (on [Ideaflow](/notes/ideaflow), [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research), and brain interfaces) extend the same sourcing thread.

**Context:** "Tools for thought" is a phrase popularized by Howard Rheingold (and rooted in the work of Douglas Engelbart, Vannevar Bush, and Ted Nelson) for software meant to augment human reasoning. It became a movement label around 2020 for projects like Roam Research, Andy Matuschak and Michael Nielsen's mnemonic-medium experiments, and networked-notes apps.
