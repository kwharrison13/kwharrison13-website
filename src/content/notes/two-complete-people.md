# Two Complete People

Kyle's tag for a recurring principle he drew out of the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters): in both business partnership and marriage, **"don't look for someone you can fix, or whose glaring flaws you think you can patch over with your own strengths. Find people who will excel with or without you."** The note attaches to [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s observation that Berkshire's managers "just came that way" — Buffett's job is merely to identify already-talented people and give them room — which Kyle reads, explicitly, as the same rule that applies to choosing a spouse.

The idea is one pole of the [Partnership](/notes/partnership) concept: the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) supply the positive counterpart to partnership-as-multiplier, with the Buffett–[Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) relationship ("interchangeable in business decisions," "the spine" across the Berkshire reads) as the worked example. "Two complete people" is the requirement that makes that multiplier real — two people each whole on their own, compounding together rather than one propping up the other.

## Where this appears

- [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) — the source: "find people who will excel with or without you," tagged in both a marriage analogy and the "working with people you respect / finding multipliers" takeaway.
- [Partnership](/notes/partnership) — sibling concept; "two complete people" is the positive pole where genuine mutual respect makes a partnership compound.
