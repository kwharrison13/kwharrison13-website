# Authority-Misinfluence Tendency

The Authority-Misinfluence Tendency is one of the psychological tendencies in [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s [Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) — the human disposition to follow authority figures, often into serious error. Munger's grounded illustration is [Stanley Milgram](/notes/stanley-milgram)'s post-WWII obedience experiment, in which a man posing as an authority figure tricked many ordinary people into believing they were administering massive, torturous electric shocks to innocent strangers; Munger reads the result as "contributed to by [Authority-Misinfluence Tendency]." His practical implication, stated in the same talk, is that "a dominant authority figure will often be hard to remove," so one should "be careful whom you appoint to power."

**Context:** In Munger's framework — drawn from his capstone talk in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — humans are evolutionarily wired to follow leaders, which usually serves the group but produces catastrophic outcomes when the authority is wrong or malign. It overlaps with the social-proof and doubt-avoidance tendencies in his system of biases behind misjudgment.
