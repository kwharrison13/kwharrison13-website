# Sanjay Bhargava

Sanjay Bhargava appears in Kyle's notes as the early X.com engineer whose "random deposit" account-verification scheme is the canonical example on the [First Principles Thinking](/notes/first-principles-thinking) page. As told in [The Founders](/books/the-founders), [Elon Musk](/notes/elon-musk) recruited him aggressively — "No, no, I'm buying you a ticket, you've got to come down right now" — and Bhargava became part of the hundred-hour-week founding crew sketching the product "until two or three o'clock in the morning." His breakthrough came from reasoning by analogy to Claude Shannon's signal/noise theory: X.com needed a "cleaner, quicker signal" than voided checks or faxes to confirm bank-account ownership, so the company would generate its own one-time code by sending two random sub-dollar deposits (e.g. $0.35 and $0.07 → passcode "3507"). The user reads the amounts off their statement to prove access.

Kyle frames this as first-principles reasoning by a non-domain-expert — engineer [Santosh Janardhan](/notes/santosh-janardhan) credits the win precisely to the team's lack of banking expertise: they "had no preconceived notions… and converted [fraud] to a tractable, mathematical thing." The innovation endured: Musk called it "a fundamental breakthrough," [David Sacks](/notes/david-sacks) likened it to Velcro ("an idea you wish you had thought of"), and random deposit is now standard across banking.
