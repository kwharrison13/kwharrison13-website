# Flannery O'Connor

Across Kyle's corpus, Flannery O'Connor surfaces through two recurring quotations rather than as a biographical subject. The first — "I write because I don't know what I think until I read what I say" — is the load-bearing one: it appears in [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), in the September 13th, 2022 — Harry Stebbings Podcast notes, and is invoked at the very top of The First Principles of Faithful Pragmatism as Kyle's own justification for writing to structure his thinking. It anchors the wiki's writing-to-think thread (the same impulse behind [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) and the Zettelkasten method).

The second quotation appears in [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt), where O'Connor is cited on the cost of faith: "Religion costs. They think faith is a big electric blanket, when of course it's a cross." Together the two mentions place her at the intersection of Kyle's two largest themes — disciplined writing as a tool of thought, and a clear-eyed, costly view of faith.

**Context:** Flannery O'Connor (1925–1964) was an American novelist and short-story writer from Georgia, a devout Catholic whose Southern Gothic fiction (Wise Blood, A Good Man Is Hard to Find) is preoccupied with grace, sin, and grotesque moments of spiritual reckoning.
