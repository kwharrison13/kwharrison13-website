# Genentech

Genentech is the venture bet at the center of [Tom Perkins](/notes/tom-perkins)'s legend in Kyle's notes. In [The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future), Sebastian Mallaby uses Genentech (alongside Tandem) to illustrate [Perkins's law](/notes/perkins-s-law) — *"market risk is inversely proportional to technical risk"* — because by devising a strategy to isolate and neutralize the "white-hot risks," Perkins transformed a daunting bet into "an irresistible one." Perkins privately estimated Genentech's odds not at one in a hundred but a bit under fifty-fifty. Kleiner & Perkins backed it knowing it was "highly speculative," because, as Eugene Kleiner wrote, "we are in the business of making highly speculative investments."

The book also credits Perkins with shaping Genentech's culture: he was "the first venture capitalist to revel unashamedly in the role of promoter and front man," signaling to the scientists that they had left academia and joined something glamorous. Mallaby pairs Genentech with Atari and Apple to argue that follow-on VCs only wrote checks once the early risks were neutralized — and uses it as evidence that great VCs "stamp their will" on portfolio companies rather than passively showing up.

**Context:** Genentech, founded in 1976 by venture capitalist Robert Swanson and biochemist Herbert Boyer, was one of the first biotechnology companies and pioneered the commercial use of recombinant DNA. It was an early Kleiner Perkins investment and is now a member of the Roche group.
