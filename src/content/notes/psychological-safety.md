# Psychological Safety

In [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), psychological safety is identified as the load-bearing condition for real learning on a team. Kyle's notes frame learning as a four-step loop — gain knowledge → [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection) → [Critical Thinking](/notes/critical-thinking) → feedback — that most people never complete, halting at "gaining knowledge." Closing the loop depends on [Peer-to-Peer Learning](/notes/peer-to-peer-learning) and honest feedback, which in turn require psychological safety and a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility).

The book grounds this in Google's research: "psychological safety, or a shared belief that team members feel safe enough to express themselves honestly and openly without fear of judgment or retribution, was the key ingredient in binding team members together." In other words, the willingness to admit ignorance and give/receive candid feedback — the behaviors learning depends on — only emerges when people feel safe enough to be vulnerable.

**Context:** The term was popularized by Harvard's Amy Edmondson and brought to wide attention by Google's Project Aristotle, which studied what made teams effective and found psychological safety the strongest single predictor.
