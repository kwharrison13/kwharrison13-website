# Warren Buffett

The investor at the center of Kyle's capital-allocation reading, traced most directly through [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) (Roger Lowenstein) and the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters). The biography follows his arc from [Benjamin Graham](/notes/ben-graham)-style "cigar butt" bargains to buying great businesses at fair prices — an evolution Kyle attributes to [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) and [Philip Fisher](/notes/phillip-fisher), who "stressed good, well-managed companies as distinct from statistically cheap ones." Buffett is the counter-archetype to [Berkshire Hathaway](/notes/berkshire-hathaway)'s own scale: he demanded that operating managers convince him they could do more with a retained dollar than he could elsewhere, and applied the same test to himself ("if he and Munger could not find superior investments, it would be time for Berkshire to stop growing and pay dividends"). His investing temperament — evaluating a stock as a share of a business rather than a blip on a screen, with "no exit strategies" — is the lens Kyle carries into venture.

[The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money) supplies the through-line Kyle weights most heavily: Buffett's edge is *time*, not stock-picking genius. "His fortune isn't due to just being a good investor, but being a good investor since he was literally a child" — survival plus longevity (investing from age 10 to 89) is what made [Compounding](/notes/compounding) work wonders. The same book quotes his 2008 pledge to run Berkshire "with more than ample cash… I will not trade even a night's sleep for the chance of extra profits." [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism) complicates the hagiography, casting Buffett as the operational-ownership counter to "Welchism" while noting Berkshire laid off 13,000 workers in 2020 — a tension Kyle's notes hold rather than resolve.

**Context:** Warren Buffett (b. 1930) is the chairman and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway and one of history's most successful investors, known for value investing, long-term ownership of operating businesses, and his widely read annual shareholder letters.
