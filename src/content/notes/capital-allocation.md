# Capital Allocation

The decision of where to deploy a company's earnings — the discipline Kyle's reading treats as the central job of an owner-operator. The richest source is the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), where [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) frames it as his "one subject": "The decision to retain earnings is the same as evaluating a new investment" — capital should flow to "the greenest possible pastures," and "what is smart at one price is dumb at another." Buffett prizes Berkshire's *flexibility* — measuring operating businesses against marketable securities, taking cash thrown off by See's Candies or Business Wire and redeploying it into BNSF — as the structural advantage that lets a [Holding Company](/notes/holding-company) funnel 80%+ of subsidiary earnings to the center "for use in new business and investment opportunities." Kyle's margin note distills it: a holding company stays "laser focused on Capital Allocation and returns regardless of where in the [Family of Companies](/notes/family-of-companies) they leave the capital."

In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) the same idea appears as Buffett reserving capital-deployment decisions to himself, and it threads into [Compounding](/notes/compounding) — deferring the tax man by holding. The concept recurs as a measure of operator quality: [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) (adventur.es / [Brent Beshore](/notes/brent-beshore)) sets return expectations of 20–35% for smaller private-equity, search funds, and individuals, contrasting [Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital) against the short-term decisions forced by fixed-life fund schedules; and [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes) ([Stephen A. Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-a-schwarzman)) ties it to deriving value from ownership rather than price.

Its dark mirror is [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism), where [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch)-style [Financialization](/notes/financialization) redirects profits to investors via record [Share Buybacks](/notes/share-buybacks) rather than R&D, wages, or reinvestment — capital allocation used to extract rather than compound. Buffett himself nods to the canonical text: he recommends *The Outsiders* by William Thorndike as "an outstanding book about CEOs who excelled at capital allocation."
