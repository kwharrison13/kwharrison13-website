# Maria Montessori

Education-reform figure cited in [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning), TIME's special edition on how the brain learns. The piece places her in a 50–60-year lineage of education reformers alongside [John Dewey](/notes/john-dewey) and [Jean Piaget](/notes/jean-piaget), crediting her with **stressing the development of a child's own initiative and natural ability**. Her method is invoked as concrete evidence that pedagogy compounds: the piece reports that [Montessori](/notes/montessori)-educated kids showed different patterns of behavior and brain activity than kids in traditional schools — "Montessori students were more effective at directing their own learning, and by adolescence were much faster and more likely to correct their own mistakes."

**Context:** Maria Montessori (1870–1952) was an Italian physician and educator who developed the Montessori method, a child-centered approach built on self-directed activity, hands-on learning, and prepared environments. It remains one of the most widely adopted alternative-education philosophies worldwide.
