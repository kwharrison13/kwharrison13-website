# Carbon Footprint

Carbon footprint — the total greenhouse-gas emissions attributable to a company across Scopes 1, 2, and 3 — is the measurement layer at the center of the Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 dossier and [Watershed Climate](/notes/watershed-climate)'s product. In that material, "measuring your carbon footprint" (carbon accounting) is the first of Watershed's three modules, followed by emission-reduction planning and [Carbon Offsets](/notes/carbon-offsets). The hard part is data: a company's footprint is "buried in thousands of purchase orders, utility bills, corporate reports," mostly stored in PDFs and spreadsheets — Sweetgreen started by uploading its food purchases, Square a bill of materials, Stripe its AWS bill. Watershed's normalizing of that data (with a data network effect — each new bill processed improves accuracy for the next customer) is treated as the moat.

The diligence repeatedly emphasizes that **Scope 3 is the dominant share** of most companies' footprints (often 80–90%), since "carbon emitted producing or using your product is your carbon" — and that "every company inherits the carbon footprint of their suppliers" ([Christiana Figueres](/notes/christiana-figueres)). Expert calls underscore how immature the underlying data is: one customer ([Madeline Rotman](/notes/madeline-rotman) at [Imperfect Foods](/notes/imperfect-foods)) notes "no company has this data sorted," so high-touch service is really a function of messy customer data, not the software. Reporting aligns to the [GHG Protocol](/notes/ghg-protocol) standard, and the thesis hinges on regulatory tailwinds (UK/EU disclosure requirements, anticipated SEC guidance) making footprint measurement mandatory.

**Context:** A carbon footprint is the total greenhouse-gas emissions caused directly and indirectly by an entity, conventionally partitioned under the GHG Protocol into Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (purchased energy), and Scope 3 (all other value-chain emissions, upstream and downstream).
