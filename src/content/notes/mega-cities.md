# Mega Cities

In Kyle's reading of [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis), "mega cities" names one of the two failure modes of urbanization he wants to avoid — the dense, superstar metropolises whose clustering of talent drives growth but also drives inequality and prices everyone else out. Responding to [Richard Florida](/notes/richard-florida), Kyle's note frames the choice starkly: "Instead of [suburban sprawl](/notes/suburban-sprawl) or [mega cities](/notes/mega-cities) of inequality, we need [micro cities](/notes/micro-cities) deliberately designed." Mega cities are thus the inequality-producing extreme on one end, sprawl the inefficient extreme on the other, with deliberately designed [Micro Cities](/notes/micro-cities) as the throughline answer.

The concept connects directly to the [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) / [Prosperable](/notes/prosperable) project and the [EPCOT](/notes/epcot) hub-and-spoke idea from [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) — alternatives to concentrating ever more people and capital in a handful of crowded, expensive cores.

**Context:** "Megacity" is the demographic term for an urban area with a population over ten million. The coming wave of urbanization — Florida notes another 7–8 billion people moving to cities, mostly in the developing world — is concentrated heavily in such mega cities.
