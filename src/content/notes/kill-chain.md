# Kill Chain

In the [Anduril](/notes/anduril) page, the "kill chain" is the organizing metaphor for the company's whole thesis — and is the networked-lattice idea drawn from the book [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) (distinct from this concept page). The argument: "the exquisite-platform paradigm (carriers, bombers, the F-35) is the wrong shape for the next conflict; the right shape is many cheaper, more autonomous nodes networked into a 'kill chain' lattice." The future of military power, on this view, "belongs to autonomous, networked, attritable systems built at startup speed, not to a handful of exquisite legacy platforms" — and Anduril's Lattice software is the connective tissue that wires those nodes into a coherent chain.

The concept sits at the center of Kyle's defense-reading cluster: book sources framing the thesis include [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain), [Chip War](/books/chip-war), [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works), [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), and [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future), and it connects to the [Dependencies](/notes/dependencies) and [American Dynamism](/notes/american-dynamism) threads on the Anduril page.

**Context:** In military doctrine, the "kill chain" is the sequence of steps to engage a target — find, fix, track, target, engage, assess (often abbreviated F2T2EA). The strategic argument Kyle tracks holds that whoever can *complete* that chain faster and more resiliently — by distributing it across many cheap, software-networked sensors and shooters — wins, displacing reliance on a few high-cost "exquisite" platforms.
