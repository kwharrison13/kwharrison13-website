# Antifragility

[Nassim Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb)'s concept, quoted directly in [The Great Mental Models Volume 1 — General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts), sorts objects into three categories by their response to volatility and unpredictability: things harmed by it, things neutral to it, and things that benefit from it. The third category is *antifragile* — "like a package that wants to be mishandled" — and Taleb's argument is that we should aspire to be in it, because the world is fundamentally unpredictable and large events (panics, crashes, wars, bubbles) have a disproportionate impact on outcomes.

The book pairs the framework with a practical corollary: because expert prediction repeatedly fails for rare, high-impact events, "it's more efficient to prepare" than to forecast. Failing properly has two components — never take a risk that can take you out of the game completely, and develop the personal resilience to learn from failure and start again — so that you can only fail temporarily. This connects to the [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) thread on the brittleness of models under tail events, tracked separately in [Antifragility / Tail Risk](/notes/antifragility-tail-risk).

**Context:** Antifragility is the title concept of Nassim Nicholas Taleb's 2012 book *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder*, the fourth volume of his *Incerto* series. It extends the "black swan" argument by naming a property — gaining from stressors, shocks, and randomness up to a point — distinct from mere robustness or resilience.
