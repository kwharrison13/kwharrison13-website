# University Disruption

The thesis that the traditional university and classroom are obsolete in an age where information is a free commodity — and that the status quo is ripe for "demolition." In Kyle's wiki the concept is anchored by two early-career [David Perell](/notes/david-perell) pieces. [It's Time To Disrupt Our Archaic Education System](/notes/it-s-time-to-disrupt-our-archaic-education-system) (2016) argues that current methods of education are "boring and increasingly obsolete" for students who are now "digital natives" (a frame borrowed from [Marc Prensky](/notes/marc-prensky)), that rising tuition coincides with an explosion of free online learning, and ends with the line "fasten your seat belts and prepare for the unprecedented demolition of a decrepit classroom status quo." [A Message to Every Entrepreneur](/notes/a-message-to-every-entrepreneur) makes the constructive counterpart case: the entrepreneur — who learns by chasing ambitious goals and learning from mistakes — is the model for how learning should actually work, since "students graduate from college with similar skill sets and a base of information that could have been acquired on the internet… for free."

The concept sits inside a cluster of related education threads in Kyle's notes: [Online Education](/notes/online-education) (YouTube, Coursera, Khan Academy outpacing the lecture hall), [Alternative Education](/notes/alternative-education) (routes to competence that bypass the credential), and [Teacher Celebrities](/notes/teacher-celebrities) (the rare great teachers who keep the classroom worth attending, since "the quality of an education system cannot exceed the quality of its teachers"). The shared move across all of them is that the *institution* and credential are a weak proxy for capability, while self-directed, apprenticeship-style, internet-distributed learning is the rising alternative.

**Context:** "University disruption" is a broad framing (popularized by writers like Clayton Christensen, Peter Thiel, and others) that the higher-education model — high fixed cost, slow-changing curriculum, credential-as-signal — is vulnerable to cheaper, faster, online and unbundled alternatives, much as other incumbent industries were disrupted by digital technology.
