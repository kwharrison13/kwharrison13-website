# Yochai Benkler

In [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), [Nadia Eghbal](/notes/nadia-eghbal) draws on Benkler's theory of **commons-based peer production** to explain how open-source software gets built. Benkler reasons that if individuals are personally motivated to do something, coordination costs will be lower — so [Intrinsic Motivation](/notes/intrinsic-motivation) is a foundational condition. He identifies the conditions necessary to pull off commons-based peer production as intrinsic motivation, **modular and granular tasks**, and **low coordination costs**. When a project is broken into small pieces each performable in a short time, the motivation needed from any one contributor "need only be very small." For open source specifically, coordination costs include quality control over modules (reviewing code) and integrating contributions (merging pull requests).

Benkler sits alongside [Elinor Ostrom](/notes/elinor-ostrom) in the book as one of the two theorists Eghbal uses to frame open source as a commons — Ostrom on governing shared resources, Benkler on how production itself is organized in a commons.

**Context:** Yochai Benkler is a Harvard Law School professor and a leading scholar of the networked information economy. His 2006 book *The Wealth of Networks* developed the concept of "commons-based peer production," using Wikipedia and open-source software as paradigm cases.
