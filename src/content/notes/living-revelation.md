# Living Revelation

One of Kyle's load-bearing theological concepts: the doctrine that revelation is **ongoing** rather than fixed in creeds. He inherits it from the [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) tradition and reads [Russell M. Nelson](/notes/russell-m-nelson) as its modern emphasizer — Nelson preaches continuing revelation against fixed creeds ([Russell M. Nelson](/notes/russell-m-nelson)). Kyle deploys it most pointedly as a reply to secular meaning-loss: against [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari)'s frame (in [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus) and [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century)) that humans have become the source of meaning after the "death of God," Kyle counters that "the worst response that mainstream Christianity chose to offer was 'no, he's just quiet.' We declare that he speaks, though not everyone will love what he has to say." He also flags Harari's warning that "religions that lose touch with the technological realities of the day forfeit their ability even to understand the questions being asked" — reading living revelation as exactly the religious response that *keeps* touch ([Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus), [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari)).

The hardest version of the doctrine shows up in [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand), where Wilford Woodruff's Manifesto forces the live question: "inspiration *is* revelation… a man is not always required to say 'Thus saith the Lord.'" Kyle probes the edges in the margins — why Woodruff's medium of revelatory reception differs from Joseph Smith's, and whether a commandment can ever be irrevocable — placing the concept right alongside [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept). It also functions in his civic thinking as a counterweight to crowd-driven certainty ([Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people)).

**Context:** "Continuing revelation" is a central Latter-day Saint tenet — the belief that God still speaks to living prophets and individuals, so doctrine and practice can develop ("line upon line") rather than being closed at the canon. Wilford Woodruff's 1890 Manifesto ending plural marriage is the historical case Kyle wrestles with as living revelation in action.
