# Character of God

In [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s theology, knowing the character of God is "the first principle of the gospel" — not merely knowing *that* God exists, but knowing *what kind of being* He is. The King Follett Sermon (April 7, 1844, Nauvoo) makes this its throughline: to be consoled about the dead, one must first understand the character of God, because "if we start right, it is easy to go right all the time." Joseph quotes [John 17:3](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/john/17.3?lang=eng) — "this is life eternal, that they might know thee the only true God" — and teaches that God "was once a man like us" who "dwelt on an earth, the same as Jesus Christ Himself did," the doctrinal foundation for [Exaltation](/notes/exaltation) ("you have got to learn how to be gods yourselves").

In Truman G. Madsen's first lecture, the same principle is read backward into the [First Vision](/notes/first-vision): Madsen explicitly treats the King Follett discourse as an extension of the [Sacred Grove](/notes/sacred-grove) experience, the two "doctrinal bookends of the same seed of insight." Madsen sharpens the distinction — Joseph never argues for God's *existence* ("you can't find one argument in Joseph Smith for the existence of God"), because arguments arise only where serious doubts have. Joseph instead claims a direct, experiential knowledge of God's character, available even to "the least Saint" by the [Holy Ghost](/notes/holy-ghost).

## Where this appears

- The King Follett Sermon — "it is the first principle of the gospel to know for a certainty the character of God"; God was once a man, the foundation for [Exaltation](/notes/exaltation).
- Joseph Smith Lecture 1 — The First Vision and Its Aftermath — Madsen reads the [First Vision](/notes/first-vision) forward into King Follett; knowing God's character (not arguing His existence) is the first principle.
