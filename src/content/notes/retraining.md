# Retraining

Across Kyle's reading, **retraining is the proposed but stubbornly under-delivering answer to automation-driven job displacement** — the policy hope that displaced workers can be reskilled into the new economy, repeatedly undercut by the empirical record. [Janesville](/books/janesville) is the central case study: [Amy Goldstein](/notes/amy-goldstein) documents that GM workers laid off after the plant closure who went back to school at Blackhawk Technical College actually fared *worse* on employment and pay than those who didn't, despite a bipartisan "retraining gospel" embraced by Paul Ryan and Obama alike — the retrained were earning roughly a third less than before, versus 8 percent less for those who skipped it. [The War on Normal People](/books/the-war-on-normal-people) reinforces the finding from the policy side: retraining programs "as currently practiced, tend to show few, if any benefits."

[The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) frames the scale problem — "there are few precedents in which societies have successfully retrained such large numbers of people," and the speed is the novelty: workforce transitions that historically took decades or centuries are now compressed. [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) pushes this furthest: [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari) argues retraining won't be a one-time event but "a cascade of ever-bigger disruptions," with the very idea of a job — or even a profession — for life becoming antediluvian by 2050, and notes the mental-balance cost of perpetual reinvention.

The two people-pages attached to this concept hold the more optimistic counter-positions: [Banu Ozkazanc-Pan](/notes/banu-ozkazanc-pan) sees reskilling (e.g. teaching displaced workers to code) as a short-term fix globally but transformative for the individual, and [Mark Cuban](/notes/mark-cuban) proposes data labeling and expanded national-service programs as an absorption path for displaced low-skilled workers.
