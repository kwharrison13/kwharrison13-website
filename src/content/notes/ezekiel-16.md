# Ezekiel 16

A chapter of Ezekiel cited in Born Again for its covenant-bride imagery. The study reads God's people as a covenant *bride*, with the offering of clothing or garments standing as a covenant act — "Now when I passed by thee, and looked upon thee, behold, thy time was the time of love; and I **spread my skirt over thee**" ([Ezekiel 16](/notes/ezekiel-16):8). The same gesture appears in [Ruth 3](/notes/ruth-3):9. In the study's framing we are the adulterous wife taken in lovingly; though we play the harlot, the Lord "will never break the covenant… only we can do that" and ever invites us back — a theme it carries forward into [Hosea 2](/notes/hosea-2).

**Context:** Ezekiel 16 is an extended allegory in the Hebrew Bible in which the prophet Ezekiel depicts Jerusalem as an abandoned infant whom God rescues, raises, and takes as a bride, who then turns unfaithful — a recurring marriage-and-covenant metaphor for Israel's relationship with God.
