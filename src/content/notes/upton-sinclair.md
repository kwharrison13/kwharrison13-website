# Upton Sinclair

In Kyle's corpus, Upton Sinclair functions almost entirely as the author of one recurring quote about [Incentives](/notes/incentives): *"It is difficult to get a man to understand something, when his salary depends on his not understanding it."* It is one of Kyle's most-reused frames for motivated reasoning and institutional self-deception, and it shows up across four very different sources.

In [Romney — A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) Kyle tags it against Mitt Romney's observation that people "see circumstances in the light that's most amenable to their maintaining power" — job preservation above principle preservation. In [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor) he applies it to Mormon institutional defensiveness, including the apologetic biography itself. In his [Alma 13](/notes/alma-13)–16 gospel study it frames how the wicked build flimsy cases against doctrine they're paid (or positioned) not to understand. And in [Charlie Munger — The Great Financial Scandal of 2003](/notes/charlie-munger-the-great-financial-scandal-of-2003), the Sinclair line appears inside Munger's "Quant Tech" parable about a board optimizing for market capitalization and the incentive-driven accounting fictions that follow — Kyle's note there is *"What are you optimizing for?"*

**Context:** Upton Sinclair (1878–1968) was an American muckraking novelist and socialist, best known for *The Jungle* (1906). The salary quote comes from *I, Candidate for Governor: And How I Got Licked* (1935).
