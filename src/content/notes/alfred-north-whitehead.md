# Alfred North Whitehead

The British philosopher-mathematician whom [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) repeatedly invokes as the patron of multidisciplinary thinking. Whitehead recurs throughout [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) and Munger's commencement addresses as the source of the critique of "the fatal unconnectedness of academic disciplines" — the idea that each professor "didn't even know the models of the other disciplines, much less try to synthesize those disciplines with his own," which Munger calls, in modern terms, "bonkers" ([Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics)). Munger uses Whitehead to ground his case for a [latticework of mental models](/notes/latticework) and [multidisciplinary](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) education. Other Whitehead lines Munger quotes: "Not ignorance, but ignorance of ignorance is the death of knowledge" — which Kyle annotates with [Process and Reality](/notes/process-and-reality) and the parallel [Mark Twain](/notes/mark-twain) line "it ain't what I don't know that is dangerous; it's what I know for certain that just ain't so" — and "The 'silly' question is the first intimation of some totally new development." In the [USC address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address), Munger credits Whitehead's observation that civilization advanced rapidly only once "man invented the method of invention," extending it to the idea that "you can progress only when you learn the method of learning."

Whitehead appears in a second, unrelated register in [American Sphinx](/books/american-sphinx): [Joseph J. Ellis](/notes/joseph-j-ellis) quotes him on the revolutionary era as one of only two moments in recorded history — the other being Rome under Caesar Augustus — when "the political leadership of an emerging empire performed as well as one could realistically expect," a verdict Ellis applies to [Thomas Jefferson](/notes/thomas-jefferson)'s generation.

**Context:** Alfred North Whitehead (1861–1947) was an English mathematician and philosopher, co-author with [Bertrand Russell](/notes/bertrand-russell) of *Principia Mathematica* and, in his later metaphysical phase, author of *Process and Reality* and the foundational text of process philosophy. He also wrote influentially on education and the aims of learning.
