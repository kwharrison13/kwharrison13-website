# Positive Proximity

"Positive proximity" is the coined framework at the center of [What I Found in a Thousand Towns](/books/what-i-found-in-a-thousand-towns) by touring musician-author [Dar Williams](/notes/dar-williams), and it organizes the whole book. The defining highlight: what a thriving town is building "aside from new trails and a better school" is "positive proximity, or a state of being where living side by side with other people is experienced as beneficial." It is the opposite of the myth that proximity means conflict and invasion of privacy; when people transcend that myth, "they gravitate toward finding ways to integrate the talents and skills of their community members." Williams frames it as valuing "what we have and knowing that much of it is built by us and our neighbors, not bought and inhabited by pure profiteers."

In Kyle's notes, positive proximity is the lever that connects the book's other concepts. It is the precondition for civic engagement — "towns cannot create civic engagement from scratch. Without positive proximity, there can be no grist for civic connections" — which ties it to [Community](/notes/community) (bridging social capital that grows rather than being manufactured). It is what [Gentrification](/notes/gentrification) threatens: financial displacement that pushes out the residents who built a town's identity "event by event" can "squash vitality... and the sense of positive proximity." It feeds local, self-perpetuating culture (buy-local ethics, local food economies that "jump the groove of potential social divisions") and underwrites bottom-up [City Building](/notes/city-building) — the main-street fabric, schools, and cultural hubs that decide which small towns thrive after manufacturing or extraction leaves. Strong positive proximity, Williams argues, is also a diagnostic: it lets a town "survey the terrain and 'know' what the reserves of social capital are" and decide "how will we manage this?" in the face of change.

**Context:** *What I Found in a Thousand Towns: A Traveling Musician's Guide to Rebuilding America's Communities — One Coffee Shop, Dog Run, and Open-Mic Night at a Time* (2017) is Dar Williams's nonfiction book drawing on decades of touring through small American towns.
