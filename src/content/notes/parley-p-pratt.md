# Parley P. Pratt

Pratt is an early LDS apostle who recurs across Kyle's church-history reading as a missionary, doctrinal writer, and contemporary witness of [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith). In [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor), Pratt is the missionary who converted [John Taylor](/notes/john-taylor) in Canada — and whom Taylor later steadied when Pratt himself was "floundering in darkness," reminding him that he had borne a "strong testimony to Joseph Smith being a Prophet of God" before his doubts set in. In [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) he appears en route to England, correcting the false notion that "the churches have no gifts unless they have tongues."

Doctrinally, Pratt is the source of two ideas Kyle has flagged. In [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt), the Givenses cite his 1844 pamphlet *The Fountain of Knowledge* for the argument that scripture is the product of revelation, not the other way around — "we do well to look to a stream for nourishing water, but we do better to secure the fountain" — and his "doctrine of equality," the claim that God wants all to be "joint-heirs with Christ." In [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book) he is the source of a long passage on the spirit body and the premortal "school of love," and in Plan of Salvation — Topical Study Kyle paraphrases Pratt on post-mortal perception: "with the veil removed, your life will become like an eye." [Joseph Smith Lecture 2 — Joseph's Personality and Character](/notes/joseph-smith-lecture-2-joseph-s-personality-and-character) uses Pratt's tearful reunion with Joseph at Nauvoo as a character witness to the Prophet's emotional warmth.

**Context:** Parley P. Pratt (1807–1857) was one of the original members of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, a prolific missionary and writer (*A Voice of Warning*, *Key to the Science of Theology*), and brother of fellow apostle Orson Pratt. He was murdered in Arkansas in 1857.
