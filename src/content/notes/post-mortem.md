# Post-Mortem

The post-mortem — disciplined study of what went wrong — is one of Kyle's load-bearing investing and learning themes, and the subject of his published essay [What's In a Post-Mortem](/essays/whats-in-a-post-mortem) ("Learn To Keep From Dying"). His research frames it as a practice that migrated from medical autopsies to manufacturing, software engineering, and startup investing, whose core question is simply "what went wrong?" — which first requires the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) to acknowledge that something went wrong. The thesis is that the biggest blocker to learning from experience is not method but *willingness*, and that done right a post-mortem (and its sibling the [Pre-Mortem](/notes/pre-mortem)) lets you learn as much from success as from failure.

The idea recurs across Kyle's reading. In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) emphasizes "the study of mistakes rather than successes," and Buffett asks why post-mortems "useful for hospitals and football teams" aren't applied to businesses and investors — a frame Kyle applies to his own hindsight-error reflections (e.g. a post-mortem on [Scalefactor](/notes/scalefactor)). [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound) supplies the operational version: Amazon's "correction of error" reports and the "five whys." [The Founders](/books/the-founders) notes [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel)'s failed Smart Calendar as "a paid crash course," paired with [David Sacks](/notes/david-sacks)'s pre-mortem ("if I were running eBay Payments, what would I do to kill PayPal?"). [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) offers Bell Labs' failure taxonomy — the waveguide as a "mistake of perception" vs. the Picturephone as a "mistake of judgment."

**Context:** A post-mortem (or "retrospective") is a structured after-the-fact analysis of a project or decision to surface causes and lessons; the term comes from medicine, and the practice is now standard in fields like site-reliability engineering ("blameless post-mortems").
