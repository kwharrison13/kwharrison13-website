# Harry G. Frankfurt

In [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness), Harry G. Frankfurt supplies the book's central distinction between bullshit and lying, drawn from his essay *On Bullshit* and quoted at length. Frankfurt's argument is that the liar and the truth-teller play opposite sides of the same game — both respond to the facts as they understand them — while the bullshitter "pays no attention to it at all," making bullshit "a greater enemy of the truth than lies are." [Brené Brown](/notes/bren-brown) frames lying as a defiance of truth and bullshitting as a wholesale dismissal of it, and cites Frankfurt's deeper diagnosis: that the spread of bullshit grows from skepticism that we can ever know how things truly are, a giving-up on objective inquiry. The book pairs this with the idea of [Truth](/notes/truth) as a shared starting point and with Brandolini's law on the asymmetric cost of refuting bullshit.

**Context:** Harry G. Frankfurt (1929–2023) was an American philosopher at Princeton best known for his 2005 essay *On Bullshit* and for influential work on free will, moral responsibility, and the nature of love.
