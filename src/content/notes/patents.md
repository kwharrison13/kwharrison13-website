# Patents

In Kyle's wiki, patents function less as a legal instrument than as a **measured outcome of innovation**. The page enters the corpus through [Matt Clancy](/notes/matt-clancy), the innovation economist whose "Free Knowledge and Innovation" synthesizes evidence that new access to knowledge — public libraries, patent depositories, [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia) — produces measurable effects on **patenting and citations**. Patenting is the dependent variable in that argument: when people gain better access to knowledge, the visible signal is that they patent more and cite more. This makes patents one of the concrete, countable proxies underpinning the [Open-Source Knowledge](/essays/open-source-knowledge) thesis that better access to [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) drives [Innovation](/notes/innovation).

**Context:** A patent is a time-limited, government-granted exclusive right to an invention, disclosed publicly in exchange for that protection. Patent counts and citation networks are a standard (if imperfect) empirical proxy economists use to measure inventive activity.
