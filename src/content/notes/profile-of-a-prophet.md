# Profile of a Prophet

"Profile of a Prophet" is the prophet-typology thread Kyle uses to evaluate [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) against the marks of a true prophet rather than against a perfect ideal. The phrase originates with [Hugh B. Brown](/notes/hugh-b-brown)'s BYU speech of that name, which Kyle cites in The First Principles of Faithful Pragmatism as helping articulate "the unique characteristics of Joseph Smith's ministry." In Joseph Smith Lecture 1 — The First Vision and Its Aftermath, [Truman G. Madsen](/notes/truman-g-madsen) supplies a parallel ten-mark checklist (foreteller, forth-teller, authority, recoverer of truth, advocate of righteousness, charismatic, sufferer, embodiment of love, seer, martyr) and finds Joseph satisfies all of them — before turning from these external "captions" to the subjective, interior approach to the [First Vision](/notes/first-vision).

Kyle makes this the first of three organizing pillars in his reading of [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling) (alongside City Building and Gods in Embryo). His distilled profile: a prophet sees what others cannot and drives forward with oracular conviction ("his storytelling was oracular rather than argumentative"); points toward the Savior rather than himself; states fact — "thus saith the Lord" — rather than arguing; reveals genuinely new things rather than rehashing old ones; and equips followers to seek their own revelation rather than demanding blind obedience. Critically, prophets are "not protected from failure" — by June 1837 "not twenty men in Kirtland believed Joseph was a prophet."

## Where this appears

- [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling) — the first of Kyle's three reading pillars: seeing what others can't, oracular conviction, equipping followers to seek revelation, no protection from failure
- Joseph Smith Lecture 1 — The First Vision and Its Aftermath — Madsen's ten marks of a prophet as the external test, before the subjective turn to the First Vision
- The First Principles of Faithful Pragmatism — cites Hugh B. Brown's "Profile of a Prophet" BYU speech on the characteristics of a true prophet
