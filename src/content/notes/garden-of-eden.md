# Garden of Eden

In Kyle's wiki the Garden of Eden functions as a test case for one of his recurring tensions — [Primitivism](/notes/primitivism) versus [Progressivism](/notes/progressivism). In The Hardening Of The Great Softening - Research he poses it as a direct question: "The Garden of Eden was about progression? Or primitivism?" — using the Fall and the departure from Eden to ask whether the human ideal is a *return* to a simpler, prelapsarian state or a *movement forward* through it. The question sits alongside a parallel one about whether thought leaders who pass the [Lindy](/notes/lindy-principle) test are primitivists or progressivists. Kyle's own answer in those notes is that he is "a progressivist (technological, spiritual, ideological)" — so for him Eden reads as a stage to progress *through*, not a paradise to return to.

Both poles cross-reference Eden as the pivot: [Primitivism](/notes/primitivism) names it "the test case for whether Eden was about progression or primitivism," and [Progressivism](/notes/progressivism) frames it as "the primitivist ideal of return that progressivism pushes against."

**Context:** The Garden of Eden is the paradise of the Genesis creation narrative, from which Adam and Eve are expelled after the Fall. It is a foundational image in Jewish, Christian, and Islamic thought, and a frequent reference point in debates over whether human flourishing means returning to an original innocence or advancing beyond it. In Latter-day Saint theology especially, the Fall is read as a necessary step toward progression rather than a pure loss.
