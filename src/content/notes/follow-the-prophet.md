# Follow the Prophet

The principle that members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints should heed the teachings of the living prophet — a theme Kyle threads through both his scripture study and his sociology-of-Mormonism reading. In the Alma 13–16 study, Kyle anchors it on Brigham Young's "[living oracles](/notes/living-oracles)" sermon: with the standard works laid before him, Young declared the written word "nothing to me" compared to "the words of a Prophet... in our day," because the living oracles convey the word of God "direct to us now." The point is the primacy of present, ongoing revelation over scripture alone.

[The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) examines the same ideal sociologically and finds it held in tension. At one pole is the "rulebook" version, encoded by [Ezra Taft Benson](/notes/ezra-taft-benson)'s "fourteen fundamentals" — the living prophet supersedes former ones, is "more vital than the standard works," and will never lead the church astray. (The book notes that even calling the church president "the prophet" is a mid-20th-century innovation, dating to 1955 under [David O. McKay](/notes/david-o-mckay).) At the other pole is a countervailing acknowledgment that prophets are fallible. Riess's data show Millennials drifting from this rulebook obedience; she quotes a believer who values following the prophet "even if you don't know the answer" — "I disagree but I follow... and then we update our belief system when there is new information. Line upon line." Across the two sources, "Follow the Prophet" runs from a devotional affirmation of living revelation to a contested marker of authority and generational change.

## Where this appears

- Alma 13–16 — Enter into the Rest of the Lord — Brigham Young's "living oracles" sermon as the case for present revelation over the written word.
- [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) — the "rulebook" (Benson's fourteen fundamentals) vs. fallibility tension, and Millennials' drift from prophetic obedience.
