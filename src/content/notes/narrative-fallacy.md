# Narrative Fallacy

The narrative fallacy is a named, load-bearing concept in [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) by [Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb): our predilection for compact, causal stories over the raw, messier truth of randomness. Taleb describes the "triplet of opacity" — the illusion of understanding, retrospective distortion (history "seems clearer and more organized in history books than in empirical reality"), and the overvaluation of factual information — all of which feed our tendency to impose tidy narratives after the fact. He pairs it with a warning about asymmetry: "You need a story to displace a story. Metaphors and stories are far more potent (alas) than ideas; they are also easier to remember and more fun to read." Kyle annotated this passage simply as "Storytelling," tying it to the broader [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) theme — but the narrative fallacy is the distinct epistemic-error half of that pairing: stories as a source of distortion, not just persuasion.

**Context:** Coined by Taleb in *The Black Swan* (2007), the narrative fallacy describes the human compulsion to construct cause-and-effect stories about sequences of facts, which makes the past seem more predictable than it was and blinds us to the role of rare, high-impact ("black swan") events.
