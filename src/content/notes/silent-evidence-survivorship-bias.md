# Silent Evidence / Survivorship Bias

"Silent evidence" is [Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb)'s name in [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) for the losers history doesn't record — the distortion that produces survivorship bias. His one-line image: "As drowned worshippers do not write histories of their experiences... so it is with the losers in history, whether people or ideas." Taleb notes that historians and humanities scholars, who most need to understand it, never even gave it a name. The book stacks up illustrations: the Phoenicians dismissed as a non-literary "merchant race" only because their perishable papyrus didn't survive ("the cemetery of letters"); Balzac's *Lost Illusions* and its "nightingales" (unsold books gathering dust in the backs of shops); and the graveyard of failed risk-takers who shared the exact same traits as the millionaires — courage, optimism, risk-taking — separated only by "plain luck."

In Kyle's reading the concept sits alongside [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias) and the narrative fallacy ([Storytelling](/notes/storytelling)) as one of the manifestations of our blindness to Black Swans, and it cuts both ways: silent evidence can make history look *less* stable than it is (overweighting literary "success" stories) and *more* risky than it is (counting cancer survival only from diagnosed cases biases the danger upward). It directly shapes how Taleb reads [Venture Capital](/notes/venture-capital) and [Entrepreneurship](/notes/entrepreneurship) — returns on independent inventions, once you "take the cemetery into account," are far lower than VC returns; some blindness to the odds, "a touch of madness," is necessary for entrepreneurs to function, while the venture capitalist "gets the shekels."

**Context:** Survivorship bias is the logical error of concentrating on the people or things that made it past a selection process and overlooking those that did not — a staple of statistics and decision theory, classically illustrated by Abraham Wald's WWII analysis of where to armor returning bombers (reinforce the spots with *no* bullet holes, because planes hit there never returned).
