# B.F. Skinner

The Harvard behaviorist psychologist appears across Kyle's corpus in two opposed registers. In [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) uses Skinner as the foil for his defense of the individual against environmental scientism — quoting Skinner that "as scientific explanation becomes more and more comprehensive, the contribution which may be claimed by the individual himself appears to approach zero," and rebutting it by invoking [William James](/notes/william-james)'s "physiological forces" and the genetic variability that Skinner dismissed "in less than a page." Huxley also reads Skinner's utopian novel *Walden Two* as a vision of a scientifically organized community where no one is ever led into anti-social temptation. Kyle's marginalia on this book is notably adversarial, and Skinner's behaviorist determinism is one of the threads he rejects (see [Free Will](/notes/free-will)).

In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), by contrast, Skinner is a constructive reference: [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) draws on operant conditioning for his psychology-of-misjudgment framework, citing Skinner's finding that "prompt rewards work best" (the "Granny's Rule" example of doing unpleasant tasks before the pleasant ones). One of Kyle's own notes on that book reads: *"Humans learn by biologically-enhanced trial and error"* — sourced to the [Daniel W. Bjork](/notes/daniel-w-bjork) biography *B.F. Skinner*.

**Context:** B. F. Skinner (1904–1990) was an American psychologist and the leading figure of radical behaviorism, known for operant conditioning, the "Skinner box," *Walden Two*, and *Beyond Freedom and Dignity*.

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) — Huxley's extended takedown of Skinner's behaviorist determinism; the "contribution approaches zero" quote and *Walden Two*.
- [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) — Munger's use of Skinner's operant conditioning ("prompt rewards work best," Granny's Rule); Kyle's trial-and-error note.
