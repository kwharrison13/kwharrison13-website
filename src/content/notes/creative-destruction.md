# Creative Destruction

Creative destruction recurs across Kyle's corpus as the engine that markets unleash and that protectionism or distorted incentives suppress. Business Creativity gives the source definition, quoting Joseph Schumpeter directly: "Any existing structures and all the conditions of doing business are always in a process of change... Economic progress, in a capitalist society, means turmoil, through a process of creative destruction." It frames the entrepreneurial "motors of change" in that learning resource.

The two book pages show the idea applied to policy. In [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care), David Goldhill argues that health care's "green shoots" of innovation (concierge primary care, medical tourism, price-transparency startups) will keep appearing, but that "creative destruction — the transformation of health care to produce truly better medicine at lower cost and risk — will almost certainly have to wait until consumers are in charge," because surrogate payers sever the market discipline that drives it. In [No Apology](/books/no-apology), Mitt Romney makes the governance argument: "It takes a leap of faith for governments to stand aside and allow the creative destruction inherent in a free economy, but it's a leap that has been successfully made by every advanced economy in the world." Both invoke it as the thing a healthy [Innovation](/notes/innovation)-driven market produces when consumers and competition, not protection or surrogates, are in control.

**Context:** "Creative destruction" was coined by economist Joseph Schumpeter in *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* (1942) to describe how capitalism continually revolutionizes its economic structure from within — new technologies, products, and firms destroying old ones. He saw it as "the essential fact about capitalism," the source of both its dynamism and its disruption.
