# Orson Pratt

The early Latter-day Saint apostle Kyle's reading consistently frames as the church's foremost original theologian. [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) calls him "the most highly regarded speculative theologian of the new faith" and "the most original theologian the Latter-day Saint tradition ever produced," repeatedly reined in by the authoritarian [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) — and it draws [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england) as Pratt's twentieth-century parallel (both "publicly rebuked from the stand and required to repudiate some of [their] teachings"). [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) corroborates the friction: Young's "heavyhanded" later leadership "bruised" the feelings of high-ranking leaders including Pratt. The same book carries Young's famous tribute — "If Elder Pratt was chopped up in pieces, every piece would cry out 'Mormonism is true.'"

Across Kyle's gospel-study notes Pratt recurs as a doctrinal source. He is the author of the line on faith that Kyle returns to in Faith — Topical Study and Miscellaneous Scriptures: *"Faith is the result of evidence presented to the mind. Without evidence, the mind cannot have faith."* He is quoted in Spiritual Gifts ("No one who receives the Holy Ghost is left destitute of a spiritual gift"), and his 1840 *Interesting Account of Several Remarkable Visions* supplies the "leaves and boughs of the trees consumed" detail in Joseph Smith Lecture 1 — The First Vision and Its Aftermath. [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) places him (with [James E. Talmage](/notes/james-e-talmage) and [John A. Widtsoe](/notes/john-widtsoe)) among the LDS thinkers [Henry Eyring](/notes/henry-eyring) turned to for "the right slant on the Gospel for scientific friends," and the [Madsen lecture](/notes/joseph-smith-lecture-2-joseph-s-personality-and-character) names him as the second-strongest student (after Joseph Smith) in the Kirtland Hebrew school.

**Context:** Orson Pratt (1811–1881) was an early apostle of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, a self-taught mathematician and astronomer, and an influential (if often controversial) systematizer of early Mormon theology. He was the younger brother of fellow apostle [Parley P. Pratt](/notes/parley-p-pratt).
