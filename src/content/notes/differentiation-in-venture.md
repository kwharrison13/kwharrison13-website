# Differentiation in Venture

Kyle's thesis that **"differentiation is going to kill the long tail of so-so venture firms."** As he framed it for the September 13th, 2022 — Harry Stebbings Podcast, founders will increasingly ask "what is the [job-to-be-done](/notes/jobs-to-be-done) that I'm hiring this firm for?" — a Clayton Christensen framing borrowed from [The Innovator's Dilemma](/books/the-innovator-s-dilemma). Firms that can't answer that question get stuck in what [Everett Randle](/notes/everett-randle) called the **"[Dead Zone](/notes/dead-zone)."** The argument rests on [Josh Wolfe](/notes/josh-wolfe)'s prediction that between 50% and 75% of active private-market investors will disappear within a few years: as that culling happens, the survivors are the firms with a distinct character and a real product. Kyle cites Insight's roll-up product, Ribbit's fintech product, and Paradigm's crypto product as examples — differentiation doesn't require the product to be new, only to be a genuine answer to a founder's question.

The thesis is closely tied to the [Naked Brands](/notes/naked-brands) framing (David Perell's idea, applied to venture) that brand and positioning — people knowing what a specific firm is for — become the surviving differentiator in a transparent world. It underpins Kyle's essay-research for [The Unbundling of Venture Capital](/essays/the-unbundling-of-venture-capital) and the sibling idea of [The Productization of Venture Capital](/essays/productization). The notes also record the obvious pushback: DPI is a lagging indicator, so a "so-so" firm that recently returned cash can keep raising for years — venture firms take decades to die.
