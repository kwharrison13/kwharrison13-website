# Trends

Across the corpus, "trends" carries a deliberately ambivalent charge — sometimes the thing to ride, sometimes the thing to ignore. Kyle's own note on [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) reframes investing as the work of tracking "massive [Trends](/notes/trends) and the companies riding them," and a related note observes that rapid environmental changes (recession, COVID) "can expose trends that were likely already happening but that may have been accelerated." Yet [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s letters cut the other way too: Berkshire has "no master strategy, no corporate planners delivering us insights about socioeconomic trends," and Buffett warns against becoming "mesmerized by prospective near-term earnings or recent trends in earnings."

[How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital) gives the operating playbook: don't chase the trend itself, **sell pickaxes to gold miners** — "the gold mine is the hot trend... the pickax is the part of the iceberg below the water... that the hot trend relies on to function." This connects to [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting), where the wiki notes most financiers "extrapolate trends from the past, disregarding the risk of extreme 'tail' events," while venture capitalists look instead for radical departures. The technology-specific case lives at [Internet Trends](/notes/internet-trends). On the people side, [Matt Curwood](/notes/matt-curwood) flags water transportation and big-city commuter services as the key mobility trends to watch.
