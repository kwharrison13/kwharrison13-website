# Clayton Christensen

The Harvard Business School theorist of disruptive innovation, and one of the most cross-referenced authors in Kyle's library. As an author he appears via [How Will You Measure Your Life?](/books/how-will-you-measure-your-life) — his values-as-strategy book — whose "how will you measure your life" frame Kyle invokes directly in [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) notes ("How will your kids measure their life?"). His signature work [The Innovator's Dilemma](/books/the-innovator-s-dilemma) threads through the business reading: in both [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store) and [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound), [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) "devoured and raptly discussed" it with his executives, using its logic — great companies fail by refusing to cannibalize their own businesses — to justify the Kindle ("kill your own business") and Barnes & Noble's failure to compete online.

The library also captures Christensen's range and his self-revision. [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care) quotes him on how the Medicaid lowest-price rule instills pricing "discipline" that makes discounting prohibitively expensive across health care. [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind) traces how his "skate to where the money will be" framing inspired Boeing's financialization — and then how he recanted in "The Capitalist's Dilemma" (2014), arguing for market-creating over efficiency innovations and conceding "a lot of the blame… rests with our great schools of business, including our own." [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) presents another side entirely: Christensen the devout Latter-day Saint and New England church leader, whose diagnosis tied single-member activity rates to whether singles were given real callings.

**Context:** Clayton M. Christensen (1952–2020) was a Harvard Business School professor who coined the theory of "disruptive innovation" in *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997); he was also a prominent Latter-day Saint who served in church leadership.
