# Physics Envy

Physics envy is the central named failure mode in [Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics) (Talk 9 of [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack)). [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) wants economics to "pick up the basic ethos of hard science, the full attribution habit, but **not the craving for an unattainable precision that comes from physics envy.**" His point is that economics involves "too complex a system" for physics-style formulas — there is no Boltzmann's constant for human behavior — and that chasing such precision gets you "in terrible trouble." The canonical example in the talk is the original Medicare cost forecast, which experts produced by simple extrapolation and which "was off by a factor of more than one thousand percent" once new incentives changed behavior. Physics envy thus links directly to Munger's distrust of formal projections and to his case for [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) over false, single-discipline precision.

**Context:** "Physics envy" is a coined critique (used across economics, psychology, and other social sciences) of the tendency to imitate physics' mathematical rigor and exactness in domains where the underlying systems are too complex or reflexive to support it.
