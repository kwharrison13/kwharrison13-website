# Worldly Wisdom

[Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s term for the broad, cross-disciplinary competence he treats as the goal of a real education — "broad scale worldly wisdom" and "multidisciplinary worldly wisdom." In the [Charlie Munger — Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address) (Talk 5 of [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack)), he prescribes how to get it: "multidisciplinary coverage of immense amplitude, with all needed skills raised to an ever-maintained practice-based fluency," synthesis at the boundaries between disciplines, forward-and-reverse thinking (an echo of [Inversion](/notes/inversion)), and checklist routines made "a permanent part of the knowledge system." He frames worldly wisdom as attainable — "I've seen it done" — pointing to "modern [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)s" who achieved a massive multidisciplinary synthesis and became *better* performers in their own disciplines for it, not worse.

Munger's method is the practice of assimilating "the fundamental organizing ethos of hard science" and pushing it "further and further into softer and softer fare" as a filing system in the search for "whatever multidisciplinary worldly wisdom it would be easy to get." The address's reform prescriptions — more mandatory courses, avoiding "passionate ideologues" whose minds are in "ideological fetters" — all serve that end, since the difficult synthesis worldly wisdom requires demands an objectivity the ideologically captured have lost.

**Context:** "The first rule is that you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts… You've got to have models in your head" — Munger's broader "worldly wisdom" doctrine, delivered most famously in his 1994 USC Business School talk and collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*, centers on building a "latticework of mental models" drawn from many disciplines.
