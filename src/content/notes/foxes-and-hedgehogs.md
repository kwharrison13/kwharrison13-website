# Foxes and Hedgehogs

In [Range](/books/range), [David Epstein](/notes/david-epstein) draws on [Philip Tetlock](/notes/philip-tetlock)'s long-running forecasting research to contrast two cognitive styles: hedgehogs, who know "one big thing" and force events through a single dominant theory, and foxes, who draw on many small ideas, hold their hunches lightly, and remain self-doubting. Tetlock's twenty-year study found that breadth wins — broad, self-questioning foxes out-predict narrow experts. The book pairs this with two damning findings: there was "a curiously inverse relationship between how well forecasters thought they were doing and how well they did," and a "perverse inverse relationship" between fame and accuracy — the experts most likely to be featured on op-ed pages and television were "roughly as accurate as a dart-throwing chimpanzee."

The deeper distinction Epstein draws is about belief-updating. In Tetlock's study, both types reinforced beliefs after correct predictions, but when surprised, foxes adjusted while hedgehogs barely budged — some hedgehogs even updated in the wrong direction, becoming more convinced of the beliefs that misled them. "Good judges are good belief updaters," and updating after a loss is, "in a word: learning." Epstein connects this to Dan Kahan's finding that more scientifically literate adults can become *more* dogmatic on polarizing topics, growing more hedgehog-like the more they engage. The typology underpins the book's broader argument linking [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting), [Judgment](/notes/judgment), and [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking).

**Context:** The fox/hedgehog distinction comes from Isaiah Berlin's essay "The Hedgehog and the Fox," which borrowed the line from the Greek poet Archilochus: "The fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing." Tetlock adopted it as the central metaphor of his work on expert political judgment.

## Where this appears

- [Range](/books/range) — uses Tetlock's foxes-vs-hedgehogs typology as the spine of its forecasting argument: broad, self-doubting foxes out-predict narrow hedgehog experts, and good forecasters are good belief-updaters.
