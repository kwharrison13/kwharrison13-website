# Self-control

Self-control recurs across Kyle's notes as composure under provocation — the ability to defer reaction rather than be carried away by hostility. [Hinckley](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) supplies the defining anecdote: a fifty-year-old switchman flying into a profanity-laced tantrum over written instructions, which the young Gordon found "totally repugnant" — "I could never again give him my full respect." [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) frames the same scene as the practical antidote in [The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment): "the standard antidote to one's overactive hostility is to train oneself to defer reaction," quoting Tom Murphy — "You can always tell the man off tomorrow, if it is such a good idea."

[How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) complicates the willpower-based view of self-control: like [Self-discipline](/notes/self-discipline), it "is not that easy to achieve with willpower alone," since "willpower is… a limited resource that depletes quickly" and isn't much improvable over the long term (Baumeister et al.). The book's resolution is to design environments and workflows so that the work carries itself, rather than flogging oneself into focus.

**Context:** Self-control is a well-studied construct in psychology; the "ego-depletion" model of willpower as a finite resource (Baumeister and colleagues) cited here has since been the subject of replication debate.
