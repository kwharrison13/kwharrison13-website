# Robert Cialdini

A psychologist of persuasion whose work runs through Kyle's notes as a touchstone for the mechanics of influence. In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) credits Cialdini's book with the "push" that sent him through three psychology textbooks to build his own checklist of the psychology of misjudgment; Munger recounts in detail Cialdini's famous compliance experiment — asking strangers for an absurdly large commitment (two years supervising juvenile delinquents), then following up with a smaller "will you at least take them to the zoo one afternoon?" — which tripled the acceptance rate by triggering the subconscious reciprocation-of-a-concession tendency. His commitment-and-consistency and incentive-caused-bias ideas appear directly in Munger's framework, which is why Cialdini's *[Influence](/notes/influence)* sits on Munger's recommended-reading list as a [books-to-read](/notes/books-to-read) anti-library entry.

Across the essays-research notes, Cialdini is cast as a source on persuasion and information warfare: The Storytelling of Investing - Research places *Influence* alongside Richard Brodie's *Virus of the Mind* on Bill Gurley's reading list and links Cialdini to [Memetics](/notes/memetics), while Open-Source Knowledge - Research treats his principles (with Dale Carnegie's) as the classic social-engineering playbook now running at internet scale and always-on intensity.

**Context:** Robert Cialdini is an American social psychologist, professor emeritus at Arizona State University, best known for his 1984 book *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*. It codified six principles of persuasion — reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, and scarcity (he later added unity).
