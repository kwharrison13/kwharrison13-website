# Carbon Neutral

"Carbon neutral" appears in the Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 notes as the older, weaker corporate climate standard that net-zero is displacing. In the diligence, customers and experts repeatedly distinguish the two: companies "talk about carbon neutrality, but they're unsure if they have a very solid plan of getting there," and one of the explicit jobs Watershed's tool does is help a company "decide if to be net zero vs. carbon neutral." Several reference customers describe starting with a carbon-neutral goal — "Initial goal was to go carbon neutral, purchase offsets, etc." — and then graduating toward a more rigorous net-zero target backed by actual emissions reduction rather than pure offsetting.

The distinction matters to the investment thesis. The diligence frames a $100B+ pool of demand for carbon offsets driven by net-zero commitments, with the number of companies setting net-zero targets since early 2018 growing nearly 2,100%. Carbon neutrality — offsetting as much carbon as you emit — is treated as the legacy baseline; the durable market Watershed is built for is the harder net-zero transition, which "requires an entire reimagining of the infrastructure in the way you approach that problem." Notes flag that a marketplace of offsets is most useful precisely when it *informs reduction plans* toward stated net-zero targets, not when it substitutes for them.

**Context:** "Carbon neutral" means balancing emitted CO2 with an equivalent amount offset or removed, typically via purchased carbon credits. "Net zero" is a stricter, broader standard that emphasizes deep absolute emissions cuts across scopes before residual offsetting/removal — which is why it has largely superseded carbon-neutral claims in corporate climate commitments.
