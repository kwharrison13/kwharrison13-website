# W.H. Auden

Mid-20th-century poet whose lines carry the humanist defense in [We Need a Renaissance of Rhetoric](/essays/we-need-a-renaissance-of-rhetoric). Kyle cites two aphorisms attributed to Auden: "Language is the mother, not the handmaiden, of thought" and "Words will tell you things you never thought or felt before." Both arrive in the essay's "Your Language is Your World" section, where they back the load-bearing premise that writing is *how* thinking happens — so losing the practice of writing (to AI) means losing the worldview that the practice builds.

The first quote reaches Kyle through Duke professor Orin Starn's column "My Losing Battle Against AI Cheating," which attributes it to Auden; Kyle flags that the line is variously attributed (Starn cites Auden; Kyle notes it elsewhere credited to Karl Kraus). In [AI Cheating in Education](/notes/ai-cheating-in-education) Auden sits with Starn and [Troy Jollimore](/notes/troy-jollimore) on the humanist side of the argument, and the same "language is the mother of thought" idea connects to the [Linguistic Relativity](/notes/linguistic-relativity) page (the Whorf/Wittgenstein premise that "the limits of your language are the limits of your world").

**Context:** W. H. Auden (1907–1973) was an English-American poet, one of the most influential of the 20th century, known for works including "September 1, 1939" and "Funeral Blues."
