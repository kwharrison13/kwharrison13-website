# Public Domain

In Kyle's corpus the public domain shows up as a deliberate *strategy* rather than a passive legal default. The central thread is [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann)'s "Cold War open-source instinct": as told in [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future), von Neumann deliberately kept his work "in the public domain" — declining to capture it via patents — in order to accelerate the whole field rather than capture the value for himself. The [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) page flags this as a case that genuinely bridges two adjacent ideas: knowledge/pedagogy shared openly, and the software-licensing/development-economics sense of [Open Source](/notes/open-source). The choice to release rather than enclose is framed as compounding the commons.

**Context:** The public domain is the body of creative work and knowledge not protected by intellectual-property rights — because protection expired, was never granted, or was deliberately waived — and is therefore freely usable by anyone. Choosing to place work in the public domain (vs. patenting or copyrighting it) is a recognized way to maximize diffusion and downstream reuse.
