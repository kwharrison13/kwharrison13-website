# Sam Walton

The founder of [Walmart](/notes/walmart), who recurs across Kyle's reading as the archetype of a humble operator who won through a better system and relentless learning. In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) uses Walton as a case study in scale-plus-better-system: "with his more efficient system… he could sure as hell destroy those small town merchants… Then, as he got bigger, he started destroying the big boys," and offers Walmart-at-IPO as the model of "finding them small." The Almanack also preserves Walton's own statement of the learn-from-everyone ethic — "you can learn from everybody… I probably learned the most from studying what my competitor was doing across the street" — which Kyle ties to the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) and "the willingness to change his mind."

In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store), Walton is the giant on whose shoulders Amazon stands: [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) underlined Walton's passage on borrowing competitors' best ideas, and Walmart's "marketing strategy is our pricing strategy" (everyday low pricing) sits behind Bezos's own low-margin moat. [Buffett: The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography) groups Walton — "the humble retailer" — with [Bill Gates](/notes/bill-gates) and the great capitalists who each owe their fortune to a single product or innovation, in contrast to Buffett the pure investor. Kyle separately connects the Walton story to a [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley) anecdote on humility. [Sam Walton: Made in America](/notes/sam-walton-made-in-america) (Walton's autobiography) is logged in Kyle's anti-library, surfaced via [David Perell](/notes/david-perell)'s [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart), which distills Walton's operating philosophy: competitiveness as relentless self-correction, frugality, and [Discounting](/notes/discounting) as a flywheel.

**Context:** Sam Walton (1918–1992) founded Walmart in 1962 in Bentonville, Arkansas, building it into the world's largest retailer through everyday-low-pricing, aggressive cost discipline, and small-town expansion. His 1992 autobiography *Sam Walton: Made in America* is a widely cited primer on his operating principles.
