# Career Technical Education

Career Technical Education (the modern reframing of "vocational education") surfaces in the wiki as the counter-vision to college-as-cull. In [The War on Normal People](/books/the-war-on-normal-people), [Andrew Yang](/notes/andrew-yang) indicts an education system that treats college as "a mass sort" producing "brittle" students and prizing "finance as the new courage, branding as the new compassion, coding as the new contemplation." His counter is a serious embrace of vocational / career technical pathways, anchored by the statistic that only 6% of American high-schoolers were enrolled in a vocational course of study in 2013, versus 42% in the UK, 59% in Germany, and 67% in the Netherlands. The argument pairs with Sal Khan's framing that "virtually everyone is capable" of far more than we assume — education should build capacity rather than cull.

In Yang's framework, career technical education is one of the practical responses to [Automation](/notes/automation) and the displacement of middle-class work — a building block of a workable [Future of Work](/notes/future-of-work) alongside the Freedom Dividend ([Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income)).

**Context:** Career and Technical Education (CTE) is the U.S. policy term (formalized under the federal Perkins Act) for hands-on, occupation-oriented secondary and post-secondary programs — trades, healthcare, IT, manufacturing — that rebranded "vocational education" to shed its tracking-into-dead-ends stigma. Germany's dual-apprenticeship system is the most-cited international model.
