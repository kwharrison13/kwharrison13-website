# Pride and the Priesthood

"Pride and the Priesthood" is an LDS general-conference address (Ezra Taft Benson's well-known talk on pride) that recurs in Kyle's notes as a touchstone for the doctrine that pride is the universal sin and the root of spiritual decline. It appears as a "see also" reference in the Helaman 1–6 — The Rock of Our Redeemer Come Follow Me study, where the central theme is [The Pride Cycle](/notes/the-pride-cycle) — Mormon's repeated demonstration that riches breed pride, pride breeds destruction, and the cycle runs "again and again" until people build their foundation on "the rock of our Redeemer."

It also surfaces in Kyle's reading of [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment), tagged alongside [Beware of Pride](/notes/beware-of-pride) on Munger's distinction between destructive and constructive pride — Munger's argument that "self-regard in the form of a justified pride in a job well done, or a life well lived, is a large constructive force," and that it is "not a good idea to construe the bible's parable about the Pharisee and the Publican as condemning all pride." Across both sources the concept functions as the bridge between the secular psychology of [Excessive Self-Regard Tendency](/notes/excessive-self-regard-tendency) and the scriptural framing of pride as the threshold sin.

**Context:** "Beware of Pride" is the title of Ezra Taft Benson's April 1989 general-conference address, which frames pride (enmity — "my will against God's") as the central, universal sin that "is the great stumbling block to Zion."

## Where this appears

- Helaman 1–6 — The Rock of Our Redeemer — cited as a "see also" reference in a study centered on [The Pride Cycle](/notes/the-pride-cycle) and pride entering "the hearts of the people who professed to belong to the church of God."
- [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) — tagged with [Beware of Pride](/notes/beware-of-pride) on Munger's passage distinguishing destructive self-regard from the constructive pride of "a job well done."
