# Thomas Misa

Historian of technology, author of *Leonardo to the Internet*, whose argument grounds the anti-inevitability move in [The Value Chain of Capital](/essays/the-value-chain-of-capital). Quoted (via [L.M. Sacasas](/notes/l-m-sacasas), who cites him) for the line: "we lack a full picture of the technological alternatives that once existed… We see only the results and assume, understandably but in error, that there was no other path to the present. Yet it is a truism that the victors write the history, in technology as in war, and the technological 'paths not taken' are often suppressed or ignored."

In Kyle's research, Misa supplies one half of the essay's closing argument. Where [Aswath Damodaran](/notes/aswath-damodaran) gives the "capital chain is a pricing game" half, Sacasas — leaning on Misa — gives the "progress is not inevitable, the victors just write the history" half. Together they license the pivot from the **capital value chain** to the **innovation value chain**: if progress isn't a guaranteed output of capital, then "what you measure is what you get," and we should measure the drivers of creation rather than just capital in and out. The same framing seeds the technological strand of the [Determinism](/notes/determinism) page.

**Context:** Thomas J. Misa is an American historian of technology; his survey *Leonardo to the Internet: Technology and Culture from the Renaissance to the Present* traces how technological development is shaped by social, economic, and political forces rather than unfolding inevitably.
