# Wernher von Braun

In [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation), Wernher von Braun anchors two of the book's threads. First, as a model of decentralized organization: his system of "Monday notes" required engineers to surface the most salient issues on a single page, which von Braun would annotate in the margins and circulate, serving as the information hub while pushing solutions outward — an early template for tapping an organization's collective knowledge.

Second, and more central to the book, von Braun is its avatar for the religious, even eschatological, case for spaceflight. The book quotes him arguing that manned space flight should be evaluated "less in terms of economic efficiency and more in terms of spiritual effectiveness," and that it is "profoundly important for religious reasons that [mankind] travel to other worlds… for it may be Man's destiny to assure immortality, not only of his race but even of the life spark itself." The book explicitly frames [Elon Musk](/notes/elon-musk) as von Braun's heir — Musk's "we must preserve the light of consciousness by becoming a spacefaring civilization" echoing von Braun's vision, and Musk's almost-impossible Mars deadlines mirroring von Braun's 1969 plan to launch ships for Mars by 1981.

**Context:** Wernher von Braun (1912–1977) was a German-American rocket engineer who led development of the V-2 rocket for Nazi Germany, was brought to the US after WWII via Operation Paperclip, and became the chief architect of the Saturn V launch vehicle that carried Apollo astronauts to the Moon.
