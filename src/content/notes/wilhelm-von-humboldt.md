# Wilhelm von Humboldt

In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Wilhelm von Humboldt — described as the founder of the Humboldt University of Berlin and brother to the explorer Alexander von Humboldt — supplies one of the book's load-bearing claims about why writing *is* research. His formulation that "the professor is not there for the student and the student not for the professor; both are only there for the truth — and truth is always a public matter" anchors the book's argument that everything within a university aims at some form of publication. The key move Kyle preserves: "The moment the author can be removed from the scene, the written piece is a public claim on truth." This grounds the [Learning in Public](/notes/learning-in-public) thread — truth belongs to no one, it is the outcome of the scientific exchange of written ideas, so producing and presenting knowledge are two sides of one coin.

**Context:** Wilhelm von Humboldt (1767–1835) was a Prussian philosopher, linguist, and educational reformer whose model for the University of Berlin (founded 1810) fused teaching with research and academic freedom — the basis of the modern research university.
