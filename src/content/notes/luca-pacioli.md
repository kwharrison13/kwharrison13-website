# Luca Pacioli

The Renaissance mathematician whose 1494 treatise *Particularis de computis et scripturis* codified Venetian double-entry bookkeeping for print. He is the through-line between two of Kyle's books. In [Double Entry](/books/double-entry), Pacioli is the central historical figure: a teacher of both commercial (abbaco) and university mathematics and a former merchant's assistant in Venice, his *Summa* was the first printed book to deal with Hindu–Arabic arithmetic and contained the first printed treatise on Venetian bookkeeping — a method that, five hundred years later, "remains the foundation of modern accounting." The book credits him with translating business into the discardable, decision-ready numbers of "profit," a "major innovation in economic history."

In [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) by [Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson), Pacioli plays a quieter but pointed role: he is the disciplined collaborator whose geometric-proportions text Leonardo illustrated, and whose explanations Leonardo copied verbatim into his notebooks when a concept proved hard. The book uses Pacioli as a counterfactual — the rigorous partner who might have helped the undisciplined Leonardo actually *ship* and publish his work ([Learning in Public](/notes/learning-in-public) / shipping as the recurring theme).

**Context:** Luca Pacioli (c. 1447–1517) was an Italian Franciscan friar and mathematician, often called the "father of accounting" for systematizing double-entry bookkeeping; he collaborated with Leonardo da Vinci, who illustrated his *De divina proportione*.
