# Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius is the central exemplar of [Stoicism](/notes/stoicism) and self-discipline in Ryan Holiday's [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny). The book uses him to illustrate "restraint within abundance": despite his wealth and power, he "lived humbly… spending most of his reign not in glamorous palaces of marble but in the simple tent of a soldier at the front." Holiday notes that the *Meditations* were chiefly commands to himself — per translator Robin Waterfield, "300 of the 488 entries in Meditations are rules Marcus gave himself." Several of the book's operating maxims come from him directly: "Tolerant with others, strict with yourself," and his reminder not to "feel exasperated, or defeated, or despondent because your days aren't packed with wise and moral actions," but to "get back up when you fail." Holiday is careful to insist he was a real human being, not a "two-dimensional figure printed on the pages of history books" — "if they were perfect, they would not give us hope."

He also surfaces in [How to Maximize Creativity](/notes/how-to-maximize-creativity) ([David Perell](/notes/david-perell)), quoted on how thought shapes character: "Your mind will take the shape of what you frequently hold in thought, for the human spirit is colored by such impressions."

**Context:** Marcus Aurelius (121–180 CE) was Roman emperor from 161 to 180 CE and a Stoic philosopher; his private notebook, the *Meditations*, is one of the foundational surviving texts of Stoic thought.
