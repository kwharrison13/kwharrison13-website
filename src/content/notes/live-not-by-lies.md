# Live Not by Lies

"Live Not by Lies" is the 1974 essay-letter [Alexander Solzhenitsyn](/notes/alexander-solzhenitsyn) wrote to the Soviet intelligentsia on the day of his arrest, after *[The Gulag Archipelago](/notes/the-gulag-archipelago)* was published in France and he was stripped of Soviet citizenship and expelled. In the wiki it functions as the moral spine of [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) by [Arkady Ostrovsky](/notes/arkady-ostrovsky) — the touchstone the book keeps returning to. Per the book, Solzhenitsyn "knew there was only one way to defeat" the Soviet system, and the essay ends with a commandment in capital letters: "DON'T LIE! DON'T PARTICIPATE IN A LIE! DON'T SUPPORT A LIE!" He framed the daily lie not as "the whim of corrupt nature, but a mode of existence… incorporated into the state system as the vital link holding everything together, with billions of tiny fasteners."

The Invention of Russia uses the essay as a recurring lens on Soviet and post-Soviet media: it records that a later editorial "echoed Solzhenitsyn's 'Live Not by Lies'" with its own appeal to journalists — "If you don't have the strength or ability to tell the truth, at least do not participate in lies." The paradox the book draws out is that the opening of Soviet media could only be achieved through half-truths, yet once reality broke through, the system crumbled.

**Context:** "Live Not by Lies" (Russian: *Жить не по лжи*) is one of Solzhenitsyn's best-known short works — a call to ordinary individual non-participation in state falsehood as the minimal, achievable form of moral resistance.
