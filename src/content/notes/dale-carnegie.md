# Dale Carnegie

Dale Carnegie surfaces in Kyle's book notes as a hinge figure in the **self-help / influence lineage**. In [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life), Walter Isaacson notes that Carnegie "studied the Autobiography when writing *How To Win Friends And Influence People*" (1937) — which is why Kyle tags Franklin as the "patron saint of the self-help movement," the source of the craze for books featuring "simple rules and secrets about how to succeed in business and in life."

He appears again in [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked), where the Adler-expounding dialogue cites Carnegie praising [Alfred Adler](/notes/alfred-adler) as "a great psychologist who devoted his life to researching humans and their latent abilities," and frames Adler's teaching on recognition-seeking against Carnegie-style influence. Across both books, Carnegie functions as a node connecting Franklin's practical virtue-tracking, Adlerian psychology, and the modern self-improvement genre.

**Context:** Dale Carnegie (1888–1955) was an American writer and lecturer best known for *How to Win Friends and Influence People* (1936), one of the best-selling self-help books of all time, and for the Dale Carnegie Course in public speaking and interpersonal skills.
