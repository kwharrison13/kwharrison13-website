# Revelation

Across Kyle's reading, revelation is treated less as a dramatic lightning-bolt event and more as an iterative, often quiet process. [Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) makes this its central theme: the smaller-temples idea "developed over many years and a variety of experiences" and "didn't require a visitation from God or angels." [John A. Widtsoe](/notes/john-widtsoe), asked how long since the church last had revelation, replied "Oh, about last Thursday" — earnest prayer and sincere discussion of weighty matters open the way for the Lord to direct His church. [Henry B. Eyring](/notes/henry-b-eyring) frames a prophet's gift as bringing revelation *to other people*: "he brings down the power of heaven into my life."

In [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt), the Givens use [Parley P. Pratt](/notes/parley-p-pratt)'s *The Fountain of Knowledge* to argue that revelation is the fountain *behind* scripture, not the reverse — "if ye are not led by revelation how can ye escape the damnation of Hell." Scripture is the product of revealed truth; the wiser move is to "secure the fountain." [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) himself acknowledged that "many things in the scriptures . . . do not, as they now stand, accord with the revelation of the Holy Ghost to me," and [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) warned against slavish obedience that isn't ratified by "the spirit of revelation" — "I wish them to know for themselves." [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) sets revelation alongside scientific inference as one of "two ways of proceeding," distinguishing truths known "by revelation and by long experience" from speculation, and insisting fact and fancy be kept separated in religion as in science.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint theology, revelation is communication from God to humans — personal (through the [Holy Ghost](/notes/holy-ghost)) or institutional (through prophets and apostles) — and is doctrinally treated as ongoing rather than confined to a closed scriptural canon.
