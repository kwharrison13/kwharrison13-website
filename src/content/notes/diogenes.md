# Diogenes

In Kyle's notes, Diogenes appears only as a quotation [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) invokes in *The Psychology of Human Misjudgment* (collected in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack)). Munger cites Diogenes asking, **"Of what use is a philosopher who never offends anybody?"** — using the line to defend speaking plainly and unconventionally even at the cost of seeming arrogant. Kyle annotates the highlight with his own gloss: *"If you're not offending somebody then you're not thinking radically enough."* So the entity functions here as a one-line emblem of intellectual courage, sitting alongside Munger's "shoe button complex" passage on when it is and isn't acceptable to speak declaratively outside your expertise.

**Context:** Diogenes of Sinope (c. 412–323 BC) was a Greek philosopher and founding figure of Cynicism, famous for deliberately provocative public behavior — living in a barrel, carrying a lamp in daylight "looking for an honest man" — meant to expose social pretense. His tradition of confrontational truth-telling is what the Munger quotation draws on.
