# Interest Discovery

A recurring frame in Kyle's reading: figuring out what you are actually suited for, where the originating source is [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child). Two complementary versions show up across his book notes. In [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits), it's the fit test — "the mark of whether you are made for a task is not whether you love it but whether you can handle the difficulty," captured as "what feels like fun to me, but work to others," and tied to the explore/exploit trade-off of discovering where your edge lies. The note pairs this with the warning that no habit stays interesting forever, so genuine fit is what carries you through the boredom.

In [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime), the frame appears as the recognition side of the same skill: Bob Iger's observation that the career moments that mattered "weren't the obvious ones" — quiet inflection points rather than dramatic ones. Kyle's note treats recognizing those moments as itself a *learnable* skill, sitting alongside the related [Lily Pads of Your Career](/notes/lily-pads-of-your-career) and [Natural Selection of Time](/notes/natural-selection-of-time) frames for how careers actually unfold. Across both books, Interest Discovery is less about chasing what excites you and more about reading the signal of what you can sustain and where significance is quietly accruing.

## Where this appears

- [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) — the explore/exploit trade-off and "what feels like fun to me, but work to others" as the mark of fit; "you have to fall in love with boredom."
- [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime) — recognizing quiet career inflection points as a learnable skill, pairing the frame with [Lily Pads of Your Career](/notes/lily-pads-of-your-career).
