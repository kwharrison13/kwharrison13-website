# Cyberwarfare

In [2034 — A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war), cyberwarfare is the decisive front. The novel imagines "Chinese cyber dominance of the American forces" as total: a "highly sophisticated artificial intelligence capability" aboard the carrier *Zheng He* deploys cyber tools "at precisely the right moment to infiltrate US systems." The book's central argument is an inversion of conventional wisdom about American strength: "the deregulation that had resulted in so much American innovation and economic strength was now an American weakness." America's "disaggregated online infrastructure" is portrayed as vulnerable in a way China's centralized infrastructure is not — the Americans "lacked the capability to hack into China's online infrastructure" while China could "shut down much of their country's electric grid with a single keystroke," rendering nuclear retaliation "outdated and absurd." This ties directly into the wiki's [Innovation](/notes/innovation) thread: the same deregulated, decentralized tech ecosystem that drives American dynamism becomes its strategic soft spot.

**Context:** *2034: A Novel of the Next World War* is a 2021 speculative-fiction novel by [Elliot Ackerman](/notes/elliot-ackerman) and retired admiral [James Stavridis](/notes/james-stavridis), dramatizing a near-future US–China conflict in which cyber and electronic warfare, rather than conventional firepower, prove decisive.
