# Neal A. Maxwell

An apostle of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints whose words recur across Kyle's gospel-study corpus, almost always on the theme of discipleship as costly, refining submission. In [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) he is cited for urging BYU professors "to take both scholarship and discipleship seriously, because faithful scholarship brings together both the life of the mind and the life of the spirit," and for the line "If we are serious about our discipleship, Jesus will eventually request each of us to do those very things which are most difficult for us to do." In the Patience — Topical Study he supplies the study's hinge idea — that "God can educate our desires" and "what we insistently desire, over time, is what we will eventually become" — and in Self-Mastery — Topical Study he is quoted on meekness "when one feels forsaken and forgotten and alone 'amid the encircling gloom.'" He appears as a recurring voice in [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book), "quoted on God's individual care and the spiritually improving but imperfect person."

[Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) gives the most personal portrait: Maxwell was the apostle and correspondent whose writing Eugene England edited, and the recipient of England's 1990 "Abrahamic test" letter. The book shows their friction and friendship — Maxwell "gamely" inviting England's editorial help with his "mannered" syntax ("I must go on learning and growing before the laws of nature make that too difficult"), and later, in a line that "aggravated England's already considerable misery," warning that England's "struggles may reflect having lived so long in the intellectual world that you might be 'past feeling.'" He is described there as "a man who came increasingly to be revered for his meekness and humility."

**Context:** Neal A. Maxwell (1926–2004) was a member of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, known for his distinctive, alliterative prose and his emphasis on discipleship, consecration, and submitting one's will to God.
