# Terrorism

In [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century), Harari develops a distinctive theory of terrorism built on the asymmetry between its material weakness and its psychological leverage. "Terrorism is the weapon of a marginal and weak segment of humanity" — terrorists "can barely make a dent in the enemy's material power," so they win only by provoking the enemy to "misuse his intact strength and overreact." His central image is the **fly in the bull's ear**: a fly too weak to move a single teacup destroys a china shop not by force but by getting inside a bull's ear and buzzing until the enraged bull wrecks the shop itself. "This is what happened after 9/11, as Islamic fundamentalists incited the American bull to destroy the Middle Eastern china shop. Now they flourish in the wreckage."

The argument's payoff is a reversal of where the real danger lies: "in most cases, this overreaction to terrorism poses a far greater threat to our security than the terrorists themselves." Harari urges that counter-terrorism "think more like theater producers and less like army generals," since "nothing the terrorists do can defeat us. We are the only ones who can defeat ourselves." He also implicates the media, which "obsessively reports terrorist attacks and greatly inflates their danger" because terror coverage outsells reports on diabetes or air pollution — providing the publicity that the "theater of terror" depends on.

**Context:** Terrorism is the use of violence or intimidation against civilians to advance political, religious, or ideological aims, typically by an actor too weak to confront a state directly. The "provocation strategy" Harari describes — baiting a stronger adversary into self-damaging overreaction — is a recognized concept in terrorism studies.

- [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) — the "fly in the bull's ear" theory: terrorism as a weapon of the weak whose real damage comes from the target's overreaction, amplified by media coverage.
