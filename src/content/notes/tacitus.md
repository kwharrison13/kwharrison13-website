# Tacitus

The Roman historian Tacitus appears in [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) as part of the classical canon that formed the early American "education for citizenship." The book lists him alongside [Cicero](/notes/cicero), [Plutarch](/notes/plutarch), [Sallust](/notes/sallust), and [Thucydides](/notes/thucydides) as the core of an education built on ancient history — framing "the classics" not as a discrete subject "like geography or history, but rather a lens through which to examine and understand the life around you." The reference grounds Kyle's tags on #classics and #literature in the founding-era idea that studying antiquity trained civic judgment.

**Context:** Tacitus (c. 56–120 CE) was a Roman senator and historian whose *Annals* and *Histories* are major sources for the early Roman Empire; his terse, morally probing style made him a touchstone for republican thinkers wary of tyranny.

## Where this appears

- [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) — named among Cicero, Plutarch, Sallust, and Thucydides as the classical historians at the core of the era's "education for citizenship."
