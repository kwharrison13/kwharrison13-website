# Imagined Orders

"Imagined orders" is [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari)'s central thesis as Kyle engages it across [Sapiens](/books/sapiens), [Homo Deus](/books/homo-deus), and [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century): large-scale human cooperation depends on shared fictional narratives. In Harari's framing, "there are no gods in the universe, no nations, no money, no human rights, no laws, and no justice outside the common imagination of human beings," and "humans think in stories rather than in facts, numbers, or equations, and the simpler the story, the better." The founding statement of the idea lives in [Sapiens](/books/sapiens); it ties directly into the wiki's [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) concept (Harari's narrative theory of cooperation).

Kyle reads the thesis with "sustained, respectful disagreement." His recurring objection lands on the line between a fiction and a shared narrative of *real events*: against Harari's claim that "all mass identities are based on fictional stories, not on scientific facts," Kyle counters that American identity built on the actual events of the Revolution is a shared narrative, not a fabrication — and that Harari collapses the two (see [Identity Formation](/notes/identity-formation)). The disagreement extends to meaning: Kyle reads [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation) as a live theological alternative to Harari's secular resolution of meaning-loss after God.

**Context:** "Imagined orders" is the term Yuval Noah Harari coined in *Sapiens* (2011) for the shared myths — money, nations, laws, corporations, human rights — that have no objective existence but enable cooperation among large numbers of strangers, the capacity he argues distinguishes *Homo sapiens* from other species.
