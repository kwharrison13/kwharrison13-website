# Religious Freedom

Across Kyle's Mormon-history and gospel-study reading, **religious freedom** is the recurring stake — the public-square question of how much religious practice and cultural diversity a democracy can incorporate. [American Zion](/books/american-zion) frames the founding conflict directly: the federal war on Mormon polygamy and theocracy produced legal disputes that "still define the limits of religious liberty today," with the underlying question being "how much religious freedom and cultural diversity a democracy could incorporate." [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor) supplies the rhetorical core in Taylor's Constitution- and Declaration-grounded defense against Schuyler Colfax — that "the faith of every man is a matter between himself and God alone" — tied closely to Taylor's instinct for [Agency](/notes/agency) ("I was not born a slave!").

Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks turns it into a prescriptive program: believers should generally not seek laws to enforce distinctive acts of worship, but *should* defend religious freedom, always tolerantly, refusing to be deterred by the "legislating morality" charge and remaining "sensitive to the views of the minority" where they hold the majority. Oaks ties the threat to the "ascendancy of [Moral Relativism](/notes/moral-relativism)" and a declining public esteem for religion. [Restoration: God's Call To The 21st Century](/books/restoration-god-s-call-to-the-21st-century) reads the eleventh Article of Faith's reciprocal "claim" and "allowance" as a model for engaging pluralism — religious freedom as a "crop planted in the commons" open to all to nurture and harvest.

**Context:** Religious freedom (or religious liberty) is the principle, protected in the U.S. by the First Amendment's Free Exercise and Establishment clauses, that individuals may hold and practice their beliefs without government coercion. It has long been a central concern of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints given the 19th-century legal conflicts over polygamy.
