# Liberal Arts

The liberal arts surface in Kyle's notes from two angles. In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), Adler frames them as the arts that regulate reading and writing — "all knowledge was once ordered in relation to the seven liberal arts: grammar, rhetoric, and logic, the trivium; arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, and music, the quadrivium." Adler makes the term load-bearing: the liberal arts "acquire their name" from the reader's duty to take a position and own his own judgment ("To regard anyone except yourself as responsible for your judgment is to be a slave, not a free man"). The standard he sets is concrete — a good liberal-arts high school "ought to produce graduates who are competent analytical readers," and a good college, "competent syntopical readers."

In [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), the liberal arts appear as a workforce argument rather than a reading one: "Core skills taught in the liberal arts like critical thinking, problem solving, and communications are in high demand today in every industry." Together the two sources position the liberal arts as the transferable, foundational skills — of reasoning, reading, and communicating — that underwrite both [Active Reading](/notes/active-reading) and modern employability.

**Context:** The liberal arts are the classical "arts of a free person" — historically the trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric) and quadrivium (arithmetic, geometry, music, astronomy) — and in modern usage a broad education in the humanities, sciences, and reasoning skills rather than narrow vocational training.

## Where this appears

- [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) — Adler's seven liberal arts (trivium + quadrivium) as the arts regulating reading/writing, and the source of the reader's duty to own his own judgment
- [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — liberal-arts skills (critical thinking, problem solving, communication) framed as in high demand across every industry
