# Checklists

Checklists are one of the most-tagged ideas in Kyle's [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) corpus: the disciplined routine of running every problem against a fixed set of factors so you don't miss the obvious. Munger describes his own method as "a simple search engine in my mind to go through checklist-style" ([Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics)), and insists checklist routines should be "a permanent part of the knowledge system" of any broadly educated person ([Charlie Munger — Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address)). His sharpest version is a rebuke of overconfidence: "How can smart people so often be wrong? They don't do what I'm telling you to do: use a checklist to be sure you get all the main models and use them together in a multimodular way" — and "these psychology professors think they're so smart that they don't need a checklist. But they aren't that smart. Almost nobody is." The [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) highlights catalog this concretely in Munger's multi-part **Investing Principles Checklist** (Risk, Independence, Preparation, Intellectual Humility, Analytic Rigor, Allocation, Patience, Focus), where Kyle annotates the connection to his "Founder's Fund diligence questions from Cedar work."

In Kyle's own practice the idea translates directly into investing process. The [Networked Conviction — Roam + Investing](/essays/networked-conviction-roam-investing) research logs investor templates and stage-gate checklists — "five sections that each have 5 key questions all of which they need to answer before they get to LOI stage" — and catalogs canonical examples: [Sequoia](/notes/sequoia)'s original criteria, Chase Coleman's [Tiger Global Management](/notes/tiger-global-management) portfolio rules, [Don Valentine](/notes/don-valentine)'s "why should I care about this?" The throughline is that checklists are an antidote to the human tendency to act on incomplete analysis, and a way to make [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) operational rather than aspirational.
