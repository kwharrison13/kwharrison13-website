# Networking

Across Kyle's reading, networking recurs as a deliberate, compounding investment rather than a transactional scramble. [Recession Proof](/books/recession-proof) frames it as the core "build your network" prescription — "a huge part of building yourself is building your network... Network, network, network. You need that list of five to 10 people you can call any time that would give you a job" — with the crucial timing caveat that **the best time to network is during the good years, not during a recession**, so the relationships are already in place when the downturn comes. [The Start-Up of You](/books/the-start-up-of-you) ([Reid Hoffman](/notes/reid-hoffman) / [Ben Casnocha](/notes/ben-casnocha)) sharpens it into a skill: career breakouts come from "an optimal blend of cohesion and creativity — strong ties and weak ties," and the advanced move is **network intelligence**: identifying the right people, asking questions that invite useful answers, and synthesizing the result into a competitive edge.

The throughline across these sources is that good networking is engineered and indirect. [How to Maximize Serendipity](/notes/how-to-maximize-serendipity) argues "writing is the best kind of networking" — publishing regularly creates "a vehicle for serendipity" — and that "the people you want to meet don't go to networking events," so meet them on the basketball court or over a meal instead. The constraint side shows up as [Dunbar's Number](/notes/dunbar-s-number) (the ~150-relationship ceiling that bounds how many ties anyone can actively maintain) and as Kyle's own practice in [Networking Calls](/notes/networking-calls), the sourcing/relationship logs from his investing work. The open-source-knowledge notes extend it to talent identification — mapping the "rivers and streams" of a community to surface under-appreciated talent pools.
