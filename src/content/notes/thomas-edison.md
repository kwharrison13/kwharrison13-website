# Thomas Edison

In Kyle's wiki, Edison is primarily a voice on [Focus](/notes/focus) — the discipline of applying effort in one direction, to one object, rather than scattering it. The quote Kyle saved in his July 2nd, 2020 daily log frames it sharply: everyone works all day, but most "do it about a great many things," whereas "success is the product of the severest kind of mental and physical application" — sticking to "one thing… letting all else go." That line anchors both the [Focus](/notes/focus) and [Thinking](/notes/thinking) concept pages (and is logged under [Quotes](/notes/quotes)), where it pairs with [The Magic of Elon Musk](/notes/the-magic-of-elon-musk)'s causal chain — "Goals breed commitment. Commitment breeds focus. Focus breeds achievement" — and with the [Capital Allocator Mindset](/notes/capital-allocator-mindset) idea that a budget constraint *forces* focus.

Edison also surfaces as a reference point in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) (the history of Bell Labs), invoked for the theory-vs-experiment contrast in industrial research.

**Context:** Thomas Edison (1847–1931) was the prolific American inventor and entrepreneur behind the phonograph, the practical incandescent light bulb, and the first industrial research laboratory at Menlo Park, holding over a thousand U.S. patents.

## Quotes

> "You do something all day long, don't you? Every one does. If you get up at seven o'clock and go to bed at eleven, you have put in sixteen good hours, and it is certain with most people, that they have been doing something all the time. They have been either walking, or [Reading](/notes/reading), or [Writing](/notes/writing), or [Thinking](/notes/thinking). The only trouble is that they do it about a great many things and I do it about one. **If they took the time in question and applied it in one direction, to one object, they would succeed. Success is sure to follow such application. The trouble lies in the fact that people do not have an object, one thing, to which they stick, letting all else go. Success is the product of the severest kind of mental and physical application.**" — [Thomas Edison](/notes/thomas-edison)
