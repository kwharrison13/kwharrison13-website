# Heuristics

Heuristics shows up in Kyle's notes from two angles. In [Range](/books/range) (by [David Epstein](/notes/david-epstein)), the term carries [Daniel Kahneman](/notes/daniel-kahneman)'s "heuristics and biases" lens on expert judgment — the view, contrasted against Gary Klein's naturalistic decision-making, that experience breeds skill only in "kind" learning environments. Kahneman found that in many domains "experience had not helped at all" and "frequently bred confidence but not skill," which anchors Range's argument about the limits of [Expertise](/notes/expertise) in "wicked" domains where patterns don't repeat.

In [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) (by Terryl and Fiona Givens), Kyle uses "heuristics" to label paradigms as inherited assumptions that guide but also limit inquiry. He tagged it directly against the book's line that our worldview is "a collective set of assumptions we carry with us that condition every question we ask" — paradigms that "make it possible to guide inquiry, but they can also limit and impede" it, since "only when we have left a misguided assumption behind are we able to see it clearly." Here heuristics connects to the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) needed to recognize and shed limiting assumptions.

**Context:** Heuristics are mental shortcuts or rules of thumb that allow people to make judgments and decisions quickly, often studied (notably by Kahneman and Tversky) for the systematic biases they can introduce.

## Where this appears

- [Range](/books/range) — Kahneman's "heuristics and biases" view of flawed expert judgment, contrasted with naturalistic decision-making.
- [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) — paradigms as inherited assumptions that guide but also limit inquiry; tagged by Kyle to a highlight on worldview and conditioning.
