# Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition runs through Kyle's learning-and-memory reading as the mechanism for encoding knowledge into durable long-term [Memory](/notes/memory). [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work) makes it most explicit: [Andy Matuschak](/notes/andy-matuschak) treats spaced repetition as "helping people encode more into long-term memory," because "most of [a thing's] prerequisites must be not just familiar but fluent, encoded in long-term memory." It is the core mechanic of [Quantum Country](/notes/quantum-country), the "book" on quantum computation he built with [Michael Nielsen](/notes/michael-nielsen) that "interleaves prose with spaced repetition review sessions."

The books frame it as a "desirable difficulty." [Range](/books/range) describes spacing (and testing) as something that "make[s] knowledge stick… durable," while "all slow down learning and make performance suffer, in the short term." [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) connects it back to the slip-box: retrieving old ideas "on an irregular basis" is "the idea behind flashcards," though it flags flashcards' downside — isolated information that is "neither elaborated on nor embedded in some form of context," unlike notes woven into a "latticework of mental models."

**Context:** Spaced repetition is a learning technique in which review of material is scheduled at expanding intervals timed to the forgetting curve; software like Anki and SuperMemo automates it, and it is widely used for durable memorization of facts and vocabulary.
