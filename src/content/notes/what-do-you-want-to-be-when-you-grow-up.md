# What do you want to be when you grow up?

In [Becoming](/books/becoming), Michelle Obama singles out "What do you want to be when you grow up?" as "one of the most useless questions an adult can ask a child." Her objection is structural: the question implies that growing up is finite — "as if at some point you become something and that's the end." Against that destination model, she offers *becoming* as forward motion: "a way to reach continuously toward a better self. The journey doesn't end." In Kyle's notes the phrase recurs as a tag alongside #[What gives you energy?](/notes/what-gives-you-energy), pairing the rejected question with the better one — a shift from asking a child to name a fixed endpoint to asking what actually animates them.

**Context:** "What do you want to be when you grow up?" is a stock question adults pose to children, framing identity as a single future occupation to be chosen and arrived at. Critiques of it — including Michelle Obama's in *Becoming* — argue it misrepresents growth as a terminal state rather than an open-ended process.
