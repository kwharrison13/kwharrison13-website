# Progress Studies

In Kyle's corpus, Progress Studies is the intellectual movement diagnosing techno-scientific stagnation and asking how to restart it. The clearest statement is in [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) (Byrne Hobart and Tobias Huber), which Kyle reads as diagnosing stagnation — "declining TFP, diminishing research returns, the reproducibility crisis" — and prescribing bubbles as the exit ramp, against the backdrop of [Tyler Cowen](/notes/tyler-cowen)'s *Great Stagnation* thesis. Marc Andreessen's [It's Time To Build](/notes/it-s-time-to-build) is treated as "a foundational text" for the broader build/progress movement, its "want and build the things we need" framing helping catalyze it.

The movement threads through several adjacent interests. [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) connects it to von Neumann's 1955 "Can We Survive Technology?" frame ("for progress there is no cure"). Kyle's Open-Source Knowledge - Research notes pull in [Matt Clancy](/notes/matt-clancy)'s work on free knowledge and innovation, linking Progress Studies to [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia), [Academic Research](/notes/academic-research), and the historical-futurism idea that public knowledge accelerates innovation. And the charter-cities / city-building strand ([Mark Lutter](/notes/mark-lutter), [Charter Cities](/notes/city-building), [City Building](/notes/city-building)) is filed here as the institutional-experimentation wing of the same project.

**Context:** "Progress Studies" was named in a 2019 Atlantic essay by Patrick Collison and Tyler Cowen calling for a new field studying what actually drives scientific, technological, and economic progress — and how to get more of it. It has since become an umbrella for writers and builders concerned with stagnation, growth, and the institutions of innovation.
