# 1 Kings 19

The story of Elijah at Horeb in 1 Kings 19 recurs in Kyle's gospel study as a touchstone for two themes: how God speaks, and the role of fasting. In his General Conference — October 2021 notes he quotes the climactic passage — the Lord is not in the wind, earthquake, or fire, "and after the fire a still small voice" (19:11–12) — as the model for how revelation comes. The chapter reappears in his Revelation — Topical Study among the cross-references probing "the nature of that voice" of the Lord ([D&C 88](/notes/dandc-88):90; 19:9–13).

In his Fasting - September 5, 2021 study, 1 Kings 19:8 anchors the precedent of prophetic fasting: Elijah "arose, and did eat and drink, and went in the strength of that meat forty days and forty nights unto Horeb the mount of God" — paired with Moses as scriptural exemplars of the forty-day fast.

**Context:** 1 Kings 19 recounts the prophet Elijah's flight after Mount Carmel — despairing under a juniper tree, fed by an angel, traveling forty days to Horeb, and there encountering God not in wind, earthquake, or fire but in "a still small voice."

## Where this appears

- General Conference — October 2021 — the "still small voice" (19:11–12) as the pattern for how revelation comes
- Fasting - September 5, 2021 — Elijah's forty-day fast (19:8) as a prophetic precedent for fasting
- Revelation — Topical Study — cross-referenced (19:9–13) in exploring the nature of the voice of the Lord
