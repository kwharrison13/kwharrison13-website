# Deterrence

Deterrence is the central organizing concept of [Nuclear War — a Scenario](/books/nuclear-war-a-scenario), which interrogates its psychology, its failure modes, and its command-and-control machinery. [Annie Jacobsen](/notes/annie-jacobsen) traces the logic to [Thomas Schelling](/notes/thomas-schelling)'s *Arms and Influence* — "the power to hurt is bargaining power" — and dramatizes the doctrine through SIOP (the Single Integrated Operational Plan) and Launch-on-Warning, the Cold War policy of firing on sensor warning rather than absorbing a first blow. The book's tension is that "the premise of deterrence is that nuclear war is never supposed to happen," yet deterrence is built on a system of systems that, like all machines, can break: "One missile could be a misread. Two is not a mistake. Deterrence has failed." It connects to [Game Theory](/notes/game-theory) (Schelling's bargaining-as-diplomacy), [Systems Thinking](/notes/systems-thinking) (Jay Forrester's "system of systems" framing), and [Risk Management](/notes/risk-management) (FEMA's "low-probability, high-consequence" tail risk).

[The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) reframes deterrence as a *conventional* problem rather than a nuclear one. Christian Brose's argument is that the US "exquisite-platform" paradigm — carriers, bombers, the F-35 — is the wrong shape to deter a rising China, and that credible deterrence now requires "lots of cheaper, more autonomous nodes networked into a lattice" ([Anduril](/notes/anduril)'s thesis). His through-line is urgency: "you don't have the time you think you have." Together the two books bracket deterrence across the nuclear and conventional domains — the psychology of never-using on one side, the hardware of credibly-being-able-to on the other.

**Context:** Deterrence is the strategic doctrine of preventing an adversary's action by threatening unacceptable retaliation. Nuclear deterrence (mutual assured destruction, second-strike capability) dominated Cold War strategy; "conventional deterrence" concerns whether non-nuclear forces are credible enough to dissuade attack. Thomas Schelling won the 2005 Nobel in economics partly for his game-theoretic analysis of deterrence and bargaining.
