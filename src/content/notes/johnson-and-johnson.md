# Johnson & Johnson

Johnson & Johnson recurs in Kyle's notes as a case study in two virtues: clarity of guiding principle and institutional honesty about past mistakes. [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) cites the company's credo — "Customers are first; shareholders are last" — and frames the Tylenol recall as a decision driven by that pre-articulated guiding principle rather than by short-term financials, the kind of essentialist clarity that makes a hard call obvious in the moment.

The company also anchors Kyle's thinking on [Post-Mortems](/notes/post-mortems). [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in *[The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment)*, holds up J&J's "autopsy equivalents" as a model: where most corporations quietly forget a foolish acquisition once it goes bad, J&J's rules force everyone to revisit old acquisitions and compare predictions against outcomes — "a very smart thing to do." That passage feeds directly into the post-mortem research, where J&J sits alongside Pixar, [Amazon](/notes/amazon), and Quibi as organizations that build reflection into their process.

**Context:** Johnson & Johnson is an American multinational healthcare and consumer-products company founded in 1886. Its 1982 response to the Tylenol cyanide-tampering crisis — a swift nationwide recall guided by its long-standing corporate credo — is a standard business-school example of values-driven crisis management.
