# Vinod Khosla

Venture capitalist, Sun Microsystems co-founder, and founder of [Khosla Ventures](/notes/khosla-ventures). In [The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future), Khosla is the avatar of contrarian, anti-incremental investing. He demands "radical dreams, the bolder and more improbable the better," dismissing incremental ideas as *"one sheet of toilet paper, not two,"* and quotes (eclectically borrowing from George Bernard Shaw and Martin Luther King Jr.) that *"all progress depends upon the unreasonable man,"* the *"creatively maladjusted"* — *"the only thing that's important is something that's improbable."* His investing philosophy runs through a "Yoda approach": empower people who "feel the force" and let them work their magic. He is also the source of an experts-vs-outsiders thread Kyle flags — *"I can't think of a single, major innovation coming from experts in the last thirty, forty years."*

Khosla frames failure as structurally permissible rather than merely encouraged — the distinction Kyle tags under [Failure](/notes/failure). On Pat Brown's bet to eliminate animal husbandry, Khosla observed that even if Brown is "mocked" for the hubris, the mockery would be misplaced: *"Which is better: to try and fail, or to fail to try?"* In his telling, reasonable, well-adjusted people routinely fail at life's important missions by never attempting them, and there is "no glory in projects that will probably succeed." [The Power Law: Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) also notes that Khosla himself, "cocky and obnoxious," was fired from an early job and "became a venture capitalist."

He recurs through [Erik Kriessmann](/notes/erik-kriessmann), who ran the talent function at [Khosla Ventures](/notes/khosla-ventures) before moving to [Index Ventures](/notes/index-ventures). Per Erik, Khosla's only success metric for the talent partner was whether founders said *"I love him"* — once founders gave that answer, Khosla "didn't need to hear anything else," and Erik's job was simply to ensure Khosla spent the right time with the right people.

**Context:** Vinod Khosla (b. 1955) co-founded Sun Microsystems, was a partner at Kleiner Perkins, and founded Khosla Ventures in 2004, the firm known for aggressive deep-tech and clean-energy "black swan" bets.
