# Epictetus

Stoic philosopher whose lines recur across Kyle's notes as crisp instructions on humility, speech, and learning. He is one of the four Stoic voices — alongside [Marcus Aurelius](/notes/marcus-aurelius), [Seneca](/notes/seneca), and Musonius Rufus — that furnish the operating quotes for [Ryan Holiday](/notes/ryan-holiday)'s [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), the lineage the book is built on (source: [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny)).

Epictetus arrives in the wiki most heavily through [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), who closes his [Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address) with three of his lines: "First learn the meaning of what you say, and then speak"; "If you want to improve, be content to be thought foolish and stupid"; and "It is impossible to begin to learn that which one thinks one already knows." Munger deploys these in service of the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) and the duty to know what you don't know — the same Epictetus humility quote anchors the inversion-and-humility discipline in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) (sources: [Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address), [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack)). The "first learn the meaning of what you say" line also surfaces in Kyle's [Public Speaking](/notes/public-speaking) concept page as a brevity-and-substance discipline, and a paraphrase — two ears, one mouth, "so that we can listen twice as much as we speak" — appears in his Patience — Topical Study beside James 1:19.

**Context:** Epictetus (c. 50–135 AD) was a Greek Stoic philosopher, born a slave in Hierapolis, who taught in Nicopolis after gaining his freedom. He wrote nothing himself; his teachings survive through his student Arrian's *Discourses* and the *Enchiridion* (Handbook), centered on the dichotomy of control — distinguishing what is within our power from what is not.
