# Mark Twain

In Kyle's corpus, Twain functions almost entirely as a *source of aphorisms* invoked by other thinkers — most heavily by [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger). The single most-quoted line is the epistemic-humility maxim "It ain't what you don't know that gets you into trouble; it's what you know for sure that just ain't so," which recurs across [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) and three of Munger's talks ([Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment), [Charlie Munger — Harvard Law School Fiftieth Reunion Address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address)). Kyle pairs it with his own note that "a measure of intelligence is how often you've changed your mind." Munger also borrows Twain on reading ("The man who doesn't read good books has no advantage over the man who can't read them" — in [Charlie Munger — A Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom, Revisited](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom-revisited)) and on the irritation of moral example ("Few things are harder to put up with than the annoyance of a good example" — in [Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics)).

Beyond Munger, Twain appears in [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) via the experience caution "A man who carries a cat by the tail learns something he can learn in no other way" (applied to first-time M&A sellers); in [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) via the famous joke about his father seeming to learn so much between the boy's fourteenth and twenty-first years (used to illustrate adolescent prefrontal-cortex development); and in [Intellectual Property](/notes/intellectual-property) / the Hits Business research as a piece of cultural-economic history — the 1930 *Tom Sawyer* film adaptation grossing $11M is offered as evidence that "Hollywood has always banked on I.P."

**Context:** Mark Twain (Samuel Langhorne Clemens, 1835–1910) was the American author of *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* and *Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*, widely regarded as a foundational figure in American literature and a prolific source of widely-quoted aphorisms (many apocryphal).
