# Manifesto (1890)

[Wilford Woodruff](/notes/wilford-woodruff)'s 1890 declaration ending the practice of plural marriage in the LDS Church, and the dramatic spine of [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) (Volume 2 of the Church's narrative history). In Kyle's reading, the Manifesto is the climax of a "temporal–spiritual calculus": Woodruff weighs the loss of the temples — and the ordinances for the living and dead they house — against holding the line on [Polygamy](/notes/polygamy) under the federal antipolygamy crusade, and chooses "to act for the temporal salvation of the Church."

The Manifesto is also the book's most live theological case study of [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation). Woodruff's defense — "inspiration *is* revelation… a man is not always required to say 'Thus saith the Lord'" — raises the recurring questions Kyle probes in the margins: why is Woodruff's medium of revelatory reception different from Joseph Smith's, and can a commandment ever be said to be irrevocable? It connects to [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept). The human cost is rendered through Lorena Larsen's Abraham-and-Isaac "dark night" and B. H. Roberts's inability to raise his hand to sustain it.

**Context:** The 1890 Manifesto (Official Declaration 1) was issued by LDS Church President Wilford Woodruff, publicly disavowing new plural marriages amid federal anti-polygamy legislation that had seized Church property and threatened its temples.
