# Temperance

In Kyle's wiki, temperance is one rung of the 2 Peter 1 "divine nature" ladder — the progression by which a disciple becomes "a partaker of the divine nature": faith → virtue → knowledge → **temperance** → patience → godliness → brotherly kindness → charity. It appears as one of the [Christlike Attributes](/notes/christlike-attributes) enumerated in [Ezra Taft Benson](/notes/ezra-taft-benson)'s 1986 priesthood address Godly Characteristics of the Master. There, temperance is defined as self-control: a priesthood holder "is restrained in his emotions and verbal expressions… He is the master of his emotions, not the other way around." Benson illustrates it through anger and temper specifically — a man who curses or abuses his family is "guilty of grievous sin," and if he can't control his temper "it is a sad admission that he is not in control of his thoughts." He quotes [David O. McKay](/notes/david-o-mckay): "A man who cannot control his temper… moves in daily life very close to the animal plane."

In the ladder, temperance is closely linked to [Patience](/notes/patience) (the next rung), which Benson calls "another form of self-control" — composure under stress, postponing gratification, waiting on the Lord. Both are framed as expressions of [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery).

**Context:** Temperance is one of the four classical cardinal virtues (with prudence, justice, and fortitude) and, in the King James New Testament, appears in 2 Peter 1:6 in the list of qualities to add to faith — generally rendered as moderation or self-control.

## Where this appears

- Godly Characteristics of the Master — [Ezra Taft Benson](/notes/ezra-taft-benson) defines temperance as self-control and mastery of one's emotions, illustrated through the control of anger; quotes [David O. McKay](/notes/david-o-mckay).
- [Christlike Attributes](/notes/christlike-attributes) — temperance is one rung of the 2 Peter 1 divine-nature ladder of attributes a disciple is charged to develop.
