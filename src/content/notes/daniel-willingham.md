# Daniel Willingham

Daniel Willingham is the cognitive scientist quoted in [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) for the principle that "students learn best by building on ideas they already know, which is why a well-sequenced [Curriculum](/notes/curriculum) is crucial." Conversely, "burdening students with too much information at once can cause a kind of cognitive overload." On the [Curriculum](/notes/curriculum) concept page, Kyle uses Willingham to supply the pedagogical view: curriculum compounds with environment and pedagogy — cognitively rich homes, project-based and [Montessori](/notes/montessori) approaches, and a [Growth Mindset](/notes/growth-mindset) all measurably improve learning by transforming information into usable [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge).

**Context:** Daniel T. Willingham is a psychology professor at the University of Virginia who applies cognitive science to K–12 education. He is the author of *Why Don't Students Like School?* and is known for translating memory and learning research into classroom practice.

## Where this appears

- [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) — quoted on well-sequenced curriculum and the risk of cognitive overload.
- [Curriculum](/notes/curriculum) — supplies the pedagogical view that learning builds on prior knowledge and is undermined by overload.
