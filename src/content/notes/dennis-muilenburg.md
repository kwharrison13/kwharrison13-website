# Dennis Muilenburg

In [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), Dennis Muilenburg is the [Boeing](/notes/boeing) CEO whose response to the 737 MAX crashes the book holds up as "the culminating failure" of a financialized engineering culture. An engineer who, in college, had wanted to be "the world's greatest airplane designer," he rose through Boeing's defense side — including the troubled Future Combat Systems program he ran for the Army, which Robert Gates eventually killed after roughly $20 billion was spent. The book frames his MAX-era leadership through a damning contrast with Johnson & Johnson's James Burke during the Tylenol crisis: where Burke "put safety first" to save both people and product, Muilenburg "had already convinced himself of the answers — and protecting the product came before people," and repeatedly "couldn't muster an answer that sounded human or reflective" to press and victims' families.

His compensation is part of the book's indictment of capital allocation: Muilenburg made roughly $106 million from 2011–2018 while Boeing poured tens of billions into stock buybacks rather than new aircraft, and he left after his ouster with a ~$60 million golden parachute as David Calhoun was installed in his place — "the face at the top may have changed, but the playbook had not."

**Context:** Dennis Muilenburg was president and CEO of The Boeing Company from 2015 until his ouster in December 2019, following the two 737 MAX crashes (Lion Air 610 and Ethiopian Airlines 302) that killed 346 people and grounded the aircraft worldwide.
