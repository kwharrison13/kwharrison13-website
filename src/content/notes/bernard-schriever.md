# Bernard Schriever

Air Force general and German immigrant whom [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) holds up as its central case study of rewarded risk-taking. In 1954, freshly given his first star, Schriever was assigned by President Eisenhower to develop an intercontinental ballistic missile that could deliver a nuclear weapon across the planet in minutes — a feat not even close to feasible at the time. With Eisenhower's complete backing and funding, he set up shop in an old California church, awarded large fat-margin contracts to integrate companies and technologists into one military-industrial team, scraped a launch center out of Florida wetland (Cape Canaveral), and repeatedly blew up prototypes on the pad. Eisenhower defended him from bureaucrats and rivals like Curtis LeMay (who believed missiles should never displace manned bombers). Schriever's team delivered the Thor, Atlas, Titan, and Minuteman missiles — and the foundation for America's path to space and the moon — in just five years.

Schriever functions in [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) as the paired example to [Kelly Johnson](/notes/kelly-johnson) at [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works): per Kyle's note, the "founder-led innovation era" only survived because of founders like Kelly and Schriever, and champions like Eisenhower and [Bill Perry](/notes/bill-perry) who protected their risk-taking. The book treats the end of that protection — McNamara-era cost-cutting and the "Last Supper" consolidation — as the loss of the model. Strikingly, Schriever himself became its eulogist: in a devastating February 1986 letter to David Packard, he wrote that "during the last several decades we have lost the way," that timely fielding of superior weapons was no longer achieved because procurement had been "politicized by a blizzard of legislation" and strangled by "top-down micro-management" — a system that would have made his own achievements impossible. The book also invokes him as the archetypal "military maverick" who, like Moffett, needed committed civilian champions to succeed. In [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works), he appears as part of the era of Air Force systems-command leadership that shaped the procurement environment around the Skunk Works.

**Context:** General Bernard Schriever (1910–2005) led the U.S. Air Force's Western Development Division and is widely credited as the father of the American ICBM and military space programs.
