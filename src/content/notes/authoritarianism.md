# Authoritarianism

Authoritarianism is one of the corpus's recurring preoccupations, threading through Kyle's reading on media, democracy, and population. In [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) argues that overpopulation and concentrated economic and political power lead "through unrest to dictatorship," and that the future's "more efficient dictatorships" will rely less on violence than on mind-manipulation — subjects "painlessly regimented by a corps of highly trained social engineers." Kyle's longest marginal note pushes back hard on Huxley's causal claim: "the fear is that growth leads to authoritarian states, but the reality is that authoritarian states are born from problems. Growth can certainly cause some of those problems, but authoritarianism is not a response to growth directly... growth is often the path to the solutions that can avoid authoritarianism." The book's structural defense against tyranny is an informed citizenry — the [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) whose ability "to make realistic choices in the light of adequate information" is what democracy's survival depends on.

[The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) by [Arkady Ostrovsky](/notes/arkady-ostrovsky) supplies the media-centric account: in an "idea-centric country" the means of mass information matter more than the means of production, and [television](/notes/television) "both brought down the Soviet system and built the new authoritarian one." Ostrovsky's thesis is that legitimacy under Putin came "from popular support, and while he did not believe in fair elections, he paid careful attention to public opinion" — authoritarianism derived from the TV remote rather than from fair elections. He links it to a deeper elite contempt: in a poor country, the oligarchs believed, "democracy would always lead to populism and ultimately to authoritarianism," so only "the rule of the few" could be trusted. [Propaganda](/notes/propaganda) is the connective tissue between Huxley and Ostrovsky.

[Romney: A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) grounds the concept in the contemporary American case. [Mitt Romney](/notes/mitt-romney)'s 1931 Rand McNally "histomap" obsession after January 6 crystallizes the fragility theme — "Authoritarianism is like a gargoyle lurking over the cathedral, ready to pounce" — and his reading that America's self-rule "is fighting against human nature." Romney names China and Russia as actively "promoting authoritarianism around the world," and frames [Donald Trump](/notes/donald-trump)'s rigged-election narrative as the domestic threshold: "once that happens, you're now in an authoritarian regime."

**Context:** Authoritarianism is a system of governance concentrating power in a leader or small elite, with limited political pluralism and constraints on civil liberties; modern "competitive" or "electoral" authoritarian regimes often retain the forms of elections while hollowing out their fairness — the pattern Ostrovsky describes in post-Soviet Russia.

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) — Huxley's claim that population pressure breeds dictatorship; Kyle's counter-note that authoritarianism follows problems, not growth directly; the educated electorate as defense.
- [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) — media as the mechanism that "built a new authoritarian country"; legitimacy from popular support and the TV remote rather than fair elections.
- [Romney: A Reckoning](/books/romney-a-reckoning) — Romney's histomap obsession; "authoritarianism is like a gargoyle lurking over the cathedral"; China/Russia promoting it abroad.
- [Arkady Ostrovsky](/notes/arkady-ostrovsky) — author of The Invention of Russia, tracing how media led Russia "from freedom to war" into the post-Soviet authoritarian order.
