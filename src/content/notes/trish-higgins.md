# Trish Higgins

Trish Higgins (Harvard Business School 2013) is the permanent-capital practitioner quoted at length in [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) as a living example of the case's thesis. With her husband James and brother-in-law Palmer, she owns and operates [Chenmark Capital Management](/notes/chenmark-capital-management), a Portland, Maine operating-investment vehicle that acquires and builds small and medium enterprises (lawn care, landscaping, frozen dough, tourist businesses) with an **indefinite hold period** — 450 employees and $60M in annual revenue at the time of the Yale case. All three founders were previously Manhattan finance professionals who "grew weary of the buy-trade churn mindset that permeates Wall Street."

In her own words, quoted in the case, Chenmark thinks of itself as **[Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital)** — "we own and operate businesses with a forever mindset" — because avoiding taxes, transaction costs, idle time, and redeployment risk lets you build both a fantastic business and substantial wealth by [compounding](/notes/compounding) over decades. The authors note she shares her thinking in a weekly email blast. She sits in the same cluster of forever-hold exemplars the case venerates — alongside [Will Thorndike](/notes/will-thorndike), [Don MacAskill](/notes/don-macaskill) of [SmugMug](/notes/smugmug), and [Glenn Healey](/notes/glenn-healey).
