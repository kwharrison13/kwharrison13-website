# John Maynard Keynes

Economist who recurs across Kyle's library as a touchstone for two ideas: the leisure prophecy and the primacy of ideas. In [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman) cites Keynes's 1930 prediction of a 15-hour workweek — a forecast that "had become a truism" by the mid-1960s — and his line that practical men are "the slaves of some defunct economist," read by Bregman as a prompt to identify your own ideological masters. Kyle's note on the predicted short week wonders what its believers thought about how non-workers would support themselves, threading Keynes directly into the [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) question. The "15-hour week" reappears in Kyle's The Hardening Of The Great Softening - Research notes alongside John Adams's "I must study war..." line. [Double Entry](/books/double-entry) credits Keynes with the conceptual leap behind national accounting — recasting the whole economy as an aggregate balance of supply and demand, the move that underwrites [GDP](/notes/gdp).

He is not treated uncritically. [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) surfaces Oskar Morgenstern's brutal private verdict in the *Theory of Games* milieu — Keynes as "one of the biggest charlatans who has ever appeared on the economic scene... everybody is on their belly before him." [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care) uses Keynes more sympathetically, closing with his epigraph "When the facts change, I change my mind. What do you do, sir?" to frame author David Goldhill's evolution from conventional Democrat to market-skeptic-of-the-system. He also appears glancingly in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) (a biography of Keynes among the things Charlie Munger's father read so absorbedly) and is invoked there for the "failing conventionally vs. succeeding unconventionally" framing.

**Context:** John Maynard Keynes (1883–1946) was the British economist whose *General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936) founded modern macroeconomics and Keynesian demand-management policy; his 1930 essay "Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren" contains the famous 15-hour-workweek prediction.
