# The Value Way

"The Value Way" is the phrase Kyle attaches, in his notes on [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), to that book's central argument: the move away from valuing pedigree, logos, time spent learning, and test-taking ability, and toward "the language of skills." Its sharpest formulation is the book's thought experiment — "If you could have a Harvard education OR a Harvard degree, but not both, which would you choose?" — which Kyle reads as exposing how little formal credentials track actual capability, a thread he connects to [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise).

In practice the concept attaches to two moves in the book: making everyday learning *count* ("you want learning to count for something towards your goals," via personalized learning records and profiles), and cutting through [Content Overload](/notes/content-overload) so that the scarce hours left for learning are spent on what builds real skill. It is closely linked to the book's [Skills Quotient](/notes/skills-quotient) framing — skills, not pedigree, as "the currency of the future."

## Where this appears

- [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — Kyle's label for the book's thesis that skills (not pedigrees or logos) are the currency of the future, and that learning should "count for something" toward one's goals.
