# Hans Rosling

Author of [Factfulness](/notes/factfulness), and the figure other authors Kyle reads reach for whenever they want to argue that the world is less frightening and more solvable than it feels. In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel) cites Rosling on how people systematically misjudge risk — "Every group of people I ask thinks the world is more frightening, more violent, and more hopeless—in short, more dramatic—than it really is." In [How To Avoid a Climate Disaster](/books/how-to-avoid-a-climate-disaster), [Bill Gates](/notes/bill-gates) closes the book quoting his friend Rosling against fatalism: "When we have a fact-based worldview, we can see that the world is not as bad as it seems—and we can see what we have to do to keep making it better." Across both, Rosling functions as the patron saint of the fact-based, cautiously-optimistic worldview — Housel deploys him for investing psychology, Gates for climate solvability.

**Context:** Hans Rosling (1948–2017) was a Swedish physician, global-health professor, and statistics communicator (co-founder of the Gapminder Foundation), known for data-driven talks on global development and for *Factfulness* (2018), written with Ola Rosling and Anna Rosling Rönnlund.
