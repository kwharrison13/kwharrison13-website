# Michelangelo

In Walter Isaacson's [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci), Michelangelo functions as the recurring foil that sharpens Leonardo's character. Where Leonardo is presented as the collegial, collaborative thinker who "enjoyed being surrounded by friends, companions, students, assistants, fellow courtiers, and thinkers" — for whom conceiving ideas was a social endeavor — Michelangelo is the archetype of the "anguished" solo artist. The book also contrasts them as painters: "unlike Michelangelo, Leonardo was a master at painting women," with Leonardo's sympathetic, psychologically insightful portraits (from Ginevra de' Benci to the Mona Lisa) breaking from the passive-mannequin convention. The wiki's references are framed entirely through this comparison rather than treating Michelangelo's own work in its own right.

**Context:** Michelangelo Buonarroti (1475–1564) was a Florentine sculptor, painter, and architect of the High Renaissance, creator of the David and the Sistine Chapel ceiling, and a near-contemporary rival of Leonardo.
