# Progressive Summarization

Progressive summarization is the discipline of distilling a text down to its essence — capturing the unity of a whole work in a single sentence (or short paragraph), then layering structure beneath it. In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), Kyle attaches the term directly to Adler and Van Doren's Rule 2: "State the unity of the whole book in a single sentence, or at most a few sentences." His marginal note on that rule reads simply "*Progressive summarization.*" Adler's accompanying caution — that if it requires too many words "you have not seen the unity but a multiplicity" — is the heart of the practice: forced compression is what proves you've actually grasped a book rather than just felt its shape.

The concept recurs in Kyle's reading of [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment), where Munger describes building his own psychology system from textbooks "with new descriptions and titles selected to fit Munger's notion of what makes recall easy for Munger, then revised to make Munger's use easy" — Kyle tags that passage with progressive summarization (and #Honesty), reading Munger's relabeling-for-retention as the same move: rewriting source material into a personally compressed, usable form. In Kyle's notebook it pairs with [Active Reading](/notes/active-reading) and [Triangulation](/notes/triangulation) as part of a connected method for turning reading into retained, networked knowledge.

**Context:** "Progressive Summarization" is a note-taking method popularized by Tiago Forte (Building a Second Brain) — iteratively highlighting and condensing saved material in layers so the most important points surface for future reuse. Kyle uses the term more loosely here, applied to the act of distilling a work to its core.
