# The Medium is the Message

Marshall McLuhan's dictum that the form of a communication medium, not just its content, shapes thought. In [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death), [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) frames the idea as: "Each medium, like language itself, makes possible a unique mode of discourse by providing a new orientation for thought, for expression, for sensibility. Which, of course, is what McLuhan meant in saying the medium is the message." It is the load-bearing premise Postman extends across the whole book — the claim that a culture's dominant medium (print versus television) silently determines what kinds of public conversation are even possible.

**Context:** The phrase originates with Marshall McLuhan's 1964 *Understanding Media*. McLuhan argued that the medium itself ("the extension of ourselves") embeds a message independent of the content it carries, reshaping perception and social organization. Postman builds on it to argue that television, as a medium, biases public discourse toward entertainment.

## Where this appears

- [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) — Postman invokes McLuhan's dictum to argue each medium provides its own "orientation for thought," the premise underlying the book's print-versus-television argument.
