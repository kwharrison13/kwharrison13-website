# Technological Unemployment

Across the wiki, technological unemployment names the displacement of human labor by automation and AI — but the sharpest formulation, from [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century), reframes the danger as **irrelevance rather than exploitation**. Harari argues "the merger of infotech and biotech might soon push billions of humans out of the job market," concentrating wealth and power "in the hands of a tiny elite while most people suffer not from exploitation but from something far worse—irrelevance." His political prediction follows: twenty-first-century populist revolts may be staged "not against an economic elite that exploits people but against an economic elite that does not need them anymore," a battle harder to win because "it is much harder to struggle against irrelevance than against exploitation." On the page this drives the debate over [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) (Harari's "capitalist paradise") versus universal basic services as responses.

[Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) supplies the empirical scale and the redistribution question: it cites Oxford estimates that "47% of all American jobs and 54% of all those in Europe are at high risk of being usurped by machines" within twenty years, and treats [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) as the open question the mid-century optimists never answered — how non-workers get supported once labor is no longer the channel that distributes prosperity ([Automation](/notes/automation) "may force us there"). It tangles technological unemployment together with the [Bullshit Jobs](/notes/bullshit-jobs) thesis: even today many jobs are "superfluous," so the loss of work is not always the loss of value. The concept is distinct from the broader [Future of Work](/notes/future-of-work) page, which covers the wider reorganization of labor rather than displacement specifically.

**Context:** "Technological unemployment" is a term Keynes popularized in 1930 for joblessness caused by labor-saving technology outrunning the economy's ability to create new roles. It is a recurring anxiety in automation debates, frequently paired with proposals like UBI, shorter workweeks, and retraining.
