# B. H. Roberts

B. H. Roberts is a central figure in [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens), cast as "the last great practitioner of Mormon theology." A brilliant autodidact, he produced the influential five-volume *Seventies Course in Theology* (used 1907–11) and *New Witness for God* (1903–6), and by 1930 was finishing his masterwork, *The Truth, the Way, the Life* — the first attempt at a monumental, systematic synthesis of Latter-day Saint theology. The book was killed by a reading committee of the leadership, largely at the instigation of [Joseph Fielding Smith](/notes/joseph-fielding-smith), who objected to Roberts's perceived compromises with scientific theory; it was published posthumously only in 1994. Roberts also developed the closest thing to a uniquely Latter-day Saint theology of atonement (a less retributive take on God's justice), though it was little read and had no lasting impact. The notes tie him directly to the book's subject, Eugene England, who took Roberts's 1906 charge — that "Mormonism" affords opportunity for thoughtful disciples to *develop* and *enlarge* its truths rather than merely repeat them — personally to heart as his "inspiration and guide."

He also appears as both an author and a figure inside the history. He wrote [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor), the biography of the third LDS president that Kyle read "with attention to the apologetic-vs-truth-seeking tension that runs through the Mormon historiographical tradition." A passing note from that book — soldiers who seduced young women in Utah and were followed to California — surfaces in the Alma 23–29 study as an example of how a marginal detail to the author was "the whole story" for those it touched. He appears as a human cost inside [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand): listening to the Manifesto read from the pulpit "was one of the most difficult moments of his life" — though he had no desire to oppose it openly, "his earlier assurance that it was right had not returned, and he could not raise his hand to support the statement," later resolving "sure I am that it is all right… God has a purpose in it." His writing supplies recurring quotes elsewhere — on the dangers of prosperity and ease to the church ("especially is peace dangerous to the church," from *A New Witness for God*, in Diligence) and on plainly stating truth so the Holy Spirit can testify of it (in [Chad's Big Quote Book](/notes/chad-s-big-quote-book)).

**Context:** Brigham Henry Roberts (1857–1933) was a historian, theologian, and General Authority (a member of the First Council of the Seventy) of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, best known for his multi-volume *Comprehensive History of the Church* and his apologetic and historical writing.
