# Mortimer Adler

Mortimer J. Adler is the co-author (with [Charles Van Doren](/notes/charles-van-doren)) of [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), the classic Kyle read with a constant religious-text overlay and explicit cross-reference to his [Learning, No Greater Responsibility](/notes/learning-no-greater-responsibility) essay-in-progress. Adler's central thesis — reading as [Active Reading](/notes/active-reading), the reader as a "catcher in baseball" who demands more of both self and text — anchors most of the threads Kyle pulled from the book.

Across the notes, Adler supplies a chain of ideas Kyle maps onto his own framework: [Triangulation](/notes/triangulation) (reading several books on one subject in relation to one another), [Progressive Summarization](/notes/progressive-summarization) (stating a book's unity in a single sentence), and [Live the Library](/notes/live-the-library) ("reading what others have read"). Adler also surfaces in Kyle's [Death of Expertise](/notes/death-of-expertise) thread (the relatively ignorant wrongly disagreeing with the learned; opinion vs. knowledge) and the [Curse of Expertise](/notes/curse-of-expertise) note (modern science "written by experts for experts"). Kyle registers one respectful disagreement: where Adler is skeptical of fiction's reach, Kyle argues fiction can stretch the mind as much as non-fiction. Adler's examples — [Charles Darwin](/notes/charles-darwin) on "doctrines felt as facts," [Plato](/notes/plato), [Aristotle](/notes/aristotle), [Tolstoy](/notes/leo-tolstoy), [George Orwell](/notes/george-orwell), [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) — recur throughout.

**Context:** Mortimer J. Adler (1902–2001) was an American philosopher and educator, a champion of the Great Books of the Western World program and author of the 1940 classic *How to Read a Book*, revised in 1972 with Charles Van Doren.
