# Marie Antoinette

In [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life), Marie Antoinette appears as a foil that crystallizes the book's middle-class merit theme. At Versailles, the "famously haughty" queen let Franklin stand beside her at the gaming tables but dismissed him as a man who had once been "a printer's foreman" — noting that someone of that background could never have risen so high in Europe. The book's punchline, which Kyle flagged: "Franklin would have proudly agreed." The exchange compresses the whole [American-Dream](/notes/american-dream) contrast between aristocratic birth and self-made achievement into a single scene.

**Context:** Marie Antoinette (1755–1793) was the last queen of France before the French Revolution, married to Louis XVI and executed by guillotine in 1793. Franklin served as American minister to France during the Revolutionary War, when France was a crucial ally of the fledgling United States.
