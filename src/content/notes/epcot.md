# EPCOT

In Kyle's notes, EPCOT is shorthand for [Walt Disney](/notes/walt-disney)'s original vision of a planned "city of tomorrow," and it threads two of his major interests: historical futurism and city-building. In Historical Futurism — Research, EPCOT is the case study where Stephen Fjellman (in *Vinyl Leaves*) benchmarked the theme park against the utopian themes [Howard P. Segal](/notes/howard-p-segal) identified in Technological Utopianism in American Culture — and found that "Walt's vision for EPCOT shared the same themes." The research traces an "EPCOT lineage": Walt the dreamer, inspired by the World's Fair, captured by artist [Walt Peregoy](/notes/walt-peregoy) in "the drawing that inspired a generation," feeding into [Disney Imagineering](/notes/disney-imagineering).

The [Roy O. Disney](/notes/roy-o-disney) page uses EPCOT to mark the limit of vision: Walt's older brother Roy was the pragmatic operator who kept the company solvent, but his lack of vision cut Walt's most ambitious project short — "Marvin, Walt's dead... and so was EPCOT." [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) pulls EPCOT into the city-building thread directly, citing the "hub-and-spoke idea" from [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) as a concrete answer to urban inequality: micro-cities deliberately designed, where "not everyone who works in a superstar city has to live in one." Kyle ties this to his own [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) / [Prosperable](/notes/prosperable) project.

**Context:** EPCOT (Experimental Prototype Community of Tomorrow) was Walt Disney's plan, announced shortly before his death in 1966, for a real, working planned city in Florida. After Walt died the concept was abandoned as a residential city and reborn in 1982 as Epcot, a theme park at Walt Disney World.
