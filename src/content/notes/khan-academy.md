# Khan Academy

Appears in Kyle's reading as a recurring example of free, on-demand, self-directed learning displacing the traditional school as the locus of knowledge. In [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), Jaime Casap (Google's education evangelist) is quoted noting that people don't return to their university for professional development — "they turn to their company or to other solutions like [Degreed](/notes/degreed) or Khan Academy." In [Naval Ravikant — The Angel Philosopher](/notes/naval-ravikant-the-angel-philosopher), Naval cites Khan Academy alongside MIT and Yale lectures and "the great books" as evidence that the internet is "the greatest Web of Knowledge ever created," where "the tools of learning are abundant and infinite — it's the desire to learn that's incredibly scarce." His fantasy education project (a cheap, rugged Android tablet that adapts to each child) is framed as networking all the world's teachers and students "beyond just Salman Khan / Khan Academy."

**Context:** Khan Academy is a nonprofit founded by Salman Khan that offers free online lessons and practice exercises across math, science, and the humanities to a global audience.

## Where this appears

- [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — cited (via Jaime Casap) as a solution people turn to for professional development instead of their alma mater, grouped with [Degreed](/notes/degreed).
- [Naval Ravikant — The Angel Philosopher](/notes/naval-ravikant-the-angel-philosopher) — held up as evidence that learning tools are now abundant and free; Naval's tablet vision aims to extend "beyond just Salman Khan / Khan Academy."
