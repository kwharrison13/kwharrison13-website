# Adlerian Psychology

The school of individual psychology founded by [Alfred Adler](/notes/alfred-adler) that [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) expounds in full, structured as a Plato-style dialogue. The book frames Adler's work less as strict scholarship than as "a realization, a culmination of truths and of human understanding." Several distinctive claims anchor the text: **teleology** — behaviors serve present goals rather than being caused by the past (a friend "manufactur[es] a state of anxiety and fear as a means" of not going out); the **denial of trauma** — "we are not determined by our experiences, but the meaning we give them is self-determining," a deliberate, revolutionary break from the Freudian causal view; and an anti-nihilist insistence that people are controlled neither by emotion nor by the past.

The book situates Adler among contemporaries: it cites [Dale Carnegie](/notes/dale-carnegie) praising Adler as "a great psychologist," and notes that much of [Stephen Covey](/notes/stephen-covey)'s [The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People](/books/the-7-habits-of-highly-effective-people) "closely resembles Adler's ideas." In Kyle's reading, the three margin notes map Adlerian concepts onto outside vocabularies — [Inferiority and Superiority](/notes/inferiority-and-superiority) as a stimulant to growth, [Reward and Punishment](/notes/reward-and-punishment) critique glossed as "righteous in the dark," and the "feeling of contribution" as civic virtue ("putting away your shopping cart").

**Context:** Adlerian (individual) psychology, developed by Alfred Adler after his break with Freud, emphasizes social interest, goal-directed (teleological) behavior, and feelings of inferiority as drivers of growth rather than pathology.

- [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) — the named school the entire dialogue expounds, via Adler's concepts of teleology, the denial of trauma, and contribution-as-happiness.
