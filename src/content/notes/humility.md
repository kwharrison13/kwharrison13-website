# Humility

Humility runs through Kyle's notes along two reinforcing tracks: a **gospel** track and an **intellectual-honesty** track. The fullest gospel treatment is Humility — Topical Study, which distinguishes humility from meekness — meekness being the first step, "seeing yourself as God sees you, recognizing your weakness" — and centers President [Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)'s formulation that "humility is not thinking less of yourself, but less ABOUT yourself" (with [John Bytheway](/notes/john-bytheway)'s quip, "if you think being meek is weak, try being meek for a week"). The study's paradox is that the prophets — Nephi, Ammon, Alma — were spiritual giants *because* they recognized their inadequacy, and that humility is "not a sign of weakness; it is a sign of spiritual strength." [Caleb Christensen](/notes/caleb-christensen) is quoted: "as soon as I deem myself qualified and sufficiently strong to do the work (alone), I disqualify myself."

The Come Follow Me page on [Alma 32](/notes/alma-32)–35 (July 13–19, 2020. Alma 32–35: "Plant This Word in Your Hearts") develops the same idea through the poor and humble Zoramites: affliction "prepares the heart," a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) makes someone open to something new where [Pride](/notes/pride) makes it nearly impossible to accept new ideas, and there is a key difference between being "compelled to be humble" and humbling oneself "because of the word."

The secular track connects through [Intellectual Honesty](/notes/intellectual-honesty): storytelling becomes a slippery slope "if it's not coupled with significant amounts of intellectual honesty and a spirit of humility," with [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s "you're the easiest one to fool" as the operative warning. This is the same humility-as-epistemic-discipline that shows up in the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) threads across [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) and Munger's worldly-wisdom material.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint teaching, humility and meekness are closely linked virtues tied to teachability, repentance, and reliance on God; the Uchtdorf line "less about yourself" is a widely-cited modern framing.
