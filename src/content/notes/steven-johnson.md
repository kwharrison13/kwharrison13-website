# Steven Johnson

Steven Johnson recurs across the corpus as a theorist of how new ideas actually emerge — and the same argument shows up in two different books. In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Sönke Ahrens cites Johnson's notion of the "slow hunch," emphasizing "the importance of experimental spaces where ideas can freely mingle"; Ahrens offers the slip-box ([Zettelkasten](/notes/zettelkasten)) as exactly such a space, "much as intellectuals and artists freely discussed ideas in the cafés of old Paris." This sits inside the wiki's [Idea Sex](/notes/idea-sex) thread — connection as where insight lives.

The same claim anchors [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), where Johnson is cited on how "creative environments fostering idea-exchange [matter] more than competition for major advances" — the principle that explains why [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs) connected all its buildings to force chemists, physicists, and engineers into contact. Across both, Johnson stands for the idea that breakthroughs come from mixing, proximity, and the slow accumulation of half-formed hunches rather than from lone genius.

**Context:** Steven Johnson is an American popular-science and ideas writer, author of *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010), which develops the "slow hunch" and "adjacent possible" concepts both books draw on.

## Where this appears

- [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) — cited for the "slow hunch" and the importance of experimental spaces where ideas mingle; Ahrens casts the slip-box as one.
- [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) — cited on creative, idea-exchanging environments mattering more than competition, illustrating Bell Labs' physical-proximity design.
