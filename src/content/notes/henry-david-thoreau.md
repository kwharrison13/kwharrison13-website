# Henry David Thoreau

Transcendentalist author of [Walden](/books/walden), the first-person account of his experiment in "living deliberately" at Walden Pond, and a recurring touchstone across Kyle's reading. His self-reliance ethic — paired with [Ralph Waldo Emerson](/notes/ralph-waldo-emerson) — and his claim to have met all his expenses by working "about six weeks in a year" make him the anchor for the wiki's [Frugality](/notes/frugality) and [Solitude](/notes/solitude) threads. His most-cited idea is the cost-in-life accounting at the heart of [Cal Newport](/notes/cal-newport)'s [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism): "The cost of a thing is the amount of what I will call life which is required to be exchanged for it" — what Frédéric Gros calls Thoreau's "new economics." The same line ("the price of anything is the amount of life you exchange for it") seeds Kyle's extraordinary man research.

Thoreau is also a foil. In [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death), [Neil Postman](/notes/neil-postman) cites Walden's "Maine and Texas... have nothing important to communicate" as an early critique of telegraphy. And in [A Walk in the Woods](/books/a-walk-in-the-woods), [Bill Bryson](/notes/bill-bryson) deflates Thoreau's pastoral idyll — "splendid" nature so long as he could stroll to town for cakes and barley wine — noting his 1846 Katahdin visit left him "near hysterical." Digital Minimalism reconciles the two: Thoreau's intention "was not to inhabit a wilderness, but to find wildness in a suburban setting."

**Context:** Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862) was an American essayist, poet, and naturalist, best known for *Walden* (1854) and the essay "Civil Disobedience," and a central figure in New England Transcendentalism.
