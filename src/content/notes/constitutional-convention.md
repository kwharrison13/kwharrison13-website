# Constitutional Convention

In Kyle's corpus the 1787 Philadelphia Constitutional Convention is the central setting for the [Compromise](/notes/compromise) thesis that runs through [Benjamin Franklin — An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life). [Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson) treats [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)'s willingness to doubt his own judgment as the load-bearing virtue of the whole enterprise: "the older I grow, the more apt I am to doubt my own judgment and pay more respect to the judgment of others." Franklin's convention plea warned that "positiveness and warmth on one side naturally beget their like on the other," and urged every member to compromise. The book's verdict — "Compromisers may not make great heroes, but they do make great democracies" — is anchored here, with Franklin embodying an Enlightenment belief (per Henry May) that "everything can be settled by [Compromise](/notes/compromise)," a supposed fault Isaacson argues "has served the Constitution, and the nation it formed, quite well."

The Convention also surfaces as a touchstone for civic literacy. [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) retells the apocryphal exchange in which Franklin, asked what would emerge from the Convention, answered "A republic, if you can keep it" — using it to argue that modern Americans confuse "democracy" with "republic" and that the harder challenge today is finding anyone who knows what a republic actually is. In [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), the papers of the 1787 Convention appear as the kind of solitary scholarly work Adams treasured when he could escape the press of public business.

**Context:** The Constitutional Convention met in Philadelphia from May to September 1787, where delegates from twelve states drafted the U.S. Constitution, resolving disputes (notably over representation) through the Great Compromise that paired a population-based House with an equal-vote Senate.
