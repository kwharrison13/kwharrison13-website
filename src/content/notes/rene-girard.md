# Rene Girard

A French historian and literary critic, described in [Peter Thiel's Religion](/notes/peter-thiel-s-religion) as the person who influenced [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel) more than any other writer. Girard is famous for [Mimetic Theory](/notes/mimetic-theory), which the essay calls the bedrock of Thiel's worldview. The theory rests on the assumption that all cultural behavior — beginning with a child's acquisition of language — is imitative: Girard saw the world as "a theatre of envy," where, like mimes, we imitate other people's desires. Its corollary is a darker claim that human culture began with a murder, fueled by rage and rivalry, and that managing that mimetic violence is the secret reason for all religious and political institutions.

Across the wiki, Girard is the deep root behind [Memetics](/notes/memetics): if desire is imitative, then memes (Greek *mimeme*, "that which is imitated") are simply how those imitated desires propagate at the speed of the internet — a thread Kyle's The Meme Economy - Research picks up directly. Girard's evidence base is notable for drawing on "the kinds of books and people that modern people tend to ignore": the Bible, classic novelists like [Marcel Proust](/notes/marcel-proust), and playwrights like [William Shakespeare](/notes/william-shakespeare), whose plays he reads as theatres of envy and imitated desire.

**Context:** René Girard (1923–2015) was a French-American historian, literary critic, and philosopher of social science, longtime professor at Stanford, and member of the Académie française. His major works include *Deceit, Desire, and the Novel*, *Violence and the Sacred*, and *Things Hidden Since the Foundation of the World*, developing mimetic desire, the scapegoat mechanism, and a theory of the sacred.
