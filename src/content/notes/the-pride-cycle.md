# The Pride Cycle

**The Pride Cycle** is the recurring Book of Mormon pattern Kyle traces across his 2020 Come Follow Me studies: prosperity breeds [Pride](/notes/pride), pride breeds wickedness and destruction, destruction breeds humility, humility restores prosperity — "again and again" (Helaman 6:34–36). In his Helaman 1–6 notes Kyle calls sin "a cyclical process that people have a really hard time shaking off," and reads Mormon as "desperately trying to get this point across: your pride and wickedness WILL lead to destruction. Can't you see how it happens again and again?"

What strikes Kyle most is the *speed* and the *seduction*. In 3 Nephi 27–4 Nephi the cycle is "rampant… in a way that is striking given what a tight time cycle it's presented in" — nearly 200 years of [Zion](/notes/zion) peace after Christ's visit collapse back into pride. He connects this to the devil's patience: he doesn't rush, he "eases" you so you couldn't imagine being where you'd feel pride, until you are "led carefully down to hell" (2 Nephi 28:21–22). The most haunting detail (4 Nephi 1:43): a people who "see the other side so clearly because they hate you so fervently" still fall to the same pride that leads to hate and then to hell.

The cycle has a true bottom and a true escape. In Mormon 1–6, Kyle marks the real failure point as the moment "even destruction can no longer bring you to humility" — the "sorrowing of the damned" who mourn only that they can no longer "take happiness in sin" (Mormon 2:13–14), distinct from godly sorrow. The way out, from the Ether 12–15 study: humble yourself *especially* in your strength — "the stronger you are the more confident you feel that you are strong enough… to handle things on your own" — because God gives weakness so we will humble ourselves and "make weak things become strong" (Ether 12:27). See [Humble Yourselves](/notes/humble-yourselves).

**Context:** "The pride cycle" is a widely taught Latter-day Saint framework for reading the Book of Mormon — the repeating sequence of righteousness → prosperity → pride → destruction → humility → repentance — often summarized as "prosperity breeds pride."
