# Observation

Observation is, in Kyle's reading of [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci), Leonardo's "greatest skill" — and crucially "not some magical gift but a product of his own effort." This is the load-bearing move of Walter Isaacson's biography, which "refuse[s] the lightning-strike theory of genius": Leonardo's genius "was a human one, wrought by his own will and ambition… built from curiosity and intense observation, skills any of us can aspire to improve." Observation here is a *learnable* discipline, paired with [Curiosity](/notes/curious) and a "childlike sense of wonder," not an innate endowment.

The notes specify its method. Deep observation "must be done in steps" — "begin with the details… and do not go on to the second step until you have the first well fixed in memory." It feeds an experimental, anti-dogmatic stance: Leonardo "consult[s] experience first and then with reasoning show[s] why such experience is bound to operate in such a way," and he was "a forerunner of observational science," abandoning a theory the moment an experiment showed it flawed — the [Self-Criticism](/notes/self-criticism) thread Isaacson rhymes with [Charles Darwin](/notes/charles-darwin) "thinking in reverse." Mathematics, in turn, "was the key to turning observations into theories," "the language that nature used to write her laws." (A narrower, imperative-form sibling concept, [Be Observant](/notes/be-observant), already exists; this page holds the noun-form idea.)

**Context:** Observation — disciplined, attentive perception of the natural world — is foundational to the empirical/scientific method, distinguishing knowledge grounded in evidence from knowledge derived purely from received authority or abstract reasoning.
