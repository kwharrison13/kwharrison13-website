# Sterling McMurrin

In [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens), Sterling McMurrin is one of the prominent Latter-day Saint intellectuals — alongside Lowell Bennion, T. Edgar Lyon, George T. Boyd, and William Mulder — who formed the circle that called itself "the Swearing Elders." As conservatism and retrenchment displaced the intellectual heyday of Mormonism's 1930s and 1940s, this group met to discuss Mormon topics with a critical, sometimes jaundiced eye, and floated the idea of a scholarly journal unhindered by church oversight. McMurrin is quoted lamenting that "Mormon liberalism … showed some life in the thirties, [but] never quite made the grade," its liberals displaced in influence by "noisy and deceptive irrationalists who give the appearance of orthodoxy while denying its spirit."

The book pairs him with [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england) as an intellectual ally and foil: McMurrin held more sanguinely than England that "Mormonism has far more intellectual strength than is commonly supposed," and is quoted in his sharpest formulation that "Mormonism is fundamentalism turned against itself" — the claim that self-styled defenders of orthodoxy were in fact breaking faith with Joseph Smith's universalist, eclectic, and antidogmatic tendencies.

**Context:** Sterling M. McMurrin (1914–1996) was a philosopher of religion, U.S. Commissioner of Education under President Kennedy, and a longtime professor at the University of Utah, known as a leading liberal voice within Mormon intellectual life.
