# Time Symmetry

In Kyle's notes on [Story of Your Life](/books/story-of-your-life) (Ted Chiang), time symmetry is the physics premise that does the philosophical heavy lifting. The fundamental laws of physics are time-symmetric; the arrow of time is a statistical feature of [entropy](/notes/entropy) and initial conditions, not of the laws themselves. That points toward [eternalism](/notes/eternalism) / the [block universe](/notes/block-universe) — past, present, and future all exist equally, and our sense of an "open" future is a feature of our limited access, not of reality. The note quotes Einstein: "the distinction between past, present and future is only a stubbornly persistent illusion."

It connects to the story's other physics thread: the [variational](/notes/variational-principles) (vs. causal) formulation of physical law. Reading the universe variationally — as in [the Principle of Least Time](/notes/fermat-s-principle), which requires knowing the endpoint before the path begins — lets Louise know the whole shape of her life in advance and live it anyway, the way a completed sentence or a ray of light is grasped all at once rather than raced toward.

**Context:** Time symmetry (T-symmetry) is the property that the fundamental equations of physics remain valid when the direction of time is reversed; the everyday one-way "arrow of time" is generally attributed to the second law of thermodynamics and the low-entropy initial conditions of the universe rather than to the microscopic laws.
