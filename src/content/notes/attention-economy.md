# Attention Economy

In Kyle's corpus the attention economy threads through both his reading and his own writing. Two books anchor it: [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century), where [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari)'s "if the news is free, you are the product" frames attention as the thing being sold, and [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism), which carries [Tristan Harris](/notes/tristan-harris)'s "time well spent" critique of products engineered to capture attention. It also surfaces in [The Meme Economy](/essays/the-meme-economy) research, where it sits among the concept lenses alongside [Memetics](/notes/memetics) and [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling) — the argument being that in a world crammed with competing narratives, attention is the scarce resource that stories compete for, and the internet didn't create that competition so much as accelerate it.

The common thread across these sources is that when distribution is free and infinite, the binding constraint shifts to attention — and the products, platforms, and stories that win are the ones optimized to capture and hold it, often at the cost of the user.

**Context:** "Attention economy" is the idea, popularized by Herbert Simon and later Tim Wu (*The Attention Merchants*), that human attention is a scarce commodity; ad-supported media and platforms monetize it, which shapes how content and products are designed.

## Where this appears

- [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) — Harari's "if the news is free, you are the product"; attention as the monetized resource.
- [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism) — Tristan Harris's "time well spent" critique of attention-capturing product design.
- [The Meme Economy](/essays/the-meme-economy) — attention as the scarce resource stories compete for in a narrative-saturated world.
