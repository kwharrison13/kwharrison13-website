# Karl Popper

Across Kyle's reading, Popper is the patron of **falsification** — the idea that you learn by hunting for what proves you wrong, not for what confirms you. In [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan), [Nassim Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb) builds his epistemology on Popper's mechanism of conjectures and refutations: "you formulate a (bold) conjecture and you start looking for the observation that would prove you wrong" — the alternative to the human default of seeking confirmatory instances (the page links this to [Inversion](/notes/inversion) and [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias)). Taleb names Popper as one of the few thinkers he is not attacking, quoting his line that "genuine philosophical problems are always rooted outside philosophy and they die if these roots decay," and noting that Popper, like [Karl Marx](/notes/karl-marx), wrote a titular pun in reply to a rival — *The Poverty of Historicism*.

[The Great Mental Models Volume 1 — General Thinking Concepts](/books/the-great-mental-models-volume-1-general-thinking-concepts) gives the cleaner formulation, quoting Popper directly: "A theory is part of empirical science if and only if it conflicts with possible experiences and is therefore in principle falsifiable by experience" — i.e., "if you can't prove something wrong, you can't really prove it right either." The same principle resurfaces in [To Save America, Restore Our Frontier](/notes/to-save-america-restore-our-frontier), where the Frontier is defined as a **falsifiable environment** — "an environment of true accountability" — under the banner of Popper's "any real science has to be falsifiable." The thread is consistent: ideas, systems, and people that can be proven wrong are the resilient ones.

**Context:** Karl Popper (1902–1994) was an Austrian-British philosopher of science best known for the criterion of falsifiability as the demarcation of science from non-science, and for works including *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* and *The Open Society and Its Enemies*.

## Where this appears
