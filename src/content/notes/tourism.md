# Tourism

Tourism shows up in Kyle's notes as a double-edged engine for small-town economies, distinct from [Gentrification](/notes/gentrification). In [What I Found in a Thousand Towns](/books/what-i-found-in-a-thousand-towns), Dar Williams argues that *"tourism alone, as an industry, will disproportionately fill up a town with zero-mobility jobs, and the service economy employment is somewhat cruelly coupled with a real-estate problem in which food servers can't afford a place to live while part-time residents think nothing of spending six figures or more for their second and third homes."* The book's Moab example extends the point: townspeople know they need an institution "not related to tourism" — a university — to give people employment that isn't a dead-end service job. This ties tourism directly to the wiki's [Real Estate](/notes/real-estate) / affordability thread and to [City Building](/notes/city-building), where the question is how a place "retains its character and avoids economic displacement" as "bigger and bigger waves of tourists" arrive.

Tourism is also part of how Kyle frames his own interests. In [Family Towns](/essays/family-towns) he names "city building" as his portfolio idea and lists "tourism as an industry" among the rabbit holes it has led him down. [In Defense of Disney](/essays/in-defense-of-disney) is the worked example of tourism at scale — Disneyland alone drawing ~30M visitors a year — read through the lens of income inequality and the different economics behind the experiences people can each afford.

**Context:** Tourism is the industry built around travel for leisure, recreation, or culture. In economic-development discourse it is frequently flagged as a sector that creates large numbers of low-wage, low-mobility service jobs and can drive up local housing costs through second-home and short-term-rental demand — exactly the tension Williams describes.
