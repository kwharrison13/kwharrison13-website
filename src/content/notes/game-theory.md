# Game Theory

Game theory is a throughline across Kyle's strategy and defense reading, with [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann) as its origin point. [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) treats von Neumann's minimax theorem and *Theory of Games* as the book's load-bearing concept, traces its descent into Cold War nuclear strategy (counterforce, the Polaris arc, RAND), and registers its darker side — von Neumann's late "I feel the opposite of nostalgia" as "an honest reckoning with what the math actually said about human cooperation," a dim, Hobbesian read that is "the political-philosophy underpinning of why deterrence works." [Nuclear War — a Scenario](/books/nuclear-war-a-scenario) carries that forward into the present tense: deterrence, "use them or lose them," and [Thomas Schelling](/notes/thomas-schelling)'s bargaining-as-diplomacy are "the strategic-interaction frame underpinning the whole scenario."

The concept also surfaces in older and softer registers. [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war) is read game-theoretically — "All warfare is based on deception," and Wellington framing his plans entirely on the opponent's expected moves. [Stories of Your Life and Others](/books/story-of-your-life) (Ted Chiang) turns it gentle: human-heptapod contact as a "non-zero-sum game," with the daughter asking for "the math word" for when both sides can win. And in [Naval Ravikant — The Angel Philosopher](/notes/naval-ravikant-the-angel-philosopher), Naval names microeconomics and game theory "fundamental" — you can't succeed in business or modern capitalist society without a grasp of supply and demand, labor vs. capital, and game theory — even as he dismisses macroeconomics as junk science.

**Context:** Game theory is the mathematical study of strategic decision-making among rational actors, formalized by John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern's 1944 *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior*; it became central to Cold War nuclear-deterrence strategy and to modern economics.
