# Polygamy

Polygamy (plural marriage) is one of the most theologically fraught threads running through Kyle's Mormon-history reading, surfacing chiefly as a **test case for revelation, obedience, and how the church changes**. Its dramatic spine is in [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand): [Wilford Woodruff](/notes/wilford-woodruff)'s 1890 Manifesto, where he weighs the loss of the temples against holding the line on plural marriage and chooses "to act for the temporal salvation of the Church." Kyle's recurring marginal probe — *can we ever say a commandment will never be revoked?* — sits directly on [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation) and [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept). [The Next Mormons](/books/the-next-mormons) generalizes the pattern: polygamy (enshrined in LDS scripture, "not just policy or precedent") was one of the doctrines the church "jettisoned" along the assimilation-vs-retrenchment pendulum, eliminated in 1890.

The sources also carry the **critical and human-cost dimensions**. [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) presents Eugene England's reading of plural marriage as "an Abrahamic test of temporary duration," "a clearly sexist practice," and lists polygamy among the episodes (with Mountain Meadows and the priesthood ban) "where deference to authority trumped personal conscience at appalling cost." [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) frames it as a "faith-wrenching practice," invoking Austin Farrer's "authority cannot make facts to be facts." [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) shows Young's contradictory patriarchal household and the visibility some plural wives (Eliza R. Snow) nonetheless achieved. Kyle's own Early Mormonism topical study flags it for "Further Study": "Polygamy socially hurt the church. Why did it happen? What was its spiritual purpose?"

**Context:** Polygamy (plural marriage) was openly practiced in The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints from the 1840s until the 1890 Manifesto by President Wilford Woodruff officially ended it — a defining and controversial chapter of 19th-century Mormon history.
