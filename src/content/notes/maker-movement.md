# Maker Movement

In [Our Towns](/books/our-towns), James and Deborah Fallows treat the maker movement as one of the civic "tells" of a town on the rise — makerspaces and low-volume, close-to-the-customer manufacturing reshoring small-batch production into American communities. A makerspace, in the book's description, is a communal twenty-first-century workshop where hobbyists, artists, entrepreneurs, and designers share hardware and software (3-D printers, laser cutters, wire benders); some are franchised like gyms with memberships, others sit inside libraries free and open to all. The Fallowses frame it through the question "what would it take to foster a new wave of manufacturing?", with the answer being fast-turnaround, customer-adjacent making within the United States.

Kyle's notes connect the movement outward: makerspaces "franchised like gyms" link to [Franchises](/notes/franchises) and [Entity Ideas](/notes/entity-ideas), while the maker movement itself ties to [Solopreneurs](/notes/solopreneurs) and [Entity Ideas](/notes/entity-ideas) as a model for small-scale, owner-operated manufacturing. It sits alongside [City Building](/notes/city-building), [Education](/notes/education) (STEAM, career-technical training), and [Retraining](/notes/retraining) in the book's argument about next-generation family-wage paths.

**Context:** The "maker movement" is a broad cultural and economic trend (gaining momentum in the 2000s–2010s) centered on hands-on creation, DIY hardware, and shared community workshops (makerspaces/hackerspaces), enabled by accessible tools like 3-D printers, laser cutters, and microcontrollers.
