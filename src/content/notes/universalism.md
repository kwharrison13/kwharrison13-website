# Universalism

Universalism appears in two distinct registers across Kyle's notes. In the Alma 13–16 study it is treated **critically**: the claim that "all mankind should be saved and have eternal life" is read as a form of [Anti-Christ](/notes/anti-christ) doctrine precisely because "it comes close to the truth but perverts it." The objection is moral, not just doctrinal — universalism collapses agency: "We cannot change unless we want to, and we have 'plenty to do.'" Salvation requires our willing participation, so a blanket guarantee of salvation undercuts the work of repentance.

In [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) (Terryl Givens's biography of [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england)), the term carries a more affirming charge. England drew out what he called the Restoration's "incredible new emphases on universal salvation" — the Charlotte Haven anecdote about progression even from the lowest kingdom prompts Kyle's note: "Wait, what?! We are actually ultimate Universalists." This is presented as a [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) doctrinal thread the modern Church doesn't emphasize but that runs through the foundational sources, part of Smith's "universalist, eclectic, and antidogmatic tendencies." The tension between the two readings — universalism as anti-Christ deception vs. a generous progression theology — is part of what makes it a live thread for Kyle.

**Context:** In Christian theology universalism is the doctrine that all people will ultimately be saved. Latter-day Saint thought has a complicated relationship with it: Joseph Smith's expansive vision of degrees of glory and eternal progression has universalist overtones, while the tradition also strongly emphasizes agency, repentance, and conditional salvation.
