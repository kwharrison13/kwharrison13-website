# Fast

[Stripe](/notes/stripe)-backed one-click checkout startup, and the marquee cautionary tale in Kyle's [Cash—Kingmaker or Killer](/essays/cashkingmaker-or-killer) essay research. Per that research, Fast positioned its checkout button against PayPal Checkout, Apple Pay, Shopify, and startups like Bolt, but never found traction — it generated roughly $600K in revenue in its final full year while Bolt did ~50x that, in part because checkout pages offer many pay options and Fast only earned when shoppers used *its* button. The essay treats Fast as the embodiment of a "paranoid abundance" / cash-inferno mindset: it raised opportunistically on the logic that "the additional capital is not necessary at this stage, but the company believes it can close a round on favorable terms due to conditions in the VC market" — a line Kyle's notes flag as the definition of the bad fundraising mindset, set opposite [WeWork](/notes/wework).

The unwind is documented through a run of *The Information* reporting captured in the research: Fast put job cuts on the table (CEO Domm Holland floated cutting the 500-person staff by ≥50%), hired Morgan Stanley to explore a sale while trying to raise $100M at a $450M valuation (down from $500M), and ultimately shut down "after failing to generate more than a sliver of revenue — and spending wildly on hiring hundreds of employees." Color in the post-mortem (a full-time wellness director, $1 Fast-branded hoodies "to raise awareness") makes it the lavish-spending exemplar. The notes also flag reputational spillover onto [Stripe](/notes/stripe) as a corporate VC (Stripe led Fast's $20M Series A) and onto Index Ventures, which quietly deleted its "Phenomenal Growth, Ambitious Scale" blog post about the company. Separately, Fast appears in Kyle's sourcing log as the employer of [Pedro Marques](/notes/pedro-marques), a design lead Kyle was introduced to in March 2022.

**Context:** Fast was a San Francisco fintech founded by Domm Holland and Allison Barr Allen that built a one-click checkout product and shut down in April 2022 after roughly two years, becoming a widely cited example of venture over-funding outrunning product-market fit.
