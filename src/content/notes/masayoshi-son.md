# Masayoshi Son

In [The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future), Sebastian Mallaby casts Son as venture capital's great disruptive force — the antithesis of the cottage-industry, "back the right people" tradition. Shifting his business from Japan to America to catch the internet gold rush, Son penetrated Silicon Valley's dense networks by buying an American tech publisher and conference organizer. His audacity is captured in the Yahoo deal: when Jerry Yang said he didn't need the capital, Son retorted "Jerry, everyone needs $100 million," then threatened to fund Excite instead — "If I don't invest in Yahoo, I'll invest in Excite and I'll kill you." Mallaby frames Son's strategy in hedge-fund terms: he didn't care about alpha (skilled stock-picking) but only beta (being in the market), backing ~250 internet startups between 1996 and 2000, roughly one per week. Benchmark is repeatedly positioned as "the anti-Softbank" — its disciplined eBay win dwarfing Son's records — and Son lost more than 90% of his fortune in the bust. He resurfaces as the late-stage outsider whose "monster check" into [WeWork](/notes/wework) in 2017 gave Benchmark its escape liquidity, an example of the non-VC capital deforming startup governance.

This is the same dynamic Kyle highlights on the [Adam Neumann](/notes/adam-neumann) page, quoting Matt Levine: Neumann "figured out maybe the best and funniest way anyone has ever made money in the history of capitalism, which is 'act crazy around Masayoshi Son until money spews out of him.'"

**Context:** Masayoshi Son is the founder and CEO of SoftBank and architect of the Vision Fund, the largest technology investment fund of its era.
