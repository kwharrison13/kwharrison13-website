# Ethos, Pathos, Logos

Ethos, pathos, and logos are the three classical appeals named at the core of Kyle's [Rhetoric](/notes/rhetoric) page, which defines rhetoric as "the study of the means by which the classic authors deployed the arts of persuasion (ethos, pathos, logos)." They are the canonical triad of persuasion: appeal to character/credibility (ethos), to emotion (pathos), and to reason/argument (logos). The Rhetoric page connects pathos in particular to [Thymos](/notes/thymos) — "the spirited, persuasive register rhetoric appeals to" and "the drive for recognition" — and treats the whole apparatus as central to the essay [We Need a Renaissance of Rhetoric](/essays/we-need-a-renaissance-of-rhetoric), where rhetoric is "the battleground where language is sharpened into perspective."

**Context:** The triad comes from Aristotle's *Rhetoric*, which identifies three modes of artistic proof: *ethos* (the speaker's perceived character and trustworthiness), *pathos* (the emotional state the speech arouses in the audience), and *logos* (the logic and substance of the argument itself). They remain the standard framework taught in rhetoric and composition.
