# Self-Directed Learning

Self-directed learning is the idea that the most durable learning is owned, personalized, and built into the work rather than crammed in episodic, instructor-led sessions. [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) makes this its core indictment of corporate training: over $130B is spent in the US copying the university lecture-and-exam model, with knowledge crammed then lost. The alternative is "a culture of [Continuous Learning](/notes/continuous-learning)" that is "self-directed and personalized," where "there is no such thing as an average person" and learning becomes "something that people love to do." It links the same instinct to the future of work — "more self-directed and autonomous… people can work where, when, and how they want."

[Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) is the autodidact exemplar. In his [Harvard Law fiftieth-reunion address](/notes/charlie-munger-harvard-law-school-fiftieth-reunion-address), he reports never taking a course "in chemistry, economics, psychology, or business," yet assimilating "the fundamental organizing ethos of hard science" from elementary physics and math and pushing it "further and further into softer and softer fare" as a self-built "filing system" for [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking). He invokes "modern [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)s" who achieve "a massive multidisciplinary synthesis with less time in formal education" — the proof that broad self-directed mastery is attainable ("I've seen it done").

**Context:** Self-directed learning is an established concept in adult education (often associated with Malcolm Knowles's andragogy) describing learning in which individuals take the initiative to diagnose their needs, set goals, and evaluate outcomes.
