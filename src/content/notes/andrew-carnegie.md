# Andrew Carnegie

Andrew Carnegie appears across Kyle's corpus as the archetype of the single-product fortune. In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), Carnegie is grouped with [John D. Rockefeller](/notes/rockefeller) (oil), Sam Walton (retail), and Bill Gates (software) as "history's great capitalists" — each of whom "owes his fortune to a single product or innovation," the foil against which Buffett stands out as "a pure investor" who made his money picking diverse businesses rather than building one. So in the Buffett frame Carnegie is shorthand for the operator-titan whose wealth rests on one thing (steel).

In [VC — An American History](/books/vc), Carnegie shows up as a root of the venture "family tree": [Bessemer Venture Partners](/notes/bessemer-venture-partners) "was founded in 1981 as a spinoff from a family office created by Henry Phipps, who had partnered with the famous steel magnate Andrew Carnegie" — the same direct-lineage-from-family-wealth pattern as Venrock from the Rockefellers. Carnegie also anchors an anti-library entry: [Andrew Carnegie by Joseph Frazier Wall](/notes/andrew-carnegie-by-joseph-frazier-wall), the Bancroft Prize-winning biography, sits in Kyle's books-to-read having surfaced on Charlie Munger's [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) reading list.

**Context:** Andrew Carnegie (1835–1919) was a Scottish-American industrialist who built Carnegie Steel into the largest steel producer of its era, sold it to J.P. Morgan to form U.S. Steel in 1901, and devoted his later life to philanthropy (libraries, education) under the "Gospel of Wealth" creed.
