# Warehouse Debt

Warehouse debt appears in the wiki as the central capital-markets mechanism behind [Opendoor](/notes/opendoor)'s scaling. Per the March 5th, 2020 — JD Rasmussen (Opendoor) diligence call, Opendoor funded its home purchases with roughly $1B of credit-hedge-fund debt at a 10–15% spread across a portfolio of homes, then transitioned to lower-cost financing as the business scaled. [JD Rasmussen](/notes/jd-rasmussen) built the capital-markets function that ran this — joining at Series B (~65 people, 2 on finance) and growing the team as the company moved from <$300M to >$1B in valuation.

**Context:** Warehouse debt (a "warehouse line" or warehouse facility) is short-term, revolving credit a lender extends to a company to finance assets — mortgages, loans, or in an iBuyer's case homes — before those assets are sold or securitized into longer-term, cheaper financing. It lets a balance-sheet-heavy business hold inventory at scale; the spread between the cost of the warehouse line and the yield on the assets is a core driver of unit economics.

## Where this appears

- [Opendoor](/notes/opendoor) — described as the iBuyer that scaled single-family-rental warehouse-debt financing (10–15% spread credit-hedge-fund debt, later refinanced cheaper) as its capital-markets engine.
