# Secondhand Lions

*Secondhand Lions* (2003 film) is the opening beat of Kyle's essay [The Storytelling of Investing](/essays/the-storytelling-of-investing) — the uncle's monologue he keeps returning to: *"If you want to believe in something then believe in it. Just because something isn't true that's no reason you can't believe in it… A man should believe in those things because… those are the things worth believing in."* In the research notes (The Storytelling of Investing - Research) Kyle distills the takeaway as *"Whether or not something is true doesn't impact whether you believe it"* — belief precedes truth.

In the essay's structure the film is braided with [Sapiens](/books/sapiens) into a "We Are What We Believe" frame: the *Secondhand Lions* monologue → Yuval Noah Harari's line that "there are no gods in the universe, no nations, no money, no human rights, no laws…outside the common imagination of human beings" → investing, where money and corporations exist only because we collectively agree they do. The point that carries into the rest of the essay on [Storytelling](/notes/storytelling): in markets, the load-bearing fiction is belief, and an investor lives "constantly at the intersection of story and uncertainty."

**Context:** *Secondhand Lions* is a 2003 American film starring Michael Caine and Robert Duvall as two eccentric great-uncles who tell a young boy tall tales; the "things worth believing in" speech is its best-known monologue.
