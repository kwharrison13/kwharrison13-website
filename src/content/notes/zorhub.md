# ZorHub

ZorHub is an early-stage tool concept Kyle explored in 2018 around academic literature review and the crowd-sourcing of research. The clearest record is a July 10th, 2018 call with [Bob Bodily](/notes/bob-bodily), a Master's student in instructional design, framed as a discovery conversation about "where a tool like ZorHub could fit." Bodily walked through the painful, fragmented state of the literature-review workflow: discovering what a research topic is even *called* (e.g. "learning dashboards" vs. "educational recommender systems" vs. "open-learner models"), hunting down 100–200 PDFs through Google Scholar, discipline databases (ERIC, IEEE), EBSCO/ProQuest, university subscriptions and interlibrary loan, then coding everything by hand in Excel matrices with reference managers like Mendeley and Zotero that have weak export and note-extraction features.

The conversation surfaced several product directions ZorHub might address: a hybrid between a reference manager and a qualitative-coding tool, the ability to save quotes/figures/references clipped out of PDFs (converting PDF to HTML, with OCR being the hard part), collaborative reading groups for classes, and — the idea Bodily found most interesting given his bent toward crowd-sourcing (Wikipedia, learning resources) — crowd-sourced research "playlists." That last idea ran into the trust problem of academic Peer Review: a curated playlist is hard to qualify beyond popularity signals (upvotes/endorsements), though "certified" expert-curated playlists for foundational topics (e.g. self-regulation) might work. The notes also dwell on the brokenness of peer review itself (over-taxed, non-expert reviewers), where ZorHub's clip-and-share approach might support open peer review.
