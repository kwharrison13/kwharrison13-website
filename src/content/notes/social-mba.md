# Social MBA

Kyle's student-era thesis and program-design project: a **supplementary, bolt-on "social-impact" major** layered onto existing MBA programs — *not* a replacement degree, and *not* a non-profit-only track. The pitch to a school is "I don't want you to replace your MBA, but the current culture needs something new… add an aspect that makes it a *Social MBA*. Not just non-profits, but adding an emphasis on management, raising human capital, and helping other people succeed with and for you." The animating distinction (from a TED talk Kyle noted): *doing business for the sake of doing business focuses on the **what**; a Social MBA focuses on the **why**.*

This page is the **program / proposal** half of Kyle's MBA research. The critique-of-the-MBA dossier — school marketing copy, the Mintzberg/Heffernan/*Economist* critics, and the primary interviews with people who earned or wanted an MBA — lives on **[MBA Degrees](/notes/mba-degrees)** (alias "MBA Research") and is not duplicated here.

## The proposal

- **Supplement, don't replace.** A package of courses or a major that a school can add to its existing MBA. Schools and applicants resist the risk and expense of a brand-new program, so a bolt-on is easier to sell. The aim is to "**contaminate the MBA** with a focus on social values" — so that even students pursuing other majors get exposure to social ventures.
- **The gap it fills.** "The EMBA is only focused on creating this one idea of an entrepreneur, but there are new ideas and new cultural needs that are not captured in the MBA." The social/environmental dimension of business is increasingly central to the modern economy yet is left out of MBA programs.
- **Vision.** Create a new MBA major on two pieces of evidence: (1) the social world is increasingly important to modern economics but is absent from MBA curricula, and (2) social impact indicators will be assessed in MBA rankings within a few years (per the Academy of Management thread below).
- **The major.** Specific courses, a strong employer pipeline (non-profits and CSR functions that supply interns and new hires), and an explicit social value proposition.
- **The framing quote** Kyle anchored the proposal on, from Columbia's Ray Horton: *"The old 20th century idea that business should make as much money as possible — and the role of government and non-profits is to clean up afterward — has been replaced by the 21st century reality that the distinctive capabilities of all sectors are necessary to solve the social and environmental problems facing our world."*

## Program-design notes (handwritten)

From Kyle's notebook ([the scanned pages](attachments/social-mba/social-mba-research.pdf) — three pages, kept locally so the asset survives link rot):

- **Where to go from here.** "MBA provides the links, the brand, but not the education." Expand on the *unsustainable* nature of the current MBA and how the program meets the market's needs.
- **Structure:** a **Vision** (2–3 pages → value proposition) feeding a **Major**, which is built from **Courses** (two — *Social Entrepreneurship* and *Corporate Social Responsibility*) plus **Internships** (links to businesses — non-profits and CSR functions).
- **The pitch as a service:** IDEA → COURSES → NETWORK (internships). "Sell the idea, the whole package **+ maintenance for 2–3 years**" — helping with hiring, course improvement, and curating the internship network. **Price floated at $50,000–$100,000 + maintenance.**
- **The wedge:** "Exploit the uncertainty about new *Financial Times* indicators." Figure out how to track the FT's evolving key indicators (from graduating salary toward broader measures). "Business wants to be liked by an important sector of the world (social responsibility) regardless of how much they care about non-profits."
- **"History of BYU Social Minor"** — an interview guide for the existing BYU social-innovation minor (why was it created, how many courses were built *just* for it, biggest issues/strengths, professional attractiveness, cost to the school). Kyle's notes on the page: reference Ashoka U and the syllabus; any graduate can get it; it's a certificate for undergrad minors; only **two** courses were created, specifically modified *main* courses; "versatility of variety, but not the biggest sell"; **#1 benefit = it's interesting because it's rare.** The fully-answered version of this interview is its own section below.

## The supervision arc (Stefano Tesselli)

Kyle developed the idea under his supervision professor, **Stefano Tesselli**, who pushed it from "a paper" toward "the website for your program":

- **Figure out your perspective and your market first.** Two candidate markets: (1) undergrads who switch into business in their final year — best targeted at universities whose *undergrad* programs outshine their MBAs; or (2) "businesspeople, not managers" who want social impact, ethics, and to develop business ideas — i.e. the supplementary social-MBA aspect.
- **Why the MBA is hard to change.** "MBAs are not focused on innovation, but homogeneity… When MBA programs are standardized, you control quality. Changing the MBA would be difficult, but including some kind of *Innovation Package* might work" — the seed of the bolt-on approach.
- **Economics of the industry.** Research universities (Harvard, MIT) run on non-profit prestige and research; teaching universities (e.g. Northeastern) are effectively for-profit and lean on rich undergrads. "Higher rankings promote prestige and increase cost-price margins." Bluntly: "**MBAs are cash cows.**"
- **Tactical advice.** Write it as a 2–3 page brochure to send to MBA programs — *"Do you think it's a good idea? Could it work? Is it actionable?"* — testing the *idea*, not the pricing. Be very clear and precise in the goal and vision; follow the pattern of [Mintzberg's *Managers Not MBAs*](/books/managers-not-mbas): "The world is changing, but the MBA is not."

## The social-enterprise tailwind (Academy of Management)

Notes Kyle gathered on where the field was already moving — the evidence base for the proposal:

- **Ranking indicators are in flux.** The two classic success measures are post-MBA earnings and research quality; the Academy of Management wanted to add new indicators such as the number of MBAs employed by non-profit employers — a metric that could be folded into the *Financial Times* rankings. This is the "uncertainty" the program means to exploit.
- **Schools already on the frontier.** Columbia's masters in social enterprise (aimed at executives); **NYU's** major in social enterprise (placements with National Parks, RecycleBank, Environmental Defense); **Stanford's** social entrepreneurship program. NYU and Stanford were "on the frontier in the U.S." — and there was *no* European equivalent, which Kyle flagged as a market gap.
- **Demand without supply.** UVA (Darden), Emory, and USC (Marshall) all placed students into non-profits without offering programs to prepare them — the "extension to current MBA programs" opening.
- **The strategic conclusion:** "Most important point is to **create extensions to current MBA programs**" — sell a major or a course package rather than a standalone degree.

## Target-school strategy

The beachhead is schools with **strong undergraduate business programs but weak MBAs** — they're already geared toward business and "have the potential to have good MBAs; maybe it's time to start offering something else." Named candidates: **BYU**, **Northeastern**, and the **University of Oregon** (with Tulane and Rollins noted as other solid undergrad programs).

## History of the BYU Social Innovation Minor

The fully-answered version of the interview above — BYU's existing social-innovation minor as the working precedent for the Social MBA, run through **Todd**, the Ballard Center director. Todd's answers are verbatim; Kyle's notes are his read on what each answer means for the business case.

**1. Why was the minor created? How long did it take? How hard was it to create?**
> Todd: *"Several students were completing significant course work in social innovation and wanted to have something to acknowledge that experience. Once it was determined that it was wanted, the bureaucracy was not complicated."*

  > **Kyle:** *We could do a TON of research here. We know from experience it's a very millennial attitude to want to change the world — so businesses will want it (for their companies and their MBA rankings) AND students will want it regardless of whether employers do. That's basically our market valuation: the market is huge on both the individual-consumer level and the enterprise level.*

**2. How many courses were created just for the minor? How many previously existed?**
> Todd: *"All of the courses already existed for the most part, but the two MAIN courses were adjusted to introduce the minor."*

  > **Kyle:** *I've emailed Todd, Aaron Miller and Paul Godfrey for syllabi from key classes in the MPA program and the social-innovation minor — that content will be really telling. Next step: figure out how to get approvals/endorsements for our program (like the quotes on the back of a book) from key people — leaders of successful non-profits AND big businesses that want socially-minded employees (maybe quotes from Heather's or Jacqueline's bosses).*

**3. What have been the largest issues? What are its biggest strengths?**
> Todd: *"None of particular note."*

  > **Kyle:** *We need to play the "what if" game for best/worst-case scenarios. Hypothetical: Jace and I fly out to meet the MBA director at the University of San Francisco — what could he or she get excited about, and what could they throw at us?*

**4. How attractive is it in the professional world? Is that increasing? Examples?**
> Todd: *"Its #1 appeal is as an interesting conversation topic in interviews. It allows students to bring it up more officially."*

  > **Kyle:** *Lots of research to do here. Talking with Heather and Jacqueline will give insight, but the bigger lever is the changing metrics — see [Social Sustainability Reporting Among Companies](/notes/social-sustainability-reporting-among-companies).*

**5. If you were to estimate how much it costs BYU (curriculum, faculty, etc.)?**
> Todd: *"No one has placed a hard number to it, but it isn't significant. The Marriott School is really only interested in its demand on their resources (faculty, classrooms, etc.). Because they already had the classes and the curriculum for the most part, it was fairly easy to organize. The courses represent 10 years of teaching in this area. We attend Ashoka U's Exchange every year and bring some things back to our classes."*

  > **Kyle:** *The question: how do we sell a program BYU assembled from existing parts? Most colleges won't have access to BYU-level resources — most don't have a Ballard Center equivalent. So how do we package this in a way that's financially viable?*

The value-proposition engine for all of this — why social-impact exposure shifts from "nice to have" to *needed* — is the parallel change in MBA rankings and corporate financial reporting, captured on **[Social Sustainability Reporting Among Companies](/notes/social-sustainability-reporting-among-companies)**.

## Corporate education vs. the MBA (Lindsay Ryan)

A parallel research thread, from a Q&A with corporate educator **Lindsay Ryan** (7.5 years Director of Strategic Partnerships at the University of South Australia, delivering customized in-house MBAs to industries from automotive and defence to credit unions and local government — including a South Australia Police program that grew into a *Bachelor of Management (Policing)*). His framing quote anchors Kyle's "improvement over homogeneity" argument:

> "Corporate education involves a **facilitator**, rather than an instructor or trainer, to engage participants and encourage them to think about the **what, how and why** of what they are doing and to challenge their current paradigms."

Ryan's key points:

- **Weakness of standard MBAs:** they're "a collection of subjects bolted together like a check-list," a series of silo subjects. The exception he admired was a former VC's overhaul that ran **strategic thinking as a theme through every subject** (HRM, accounting, marketing all viewed from a strategy lens) — and he singled out the **Yale Integrated MBA** for combining theory with practice. He dislikes exams (30–60% of assessment in most programs) and prefers work-based projects.
- **In-house / tailored MBAs:** content fitted to a specific organization or industry; students run work-based projects on real organizational issues — cheaper than hiring a consultant, with a lecturer assessing rigor.
- **Strengths of the MBA:** an internationally recognized qualification; research finds some mid-priced MBAs are as good as the most expensive ones — the high-end premium is really the *network*. The first MBAs (US) existed to teach engineers the functions of a business: accounting, sales/marketing, operations, finance, HR.
- **On homogeneity:** the MBA "creates a formula for how managers think," but Ryan attributes that to a lack of student discernment, not the curriculum — the textbook is only a starting point; read widely, watch TED/YouTube. "Society needs more **leaders**, not managers."
- **On specialized MBAs:** the purpose of an MBA is *general* management; spinning out majors (Entrepreneurship, Innovation, etc.) is a marketing tactic that "dilutes the value of the MBA" and helps explain the glut of mediocre managers.
- **The 70-20-10 model:** ~80% of employee learning is informal/workplace, ~20% formal — or, in another framing, **70% workplace / 20% coaching & mentoring / 10% formal education.** Of formal training budget, ~95% is operational training and only ~5% is graduate business programs.
- **Converting theory to practice:** part-time work and volunteering (with a learning log), case studies, and having senior managers *write* their own case studies.
- **The future of business education:** real-world projects and international placements; **business ethics woven through** as a theme (post-GFC and Enron); a revival of in-house corporate MBAs; and corporate education evolving into a "**learning laboratory**" — a return to what Motorola (Six Sigma) and GE under Jack Welch did. See [Corporate University](/notes/corporate-university), which this thread enriches.

## Harry Costin's reframe of Mintzberg

A counter-argument Kyle collected (Harry I. Costin, [Mintzberg](/notes/henry-mintzberg) critique): the MBA teaches the *functions of business*, not the *practice of management* — but **M.B.A. literally means Master of Business *Administration***, and *administration* ≠ *management*. Administration is the allocation of resources to routine tasks (Thompson's "technical core"); management is a *mediating* function that engages people. Drucker (1988) predicted a knowledge society with fewer managers and more specialized professionals — for whom a generalist MBA is a valuable **common business language**. Engineers, Costin notes from experience, "greatly benefit from the general business perspective" of even a plain-vanilla MBA.

### Social principles Kyle argued every business student needs

- Stewardship over the money and resources you've been given
- The ability to act
- The competence to be self-observant and place yourself effectively by your skill set
- A focus on **impact**, not measurements like raw numbers
- Analytical observation of forces — and the ability to manipulate them
- An understanding that **poverty is not income; it's the ability to make meaningful choices in your life**
