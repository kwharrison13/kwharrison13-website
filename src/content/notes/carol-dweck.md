# Carol Dweck

Carol Dweck is the psychologist whose [Growth Mindset](/notes/growth-mindset) research recurs across Kyle's reading on learning, parenting, and expertise — the fixed-vs-growth distinction that anchors how several of these books argue about motivation. In *[The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child)*, Dweck's work is "a load-bearing chapter": she posits that students with a "fixed mindset" see mistakes as evidence of unchangeable lack of ability, while those with a "growth mindset" focus on their own effort. The book's prescription is hers — praise effort and strategy over innate ability ("Your curiosity is really fun for me to see" over "You're so smart") — and it calls a growth mindset "the MVP of the self-motivated child." Dweck also reframes competence as feeling "that we can handle a situation" rather than being objectively great at something — an internal rather than external barometer.

In *[The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy)*, Dweck supplies the "Not yet" framing of failure: "the difference between being told we have failed something and 'not yet' is significant... if you get the grade 'Not yet,' you understand that you're on a learning curve. It gives you a path to the future." And in the long-read *[The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning)* (TIME's special edition), she appears in the lineage of education reformers from Hall and Dewey through Montessori and Piaget, defined succinctly as the source of the belief "that one's ability and knowledge are capable of evolving." Across all three, Dweck functions as the intellectual backbone for [Growth Mindset](/notes/growth-mindset) in Kyle's corpus.

**Context:** Carol Dweck is a Stanford psychologist best known for her research on "mindset" and her 2006 book *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*, which popularized the distinction between fixed and growth mindsets — the belief that abilities are static versus developable through effort and learning.
