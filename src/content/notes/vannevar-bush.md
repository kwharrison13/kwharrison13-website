# Vannevar Bush

Bush surfaces across three of Kyle's book pages as two distinct figures: the MIT engineering dean who mentored [Claude Shannon](/notes/claude-shannon), and the author of the 1945 essay "As We May Think." In [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), Bush is Shannon's MIT advisor — he called his young student "a decidedly unconventional type of youngster," and the not-yet-famous Shannon wrote to Bush asking what to work on next, hinting at the ideas that would become information theory. In [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) and [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet), it is "As We May Think" that matters: [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) frames the 1945 essay as a "prescient" vision of a networked world where documents lived on microfilm rather than paper — a PARC-shaping prophecy that anticipated the computer-stock enthusiasm of the mid-1960s.

**Context:** Vannevar Bush (1890–1974) was an American engineer who, as head of the U.S. Office of Scientific Research and Development during World War II, oversaw the Manhattan Project and the wartime mobilization of American science. His 1945 essay "As We May Think" imagined the "memex," a desk-based associative information machine widely cited as an intellectual ancestor of hypertext and the web.
