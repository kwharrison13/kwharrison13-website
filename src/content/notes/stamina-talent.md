# Stamina (talent)

In [Talent (Book)](/books/talent), stamina is one of Cowen and Gross's "great underrated concepts for talent search," especially when identifying top performers, leaders, and major achievers. They build the concept partly on economist [Robin Hanson](/notes/robin-hanson)'s observation: "the most successful have a lot more energy and stamina than do others… I think this helps explain many cases of 'why didn't this brilliant young prodigy succeed?' Often they didn't have the stamina, or the will, to apply it." Hanson also notes that high-status professions — medicine, law, academia — subject young entrants to "pretty brutal stamina tests" early in their careers, in effect screening for the stamina required for later achievement.

The authors deliberately prefer "stamina" over the more common term "grit." Grit bundles two dimensions, passion and perseverance, and is strongly correlated with conscientiousness; the one feature of grit that "still seems to matter statistically, after adjusting for conscientiousness, is perseverance of effort, not passion." That residual is close to what they call stamina, which "seems to transcend conscientiousness and to be the more relevant portion of grit." Consistent with the book's broader method, they advise evaluating not absolute levels of a trait but "positive rates of change" — looking at whether a person's dynamism, ambition, and stamina are increasing over time.

## Where this appears

- [Talent (Book)](/books/talent) — central Cowen–Gross thesis: stamina as the underrated, conscientiousness-transcending core of grit, grounded in Robin Hanson's energy-of-the-successful observation.
