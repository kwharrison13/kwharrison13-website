# Henry Ford

The industrialist and assembly-line pioneer who recurs across Kyle's reading as a touchstone for several different arguments — and is read as much against the grain as with it. In [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman) cites Ford as the capitalist case for leisure: "a dyed-in-the-wool capitalist," Ford "discovered that a shorter workweek actually increased productivity" and called leisure a "cold business fact," insisting "it is high time to rid ourselves of the notion that leisure for workmen is either 'lost time' or a class privilege." In [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city), Ford supplies the city-building maxim Walt heeded for EPCOT — "We shall solve the City Problem by leaving the City" — i.e. build from scratch on virgin land rather than reform the existing city (#[City Building](/notes/city-building)).

But Ford is also the negative example. In [George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy), Johnson explicitly rejects Ford's maxim that "it is not necessary for the employer to love the employee… What is necessary is for each to try to do justice to the other," saying that if that is Ford's philosophy "then he will die one of the greatest failures the world has ever known" — Johnson's "Square Deal" / "Live and Help Live" welfare capitalism as the alternative. In [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) turns Ford into the deified patron of a mass-production dystopia — "Our Ford," the sign of the T, "History is bunk" — the cult built on the assembly line. In [Our Towns](/books/our-towns), the Fallowses quote the apocryphal Ford line on willed optimism: "Whether you think you can do something, or think you can't, you're right either way." (His cost-control lineage also surfaces in [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works), where [Robert McNamara](/notes/robert-mcnamara)'s philosophy is traced back to his time at Ford.)

**Context:** Henry Ford (1863–1947) founded the Ford Motor Company, popularized the moving assembly line and the Model T, and instituted the $5 workday — making him a perennial symbol of mass production, mass consumption, and the social bargain of industrial capitalism (with a darker legacy of antisemitism that Kyle's sources do not address).

## Where this appears

- [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) — the capitalist case that leisure is a "cold business fact," not lost time or a class privilege.
- [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) — "We shall solve the City Problem by leaving the City," the maxim behind EPCOT's blank-slate site.
- [George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy) — Johnson rejects Ford's "justice not love" employer philosophy as a path to failure.
- [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) — "Our Ford" as the deified patron of mass production; "History is bunk" and the sign of the T.
- [Our Towns](/books/our-towns) — the "whether you think you can or can't, you're right" line on Eastport's willed optimism.
- [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) — McNamara's Ford lineage tied to early cost-plus contracting.
- [James Fallows](/notes/james-fallows) — author of Our Towns, where the optimism quote appears.
