# John Stuart Mill

The 19th-century English philosopher recurs across Kyle's reading as the canonical voice for the **marketplace of ideas** and the case for free, open discourse. In [The Assault on Reason](/books/the-assault-on-reason), [Al Gore](/notes/al-gore) credits Mill with the framing that truths are "discovered and refined through 'the fullest and freest comparison of opposite opinions.'" [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins) draws on the same logic: resisting an opinion is a "peculiar evil," because if it is right we lose "the opportunity of exchanging error for truth," and if wrong we are robbed of a deeper understanding of truth "in its collision with error" — the Mill/Jefferson thread on [Free Speech](/notes/free-speech).

A second recurring Mill is the optimist about literacy and democracy. Both [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) and [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) quote Mill's autobiography on his father James Mill's faith that "all would be gained if the whole population were taught to read… and if, by means of the suffrage, they could nominate a legislature." [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) makes this the structural defense of democracy — the [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) — the line that runs "across Jefferson, Mill, and the suggestibility chapters." [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) cites his "Better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." And [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) quotes the sharper, ironic Mill: "I never meant to say that the Conservatives are generally stupid. I meant to say that stupid people are generally Conservative."

**Context:** John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was a British philosopher, political economist, and utilitarian whose *On Liberty* (1859) is the foundational modern defense of free speech and individual liberty, and whose work on representative government shaped liberal democratic theory.
