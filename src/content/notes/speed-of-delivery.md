# Speed of Delivery

"Speed of delivery" is one of the central takeaways Kyle pulls from [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) (Ben R. Rich): the idea that raw speed — compressing development timelines — is itself the competitive advantage. The book's emblematic data point is the Echo I stealth-shape program, where Rich gave his team three months instead of the requested six, and they "delivered the goods in only five weeks." Kyle's marginalia repeatedly tracks this compression: "6 months to 3 months to 5 weeks." The Have Blue prototypes flew twenty months after contract award; the Blackbird was promised twenty months out; the U-2 was delivered in eight.

The thread carries a sharper provocation Kyle flags directly: "the last great speed-of-delivery feat" may have been as far back as 1977 — today's defense primes can't operate at that cadence, and that gap is the opening [Anduril](/notes/anduril) is built into. Rich frames speed as flowing from founder-led, missionary engineering ([Kelly Johnson](/notes/kelly-johnson)) and from building to the mission rather than the spec ([Built to Mission vs Spec](/notes/built-to-mission-vs-spec): "trust the customer to know the problem but not the solution"). Faster also means an edge on competitors, less tied-up capital, and quicker response to shifting needs.

## Where this appears

- [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) — speed as the competitive advantage; Echo I delivered in five weeks vs. six months requested, and Kyle's open question of whether 1977 was "the last great speed-of-delivery feat" before [Anduril](/notes/anduril).
