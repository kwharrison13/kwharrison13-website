# Censorship

In [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) frames censorship as one face of how mass-communication technology gets bent toward authoritarian ends — and crucially, he splits it into two forms. In the "totalitarian East," there is overt *political* censorship: the media are owned and controlled by the State. In the "democratic West," there is *economic* censorship: the media are controlled by concentrated commercial ownership, because the cost of wood-pulp, modern printing machinery, and syndicated news priced out the "Little Man" and his thousands of small local papers. Either way, the diversity of independent voices collapses. Huxley's point is that mass communication "is neither good nor bad; it is simply a force" — indispensable to democracy when used one way, and "among the most powerful weapons in the dictator's armory" when used another.

The throughline in Huxley's argument is that a dictatorship "maintains itself by censoring or distorting the facts," appealing to passion and prejudice rather than reason, while the survival of [Democracy](/notes/democracy) depends on large numbers of people making realistic choices "in the light of adequate information." Censorship is therefore the negative counterpart to the [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) defense Huxley keeps returning to. He also notes the chilling acceptance of it: opinion polls of teenage "voters of tomorrow" who "see no objection to the censorship of unpopular ideas."

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) — Huxley distinguishes the totalitarian East's political censorship (State-owned media) from the democratic West's "economic censorship" (concentrated commercial media ownership), both as threats to an informed citizenry.
