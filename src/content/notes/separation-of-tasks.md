# Separation of Tasks

The separation of tasks is the central practical doctrine of [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) — the Adlerian discipline of deciding "whose task is it?" The test is simple: think about who ultimately receives the result of a given choice; that person owns the task. The book's claim is sweeping — "all interpersonal relationship troubles are caused by intruding on other people's tasks, or having one's own tasks intruded on," and merely carrying out the separation "is enough to change one's interpersonal relationships dramatically." Craving recognition is diagnosed as a failure to separate tasks: what others think of you is *their* task, not something you control. The book likens cutting these tangled relational bonds to severing the Gordian knot, and ties the doctrine to its critique of [Reward and Punishment](/notes/reward-and-punishment) — when reward grounds a relationship, "I gave this much, so you should give me that much back," which is the opposite of separation. The payoff is agency: grasp the separation of tasks and "you are holding all the cards."

**Context:** "Separation of tasks" (課題の分離) is a concept from Alfred Adler's individual psychology, popularized in the West by Ichiro Kishimi and Fumitake Koga's *The Courage to Be Disliked*. It holds that anxiety and conflict stem from confusing one's own responsibilities with those that properly belong to others.
