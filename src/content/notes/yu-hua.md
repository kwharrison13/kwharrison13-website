# Yu Hua

Author of [China in Ten Words](/books/china-in-ten-words), the essay collection Kyle read for its frame on Chinese collective experience. Yu Hua's central thread — "In America it feels like we have lost our sense of 'the people'" — reads China's shared identity as a comparison highlighting what the US has lost. He anchors the "we survive in adversity" idea in Mencius (the Confucian philosopher, see [Confucius](/notes/confucius)) and traces China's thirty-year transformation from a country "ruled by politics" into one "where money is king," touching grassroots [Entrepreneurship](/notes/entrepreneurship) (the "Garbage King"), the copycat "shanzhai" [Innovation](/notes/innovation) phenomenon, the Cultural Revolution's "reading famine," and his own answer to how one becomes a writer: "By writing."

**Context:** Yu Hua (b. 1960) is a Chinese novelist associated with the avant-garde fiction of the late 1980s, best known for the novels *To Live*, *Chronicle of a Blood Merchant*, and *Brothers*; *China in Ten Words* (2011) is a nonfiction essay collection structured around ten keywords.
