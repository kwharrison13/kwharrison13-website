# Metrics

A recurring business and gospel-study thread in Kyle's notes: **what you measure, how honest the measurement is, and how easily it gets gamed**. The clearest positive case is [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart), where Sam Walton focused on a *single* metric — the ratio of sales to inventory — to drive efficiency, alongside the discipline of holding general office expense below 2% of sales "from five stores to two thousand stores." The clearest cautionary case is [How to Trick Investors and VCs](/notes/how-to-trick-investors-and-vcs), where efficiency metrics and the Burn Multiple (Cash Burn / Net New ARR) "get gamed by varying how each component is defined" — the article's larger claim being that many VCs "don't dig deep," focusing on topline and assuming the rest works out (see [Due Diligence](/notes/due-diligence), [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation)).

The same idea appears in [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest) in a non-business register: honest measurement as the *precondition for improvement*. Stewart's line — "It is impossible to achieve meaningful improvement without awareness of present reality" — sits against a "social-acceptability reporting bias" that filters out the most useful information (quick-baptize tactics that generate inactive converts being the live example). In [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era), metrics shows up as the surrounding vocabulary of the PLG playbook — [CAC](/notes/customer-acquisition-cost), [Churn](/notes/churn), [Net Revenue Retention](/notes/net-revenue-retention), [Net Promoter Score](/notes/net-promoter-score) — the instrumentation a product-led company runs on.
