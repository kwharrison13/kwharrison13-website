# Operations Research

In the notes on [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future), operations research is "the wartime data-driven method that fed [RAND](/notes/rand-corporation)." Pioneered in Britain by physicist Patrick Blackett during the Second World War, its idea was simple: "collect and analyse as much data as possible, test hypotheses in the field and use the results to home in on solutions" — bringing the methods of the sciences to bear on military problems. After the war, Warren Weaver framed RAND's mission as continuing in peacetime the techniques operations research had produced "only from the pressure and necessity of war," extending them to "analyzing general theories of warfare."

The book treats this lineage as a proto-[Anduril](/notes/anduril) pattern: scientists and embedded academics self-organizing around national-security problems — a thread Kyle ties to [Civil-Military Fusion](/notes/civil-military-fusion). The notes also distinguish operations research (a "science of the possible" — what can be done with available equipment) from the goal-oriented "systems analysis" that RAND developed next (what future weapons and strategies a mission would require).

**Context:** Operations research (OR) is the discipline of applying mathematical and statistical analysis — optimization, queuing theory, modeling — to decision-making, born in WWII military logistics and later foundational to management science and industrial engineering.
