# Deadpool

The Deadpool franchise is, in the wiki, the working illustration of [Ryan Reynolds](/notes/ryan-reynolds) as an [auteur](/notes/auteur-theory) across both film and business. Reynolds — with writers Rhett Reese and Paul Wernick — "acted the role of auteurs" in shaping Deadpool as a character, giving the franchise a distinct creative voice rather than treating it as interchangeable studio output.

The franchise also serves the broader argument that big [Intellectual Property](/notes/intellectual-property) can still be *good*. The flagship data point, drawn from the [Ryan Reynolds](/notes/ryan-reynolds) page, is [Deadpool & Wolverine](/notes/deadpool-and-wolverine) ($200M budget, 78% Rotten Tomatoes) set against *Batman v Superman* ($325M, 29%) — the basis for "Bigger does not mean better. But good does not mean small." That same argument anchors Kyle's essay [The Hits Business](/essays/the-hits-business).

**Context:** Deadpool is a Marvel Comics antihero (created by Fabian Nicieza and Rob Liefeld) brought to the screen by Ryan Reynolds across *Deadpool* (2016), *Deadpool 2* (2018), and *Deadpool & Wolverine* (2024); the R-rated, fourth-wall-breaking tone defined the series.
