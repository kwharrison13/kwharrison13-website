# Credentialism

In [Talent](/books/talent), credentialism is framed as a useful-but-blunt screening shortcut. Tyler Cowen and Daniel Gross grant that "credentialism plays an important role in helping us narrow down who is best for the job" — but argue that when it misses the mark it "hurts the candidate and employer, limits the economic and social mobility of those who can't afford an advanced degree, and encourages overinvestment in formal education." The book's prescription is that combating *excess* credentialism — and restoring America "as a land of true opportunity" — requires getting better at talent search itself, rather than leaning on degrees as a proxy. The concept is the negative space against which the book's whole project (finding undervalued talent that conventional credentials overlook) is defined.

**Context:** "Credentialism" generally names the reliance on formal qualifications (degrees, certifications) as gatekeepers for jobs and status, often critiqued for entrenching inequality and pricing out capable people who lack expensive credentials.
