# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

Rousseau appears in [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) as the historical root of a strain of educational thinking the piece critiques. Quoting [Kate Walsh](/notes/kate-walsh) of the [National Council on Teacher Quality](/notes/national-council-on-teacher-quality), the source traces teacher-educators' reflexive rejection of proven methods to "a romanticism dating back to 18th century Swiss Philosopher Jean-Jacques Rousseau, a distrust of formal education for threatening children's true and good natural state." The piece frames this as "a kind of anti-intellectualism that almost disdains [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge)" — positioning Rousseau as the lineage behind the romantic-naturalist resistance to structured pedagogy that the rest of the article (favoring well-sequenced [Curriculum](/notes/curriculum) and evidence-based teaching) pushes against.

**Context:** Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Geneva-born philosopher of the Enlightenment whose treatise *Emile* argued for education that follows a child's natural development. His ideas profoundly shaped progressive and child-centered education.
