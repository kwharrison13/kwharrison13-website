# Church Growth

Across two books in Kyle's library, "Church Growth" is the question of what actually makes a religious movement grow — and the recurring answer is that **strictness and retention, not raw conversion counts, drive sustained growth**. In [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest), David G. Stewart's data-driven critique of LDS missionary work makes this its central thesis: the real metric is fruit that *remains* (John 15:16), not baptisms. With roughly two-thirds of members inactive, raw baptism counts mislead; "poor retention is no more a necessary part of the growth of the Church than sickness is a prerequisite to health." Stewart pairs this with the sociological finding that high-demand faiths grow *faster*, not slower — Seventh-Day Adventists (8–11% annually) and Jehovah's Witnesses retain better precisely because they ask more, since "faiths that require little offer little."

In [Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith), church growth appears through Hinckley's own framing of why a demanding church attracts people: "We have great expectations concerning our people… It is demanding. And that is one of the things that attracts people to the Church. It stands as an anchor in a world of shifting values." Both sources converge on the same counterintuitive point — that high expectations are a feature, not a barrier, to growth — and connect to [Missionary Work](/notes/missionary-work), [Convert Retention](/notes/convert-retention), and [High-Demand Religion](/notes/high-demand-religion).

## Where this appears

- [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest) — the book's whole argument is a church-growth thesis: retention over baptisms, strictness as a growth driver, methodology over inevitability.
- [Gordon B. Hinckley — Go Forward With Faith](/books/gordon-b-hinckley-go-forward-with-faith) — Hinckley explains rapid growth via high expectations: the Church "as an anchor in a world of shifting values."
