# Notability

Notability enters the wiki through [Catherine Yeo](/notes/catherine-yeo)'s essay "Wikipedia's Struggle with Creators," whose signature claim Kyle quotes directly: "**But notability, as its own notion, is inherently subjective.**" Yeo's point is that creators grow real audience and impact — to the degree that 92% of consumers trust an influencer's product recommendation over a celebrity endorsement — yet whether that counts as *notable enough* to merit, say, a [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia) page is decided by gatekeepers, not by an objective threshold. She frames Wikipedia editing as controlled by a small (roughly 90% male) group of top editors, with Wikipedia and the mainstream media reinforcing each other's definitions of who matters.

In Kyle's essay [Open-Source Knowledge](/essays/open-source-knowledge), notability becomes a hinge in the larger argument: because notability is subjective, "whether knowledge specialists (i.e. contributors) hold sway depends on how broadly they are listened to." That subjectivity makes widespread adoption harder but not impossible — the internet has made esoteric experts more accessible and let communities form around "idea centers (or repos)" that the old, centralized arbiters of notability would have ignored.

**Context:** "Notability" is the formal Wikipedia guideline used to decide whether a subject merits a standalone article, typically gauged by significant coverage in reliable, independent sources — a standard creators and the open-source-knowledge world frequently criticize as circular and gatekept.

## Where this appears

- [Catherine Yeo](/notes/catherine-yeo) — source of the "notability is inherently subjective" quote; argues Wikipedia notability is gatekept by a small group of editors.
