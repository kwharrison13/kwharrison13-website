# McDonnell Douglas

McDonnell Douglas is the central villain-by-acquisition in [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), [Peter Robison](/notes/peter-robison)'s account of [Boeing](/notes/boeing)'s decline. The book frames Boeing's 1997 purchase of McDonnell Douglas as a "reverse takeover": the McDonnell ethos — a "win-at-all-costs" defense-contracting mindset borrowed from [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch)'s General Electric playbook (anti-union, regulation-light, outsourcing-heavy, buyback-driven) — came to overrun Boeing's "professorial," engineering-driven culture. A federal mediator described the merger as "hunter killer assassins" meeting "Boy Scouts," and "the assassins won." The famous line that circulated inside Boeing: *"McDonnell Douglas has bought Boeing with Boeing's money."*

The book traces the structural backstory: Douglas, an "overly sales-oriented" aircraft division with a backlog but no funds to build, was forced to sell to McDonnell Aircraft of St. Louis. After [Bill Perry](/notes/bill-perry)'s 1994 "Last Supper" pushed defense-contractor consolidation, John McDonnell hired [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch) protégé Harry Stonecipher to run the family business, then merged with Boeing. McDonnell Douglas had already ramped buybacks while cutting R&D 60%, and that capital-allocation logic — outsourcing pieces of the plane "to its detriment" — became Boeing's, setting up the [financialization](/notes/financialization) failure mode the 737 MAX crashes (and [Dennis Muilenburg](/notes/dennis-muilenburg)'s response) later exposed. The book notes McDonnell Douglas's only model grounding was the DC-10 — a "grim milestone that even much-maligned McDonnell Douglas never reached" twice, unlike Boeing's Dreamliner-then-MAX double.

**Context:** McDonnell Douglas was a major US aerospace and defense manufacturer (maker of the F-15, Apache helicopter, and DC-10/MD-80 jetliners), formed by the 1967 merger of McDonnell Aircraft and Douglas Aircraft, and merged into Boeing in 1997.
