# Confucius

Across Kyle's notes Confucius recurs as the archetype of wisdom as a public, moral duty. In [The Greatest Minds and Ideas of All Time](/books/the-greatest-minds-and-ideas-of-all-time), [Will Durant](/notes/will-durant) opens his list of the ten greatest minds with Confucius (ahead of Plato, Aristotle, and Darwin), and Confucius anchors the book's "educated electorate" thread: a republic depends on keeping "ignorant persons from public office" and securing the wisest to rule. Kyle's own margin note distills it — "Confucius — America depends on an educated public" (see [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate)).

In [China in Ten Words](/books/china-in-ten-words), Yu Hua anchors the "we survive in adversity" thread in the Confucian tradition (via Mencius), framing human progress as "the unceasing correction of error." And in the [Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address), [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) reaches for Confucius to make wisdom a lifelong obligation: "the acquisition of wisdom is a moral duty… it requires that you're hooked on lifetime learning" — a passage Kyle also pulled into his Books 2.0 research. The connective tissue across the three sources is consistent: Confucius stands for the idea that learning is not self-advancement but a civic and moral responsibility.

**Context:** Confucius (c. 551–479 BCE) was a Chinese philosopher whose teachings on ethics, education, social harmony, and good government — collected in the *Analects* — shaped East Asian thought for over two millennia and remain a touchstone for ideals of the scholar-statesman and the cultivated, educated public.
