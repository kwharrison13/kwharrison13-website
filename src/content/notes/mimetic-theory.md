# Mimetic Theory

**Mimetic Theory** is [Rene Girard](/notes/rene-girard)'s claim — central to Kyle's notes — that all cultural behavior, beginning with a child's acquisition of language, is imitative: the world as "a theatre of envy, where, like mimes, we imitate other people's desires." In raw/clippings/Peter Thiel's Religion ([David Perell](/notes/david-perell)), it is the bedrock of [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel)'s worldview: human culture began with a murder fueled by rage and rivalry, and "managing that violence is the secret reason for all religious and political institutions." From it Thiel derives three statements — "don't copy your neighbors; time moves forward; the future will be different from the present." Girard's evidence base is precisely the material modern people ignore: the Bible, classic fiction ([Marcel Proust](/notes/marcel-proust)), and playwrights like [William Shakespeare](/notes/william-shakespeare), whose plays Girard reads as theatres of envy and imitated desire.

In Kyle's writing, Mimetic Theory is the *deep root* of [Memetics](/notes/memetics), and the two are deliberately distinguished: mimetics is the mimicry of *people* (desire is imitative), while memetics is the virality of *ideas*. The link is etymological — if desire is imitative, then memes (Greek *mimeme*, "that which is imitated") are how those imitated desires propagate at internet speed. The Meme Economy - Research uses Mimetic Theory as one of the lenses (alongside [Memetics](/notes/memetics) and the [Reality Distortion Field](/notes/reality-distortion-field)) on its thesis that "belief precedes truth, and that has *always* been true," with [Silicon Valley Bank](/notes/silicon-valley-bank)'s social-media bank run as the case study.

**Context:** René Girard (1923–2015) was a French historian and literary critic whose mimetic theory of desire — and its corollary, the scapegoat mechanism — influenced fields from anthropology to economics, and was famously absorbed by Peter Thiel, who studied under Girard at Stanford.
