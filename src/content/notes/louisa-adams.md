# Louisa Adams

Louisa Adams is the "long-suffering wife" of [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) in James Traub's biography. The book draws her as the emotional and expressive counterweight to her rational, withdrawn husband — "a woman of uncommon intelligence who would later dabble in writing poetry, drama, and fiction," who "needed friends, and she had a gift for making them." The marriage was respectful but never warm: Adams worried she could not master the etiquette of a diplomatic wife, instructed her to "express no general or national reflections" and "suppress some of the little attachments to splendor," and her discovery that she had "brought nothing to the marriage — nothing, that is, save herself" left her feeling unworthy of her brilliant husband. Between them "there would be respect and compassion and sometimes love — but never the sense of perfect trust and mutuality."

Frail in constitution and prone to depression, Louisa was "preoccupied with death, and actively yearned for it, since the death of her infant daughter," at times blaming her husband for her grief and at others accusing herself. Yet she was a shrewd observer: her sketch of [Henry Clay](/notes/henry-clay) — "his heart is generous and good… but a neglected education, vicious habits and bad company… have made him blush to act the better part" — is quoted as "penetrating but notably sympathetic." She differed with Adams on raising their sons (including [Charles Francis Adams](/notes/charles-francis-adams)), preferring "to raise the boys with honey rather than vinegar," while she "quietly acquiesced to his right of control." As First Lady she "languished in her solitude," writing that she was "unpleasant to myself and to everyone else."

**Context:** Louisa Catherine Adams (1775–1852) was the British-born wife of President John Quincy Adams and the only foreign-born First Lady until the 21st century; she left voluminous diaries and memoirs now valued as a record of early-republic political and domestic life.
