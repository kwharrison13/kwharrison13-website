# Herb Kelleher

The [Southwest Airlines](/notes/southwest-airlines) founder whose strategy serves as a centerpiece case study in [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) for [Tradeoffs](/notes/tradeoffs) made "by design" rather than "by default." Greg McKeown recounts attending a talk where Kelleher described how deliberate he was about the trade-offs he made at Southwest: point-to-point flights instead of flying everywhere, no meals instead of raising prices to cover them, open seating instead of assigned seats, coach-only instead of glitzy first-class. The point that McKeown takes from him is clarity: "Kelleher was totally clear about what the company was — a low-cost airline — and what they were not," and accepted the risk of alienating some customers as the price of a coherent strategy. The book contrasts this with Continental's incoherent, margin-driven sacrifices. In the highlights, Kyle flags the Southwest simplicity story and notes it also appears in *Good to Great*.

**Context:** Herb Kelleher (1931–2019) co-founded Southwest Airlines in 1967 and led it for decades as CEO and chairman, building it into the model low-cost carrier and a frequently cited example of culture-driven, disciplined business strategy.

## Where this appears

- [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) — the Southwest tradeoffs (point-to-point, no meals, open seating, coach-only) as the book's flagship example of strategic choices made "by design, not by default."
