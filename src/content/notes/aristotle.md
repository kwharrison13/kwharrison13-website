# Aristotle

Aristotle recurs across Kyle's reading as a touchstone quoted for whichever of his maxims a given author needs. In [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci), Isaacson notes that Aristotle and [Alexander the Great](/notes/alexander-the-great) "were teachers of one another" — the tutor/king reciprocity Leonardo mirrored with Francis I. In [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism), [Cal Newport](/notes/cal-newport) cites Aristotle's claim that "the best and most pleasant life is the life of the intellect," contemplation valued for its own sake, as the philosophical root of the book's case for high-quality leisure. In [How To Be a Capitalist Without Any Capital](/books/how-to-be-a-capitalist-without-any-capital) he supplies the habit principle — "Men acquire a particular quality by constantly acting a certain way."

[Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in [Practical Thought About Practical Thought](/notes/charlie-munger-practical-thought-about-practical-thought), invokes Aristotle on [Envy](/notes/envy): the best way to avoid envy is to "plainly deserve the success we get." Aristotle also appears in [Why You're Christian](/notes/why-you-re-christian) as one of the Greeks "interpreted through a Christian lens" ([Aristotle](/notes/aristotle) through [Aquinas](/notes/thomas-aquinas), [Plato](/notes/plato) through [St. Augustine](/notes/st-augustine)), and in The Process of Zion via the line "Wicked men obey because of fear. Good men obey because of love."

**Context:** Aristotle (384–322 BC) was a Greek philosopher, student of Plato and tutor of Alexander the Great, whose work on logic, ethics, metaphysics, and natural science underpins much of the Western intellectual tradition.
