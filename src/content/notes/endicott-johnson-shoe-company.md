# Endicott-Johnson Shoe Company

The shoe manufacturer that [George F. Johnson](/notes/george-f-johnson) co-owned and ran as the working laboratory for his [Square Deal](/notes/the-square-deal) — the brand of [Welfare Capitalism](/notes/the-square-deal) that made worker welfare a competitive advantage rather than a cost. At its height the company made ~52M shoes a year, employed ~19,000 workers (1934), and supplied boots to the US military in both World Wars. It was first in the shoe industry to introduce the 8-hour workday and 40-hour workweek with comprehensive medical care, and stayed consistently profitable "despite paying some of the highest wages in the industry" (per [George F. Johnson](/notes/george-f-johnson) and [The Square Deal](/notes/the-square-deal)).

Around the factories Johnson built a near-utopian company town: free or near-free libraries, parks, hospitals, golf courses, pools, theaters, six carousels that could never be charged for, $10 bank books for newborns, and houses financed and sold to workers at a net loss through the company's own housing corporation — a concrete example of employer-led [City Building](/notes/city-building) (see [George F. Johnson](/notes/george-f-johnson), [The Square Deal](/notes/the-square-deal)). The governing motto carved on the E. J. Workers' Public Market was "Live and Help Live," and Johnson summarized the model as "See what you can make of the business, not make out of the business," dividing every saving three ways among workers, customers, and stockholders ([George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy)).

The company was engineered to make a union unnecessary: it was "neither an open shop nor a closed shop," and in a ~15,000-worker vote roughly 80% voted against organizing. Even Sam Gompers reportedly conceded E. J. had already given workers more than a union could ask ([George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy), [Unions](/notes/unions)). Critics held that the benefits were designed to make workers feel grateful rather than fairly compensated, and historian [Gerald Zahavi](/notes/gerald-zahavi) (*Workers, Managers, and Welfare Capitalism*) later judged the Square Deal "an anachronism." The company peaked in 1948 — Johnson's death year — and entered a long decline thereafter.

**Context:** Endicott Johnson Corporation was a footwear manufacturer based in the Triple Cities of New York (Endicott, Johnson City, and Binghamton). Founded out of the Lester Brothers / Lestershire operation, it became one of the largest US shoemakers in the early-to-mid 20th century before contracting and ceasing operations late in the century.
