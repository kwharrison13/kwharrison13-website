# Seneca

One of the core Stoic sources in [Ryan Holiday](/notes/ryan-holiday)'s [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), alongside Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, and Musonius Rufus — the lineage that furnishes the book's operating quotes ([Stoicism](/notes/stoicism)). Seneca recurs in the book's argument about self-discipline as practiced freedom: Holiday notes that "Seneca was no Cato day to day, but he knew from his practice that he could be if he had to," using him to make the point that success breeds softness and addiction to creature comforts, which discipline guards against. Seneca also supplies the book's measure of personal growth — after a lifetime of studying philosophy, he judged his own progress by writing, "What progress have I made? I have begun to be a friend to myself" — and its idea of moral influence: "Happy is the man who can make others better, not merely when he is in their company, but even when he is in their thoughts."

**Context:** Lucius Annaeus Seneca (c. 4 BC – AD 65) was a Roman Stoic philosopher, statesman, and dramatist, and tutor and adviser to the emperor Nero. His *Letters to Lucilius* and essays on anger, brevity of life, and tranquility are foundational Stoic texts. He was ordered by Nero to take his own life in AD 65.
