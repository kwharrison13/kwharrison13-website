# Curious

In Kyle's notes on [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), curiosity is the trait that marks Adams as a [Polymath](/notes/polymath): he "was endlessly curious about how things worked, including things that didn't turn a profit." The book portrays him as someone who "seemed to know everything about everything," reciting Homer in Greek and probing every science — a "relentless curiosity about how things work" that didn't bend toward utility or profit.

Kyle explicitly ties this to Walter Isaacson's [Leonardo Da Vinci (Book)](/notes/leonardo-da-vinci-book) and Leonardo's famous to-do list — "Describe the tongue of the woodpecker" — as the archetype of curiosity drilling into "random curiosities" for their own sake, which in Leonardo's case led from the woodpecker's tongue to a fascination with the muscles of the human tongue. The concept frames disinterested, non-instrumental curiosity (knowing for the sake of knowing) as the engine of the integrated, polymathic intellect Kyle admires in both figures.
