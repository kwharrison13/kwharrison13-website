# Distribution

How a product reaches the customers who need it — one of the three elements of [Product-Market Fit](/notes/product-market-fit) in [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era) ("the product, distribution and customers"), achieved "when you have a product with a clear value proposition that resonates with customers whom you know how to reach and convert." The PLG book treats the product itself as the distribution channel: "de-labor the distribution engine behind your product" by empowering end users to find and adopt it, and meet them where they already are — "if your end users live in Slack, distribute through the Slack App Directory." Kyle's notes flag this as the explicit counterpoint to [Zero To One](/books/zero-to-one), where Thiel argues distribution is a deliberate, under-respected discipline in its own right rather than something the product does for free.

The concept also carries Kyle's more skeptical, contemporary edge. On [Wrapper Companies](/notes/wrapper-companies) he records the common claim that "[Distribution](/notes/distribution) is all that matters" — many businesses are just a marketing/distribution layer on someone else's infrastructure ([MEDVI](/notes/medvi) as a marketing layer on rented [Telehealth](/notes/telehealth)), and "we've all matured past the point of criticizing wrapper companies." It recurs in The $1B Rorschach Test - Research and [One-Person Billion Dollar Company](/notes/one-person-billion-dollar-company) as part of that cluster — where cheap distribution plus rented infrastructure can manufacture scale fast, for better or worse. A reading note on Frederik Gieschen also cites the YouTube founders' embed-into-Myspace trick as a distribution hack borrowed from the PayPal playbook.
