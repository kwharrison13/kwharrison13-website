# Infallibility

Infallibility is one of the recurring threads Kyle marked across [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) — the wiki page names it as the "fallible men, infallible work" obsession, one of six that run through his highlights. The book is unusually candid about leaders' imperfection, and [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) is its main voice on the question: "'Can a prophet or an apostle be mistaken?' Do not ask me any such question, for I will acknowledge that all the time," and "Man having the priesthood may be fallible… I do not pretend to be infallible." Kyle flagged the first quote with a one-word note — *Infallibility.* — making the concept explicit.

The thread's resolution, as the [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) synthesis frames it, is to separate the men from the work: leaders may err, "but his fallibility did not mean God could not work through him," and "the work… emanated from God. He is its author." Kyle reads toward a posture of believing certain keystone truths that let you make right decisions — the same territory as [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation) and [Revelation](/notes/revelation). The tension (fallible leaders, a work claimed to be infallible) is one Kyle tracks across related histories like [American Zion](/books/american-zion).

**Context:** In a religious context, infallibility is the claim that a person or institution is incapable of error in matters of doctrine. The Latter-day Saint tradition, as represented here, explicitly rejects leader infallibility — distinguishing the fallibility of individual prophets from the divine origin of the work itself — a contrast often drawn against the Catholic doctrine of papal infallibility.
