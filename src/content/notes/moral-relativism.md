# Moral Relativism

The philosophical foil that [Dallin H. Oaks](/notes/dallin-h-oaks) defines truth against in Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks: "the philosophy that each person is free to choose for himself what is right and wrong," which he calls "the unofficial creed for many." His central paradox is that relativism makes [Tolerance](/notes/tolerance) *cheap* — "for them, almost anything goes" — whereas the more firmly one believes in God and [Absolute Truth](/notes/absolute-truth), the more often one is confronted with the genuine challenge of how to tolerate. Oaks contrasts survey data (79% of adults two decades earlier affirmed universal guidelines for good and evil, versus three-quarters of recent college seniors who believe right and wrong are relative) and traces the danger to teachers who practice relative morality and so shape the rising generation.

In [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) the same concept appears as a historical diagnosis rather than a moral one. "The twentieth century thus ushered in the age of relativism — not only moral relativism but scientific, philosophical, and artistic relativism." The book's "life / my life" box diagram traces how the medieval church, then Enlightenment reason, then the shocks of the twentieth century ([Viktor Frankl](/notes/viktor-frankl)'s camps, [Samuel Beckett](/notes/samuel-beckett)'s [Waiting for Godot](/notes/waiting-for-godot), relativism) dissolved the outer "life" box and left only personal preference — breeding what the book calls "cosmic loneliness." Both sources frame the Restoration / absolute truth as the replacement for the collapsed macro-frame.

**Context:** Moral relativism is the meta-ethical view that moral judgments are not objectively true or false but are relative to individuals, cultures, or historical periods — denying that there are universal moral standards binding on everyone.
