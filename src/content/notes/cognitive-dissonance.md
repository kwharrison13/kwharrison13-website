# Cognitive Dissonance

The term was coined by social psychologist [Leon Festinger](/notes/leon-festinger), whose book *When Prophecy Fails* (1956) is the seminal account. As quoted in [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), Festinger defines it as what happens "when reality clashes with our deepest convictions" — "we'd rather recalibrate reality than amend our worldview," and, crucially, "we become even more rigid in our beliefs than before." His opening line captures the dynamic: "A man with conviction is a hard man to change… Show him facts or figures and he questions your sources. Appeal to logic and he fails to see your point."

[Hillbilly Elegy](/books/hillbilly-elegy) gives the concept a concrete, social texture. [J.D. Vance](/notes/j-d-vance) uses it to name the gap between professed values and lived reality in his community: "We talk about the value of hard work but tell ourselves that the reason we're not working is some perceived unfairness… These are the lies we tell ourselves to solve the cognitive dissonance — the broken connection between the world we see and the values we preach." In Kyle's notes the concept thus sits between two registers: Festinger's psychology of belief-rigidity (also echoed in the [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) note that smart people use intellect to reach the answer they already want, via [Ezra Klein](/notes/ezra-klein)) and Vance's account of how a culture rationalizes the distance between its [Hard Work](/notes/hard-work) ethic and its outcomes.

**Context:** Cognitive dissonance is the mental discomfort experienced when holding two conflicting beliefs, or when behavior contradicts a belief; Festinger's theory (1957) holds that people resolve the tension by changing one of the cognitions — often by rationalizing rather than updating the underlying belief.
