# Numbers 21

Numbers 21 is cited in Kyle's Alma 36–38 study as the Old Testament root of the study's central image: "the easiness of the way." Verses 8–9 recount the Lord commanding Moses to make a fiery serpent of brass and set it on a pole, so that anyone bitten "when he looketh upon it... shall live." Kyle pairs this with [1 Nephi 17](/notes/1-nephi-17):41, where Nephi notes that "because of the simpleness of the way, or the easiness of it, there were many who perished" — the Israelites who would not look at the brass serpent because the labor seemed too simple. This typifies Alma's plea, "Do not let us be slothful because of the easiness of the way," tying the brazen-serpent episode to looking to Christ for salvation.

**Context:** Numbers 21 narrates Israel's journey near the end of the wilderness wandering, including the plague of "fiery serpents" and Moses' bronze serpent on a pole — an image the New Testament (John 3:14) later reads as a type of Christ lifted up.
