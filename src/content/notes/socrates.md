# Socrates

Across Kyle's corpus, Socrates surfaces in two distinct lineages. In the [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin) material he is the named model for the thirteenth of Franklin's [Thirteen Virtues](/notes/thirteen-virtues), Humility — "Imitate Jesus and Socrates" — and, per [Benjamin Franklin: An American Life](/books/benjamin-franklin-an-american-life), the inspiration for the dialectical, question-led mode Franklin adopted in the [Junto](/notes/junto) (and his early habit of winning arguments by humble inquiry rather than assertion). In [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked), Socrates appears twice: the book invokes the **Socratic paradox** that "no one desires evil" (Adlerian psychology reads even destructive behavior as a misguided pursuit of "good"), and its very form is framed as a Plato-for-Adler dialogue — author Kishimi tells Koga "Socrates's thought was conveyed by [Plato](/notes/plato); I would like to be a Plato for Adler."

He also appears in Kyle's Spiritual Quotes — Greatest Hits collection via the maxim "An unexamined life is not worth living," grouped among the secular voices Kyle threads into a corpus centered on LDS leadership. The [Plato](/notes/plato) concept page situates Socrates as Plato's teacher in the classical Athenian lineage.

**Context:** Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was a classical Athenian philosopher, the teacher of Plato, known through Plato's and Xenophon's dialogues rather than his own writing. He is credited with the Socratic method of inquiry and was executed by Athens on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth.
