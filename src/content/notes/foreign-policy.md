# Foreign Policy

A cross-corpus theme spanning history, strategy, and fiction in Kyle's reading. Its anchor is [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams), whom Traub names the **"fountainhead" of American foreign-policy realism** — Adams sharply distinguished American interests from universal goods and was deeply skeptical of America's capacity to do good abroad (connecting to [Realism](/notes/realism) and [Henry Kissinger](/notes/henry-kissinger)'s observation that Americans won't accept a "realist" president). [Chip War](/books/chip-war) reframes foreign policy as technological statecraft via Harold Brown's "High Tech Is Foreign Policy" — weaponized interdependence and semiconductor export controls as the new instruments of power.

The great-power contest recurs across several books. [No Apology](/books/no-apology) frames three rival global strategies (Western free-enterprise democracy, China's authoritarian free-enterprise, Russia's energy-based authoritarianism) and argues appeasement alienates allies. [2034: A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war) dramatizes American overreach and the Sino-Iranian-Russian alignment, with the maxim that empires "rot from within." [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia) turns to information warfare — the Kennan passage on rebuffing Moscow through internal societal health, and Russian television covering wars "to stoke domestic passions" rather than to reflect on foreign policy. [Shortest Way Home](/books/shortest-way-home) adds [Pete Buttigieg](/notes/pete-buttigieg)'s post-9/11 reflections on globalization, terrorism, and the trade-off between safety and freedom. The lens recurs for [Vladimir Putin](/notes/vladimir-putin)'s record abroad (Chechnya, Syria).
