# Risk Management

Across Kyle's reading, risk management is less a quantitative technique than a discipline of confronting downside before it arrives. The investing pole is [Stephen A. Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-a-schwarzman)'s [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes), where the rule distills to "**Don't. Lose. Money.**" — recognizing market tops and waiting for a ~10% recovery off bottoms — paired with the move to depersonalize the investment decision into a team process after the Edgcomb loss ([Build a Fund](/notes/build-a-fund)). [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) supplies the critique: Taleb attacks portfolio-manager definitions of "risk" that exclude the tail, and the turkey problem shows a data set looking safest at the moment of maximum risk — connecting risk management to [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting) ("what is surprising is not the magnitude of our forecast errors, but our absence of awareness of it"). [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) gives the venture inversion: rather than diversifying à la modern portfolio theory, Davis and Rock made concentrated bets and managed risk by "exercising a measure of control over his assets."

The frame also runs through operating and strategic settings. In [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime), Iger ties it to big swings and gut-after-the-models conviction — "you can't have big wins without big risks." [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) carries it into defense strategy, and [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) flags the under-appreciated reality that entrepreneurs "do not seek unnecessary risk; they try to avoid and mitigate risk." Throughout, risk management pairs tightly with [Failure](/notes/failure) and [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting) — pricing in uncertainty instead of forecasting it away.

**Context:** Risk management is the practice of identifying, assessing, and prioritizing uncertainties and then acting to limit or control their impact — spanning finance (downside protection, position sizing, tail hedging), operations, and strategy.

- [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes) — Schwarzman's "Don't. Lose. Money.," reading market tops, team-based decisions after the Edgcomb loss.
- [The Black Swan](/books/the-black-swan) — critique of risk definitions that exclude the tail; the turkey problem.
- [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) — venture risk managed via concentration and control, not diversification.
- [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime) — Iger's "no big wins without big risks" and gut-after-the-models conviction.
- [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) — risk as a load-bearing theme in defense strategy.
- [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) — entrepreneurs as risk *mitigators*, not risk seekers.
- [Forecasting](/notes/forecasting) — pricing in uncertainty rather than forecasting it away.
- [Failure](/notes/failure) — the paired theme; failure studied openly as the input to better risk decisions.
- [Stephen A. Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-a-schwarzman) — the investor whose risk maxims anchor the concept.
