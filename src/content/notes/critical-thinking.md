# Critical Thinking

Across Kyle's reading, critical thinking recurs as one of the most-cited "future-proof" skills and, simultaneously, the capacity most under threat. [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) defines it as "the ability to use our knowledge and intellect to analyze information or situations without judgement or bias to make balanced, thoughtful, evidence-based decisions" and ranks it among the most sought-after employee traits — citing the World Economic Forum's *Future of Jobs* report that critical-thinking demand will rise as organizations face "an ever-increasing variety of complex problems." In that book it sits inside a four-step learning loop (gain knowledge → [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection) → critical thinking → feedback) that most workplace learning never completes, halting at the "gaining knowledge" stage.

[The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) frames critical thinking as the "most important" intellectual capability and "the one most under attack in American universities" — "the ability to examine new information and competing ideas dispassionately, logically, and without emotional or personal preconceptions." [Tom Nichols](/notes/tom-nichols) argues that when students become valued clients rather than learners, they gain self-esteem but never develop the habits of critical thinking needed to evaluate complex issues as citizens; he cites a 2011 University of Chicago study finding graduates "failed to make significant gains in critical thinking and complex reasoning during their four years of college."

[21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) places it first among the "four Cs" ([Education](/notes/education)'s recommended core — critical thinking, communication, collaboration, creativity) and casts it as the antidote to the "knowledge illusion" and to echo chambers of self-confirming news feeds. [Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari) argues schools should downplay technical skills in favor of these general-purpose capacities so people can keep reinventing themselves in an unpredictable world.
