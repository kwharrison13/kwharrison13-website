# 1 Corinthians 9

Paul's first letter to the Corinthians, chapter 9, surfaces in two of Kyle's topical studies as a verse about effort and striving. In Hope — Topical Study he draws on "he that ploweth should plow in hope" (9:10) to frame struggles as real but oriented forward — the harnessed horse pushes its wagon rather than pulls, and stopping or moving backward causes the harness to fall off and the journey to halt.

In Self-Mastery — Topical Study the chapter supplies the load-bearing line "every man that striveth for the mastery is temperate in all things" (9:25). Kyle reads it to make the case that mortality is not meant to *perfect* us but to develop the ability to *try*: if we are striving — simply trying to develop self-mastery — "we're learning to be a friend of God." The verse sits inside the gospel paradox between "deny himself and take up his cross" ([Matthew 16](/notes/matthew-16):24) and "my yoke is easy" ([Matthew 11](/notes/matthew-11):29–30).

**Context:** 1 Corinthians 9 is Paul's defense of his apostolic conduct, closing with athletic imagery — the runner who runs to obtain the prize and the competitor who is "temperate in all things" — used as a metaphor for spiritual discipline.

## Where this appears

- Hope — Topical Study — "he that ploweth should plow in hope" (9:10) framing struggle as forward-pressing effort
- Self-Mastery — Topical Study — "every man that striveth for the mastery is temperate in all things" (9:25) anchoring the idea that the test of mortality is learning to try
