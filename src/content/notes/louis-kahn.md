# Louis Kahn

Louis Kahn enters Kyle's notes through [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) as the source of a defining maxim for space-making: "Architecture is the thoughtful making of space." Sam Gennawey uses Kahn to anchor the discipline behind Disney's parks — the idea that good environments are deliberately *made*, engaging all five senses (down to the cookie-scent "Smellitzers"), designed at human scale, and crucially built with places to pause and do nothing. The book draws a distinction Kahn's line implies: architects focus on the object and how it interacts with surrounding space, while city planners deal with *process*, providing direction for the patterns that lead to good design. Kahn sits in the book's roster of design thinkers alongside [Christopher Alexander](/notes/christopher-alexander), [Victor Gruen](/notes/victor-gruen), and [Jane Jacobs](/notes/jane-jacobs) who together form the conceptual spine for reading Disneyland's spaces.

**Context:** Louis Kahn (1901–1974) was an American architect known for monumental, light-driven buildings such as the Salk Institute and the National Assembly Building of Bangladesh, and for an influential, almost philosophical approach to space and materials.
