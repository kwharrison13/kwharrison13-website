# Autonomous Mobility

Autonomous mobility is the smart-city framing of self-driving technology in Kyle's notes — the bundle of vehicles, infrastructure, and municipal data that lets a city move people and goods autonomously. It centers on the [City of Dublin](/notes/city-of-dublin), Ohio, an early-adopting small city (~45K people) where [Doug McCullough](/notes/doug-mccullough) serves as CIO. Dublin is part of the 33 Smart Mobility Corridor council of cities and used a USDOT grant to build fiber/wireless infrastructure enabling [Autonomous Driving](/notes/autonomous-driving) along the corridor, while running an IBM Watson instance and partnering with Ohio State on sensors and with vendors like TTS and NoTraffic on traffic-signal timing.

The notes treat autonomous mobility as a govtech adoption challenge as much as a technology one: McCullough's "Dublin Digital Experience Lab" exists precisely to vet immature ideas and let vendors pitch, and he hunts for procurement "loopholes" to fund $10–15K experiments. It threads into [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) and [City Building](/notes/city-building) as a concrete instance of a city building new capabilities, and is closely adjacent to the [Autonomous Vehicles](/notes/autonomous-vehicles) research thread.

**Context:** "Autonomous mobility" broadly refers to deploying self-driving vehicles and connected infrastructure as part of an urban transportation system. Mid-to-late-2010s programs like USDOT's Smart City / Smart Mobility grants funded cities to pilot connected corridors and data platforms supporting these systems.
