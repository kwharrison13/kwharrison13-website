# Resource Allocation

Resource allocation is a central thesis of [How Will You Measure Your Life](/books/how-will-you-measure-your-life): "everything related to strategy is only intent until it gets to the resource allocation stage." Inside a company, [Clayton Christensen](/notes/clayton-christensen)'s argument runs, the vision, plans, opportunities, threats, and problems all "want priority, vying against one another to become the actual strategy the company implements" — and what actually gets the time, money, and energy *is* the real strategy, regardless of what was stated. The book reframes this for the individual: personal resource allocation is the real strategy of a life, and where you actually spend your hours and attention reveals your true priorities.

The book also warns about the trap on the personal side: "lifestyle demands can quickly lock in place the personal resource allocation process" — once you "can't devote less time to my job because I won't get that promotion," the allocation calcifies before you've consciously chosen it. This connects closely to [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) (the nearest existing wiki framing) and to the [Revealed Preference](/notes/revealed-preference) idea that *allocated* interest, not stated interest, is what counts.
