# Match Quality

Match quality is, in [Range](/books/range)'s words, "a term economists use to describe the degree of fit between the work someone does and who they are — their abilities and proclivities." [David Epstein](/notes/david-epstein) uses it to defend breadth and late specialization: one study found that "early career specializers jumped out to an earnings lead after college, but that later specializers made up for the head start by finding work that better fit their skills and personalities." The longer "sampling period" pays off precisely because it improves the eventual match.

The deeper claim is that good matching requires self-knowledge that only experience produces — "we learn who we are only by living, and not before." Because "the most momentous personality changes occur between age eighteen and one's late twenties, specializing early is a task of predicting match quality for a person who does not yet exist." Epstein pairs the idea with Herminia Ibarra's aphorism "I know who I am when I see what I do," and with the reframing of grit: rather than asking whether someone is gritty, ask *when* — "if you get someone into a context that suits them… it will look like grit from the outside." The concept anchors the book's case for sampling, exploration, and [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) over early hyperspecialization.
