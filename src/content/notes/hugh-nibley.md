# Hugh Nibley

A Latter-day Saint scholar who surfaces across Kyle's reading as a touchstone on consecration, focused attention, and the defensibility of the Book of Mormon. In [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) he is one of the modern LDS voices the book draws on for materialism and consecration, quoted asking "What is there to stop me from observing and keeping the law of consecration at this very day as I have already covenanted and promised to do without reservation?" In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) Kyle attaches a Nibley passage to the book's argument about attention — the "fairy-tale hero… permitted to choose… only one" gem, the rule that "I am only permitted to think of one thing at a time." His name recurs in [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) as Kyle's tentative source for a frame he keeps reaching for — "you have to want the consequences of the things you want" (the note literally reads "Nibley?").

Nibley is also the apologetic reference point in Kyle's gospel-study material. [Joseph Smith Lecture 2 — Joseph's Personality and Character](/notes/joseph-smith-lecture-2-joseph-s-personality-and-character) recounts his famous challenge: assigning a class of Middle East students to write "522 pages" replicating the Book of Mormon's characteristics — "So far he has not received the assignment back." And in [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens) Nibley wrote the preface to [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england)'s "Why the Church is as True as the Gospel," praising it as taking readers "out of our intellectual flatland."

**Context:** Hugh Nibley (1910–2005) was a professor of ancient scripture at Brigham Young University and one of the most prolific Latter-day Saint apologists of the 20th century, known for his work on the Book of Mormon, ancient Near Eastern parallels, and sharp critiques of materialism within Mormon culture.
