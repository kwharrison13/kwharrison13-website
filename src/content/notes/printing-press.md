# Printing Press

In [Double Entry](/books/double-entry), the printing press is the mechanism by which double-entry bookkeeping spread from Venice across Europe. [Luca Pacioli](/notes/luca-pacioli)'s treatise *Particularis de computis et scripturis* was published within his mathematical encyclopaedia (the *Summa*) in Venice in 1494 — "forty years after the invention of movable type in Europe." The *Summa* was "the first printed book to deal with Hindu–Arabic arithmetic and its offshoot, algebra," and "the first vernacular book printed in Europe to contain algebra," carrying both algebra and Venetian bookkeeping into wide circulation. The book credits Pacioli's lasting fame to "his passion for disseminating its secrets via the printing press to the widest possible audience in their own tongue" — the press is what turned a 27-page bookkeeping section into the foundation of modern [Accounting](/notes/accounting) still in use five centuries later.

In Kyle's notes the printing press connects [Double Entry](/books/double-entry) to the broader history of intellectual tools: the page links to [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) as "adjacent on the history of intellectual tools and the printing-press revolution that spread Pacioli's method," and to the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) as "the print-driven diffusion of Venetian bookkeeping across Europe." It is the same enabling technology that underwrites [Print Culture](/notes/print-culture) — the medium whose spread reshaped how knowledge, commerce, and discourse propagated.

**Context:** The printing press with movable type, introduced in Europe by Johannes Gutenberg around 1440, dramatically lowered the cost of reproducing text and is conventionally credited with accelerating the spread of literacy, the Renaissance, the Reformation, and the scientific revolution.
