# Gordon Moore

Gordon Moore is a recurring figure in the semiconductor history threaded through the wiki's books. In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), he is the author of the famous 1965 article that first graphed the exponential increase in transistor density — the trend Carver Mead later named "Moore's Law" — and a co-founder and early CEO of [Intel](/notes/intel) alongside [Bob Noyce](/notes/bob-noyce) and Andy Grove. The book quotes his 1973 declaration that chipmakers, not counterculture youth, were "the real revolutionaries," and records his skeptical reaction to Morris Chang's plan to found [TSMC](/notes/tsmc) ("Morris, you've had a lot of good ideas in your time. This isn't one of them"). His law recurs as a cost-curve benchmark and, by the book's end, as a prediction whose physical limits industry luminaries debate. In [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), Moore appears earlier in his career, recalling his frustrating time working under [William Shockley](/notes/william-shockley), whose competitiveness extended even to his relationships with the young physicists he supervised — the backdrop to the "traitorous eight" departure that seeded Fairchild and Silicon Valley.

**Context:** Gordon Moore (1929–2023) co-founded Fairchild Semiconductor and then Intel, where he served as CEO and chairman; his 1965 observation about transistor density became Moore's Law, the defining heuristic of the semiconductor era.

## Where this appears

- [Chip War](/books/chip-war) — Moore's Law's origin and trajectory, his role at Intel, his "revolutionaries" remark, and his dismissal of Chang's TSMC plan.
- [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) — Moore's recollection of working under William Shockley's intense competitiveness.
