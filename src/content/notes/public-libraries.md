# Public Libraries

In [Our Towns](/books/our-towns), the public library is the single clearest "tell" of a healthy American community. James and Deborah Fallows found that after visiting dozens of libraries from Maine to Arizona, the places people once went mainly to find books had become "the heart and soul of American communities" — the best place to read the spirit, needs, and energy of a town. Columbus, run by Patrick Losinski, treats library users as "customers" rather than "patrons" and engraves "Open to All" above its grand entry as a "nearly sacred message." In Bend, the Deschutes librarians rebranded as "community librarians" and embedded themselves in local groups to build partnerships — which the Fallowses read as a living instance of Tocqueville's astonishment at how readily Americans form associations. Libraries also house free, open-to-all makerspaces, tying them to the [Maker Movement](/notes/maker-movement) thread that runs through the book.

[Matt Clancy](/notes/matt-clancy) supplies the empirical counterpart: he synthesizes evidence (Berkes & Nencka on libraries; Thompson & Hanley on [Wikipedia](/notes/wikipedia)) that new access to [Knowledge](/notes/knowledge) — public libraries, patent depositories, Wikipedia — produces *measurable* effects on patenting and citations. So across Kyle's notes libraries function both as civic infrastructure (the marker of a town on the rise) and as a documented input to innovation.

**Context:** Public libraries are tax-funded institutions offering free, open access to books, information, and increasingly programming and shared tools (e.g. makerspaces) to all residents. In the U.S. they are a longstanding piece of local civic infrastructure.

## Where this appears

- [Our Towns](/books/our-towns) — libraries as the "heart and soul of American communities," Columbus's "Open to All" / "customers" ethos, Deschutes's community-librarian model, and library makerspaces.
