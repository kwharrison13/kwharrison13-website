# Optimism

Across Kyle's wiki, optimism is framed as effortful — the harder, more valuable counterpart to the easy default of [Pessimism](/notes/pessimism). In the research for [The Coward's Conviction](/essays/the-cowards-conviction), optimism is the original framing of the essay: "people don't want to take the time to think about what could happen (optimism), so they default to not thinking at all," which reads as pessimism. The one-line thesis casts intellectual cowardice — "defaulting to pessimism, refusing to change your mind, and herding toward consensus" — as the easy path, while "optimism is hard work and [Conviction](/notes/conviction) is a virtue of heresy." Kyle pairs this with the Walt/Roy [Disney](/notes/roy-o-disney) dynamic, the visionary optimist balanced by the pragmatic skeptic, as the source for the essay's "place for pessimists" section.

In [Becoming](/books/becoming), [Michelle Obama](/notes/michelle-obama) gives optimism a civic and emotional meaning: she keeps herself "connected to a force that's larger and more potent than any one election, or leader, or news story — and that's optimism," which "for me, this is a form of faith, an antidote to fear." She ties it to showing up — "a handful of votes in every precinct" decides schools, health care, and war.

## Where this appears

- The Coward's Conviction - Research — optimism as the effortful default that pessimism avoids; the essay's originating frame.
- [Becoming](/books/becoming) — Michelle Obama's optimism as "a form of faith, an antidote to fear" and a spur to civic participation.
- [Pessimism](/notes/pessimism) — the sibling concept; the two are defined against each other in Kyle's notes.
