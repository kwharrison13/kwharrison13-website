# Why I Believe

**Why I Believe** is one of Kyle's personal study tags — a thread about holding belief on defensible, articulable grounds rather than mere feeling. It recurs wherever he wrestles with the difference between conviction and prejudice. In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), the trigger is Adler's standard for opinion: "We can do no more than opine that something is true when we have no evidence or reason… other than our personal feeling or prejudice. We can say that it is true and that we know it when we have **objective evidence that other reasonable men are likely to accept**." Kyle tags that passage #[Why I Believe](/notes/why-i-believe), linking it to [Mormon Apologetics](/notes/mormon-apologetics) and the idea that knowledge is defensible opinion.

The same demand for grounds shows up in his faith study. On [Alma 36](/notes/alma-36)–38, Kyle frames belief as an unavoidable choice — "We have to choose to make a [Leap of Faith](/books/leap-of-faith) one way or another; it's just a matter of which leap we want to make" — and pairs it with a [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias) check: "Are you actively working to build your faith? Or trying to justify your reasoning for not believing anymore?" Reading [Charlie Munger — USC Gould School of Law Commencement Address](/notes/charlie-munger-usc-gould-school-of-law-commencement-address), he applies [Inversion](/notes/inversion): where Darwin spent twenty years mastering "every possible counterargument before publishing," Kyle notes "Why I Believe — Inversion would be 'Why wouldn't I believe?'"

A relational dimension surfaces in his June 24th, 2021 — Conor White-Sullivan (Roam Research) notes: a real friend is "someone who will love you enough to listen to your crazy" and "debate the merits of your point, not just tell you you're crazy" — testing belief against someone who understands it well enough to disagree.
