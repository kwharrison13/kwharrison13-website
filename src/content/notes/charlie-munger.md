# Charlie Munger

[Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett)'s longtime partner at [Berkshire Hathaway](/notes/berkshire-hathaway) and the densest single node in Kyle's reading-and-note-taking. [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) is, in Kyle's words, the book where "almost every Munger sentence becomes a hook for an adjacent thought" — the most heavily annotated book in the library. Kyle organizes Munger's thinking into recurring threads: the [Latticework of Mental Models](/notes/latticework) and [Multidisciplinary Thinking](/notes/multidisciplinary-thinking) as a personal mandate ("you can't really say 'I'm not interested in math, physics, etc.'"); "whatever you are, be a good one" paired with "don't die with your music in you"; [Inversion](/notes/inversion) and [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) as core disciplines (Darwin writing down disconfirming evidence, Epictetus's "it is impossible to begin to learn that which one thinks one already knows"); and incentives as the engine of human behavior. Kyle's most distinctive move is reading Munger's secular psychology against an LDS framework — connecting his cognitive-bias catalog to Book of Mormon verses, [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith), [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley), and others, on the premise that "Munger is reading the same human-nature material scripture has been reading, with different vocabulary."

In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), Munger is the partner who pushed Buffett past Graham-style "cigar butt" bargains: "it was better to pay a fair price for a good business than a cut rate for a stinker." He insisted the Buffett style was "perfectly learnable," kept Berkshire's operating heads at a distance ("comparing oneself to Buffett would be hard on the human ego"), and shared the discipline of paying dividends rather than reinvesting when superior opportunities weren't available. Across Kyle's notes Munger's investment-management critique gets repeatedly applied to venture capital ([Venture Returns](/notes/venture-returns)) — "not everyone should be Harvard, and not everyone should be Sequoia."

**Context:** Charles T. Munger (1924–2023) was an American investor, lawyer, and vice chairman of Berkshire Hathaway, widely known for his "latticework of mental models" approach to decision-making and his collected speeches in Poor Charlie's Almanack.
