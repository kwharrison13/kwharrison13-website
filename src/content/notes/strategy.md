# Strategy

In [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war), strategy is the hidden, long-running layer beneath visible tactics — the layer that actually produces victory. Sun Tzu draws the distinction sharply: "All men can see the tactics whereby I conquer, but what none can see is the strategy out of which victory is evolved." Everyone can observe how a battle is won; what they cannot see is the long series of plans and combinations that preceded it. Tactics are surface and reproducible; strategy is the deeper architecture.

The book's treatment is anti-formulaic. Sun Tzu warns against pinning faith to abstract principles and insists that methods be regulated by the infinite variety of circumstances — "do not repeat the tactics which have gained you one victory." A commentator in the text underlines the point with the Wellington-at-Waterloo anecdote: because Wellington's plans depended entirely on Bonaparte's, he could not state them in advance. The page sits in the wiki's strategy-and-warfare cluster alongside [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) and [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works), and connects to a strategy-as-mental-models lineage running through [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) and [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack).

**Context:** Strategy, in its general sense, is the overarching plan for achieving long-term objectives by coordinating resources and choices, as distinct from tactics — the specific maneuvers used in a given engagement. [The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war), attributed to Sun Tzu (c. 5th century BCE), is among the earliest surviving texts to draw the distinction explicitly.
