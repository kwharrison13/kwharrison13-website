# Net Promoter Score

In [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era), Net Promoter Score (NPS) is the metric Kyle's notes are explicitly *skeptical* of. Leaning on OpenView's 2018 SaaS benchmarking, the book argues NPS is "not very predictive" of retention: every 10 additional NPS points correlates to only ~0.9% higher logo retention and ~0.55% higher net-dollar retention. The takeaway — "NPS doesn't have mystical powers and it should not be exalted" — is that NPS measures how many customers *say* they would refer a product, so a better move is to track what customers *actually* do: real referrals, referrals per customer, customer health scores, and product stickiness ([Net Revenue Retention](/notes/net-revenue-retention), retention curves).

The argument ties into the book's broader retention thesis: most SaaS churn is decided in the first week of use, which makes [Onboarding](/notes/onboarding) the central lever, and leading indicators (sticky features, integrations) beat lagging ones. NPS isn't dismissed entirely — "it's certainly fine to track NPS, but let's be realistic about what it tells us… and complement NPS with other metrics" — but it's demoted from a north-star to one weak signal among many.

**Context:** Net Promoter Score is a widely used customer-loyalty metric (created by Fred Reichheld / Bain) based on a single "how likely are you to recommend…" survey question scored 0–10, with respondents bucketed into promoters, passives, and detractors.
