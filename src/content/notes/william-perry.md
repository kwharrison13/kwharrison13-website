# William Perry

Former U.S. Secretary of Defense who appears across two of Kyle's books as both a nuclear-strategy authority and a pivotal figure in the history of American defense technology. In [Nuclear War — a Scenario](/books/nuclear-war-a-scenario) he is the expert voice on the "Bolt out of the Blue" surprise attack and on what happens to American governance after a strike: Perry warns that a nuclear bomb in Washington would likely decapitate the cabinet, force an emergency government, and end democracy as "military rule would take place" — rule he believes would be "almost impossible to undo."

In [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) he is cast as a builder rather than a forecaster. As the Pentagon's leading technologist until 1981 and later Secretary of Defense, Perry pushed stealth aircraft and precision-guided munitions — many of the weapons that debuted in the Gulf War — by classifying the programs so heavily that most of the bureaucracy did not know they existed, a workaround to a slow acquisition system. He also convened the 1993 "Last Supper," gathering defense-industry CEOs and urging them to consolidate as Cold War spending fell; the field collapsed from 107 major firms to five by the end of the 1990s. Kyle flags Perry as one of the champions of a "founder-led innovation era" alongside [Kelly Johnson](/notes/kelly-johnson) at Skunk Works and Bernard Schriever, while also identifying the Last Supper consolidation (paired with [Robert McNamara](/notes/robert-mcnamara)'s cost-cutting) as a root of the "managed monopolies" that followed.

**Context:** William J. Perry served as U.S. Secretary of Defense from 1994 to 1997 under President Clinton, after earlier roles as Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering. He is widely associated with the "offset strategy" of investing in advanced technology (stealth, precision weapons) to counter Soviet numerical superiority, and in later life became a prominent advocate for nuclear-weapons risk reduction.
