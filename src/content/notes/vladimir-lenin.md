# Vladimir Lenin

In [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia), Lenin appears as the ideological bedrock of the Soviet system the book traces. The book notes that "texts by Lenin and Marx were studied in every high school and university: they defined our approach to history and view of the world," with the battles of Soviet newspapers fought "with the aid of citations from their sacred texts" — establishing the Marxist-Leninist canon as the shared scripture of Soviet public life.

Lenin also supplies one of the book's load-bearing quotes on media power: "of all the kinds of arts, the most important for us is cinema." The book uses this to argue that in an idea-centric Russia, images could reach people's consciousness in a way words could not, and could "sell" more effectively because there was less noise in the marketplace than in America — a through-line for the book's thesis (see [Arkady Ostrovsky](/notes/arkady-ostrovsky)) that controlling the "means of mass information" is how power is built and held in Russia.

**Context:** Vladimir Lenin (1870–1924) led the Bolsheviks to power in the 1917 October Revolution and founded the Soviet state, becoming the canonical theorist of Marxism-Leninism.
