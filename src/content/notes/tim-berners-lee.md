# Tim Berners-Lee

The inventor of the World Wide Web, who appears across Kyle's reading as the patron of shared, public knowledge. In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Berners-Lee anchors the [Learning in Public](/notes/learning-in-public) thread (alongside Ward Cunningham's "post the wrong answer") with his lament that "a lot of the state of knowledge of the human race at the moment is on databases, often sitting in their computers, and actually, currently not shared" — the scientists who could cure cancer or understand the brain carry half-formed ideas they don't surface. It reinforces the book's claim that "an idea kept private is as good as one you never had."

In [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public) (Nadia Eghbal), Berners-Lee's original CERN proposal — envisioning "a pool of information... which could grow and evolve," provided "the method of storage must not place its own restraints on the information" — frames the web as a "Library of Alexandria bigger than we could have ever conceived," and seeds Eghbal's parallel to a Republic of Letters 2.0.

**Context:** Sir Tim Berners-Lee (b. 1955) is the British computer scientist who invented the World Wide Web in 1989 while at CERN, writing the first browser, server, and HTTP/HTML specifications, and later founded the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C).
