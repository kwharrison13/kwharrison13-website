# Social Contract

In [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), the social contract is the book's closing frame for the expert–citizen relationship. Tom Nichols writes that "in a democracy, the expert's service to the public is part of the social contract": citizens delegate decisions on countless issues to elected representatives and their expert advisers, while experts in turn ask that their work "be received in good faith by a public that has informed itself enough to make reasoned judgments." Kyle tags the passage in the margin with "social contract; educated electorate." The whole accountability loop only closes when voters can actually evaluate expertise — the wiki page on the book names the Newt Gingrich "I'll go with how people feel" line as the failure-mode marker, the moment the contract breaks down.

This connects to [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written), where [Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson) works the adjacent civic question of *e pluribus unum* — the common-ground problem of binding a plural public into a functioning democracy. Both books circle the same tension: a self-governing public depends on a mutual, good-faith compact between informed citizens and those they trust to know things, whether experts or fellow citizens.

**Context:** The social contract is a foundational idea in political philosophy — developed by Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau — holding that legitimate political authority rests on an implicit agreement among individuals (and between the governed and those who govern) to cooperate for mutual benefit.
