# Climate Poverty

"Climate poverty" is the moral center of [Bill Gates](/notes/bill-gates)'s argument in [How To Avoid a Climate Disaster](/books/how-to-avoid-a-climate-disaster) — the observation that the people least responsible for greenhouse emissions will bear the heaviest costs of climate change. Kyle's note distills it sharply: "Irresponsible energy consumption by richer countries has mortgaged the progress of poorer countries and used the proceeds for their own gain leaving poor countries to bear the burden of climate change."

The book's recurring example is Bangladesh — a poor country making good progress out of poverty, but beset by cyclones, storm surges, and river floods that now routinely put 20–30% of the country underwater, wiping out crops and homes. The deeper tension the notes capture is that the same poor countries need *more* energy to lift their people up (as China did by building coal plants cheaply, driving down the cost of a coal plant by 75% and lifting hundreds of millions out of poverty), yet must somehow get that energy without adding emissions. Small-scale solar can charge phones, but won't deliver the cheap, always-available baseload these economies need to attract manufacturing and grow. Gates's framing ties climate poverty to his case for cheap, carbon-free, reliable power — including [Nuclear Energy](/notes/nuclear-energy) — as the only path that serves both decarbonization and development.

**Context:** "Climate poverty" describes the disproportionate burden climate change places on low-income populations and nations, who have contributed least to emissions but are most exposed to its harms and least equipped to adapt — a central concern in climate-justice and development economics.
