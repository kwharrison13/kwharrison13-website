# Incentives

Incentives is one of Kyle's most-reused [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) frames: human behavior is downstream of incentive design, and incentive design is "harder than it looks." In [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) it is one of the eight recurring threads — FedEx shift-pay, sales-rep comp, "gaming systems" — and Munger's blunt formulation, "as usual in human affairs, what determines the behavior are incentives for the decision maker… what makes sense for the investor is different from what makes sense for the manager." Kyle's own margin note there asks the corollary for service businesses (consulting, banking, accounting): "What should the incentive structure be? How do you incentivize honesty and morality?" The [Upton Sinclair](/notes/upton-sinclair) line — "It is difficult to get a man to understand something when his salary depends on his not understanding it" — is the page's recurring shorthand for motivated reasoning, and it shows up inside Munger's "Quant Tech" parable in [Charlie Munger: The Great Financial Scandal of 2003](/notes/charlie-munger-the-great-financial-scandal-of-2003), where Kyle's note is "What are you optimizing for?"

The concept's clearest worked example is [Catastrophic Care](/books/catastrophic-care): Goldhill's "core argument is that backward economic incentives reward bad behavior" — "bad behavior is often rewarded with additional revenue, and efficiency is penalized with less" — because the "Surrogates" (insurers, Medicare) sever the consumer's disciplining role. Its constructive mirror image is [George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy), the "classic alignment-of-incentives case study" — premium pay and piece work to "reward industry over time-wages that slow a man down," the same owner-mentality logic Kyle tracks in [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters). The frame also reaches into strategy ([The Art of War](/books/the-art-of-war) / [Sunzi](/notes/sunzi)) and Munger's other talks on academic economics and elementary worldly wisdom.

**Context:** "Incentives" is a foundational mental model in the Munger/behavioral-economics tradition: the observation that to predict or change behavior you must look first at how the actor is rewarded or penalized. Munger's maxim — "Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives" — is its canonical statement.
