# Mass Incarceration

Mass incarceration enters the wiki through [Evicted](/books/evicted), where [Matthew Desmond](/notes/matthew-desmond) uses it as the male analogue to eviction. His central parallel: "If incarceration had come to define the lives of men from impoverished black neighborhoods, eviction was shaping the lives of women. Poor black men were locked up. Poor black women were locked out." The two systems are gendered and racialized mirror images of the same machinery of exclusion.

The mechanism Desmond highlights is how a criminal record compounds disadvantage in the housing market. Even landlords who "tried to avoid discriminating by setting clear criteria and holding all applicants to the same standards" reproduced inequality, "because black men were disproportionately incarcerated and black women disproportionately evicted, uniformly denying housing to applicants with recent criminal or conviction records still had an incommensurate impact on African Americans." So neutral-seeming screening rules transmit the effects of mass incarceration into [Racial Segregation](/notes/racial-segregation) in housing — while a white couple with felony convictions, evictions, and an outstanding warrant gets approved "because they were white." The concept links Desmond's housing argument to the broader cycle of [Poverty](/notes/poverty) reproduction.

**Context:** "Mass incarceration" refers to the dramatic rise in U.S. imprisonment since the 1970s, which gave the United States the world's highest incarceration rate and fell disproportionately on Black men — the subject of works like Michelle Alexander's *The New Jim Crow*.

## Where this appears
