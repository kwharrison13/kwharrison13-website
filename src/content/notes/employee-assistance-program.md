# Employee Assistance Program

The Employee Assistance Program (EAP) is the legacy employer-benefits model that Kyle's [Behavioral Health](/notes/behavioral-health) sourcing notes repeatedly treat as the incumbent to displace. On the April 30th, 2020 — Jason Ockey (Tava Health) call, Tava Health's CEO noted EAPs are free to employees but utilization is "~1% or less," defying everything he knew about price elasticity — i.e., the free price tag doesn't drive usage. [Spring Health](/notes/spring-health) is positioned directly against this: it sells to large employers (Adobe, Pfizer, Gap, Pepsi) "often replacing or augmenting EAPs," and its key differentiation is that, "unlike status-quo EAPs that just match patient to provider," Spring locks in rates AND takes risk on outcomes — performance guarantees, shared risk, and reporting on "who used it and who got better." Across both sources, the EAP is the baseline of low-engagement, outcome-blind mental-health benefits that newer outcomes-based platforms aim to beat.

**Context:** An Employee Assistance Program (EAP) is an employer-sponsored benefit offering employees free, confidential short-term counseling and referral services for mental health, substance use, and personal issues. EAPs are widespread but historically suffer from very low utilization and little outcome measurement.
