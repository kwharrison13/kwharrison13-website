# John Hart-Smith

A Douglas Aircraft engineer who, in [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), serves as the analytical conscience who diagnosed [Boeing](/notes/boeing)'s structural failure mode before it played out. At around sixty, Hart-Smith wrote a paper titled "Out-Sourced Profits—the Cornerstone of Successful Subcontracting," fifteen densely worded pages drawing on his own experience that parceling out construction of the DC-10 had impoverished Douglas while enriching its suppliers. His core argument: outsourcing is never simple — design specifications have to be *more* precise because omissions trigger costly legal disputes, the extra oversight overhead is never counted, and those extra costs perversely make the outsourcing company look less efficient than the suppliers it hands business to, a vicious cycle that only encourages more of the same.

In Peter Robison's account, Hart-Smith's warning is the diagnosis Boeing ignored. When Stonecipher sold off parts-making operations (including a Wichita factory Boeing had owned for seventy-five years employing 7,200 people) to improve RONA numbers, it made everyone's jobs harder — a last-minute part or a process improvement was no longer a call with a colleague but a negotiation with lawyers, procurement-chain executives, and HR, exactly the overhead hassles Hart-Smith had warned about. He stands in the book alongside [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch) (the GE template) and [Dennis Muilenburg](/notes/dennis-muilenburg) as a key figure in the [Financialization](/notes/financialization) story of how a great engineering culture broke.
