# Erich Fromm

Philosopher-psychiatrist quoted by [Aldous Huxley](/notes/aldous-huxley) in [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited). Huxley enlists Fromm to argue that mental "normality" can itself be pathological: Fromm holds that contemporary Western society, "in spite of its material, intellectual and political progress, is increasingly less conducive to mental health," tending to turn the individual "into an automaton." In the line Huxley draws out, the truly hopeless victims of mental illness are "those who appear to be most normal" — people so well-adjusted to an abnormal society that their human voice "has been silenced so early in their lives" that they never even develop symptoms. Fromm reframes neurotic symptoms not as the enemy but as a sign that "the forces of life which strive for integration and happiness are still fighting." The citation supports Huxley's broader warning about conformity and the engineered contentment of a mass society.

**Context:** Erich Fromm (1900–1980) was a German-born psychoanalyst and social philosopher associated with the Frankfurt School, known for works such as *Escape from Freedom* and *The Sane Society*, which examined how modern social structures shape — and distort — individual psychology.

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited) — quoted by Huxley on how a "profoundly abnormal society" produces well-adjusted automatons, and on neurotic symptoms as evidence that the drive toward health is still fighting.
