# Multiple Intelligences

The idea that people learn through different channels and capacities — and the practical lens Kyle uses to ask which *medium* fits which learner. [Why Books Don't Work](/notes/why-books-don-t-work) is the page most explicitly read through this frame: it heads a "Mediums of Education" section and pairs the idea with [Self-Reflection](/notes/self-reflection), on the premise that "the ways we learn are not nearly well understood" and each person should deliberately identify their own learning preferences. [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) tags the concept against the "neuromyths" research — specifically rejecting the popular notion of a single optimal learning channel (visual/auditory): the reality is that "people actually use as many channels to learn as they can access," with [David Eagleman](/notes/david-eagleman) cited that learning is most effective when audio, visual, and touch are combined and most durable when you have to teach someone else.

In [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) the concept is associated with [Jean Piaget](/notes/jean-piaget) in the piece's developmental framing of how children build understanding. [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required) applies it differently — to the limits of riddle/quiz hiring and personality assessments, with Kyle's note flagging that such instruments only remind you of traits already observed in person, not a reliable read on a single kind of intelligence. Across these sources the through-line is skepticism toward any one-size measure of ability, in favor of matching mode of learning (or evaluation) to the individual.

**Context:** "Multiple intelligences" is most associated with psychologist Howard Gardner's 1983 theory (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic), proposing that intelligence is not a single general factor.
