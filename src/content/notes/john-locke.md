# John Locke

John Locke recurs across Kyle's notes as the Enlightenment theorist of natural rights whose ideas are "tattooed all over the Declaration of Independence." In [Why You're Christian](/notes/why-you-re-christian), Locke is the linchpin of the argument that Western law and human rights rest on Christianity, not just the Enlightenment: his theory of natural rights "assumes God owns us," so "human equality is self-evident only if you assume, as Locke did, that God has given us the natural rights that modern Americans take for granted." The piece pairs him with [John Adams](/books/john-adams)'s claim that the Constitution "was made only for a moral and religious People."

In [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written), Locke appears as a leader of the [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) — the Enlightenment milieu of Locke, Hume, Voltaire, Franklin, and Jefferson — and as the author of the *Second Treatise*, where he argued humans create private property by mixing their labor with nature, subject to the famous **Lockean Proviso**: only "where there is enough, and as good, left in common for others." He is also grouped among the Deist-leaning thinkers of the eighteenth-century Age of Reason.

**Context:** John Locke (1632–1704) was an English philosopher and physician, a founding figure of empiricism and classical liberalism. His *Two Treatises of Government* and theory of natural rights (life, liberty, property) heavily influenced the American founders and the Declaration of Independence.
