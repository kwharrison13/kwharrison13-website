# Efficient Market Theory

Efficient Market Theory (EMT) is the academic doctrine — "highly fashionable, indeed, almost holy scripture in academic circles during the 1970s" — that analyzing stocks is useless because all public information is already appropriately reflected in their prices. As [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) paraphrases it in the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), its corollary is that "someone throwing darts at the stock tables" could pick a portfolio as good as the brightest analyst's. Buffett's core rebuttal is a logical one: observing correctly that the market was *frequently* efficient, EMT's proponents went on to conclude *incorrectly* that it was *always* efficient — and "the difference between these propositions is night and day." He points to the 63-year record where the general market returned just under 10% while a 20% rate would have turned $1,000 into $97 million, evidence its adherents "never seemed interested in," likening the refusal to recant to theologians reluctant to "demystify the priesthood."

In [Buffett — The Biography](/books/buffett-the-biography), EMT is named as one of the concepts Buffett "attacked for confusing 'frequently efficient' with 'always efficient,'" and the book records his lament that scarcely a business school used [Benjamin Graham](/notes/ben-graham)'s texts — "professionals and academicians talk of efficient markets, dynamic hedging and betas" instead of price and value. [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger), in [Charlie Munger: Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics), makes the same point from Berkshire's results: the firm's "whole record has been achieved without paying one ounce of attention to the efficient market theory in its hard form," nor to its descendant the [Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)](/notes/capital-asset-pricing-model-capm). He calls it tooth-fairy thinking to believe one could outperform the market by seven points a year merely by buying high-volatility stocks. For Buffett, the practical lesson is that an investor needs only two well-taught courses — how to value a business and how to think about market prices — not beta or modern portfolio theory.

**Context:** The Efficient Market Hypothesis, formalized by Eugene Fama in the 1960s–70s, holds that asset prices fully reflect available information, making it impossible to consistently beat the market on a risk-adjusted basis. Buffett and Munger are its most prominent practitioner-critics, arguing that the long-run records of value investors (the "Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville") refute the strong form of the theory.
