# Intrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic motivation is one of the load-bearing cross-corpus threads in Kyle's library, recurring across child-development, habit-formation, and open-source reading with a single consistent claim: **intrinsic drive endures where extrinsic reward decays.** [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) states the thesis bluntly — "rewards are counterproductive to intrinsic motivation," and the way to build a healthy dopamine system is "encourage them to work hard at what they love." [The Parents' Guide to Boys](/books/the-parents-guide-to-boys) echoes it: "intrinsic motivation lasts whereas extrinsic motivation, like money, is temporary," and boys "learn well what they like." [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) gives the most quoted formulation — "the ultimate form of intrinsic motivation is when a habit becomes part of your identity" — connecting the concept to [Identity Formation](/notes/identity-formation) and [Flow](/notes/flow).

The same principle scales from the individual to the commons. In [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), Nadia Eghbal calls intrinsic motivation "the currency of the commons" — open-source contributors do the work "because they want to" — and it is one of [Yochai Benkler](/notes/yochai-benkler)'s necessary conditions for commons-based peer production (alongside modular tasks and low coordination costs). [David Heinemeier Hansson](/notes/david-heinemeier-hansson) is cited there warning that "external, expected rewards diminish the intrinsic motivation" of contributors, turning a community of peers into a transactional terminal. Kyle's [Open Source](/notes/open-source) concept page carries the same point: intrinsic motivation explains why contributors create and why maintenance — lacking that intrinsic pull — is harder to sustain.

**Context:** Intrinsic motivation is a concept from self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan) describing behavior driven by inherent satisfaction rather than external rewards or pressures. A well-known finding is the "overjustification effect," where adding extrinsic rewards can crowd out pre-existing intrinsic interest.
