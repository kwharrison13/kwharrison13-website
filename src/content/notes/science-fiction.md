# Science Fiction

For Kyle, science fiction is the genre at the heart of [Historical Futurism](/essays/historical-futurism) — the thesis that "the vast majority of science fiction is dystopian, and that shortage of optimistic dreaming has a downstream cost: to invent the future, people first need to be inspired by hopeful visions of it." The essay (subtitled *"Using Science Fiction to Invent the Future"*) treats fiction as the front end of a feedback loop — foundational futures → a literature of dreaming → dreamers → forecasting → building — a cousin of [open-source knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) he calls *open-source imagination*. Saved links in his notes, like the Guardian piece on writers imagining Palestine in 2048, reinforce the idea of fiction as a tool for imagining a real place's future.

The genre also surfaces as a recurring lens across his book reading. [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) connects it to invention through Philip K. Dick's "Autofac" stories about self-replicating factories — fiction as a cultural mirror of von Neumann's real work on automata. [2034 — A Novel of the Next World War](/books/2034-a-novel-of-the-next-world-war) is itself near-future speculative fiction "in the tradition of fiction-as-strategic-foresight." And [Josh Wolfe](/notes/josh-wolfe) of [Lux Capital](/notes/lux-capital) recurs through his "science fact vs. science fiction" framing, tying the genre into the broader [Techno-Optimism](/notes/techno-optimism) conversation. Other touchpoints in the corpus include [Stories of Your Life and Others](/books/story-of-your-life), [The Time Machine](/books/the-time-machine), [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world), and [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century).

**Context:** Science fiction is a genre of speculative fiction that explores imagined futures, technologies, and worlds, often used as a vehicle for examining the social and ethical consequences of scientific and technological change.
