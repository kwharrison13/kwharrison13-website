# Multidisciplinary Thinking

The conviction that the big ideas worth having are scattered across disciplines, and that the thinker's job is to grab them all rather than stay penned inside one field. It is the dominant thesis of [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), where Kyle reads [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s "latticework of mental models" as a personal mandate: "You can't really say 'I'm not interested in math, physics, etc.'" In [Charlie Munger: Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics), Munger names the failure mode directly via [Alfred North Whitehead](/notes/alfred-north-whitehead)'s "fatal unconnectedness of academic disciplines" — each professor ignorant of the other disciplines' models — and contrasts it with his own habit: "I couldn't stand reaching for a small idea in my own discipline when there was a big idea right over the fence in somebody else's discipline. So I just grabbed in all directions for the big ideas that would really work." His complaint about economists is that they "can't synthesize worth a damn."

[Range](/books/range) supplies the empirical, generalist case for the same instinct: breadth, analogical reasoning, and delayed specialization beat early hyperspecialization, especially in "wicked" domains where feedback is delayed. David Epstein's research line — "breadth of training predicts breadth of transfer" — frames the "T-shaped" or "π-shaped" integrator who builds a narrative and pulls in deep specialists to fill the trunk of the T, and notes that breadth "becomes increasingly important" as ambiguity and uncertainty rise in systems problems. [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) is the biographical archetype: Walter Isaacson opens the book saying his subject embodies "the ability to make connections across disciplines — arts and sciences, humanities and technology" as "a key to innovation, imagination, and genius," with Leonardo discerning "patterns across disciplines" as the engine of his leaps.

Across Kyle's wiki the concept clusters tightly with [Latticework of Mental Models](/notes/latticework), [Polymath](/notes/polymath), [Curiosity](/notes/curious), and [Big Important Ideas](/notes/big-important-ideas) — the constellation of ideas about grabbing big models across fences and compounding them into worldly wisdom.

**Context:** Multidisciplinary (or interdisciplinary) thinking is the practice of drawing concepts and methods from many fields — physics, biology, economics, psychology, history — to reason about a problem, rather than relying on a single discipline's frame. It is closely associated with Charlie Munger's "latticework of mental models."
