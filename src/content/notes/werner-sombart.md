# Werner Sombart

Werner Sombart supplies one of the load-bearing arguments in [Double Entry](/books/double-entry): his claim, from *Der moderne Kapitalismus* (1902), that "it is simply impossible to imagine capitalism without double-entry bookkeeping; they are like form and content." The book leans on Sombart to make the case that [Capitalism](/notes/capitalism) and double-entry [Accounting](/notes/accounting) are so intimately connected it is hard to tell which was cause and which effect — Sombart himself wondered "whether capitalism has procured in double-entry book-keeping a tool which activates its forces, or whether double-entry book-keeping has first given rise to capitalism out of its own spirit."

The book extends Sombart's thesis geographically: if double entry made possible the explicit pursuit of profit and accumulation of wealth, that helps explain why the northern Italian cities where it first appeared — Florence, Milan, Venice, Urbino — amassed such riches in the 14th and 15th centuries, money their rulers (the Medici, the Sforza, the doges) poured into the courts, civic buildings, and cathedrals that some argue produced the Renaissance.

**Context:** Werner Sombart (1863–1941) was a German economist and sociologist, a major figure in the historical school of economics, best known for the multi-volume *Der moderne Kapitalismus* and for coining or popularizing terms such as "capitalism" in academic usage.
