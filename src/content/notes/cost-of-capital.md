# Cost of Capital

The price of the money a business uses to operate — and a recurring lens across Kyle's reading for judging whether growth is actually creating value. In [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Buffett frames insurance "float" as a cost-of-capital question: he computes a cost of funds for the insurance operation, and the business has positive value for shareholders only when that cost (including the tax penalty) is below what alternative sources of funds would charge. Across 19 of 24 years Berkshire developed insurance funds at a cost below the government's — a negative or near-zero cost of capital that becomes a structural advantage. Kyle's notes generalize the point: "There is always a [Cost of Capital](/notes/cost-of-capital), even if just 'time spent'," and he imagines "high flying Silicon Valley companies (e.g. [WeWork](/notes/wework))" looking very different "if compensation had been tied to return on incremental cash burn vs. 'growth' without a [Cost of Capital](/notes/cost-of-capital) context."

In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), cost of capital appears as a *strategic weapon*. Jerry Sanders of AMD saw Silicon Valley's biggest disadvantage as expensive credit — Japanese chipmakers borrowed at 6–7% while US firms paid 18%+, even as Fed rates hit 21.5% fighting inflation in the 1980s. Because each chip generation demanded new fabs every ~two years, the cost of credit was decisive, and Kyle flags the open question of what happens "when the government massively subsidiz[es] the cost of capital for critical industries." The same concept anchors [The Puritans of Venture Capital](/essays/the-puritans-of-venture-capital), where the value chain of capital runs from cottage-scale early investors to AUM "Capital Agglomerators" who supply the *low-cost-of-capital downstream* that cash-burning startups depend on.

**Context:** Cost of capital is the required return a firm must earn to justify a project or investment — typically a blend of the cost of debt and the cost of equity (often estimated as a weighted-average cost of capital, or WACC). It functions as the hurdle rate below which deploying capital destroys value rather than creating it.
