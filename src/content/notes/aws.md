# AWS

Amazon Web Services is treated across Kyle's corpus as both a foundational tech-history case study and the literal substrate of modern SaaS economics. [Amazon Unbound](/books/amazon-unbound) tells its origin story most fully: [Jeff Bezos](/notes/jeff-bezos) micromanaged a business "barely understandable to many of Amazon's own employees and board members" — selling raw computing power online — that became "arguably more important than even the iPhone" as the enabler of the post-recession startup boom (Uber, Airbnb, Dropbox ran on it). The book frames AWS as "the cloud business more important than even the iPhone that funds Amazon's bets and trains Alexa's speech models," run by [Andy Jassy](/notes/andy-jassy) on Bezos's "good intentions don't work, but mechanisms do" rituals; revenue grew from a doubted "maybe $1 billion someday" to $45.4B in 2020.

In the SaaS-economics thread, AWS is the reason software gross margins are so high: per [Todd McKinnon — Creating and Defining a New Market Category](/notes/todd-mckinnon-creating-and-defining-a-new-market-category) and the [Subscription Economy](/notes/subscription-economy) page, running a service on AWS reduces COGS to the cost of the service "so we don't have capital allocation," yielding 75%+ gross margins. In diligence work AWS shows up as the assumed default infrastructure layer — Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 uses AWS bills as a carbon-accounting input (emissions vary by region); the Noname Security - Diligence and Starburst - Diligence dossiers debate API-security and data-query positioning relative to AWS's own services (API Gateway, Athena, S3, Redshift), with one note that "AWS suffers from bloat... their service catalogue is incomprehensible." The Wiz.io pre-brief cites AWS's 1M+ customers and ~$40B revenue (growing 30%) and its fragmentation as the security problem Wiz attacks. VC Contagion - Research flags early usage-based softness at AWS and [Snowflake](/notes/snowflake-snow) as a tell for inflated software TAMs.

**Context:** Amazon Web Services, launched 2006, is Amazon's cloud-computing division and the market-leading public cloud provider, offering compute, storage, and managed services (EC2, S3, Lambda, etc.).
