# Jack Morton

The transistor's development chief at [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs), as portrayed in [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory). Kyle's notes make Morton's dictum — "if you haven't sold anything you haven't innovated" — the load-bearing definition of [Innovation](/notes/innovation) for the whole book: an idea is not an innovation until it has been manufactured in substantial quantities AND found a market. Morton shepherded the transistor through the Labs' development process until it began infiltrating the mainstream economy and was licensed beyond Ma Bell (to Raytheon, RCA, GE). Yet The Idea Factory also frames him as a cautionary figure — even Morton "especially" misjudged the future, failing (unlike [Robert Noyce](/notes/bob-noyce) and Jack Kilby) to foresee the integrated circuit despite Bell Labs holding all the constituent processes.

**Context:** Jack A. Morton (1913–1971) was a Bell Labs engineer and executive who led transistor development and later vice-presided over the Labs' electronics technology, known for his writing on the management of innovation.

## Where this appears

- [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) — Morton's manufacture-and-sell definition of innovation anchors the book's view of the idea → discovery → invention → innovation chain; also cited as a case of even great minds misjudging the future of the integrated circuit.
