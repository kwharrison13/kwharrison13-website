# David Graeber

Anthropologist at the London School of Economics, best known in Kyle's corpus for the "[Bullshit Jobs](/notes/bullshit-jobs)" thesis. [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman)'s [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) builds directly on Graeber's essay "On the Phenomenon of Bullshit Jobs," quoting his analysis that "innumerable people spend their entire working lives doing jobs they consider to be pointless" — telemarketers, HR managers, PR advisors, administrative roles — jobs "even the people doing them admit are, in essence, superfluous." That thesis anchors Bregman's value-vs-pay inversion and sits inside the book's broader argument cluster alongside [Automation](/notes/automation), [The Precariat](/notes/the-precariat) ([Guy Standing](/notes/guy-standing)), and [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) as the policy response.

Graeber also surfaces in [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation), where [Byrne Hobart](/notes/byrne-hobart) and [Tobias Huber](/notes/tobias-huber) cite his essay "Of Flying Cars and the Declining Rate of Profit" on the idea that we now build better *simulations* of the future rather than the future itself — fitting Boom's stagnation diagnosis.

**Context:** David Graeber (1961–2020) was an American anthropologist and anarchist activist, a professor at the London School of Economics, and the author of *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* and *Bullshit Jobs: A Theory*. He was an early figure in the Occupy Wall Street movement and is often credited with the slogan "We are the 99%."

## Where this appears

- [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) — his "On the Phenomenon of Bullshit Jobs" essay anchors [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman)'s value-vs-pay argument.
- [Bullshit Jobs](/notes/bullshit-jobs) — the thesis he originated, that a growing share of jobs are pointless even to those doing them.
- [The Precariat](/notes/the-precariat) — part of the same automation / displaced-work argument cluster.
- [Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) — cited via "Of Flying Cars and the Declining Rate of Profit" on simulating the future instead of building it.
