# Gospel For Asia

In [Law of the Harvest](/books/law-of-the-harvest), Gospel For Asia (GFA) is the flagship counter-example of low-budget, high-volume missionary work. The book reports that GFA's missionaries in India organize "an average of six new congregations per day, or two and a half times as many new congregations as the LDS church organizes across the entire world" — and that GFA and the Seventh-Day Adventists each organize more new congregations in India every four days than the LDS Church has in the country in 150 years. The striking figure for Kyle's notes is the cost structure: GFA missionaries live "on a shoestring budget of $90 to $150 per month." Rather than repeatedly revisiting existing members for referrals, they are "constantly breaking new ground" — each two-missionary van travels to 12–15 remote villages a day with bullhorns, VCR equipment, and the "Jesus" film, while others work on bicycles with lanterns for evening meetings, sustaining vigorous tract and Bible distribution.

The book frames GFA as a model of native missionary recruitment and training (citing founder K.P. Yohannan's free book *Revolution in World Missions* as worthy of study), and uses it as a recurring benchmark for exponential, "church without walls" growth that the author contrasts with slower LDS congregational planting.

**Context:** Gospel For Asia is a missionary organization founded by K.P. Yohannan (now associated with GFA World / Believers Eastern Church) that funds and trains native missionaries across South Asia. It later drew controversy over its financial practices, though that is outside the scope of these notes.
