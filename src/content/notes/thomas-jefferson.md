# Thomas Jefferson

Across Kyle's reading, Jefferson recurs as **two things at once: the patron saint of the educated electorate and a study in self-deception**. [American Sphinx](/books/american-sphinx) ([Joseph J. Ellis](/notes/joseph-j-ellis)) is the deepest portrait — Jefferson governed "almost entirely from his writing desk," "not a profound political thinker" but "an utterly brilliant political rhetorician and visionary" whose genius was to "articulate irreconcilable human urges at a sufficiently abstract level to mask their mutual exclusiveness." Ellis renders him as "a flawed creature" combining massive learning with extraordinary naïveté and "piercing insights into others with daunting powers of self-deception." His pro-urban note — city-dwellers "can act in a body readily and at all times" — is the book's one captured observation, anticipating digital cohesion-at-distance.

Jefferson is the founding authority for the **[Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate)** thread that runs through [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), and [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death): "a people who mean to be their own Governors must arm themselves with the power which knowledge gives," and his "elementary republics of the wards" presupposed informed local participation as the unit of self-rule. The flip side appears in [The Greatest Sentence Ever Written](/books/the-greatest-sentence-ever-written) ([Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson)), which casts Jefferson's "natural aristocracy" — "raking" twenty of the best geniuses "from the rubbish annually" in his Notes on the State of Virginia — as the prefiguration of today's credentialed meritocratic elite, contrasted against [Benjamin Franklin](/notes/benjamin-franklin)'s balance-seeking ideal of "true merit." [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s USC commencement cites him too: "An educated citizenry is a vital requisite for our survival as a free people."

**Context:** Thomas Jefferson (1743–1826) was the principal author of the Declaration of Independence, the third U.S. President, founder of the University of Virginia, and a central Enlightenment figure whose legacy is complicated by his lifelong ownership of enslaved people.
