# Malcom McLean

In [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes), Sönke Ahrens uses Malcom McLean as the marquee example of how big transformations start from a deceptively simple idea taken seriously. A trucking-company owner and former trucker who kept getting stuck in traffic on crowded coastal highways, McLean's "circumvent the congestion" idea was just to ship parts of a lorry rather than drive the whole lorry — and when he converted the tanker *Ideal X* to carry 58 containers and set it sailing on 26 April 1956, he had no inkling the [Shipping Container](/notes/shipping-container) would reshape world trade, let some nations rise and others fall, make century-old professions redundant, and pave the way for Asia's economic ascent.

Kyle reads this through a religious-intellectual lens in the book notes, pairing the container with Alma 37:6 ("by small and simple things are great things brought to pass") and Alma 37:46 ("do not let us be slothful because of the easiness of the way") under the [Simplify and Intensify](/notes/simplify-and-intensify) heading.

**Context:** Malcom McLean (1913–2001) was an American businessman widely credited as the father of modern container shipping; his 1956 *Ideal X* voyage is conventionally dated as the birth of the intermodal container revolution. (His name is sometimes spelled "Malcolm.")
