# Content Overload

"Content Overload" comes from [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy), where it names the problem that the sheer volume of digital information has outpaced our capacity to learn from it. The book quantifies the scale — the digital universe "at least doubling every two years" (a 50-fold growth from 2010 to 2020) — and argues that "as a consequence of this content overload, we have become overwhelmed by the constant surge of information, impatient in our need for information 'right now,' and distracted by the never-ending stream of websites, apps, and video clips." The cost is that "we spend so much time trying to sift through information that only a fraction of a typical working week is left over for learning."

In the book's framing, content overload is the obstacle that the [Skills Quotient](/notes/skills-quotient), [Continuous Learning](/notes/continuous-learning), and [The Value Way](/notes/the-value-way) are meant to overcome — the reason curation, filtering, and deliberate learning practices matter more, not less, in a world of abundant information. A related prescription in the notes: before creating content, check whether it already exists, so effort goes to what's genuinely additive rather than duplicating existing material.

## Where this appears

- [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — the source of the term; content overload is the diagnosed condition that leaves little working time for actual learning amid an exponentially growing digital universe.
