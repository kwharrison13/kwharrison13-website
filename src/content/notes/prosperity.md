# Prosperity

In Kyle's research the word carries a religious-economic charge, not just a financial one: the question of how worldly wealth relates to righteousness, grace, and work. It surfaces through the [Protestant Work Ethic](/notes/protestant-work-ethic) thread — [Max Weber](/notes/max-weber)'s thesis that an "emphasis on grace makes you want to demonstrate the hand of God through prosperity," accumulating wealth as a sign of divine favor. Kyle connects this to a Latter-day Saint analog: the [Book of Mormon](/notes/book-of-mormon) promise that "if you're righteous you'll prosper," and the live question of "why do so many Mormons want to be wealthy."

The same note draws a sharp line between genuine prosperity (the fruit of diligence and [Hard Work](/notes/hard-work)) and what Kyle flags as **"fake prosperity"** — the get-rich-quick schemes that mimic the outward signs of blessing without the underlying work or righteousness. Prosperity here is therefore a contested concept: a possible token of grace, a possible idol, and a thing easily counterfeited.

## Where this appears

- [Protestant Work Ethic](/notes/protestant-work-ethic) — the religious roots of the work ethic, where prosperity is read as a sign of grace and contrasted with the "fake prosperity" of get-rich-quick schemes.
