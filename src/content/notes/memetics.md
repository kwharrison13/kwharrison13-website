# Memetics

In Kyle's wiki, **memetics is the study of ideas in terms of their efficacy in spreading through groups** — the virality of ideas, deliberately distinguished from [Mimetics](/notes/mimetics) (the human tendency to mimic *people*). [Dan McMurtrie](/notes/dan-mcmurtrie) draws the distinction precisely in the [Tyro Partners](/notes/tyro-partners) Q2 2021 letter: "Mimetics… broadly describes the human tendency to mimic one another. There is a related but distinct concept of Memetics, which is the study of ideas in terms of their efficacy in spreading through groups." That same letter supplies the four-level memetic model Kyle leans on — Content → Reference → Advocacy → Community — and argues markets are now "metagames" where beliefs function as useful constraints.

Memetics is one of the core lenses behind Kyle's essays. [The Meme Economy](/essays/the-meme-economy) research treats it alongside [Mimetic Theory](/notes/mimetic-theory) under the thesis that "belief precedes truth, and that has *always* been true" — the internet didn't create the meme economy, it merely accelerated it, with [Silicon Valley Bank](/notes/silicon-valley-bank)'s social-media-induced bank run as the case study. raw/clippings/Peter Thiel's Religion traces the deep root: if [Rene Girard](/notes/rene-girard)'s Mimetic Theory is correct that desire is imitative, then memes (Greek *mimeme*, "that which is imitated") are how those imitated desires propagate at internet speed. The concept also threads through [The Storytelling of Investing](/essays/the-storytelling-of-investing) (distilling a worldview into a "Dawkinsian meme" earns enduring mindshare), [The State of Startup Media](/essays/the-state-of-startup-media) (memetics as "the memes of production"), and [The Hits Business](/essays/the-hits-business) (A24's command of internet culture and fandom).

**Context:** Memetics derives from Richard Dawkins's 1976 coinage of "meme" in *The Selfish Gene* — a unit of cultural transmission that propagates and evolves analogously to a gene, subject to selection pressures of fidelity, fecundity, and longevity.
