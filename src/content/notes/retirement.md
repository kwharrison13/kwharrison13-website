# Retirement

Retirement sits at the center of a reading cluster Kyle has been collecting — early retirement and the FIRE movement, savings-rate strategies, and the regrets and failures of those who retired young — tightly linked to [Financial Independence](/notes/financial-independence) and [Personal Finance](/notes/personal-finance). Most of the grounding is a batch of saved bookmarks (June 18, 2026) on the practical end of the topic: "how much money do you need to retire," "5 things I regret not doing before retiring early at 34," an early retiree's money spreadsheet and habit-tracking strategy, FIRE-method case studies, and "a 42-year-old millionaire [who] failed at early retirement." A [Remote Work](/notes/remote-work) thread runs through it too — MarketWatch on retiring with remote work — connecting where and how one can afford to live to when one can stop.

The one synthesized source-claim comes from [Recession Proof](/books/recession-proof) ([Jason Schenker](/notes/jason-schenker)), whose retirement thesis is that "the past is the future": as a matter of necessity we'll become more family-oriented, "rely less on the government and more on our children, like we did for most of our history as a species." Schenker pairs that with a "third rule" — retire at the right time — alongside buying index bundles and buying in bust times.

**Context:** "Retirement" traditionally means leaving the workforce in one's 60s on pensions and savings; the FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early) reframes it as reaching a savings threshold (often ~25x annual expenses) well before traditional retirement age, achieved through a high savings rate.
