# Pascal's Wager

A decision-theoretic argument for belief that recurs across Kyle's gospel study and reading on faith. Kyle states it in his own words (drawn from the Mormon 7–9 Come Follow Me study): "a rational person should live as though God exists… if God does not exist the loss is finite (some pleasures, luxury), but if God does exist one stands to receive infinite gains and avoid infinite losses." There it answers [Moroni](/notes/moroni)'s message to a future age of [Atheism](/notes/atheism) in Mormon 9 — "Then will ye say that there is no God?" — the asymmetric bet framed against the consequences of unbelief (November 2–8, 2020. Mormon 7–9: "I Speak unto You As If Ye Were Present").

The same wager appears in [The Crucible of Doubt](/books/the-crucible-of-doubt) as a posture of committed [Faith](/notes/faith) — "I made a wager… to bet my entire life that the gospel was true," paired with Voltaire's "not to believe is absurd." In [Why You're Christian](/notes/why-you-re-christian) Kyle links it to the [Fyodor Dostoevsky](/notes/fyodor-dostoevsky) problem: absent God and an afterlife, why live righteously ("Why shouldn't I cut another man's throat, rob, and steal?") — the wager reframed as the claim that a world without God cannot ground morality, where the rational move is still to live as though He exists.

**Context:** Pascal's Wager comes from Blaise Pascal's *Pensées* (1670). It argues that, under uncertainty about God's existence, betting on belief is rational because the potential gain (infinite, eternal) and avoided loss vastly outweigh the finite cost of belief if God does not exist.
