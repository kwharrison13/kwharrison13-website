# Grameen

The Grameen Bank of Bangladesh, held up in [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) as a contemporary instance of an economic organizing principle distinct from both capitalism and socialism — sitting beside [Mondragon](/notes/mondragon) and [Value Added Ventures](/notes/value-added-ventures) as a model the book aligns with the [United Order](/notes/united-order) and law of [Stewardship](/notes/stewardship) ("a hand up, not a hand out"). The book traces micro-lending's origins to Grameen ("village" in Bangla): economist Muhammad Yunus, returning home with a U.S. Ph.D., offered micro-loans ($20–$50) to the poor to lift them above subsistence, organized around small peer groups jointly liable for repayment and a set of 16 principles (frugality, hygiene, savings). It records the Grameen Bank lending over $400M with a ~98% repayment rate, and the model spreading to Asia, Africa, Latin America, and U.S. programs (the Lakota Fund, Chicago's Full Circle Fund, Arkansas's Good Faith Fund). Kyle tags the surrounding micro-lending passages with [Value Added Ventures](/notes/value-added-ventures).

**Context:** Grameen Bank is a Bangladeshi microfinance institution and community-development bank founded by Muhammad Yunus in 1983; both Yunus and the bank were awarded the 2006 Nobel Peace Prize for pioneering microcredit.

## Where this appears
