# Technological Innovation

The recurring "are we still building?" theme that anchors one of Kyle's Portfolio Ideas (tweets tagged `technological-innovation` flow into its feed). The corpus frames it less as the act of invention than as the question of what gates or unblocks real-world progress. [Bilal Zuberi](/notes/bilal-zuberi)'s [How To Build If… It's Time To Build?](/notes/how-to-build-if-it-s-time-to-build) — a practitioner's reply to [Marc Andreessen](/notes/marc-andreessen)'s [It's Time To Build](/notes/it-s-time-to-build) — argues deep tech is the next frontier and lays out six concrete hurdles (lower margins, long sales cycles, fragile supply chains, regulation, capital intensity, fewer acquirors) that keep hardware from attracting mainstream investment. [Carlota Perez](/notes/carlota-perez)'s [Perez Technological Surge Cycle](/notes/perez-technological-surge-cycle) supplies the long-wave frame: techno-economic paradigms unfold through an installation phase (bubble and crash) into a deployment phase (golden age).

The saved tweets sharpen the diagnosis. One quotes [Peter Thiel](/notes/peter-thiel) arguing science shifted from "discovery" to "governance" — too centralized after the New Deal and the atomic bomb to innovate. Another (Ben Pouladian, on SanDisk replacing Atlassian in the Nasdaq-100) makes the labor-market case: the binding constraint on hard tech is trained humans, not capital — the US graduated fewer electrical-engineering bachelor's in 2020-21 than under Reagan, while CS roughly tripled, and "you cannot bootcamp a device physicist." A third tracks adoption signal: which AI tools actually convert to productivity and income. Together they triangulate a single thesis — that software-only progress left critical physical and scientific capability unbuilt, and the talent pipeline to rebuild it has atrophied.

**Context:** "Technological innovation" is the economic engine of long-run productivity growth — the development and deployment of new technologies that reshape industries. Debates over its pace (the "Great Stagnation" thesis, progress studies) are a live theme in the venture and policy worlds Kyle reads in.
