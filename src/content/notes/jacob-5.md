# Jacob 5

The Book of Mormon chapter containing **Zenos's allegory of the tame and wild olive trees** — the heart of Kyle's Olive Tree Allegory topical study (Topical Journal 1.139–1.140). He reads the allegory as a chronicle of the Lord's labor among Israel and the Gentiles: Christ ministered first among the Jews (the tame tree) and also among non-Israelites (wild trees) — Samaritans ([John 4](/notes/john-4)), the Gentile woman ([Matthew 15](/notes/matthew-15)), the Gentile centurion ([Matthew 8](/notes/matthew-8)). The branches map onto sacred history — the Exodus through the scattering of Israel (5:8, 14), the Gentiles (5:9–10), scattered Israel and the House of Lehi (5:25, where the good spot is the promised land, part-tame fruit the Nephites, part-wild the Lamanites), the Gentile Christian church losing priesthood and doctrine (5:30–32), and the preparation for the [Restoration](/notes/restoration) when the servant grafts in the House of Ephraim (5:49–52). Kyle notes [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s teaching that [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) was a pure Ephraimite.

The roots, in his reading, are **the covenants that survived** and could grow strong again for the Restoration (5:54, 59) — tying the chapter to the Abrahamic Covenant. The gathering of the tribes (patriarchal blessings) is the natural fruit gathered in (5:70–73), and at the end of the millennium Satan is bound not by chains but by the righteousness of the people. The study closes on a reflection called "Depth of the Root," which answers the Lord's anguished "What could I have done more for my vineyard?" (5:41) with [D&C 3](/notes/dandc-3):3 — "it is not the work of God that is frustrated, but the work of men."

**Context:** Jacob 5 is the longest chapter in the Book of Mormon, quoting the prophet Zenos's allegory of the olive tree (the "vineyard"). It is a foundational Latter-day Saint text on the scattering and gathering of Israel.
