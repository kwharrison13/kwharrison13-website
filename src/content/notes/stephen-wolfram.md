# Stephen Wolfram

Stephen Wolfram shows up in two corners of Kyle's corpus. In [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) (the von Neumann biography) he is the "young mathematician" who "cuts a divisive figure in the scientific world" — Eton scholarship without graduating, Oxford dropout, a CalTech physics PhD at twenty, four years at the Institute for Advanced Study, then a departure from academia to found Wolfram Research, whose flagship Mathematica he wrote in a language of his own design. The book frames his big idea (echoing Edward Fredkin) that the complexity of the natural world arises from simple computational rules executed repeatedly — possibly a single "ultimate rule," a "God's four-line computer program." His 2002 *A New Kind of Science*, the product of ten years of hermit-like isolation, is presented as his first draft of a theory of everything; the book also records the central critique, that his models make no falsifiable predictions and so can't be checked against reality.

In [What Is ChatGPT Doing and Why Does It Work](/notes/what-is-chatgpt-doing-and-why-does-it-work) Wolfram is the author. Kyle reads his walkthrough of how ChatGPT generates text — from next-word probability to neural nets — and his argument that meaningful human language may carry more structure and simplicity than we knew, illustrated by decomposing "I bought two pounds of apples for my children" into its conceptual underpinnings (individualism/[Capitalism](/notes/capitalism), measurement, agriculture, family).

**Context:** Stephen Wolfram (b. 1959) is a physicist, computer scientist, and entrepreneur — founder of Wolfram Research, creator of Mathematica and Wolfram Alpha, and author of *A New Kind of Science*.
