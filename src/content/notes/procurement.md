# Procurement

**One-line:** *The buying process that vendor-discovery and govtech products serve — cross-functional in large companies, regulation-bound in government.*

Procurement shows up in Kyle's notes along two axes. In **enterprise SaaS sourcing**, the September 13th, 2022 — Nisha Swarup call describes a vendor [Discovery + Diligence](/notes/discovery-diligence) product: in larger companies the buying process involves many stakeholders (finance and sales department heads, or BizOps people tasked with diligence), so it needs a [System of Record](/notes/system-of-record) that aggregates vendor data and surfaces the security/compliance details "people care about in procurement." The early-adopter wedge is the time-strapped buyer facing a hard deadline who won't spend four or five hours doing diligence by hand.

In **govtech / city-building**, procurement is the regulatory machinery [CoProcure](/notes/coprocure) tries to productize. [Mariel Reed](/notes/mariel-reed)'s startup aims to (1) improve the procurement marketplace, (2) enable cooperative purchasing across municipalities, and (3) turn one-off contracts into recurring ones — positioning itself as the "regulatory and legal gearbox" for city procurement, with a long tail of ~20K cities and towns that can't yet experiment but would buy proven solutions. [Arik Bronshtein](/notes/arik-bronshtein)'s [UrbanLeap](/notes/urbanleap) sits upstream of that (pilot → RFP pipeline), part of the #[The New City](/notes/the-new-city) thread. Finally, [The Kill Chain](/books/the-kill-chain) treats *broken* procurement as a national-security failure: the US defense acquisition system is "politicized by a blizzard of legislation," producing "multi-billion-dollar procurement debacles" and a model that, per General Schriever, can no longer field qualitatively superior weapons — the institutional antithesis of the founder-led [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) era.

**Context:** Procurement is the organizational function of sourcing, evaluating, and purchasing goods and services from external vendors; in large enterprises and especially in government it is heavily process- and compliance-driven, which is what makes it both slow and a recurring target for software disruption.
