# Elinor Ostrom

In [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), [Nadia Eghbal](/notes/nadia-eghbal) uses [Elinor Ostrom](/notes/elinor-ostrom)'s commons economics as the analytic frame for open source. Ostrom's research on real-world commons identified **eight design principles** for a well-managed, self-governed resource: clearly defined membership boundaries; rules that match actual conditions; participation by the affected in modifying the rules; monitors accountable to the community; graduated sanctions for violations; low-cost in-community conflict resolution; external recognition of the community's right to make its own institutions; and nested layers of authority when the commons is part of a larger system. Eghbal maps these onto open-source projects — e.g. an online forum's regular members vs. lurkers as the "membership boundary."

Eghbal notes that Ostrom "spent the latter part of her career applying the principles she had observed to the 'digital commons,' where knowledge is the shared resource," and cites her book with Charlotte Hess, *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons*, which defines users as "those appropriating digital information at any point in time." Ostrom sits alongside [Yochai Benkler](/notes/yochai-benkler)'s commons-based peer production as the economic scaffolding for the book's reframing of open source from a collaboration problem into a curation-and-[Maintenance](/notes/maintenance) problem.

**Context:** Elinor Ostrom (1933–2012) was an American political economist and the first woman to win the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences (2009), awarded for her work showing that communities can sustainably self-govern shared resources without privatization or top-down state control — a direct rebuttal to the "tragedy of the commons." Her foundational book is *Governing the Commons* (1990).
