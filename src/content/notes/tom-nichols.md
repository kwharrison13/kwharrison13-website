# Tom Nichols

Tom Nichols is the author of [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), a book Kyle read in 2021 and used as empirical scaffolding for an essay-in-progress (the "Voice of the People" / [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) thread) and the [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) concept. Across Kyle's notes Nichols is the named source for several recurring arguments: that [Critical Thinking](/notes/critical-thinking) is the "most important" intellectual capability and "the one most under attack" in universities — eroded when students become valued clients rather than learners; that the [Dunning-Kruger Effect](/notes/dunning-kruger-effect) is a measurable failure mode ("increasing confidence without increasing accuracy," a deficit of [Metacognition](/notes/metacognition)) rather than a meme, quoting David Dunning that "education, even when done skillfully, can produce illusory confidence"; and, in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack), the tension between trusting expertise and overriding it with outside judgment.

Nichols connects outward to several other entities in Kyle's corpus. He cites [Nassim Nicholas Taleb](/notes/nassim-taleb) on the "epistemic arrogance" of prediction. Kyle pairs his thesis with a [Ricky Gervais](/notes/ricky-gervais) clip on the opinions-vs-facts collapse ("my opinion is worth as much as your facts"), and the argument resurfaces in [The Hardening Of The Great Softening](/essays/the-hardening-of-the-great-softening) research on institutions failing and the slide toward anti-vaccine thinking. (Note: a [books-to-read](/notes/books-to-read) entry filed under "[The Mirage of Knowledge](/notes/the-mirage-of-knowledge)" attributes *The Death of Expertise* to Nichols and links him via the Munger "Elementary Worldly Wisdom" note.)

**Context:** Tom Nichols (b. 1960) is an American academic and writer, a longtime professor at the U.S. Naval War College and a staff writer at *The Atlantic*, best known for *The Death of Expertise* (2017), which argues that the internet, higher education's customer model, and partisan media have corroded public deference to specialized knowledge.
