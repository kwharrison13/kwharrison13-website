# Behavioral Addiction

In [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism), behavioral addiction is the mechanism [Cal Newport](/notes/cal-newport) uses to explain why apps hook users — defined as a condition in which a person repeatedly pursues a behavior because its rewarding effects provide a compelling incentive, despite detrimental consequences. Newport names the two specific forces tech companies engineer to manufacture it: **intermittent positive reinforcement** and **the drive for social approval**. The book quotes Facebook's founding president Sean Parker admitting the design intent was "How do we consume as much of your time and conscious attention as possible?" — delivering "a little dopamine hit every once in a while" when someone likes or comments. It also cites Bill Maher's framing that "checking your 'likes' is the new smoking," casting the social-media business as selling an addictive product.

The notes show the lived consequence too: a declutterer named "De" discovered how addicted he'd become to compulsively checking politically charged news, and only re-stabilized by switching to a single neutral daily check. Behavioral addiction is thus the engine behind the book's core argument for reclaiming attention.

**Context:** Behavioral (or process) addiction refers to compulsive engagement in a rewarding non-substance behavior — gambling, gaming, social-media use — that activates the brain's reward circuitry similarly to drugs. "Intermittent variable reinforcement," a schedule where rewards arrive unpredictably, is the same mechanism that makes slot machines and social-media feeds especially compelling.
