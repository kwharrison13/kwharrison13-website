# William Shockley

In [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory), Shockley is one of the central figures — co-inventor of the transistor at [Bell Labs](/notes/bell-labs) and the book's clearest case study in the gap between identifying talent and being able to retain it. He concluded that semiconductors (materials "neither good conductors... nor good insulators") could replace vacuum tubes, and was promoted by [Mervin Kelly](/notes/mervin-kelly) in the 1945 Murray Hill reorganization that put younger solid-state physicists over older Labs scientists — a move some found devastating but one colleague called "a stroke of enormously good management." Like Kelly, the book notes, Shockley "rarely lingered over any one project" and kept a frenetic schedule.

The page's load-bearing theme in Kyle's notes is the talent paradox: Shockley had an "uncanny ability to spot talent," yet "simply could not get the hang of managing people." That failure drove away the "traitorous eight," who went on to found Fairchild and seed Silicon Valley. The book's lesson — flagged in the [Talent Identification](/notes/talent-identification) synthesis — is that "spotting talent and being able to retain it are two different problems," and "whether you benefit from spotting talent depends on whether they go on to hate you or revere you." Shockley was a former MIT colleague of [Richard Feynman](/notes/richard-feynman).

**Context:** William Shockley (1910–1989) shared the 1956 Nobel Prize in Physics with Walter Brattain and John Bardeen for the transistor. He later founded Shockley Semiconductor in what became Silicon Valley, but his abrasive management drove away the eight researchers who founded Fairchild Semiconductor. His reputation was further damaged by his later promotion of racist eugenic views.
