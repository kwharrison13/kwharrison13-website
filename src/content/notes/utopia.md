# Utopia

Utopia is one of the wiki's load-bearing cross-book themes, and the corpus stages it as a genuine argument. On the warning side: [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) opens with Berdiaeff's epigraph that "utopias are attainable" and the real question is "How can one avoid their definitive attainment?" — framing the perfected society as a trap, not a goal. [The Time Machine](/books/the-time-machine) runs the same inversion biologically: the Eloi's "too-perfect security" breeds civilizational decay, "the dream of the human intellect... had committed suicide," because hardship and necessity are what drive intelligence.

On the constructive side, [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) ([Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman)) rehabilitates the idea — but only as a "vague outline," a guidepost rather than a blueprint: "a map of the world that does not include Utopia is not worth even glancing at" ([Oscar Wilde](/notes/oscar-wilde)), yet "without utopia, all that remains is a technocracy." [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) is the case study in between: Walt's EPCOT was a literal attempt to *build* a technological utopia, which [Sam Gennawey](/notes/sam-gennawey) judges "compelling as history, problematic as policy" — because the control it required (no voting residents, no landowners, no retirees) is exactly what made the utopia problematic. The synthesis across these four: blueprint-utopias are dangerous or self-undermining; guidepost-utopias that keep "imagination and hope alive and active" (Bertrand Russell, via Bregman) are the defensible kind.

**Context:** "Utopia" — from Thomas More's 1516 work, punning on Greek *ou-topos* ("no place") and *eu-topos* ("good place") — names an ideal imagined society; the literary tradition runs from More through the dystopian inversions of Huxley, Orwell, and Wells.

## Where this appears

- [Brave New World](/books/brave-new-world) — the Berdiaeff epigraph: utopias are "attainable" and worth avoiding.
- [The Time Machine](/books/the-time-machine) — "too-perfect security" inverts the utopian promise into decay; security breeds stagnation.
- [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) — Bregman's "utopia as guidepost, not blueprint" framing; Oscar Wilde's map-of-the-world epigraph.
- [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) — EPCOT as a literal technological utopia, "compelling as history, problematic as policy."
