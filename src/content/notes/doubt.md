# Doubt

Doubt is a central concern of [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) (Bruce C. and Marie K. Hafen), which treats it not as the enemy of faith but as part of faith's "natural growth process": "Encountering surprises and uncertainties is actually part of faith's natural growth process," and "faithful questions are valuable." The Hafens anchor the posture with [Dieter F. Uchtdorf](/notes/dieter-f-uchtdorf)'s line — "First doubt your doubts before you doubt your faith" — and [J.R.R. Tolkien](/notes/j-r-r-tolkien)'s "not all those who wander are lost."

The book also makes an epistemic argument about how doubt is weaponized. It draws on the "merchants of doubt" playbook — the tobacco-industry strategy to "amplify uncertainty, create doubt and undermine scientific discoveries that threaten their interests" — and argues that critics misassign the burden of proof, treating a single raised question as if it were a guilty verdict. The Hafens counter that "one unresolved question can't offset a mountain of answers that are resolved": not knowing where one sheep is doesn't mean the ninety-and-nine are lost. Social media, they note, trains people to expect short answers and to confuse manufactured doubt with knowledge.

**Context:** In Latter-day Saint discourse, "doubt your doubts before you doubt your faith" comes from a 2013 General Conference address by Dieter F. Uchtdorf; "merchants of doubt" refers to the 2010 book by historians Naomi Oreskes and Erik Conway on industry-funded campaigns to manufacture scientific uncertainty.
