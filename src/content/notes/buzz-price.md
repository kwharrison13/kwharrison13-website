# Buzz Price

Harrison "Buzz" Price was the land-use economist who advised [Walt Disney](/notes/walt-disney), and in [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) he is the disciplined counterweight to Walt's dreaming. The book credits him with "a unique ability to take Walt's abstract dreams and translate them into algorithms that could count every potential dollar" — a layer of discipline that let Walt focus on the experience while never straying too far from reality, since the money made on one project paid for the next. Price's analysis was load-bearing across Walt's ventures: he advised on the 1959 Palm Beach project (which never moved forward but is when Walt "became obsessed with building a city"), and he articulated the employability-focused mission of CalArts.

Price is the book's authoritative voice on whether EPCOT could have worked, having been "in the room with Walt and Roy." Asked directly, he answered "Absolutely yes," arguing the design was evolutionary rather than revolutionary — built on tried-and-true architectural technologies and a thoughtful blend of land uses with the built-in critical mass for sustainable economic success. His unequivocal verdict, which the book leans on as its closing judgment: "EPCOT would have been more famous than Walt Disney World." He later wrote his own account, *Walt's Revolution by the Numbers*.

**Context:** Harrison "Buzz" Price (1921–2010) was an economic-feasibility consultant who selected the Anaheim site for Disneyland and the Orlando site for Walt Disney World, and went on to consult on hundreds of theme-park and attraction projects, becoming known as the founder of the field of economic research for the leisure industry.
