# Nadia Eghbal

Author of [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), the book that seeded Kyle's recurring [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) idea — and the conceptual underpinning for the published Open-Source Knowledge essay announcing [Contrary Research](/essays/contrary-research). Eghbal reframes open source from a collaboration problem into a curation-and-maintenance problem: "creation is an intrinsic motivator, maintenance usually requires extrinsic motivation," and a finding she cites that in 85%+ of GitHub projects examined, fewer than 5% of developers were responsible for over 95% of code and social interactions. Her central economic puzzle — how to limit content's marginal cost while still creating a logical process for getting paid — became the spine of Kyle's argument that ideas should be treated "like software that is open source and in active development" rather than like a slowly-decaying bridge.

Kyle draws several threads from the book directly: the "code as commodity vs. code as community" distinction, the idea that "when content is a living organism its value is better measured in terms of people and relationships," and [Eugene Wei](/notes/eugene-wei)'s "attention arena" description of Facebook's News Feed, which Eghbal quotes.

**Context:** Nadia Eghbal (later writing as Nadia Asparouhova) is a writer and researcher on open-source software economics; her 2020 book *Working in Public: The Making and Maintenance of Open Source Software* grew out of her work funding open-source developers at GitHub.
