# Distributed Energy Model

A recurring tag in Kyle's notes for the shift away from centralized, utility-scale power toward distributed generation and "energy as a service." In [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) it is invoked against the energy inefficiency of [Suburban Sprawl](/notes/suburban-sprawl) — Kyle marks Richard Florida's line that "suburban sprawl is energy-inefficient and wasteful" with this concept, pairing it with the [Green Manhattan](/notes/green-manhattan) argument that density is green. It connects to [Jon Powers](/notes/jon-powers), co-founder of [CleanCapital](/notes/cleancapital) and former Obama Chief Sustainability Officer, whose interest in third-party finance for clean-energy assets and excitement about "energy as a service" (work beyond utility-scale solar) is the closest thing in Kyle's corpus to a person embodying this model.

The concept sits inside Kyle's broader [Decarbonization](/notes/decarbonization) and [Climate Change](/notes/climate-change) cluster, and surfaces in two long reads about why building the energy future is hard: [The Build-Nothing Country](/notes/the-build-nothing-country) (where solar, wind, and transmission lines face NIMBY opposition despite large IRA allocations) and [Rekindling US Productivity for a New Era](/notes/rekindling-us-productivity-for-a-new-era) (which frames reaching net zero as requiring an additional ~$1.7 trillion in annual investment through 2050). It is also linked to [Fusion Energy](/notes/fusion-energy) as an adjacent bet on the long-run energy transition. Most current references are associative — the concept tags energy-transition passages across these sources rather than receiving sustained treatment on any single page.

**Context:** A distributed energy model decentralizes electricity generation and storage — rooftop solar, batteries, microgrids, and demand-side resources aggregated near the point of use — as opposed to the traditional model of large central power plants feeding a one-way grid. The "energy as a service" framing sells managed energy outcomes rather than equipment.
