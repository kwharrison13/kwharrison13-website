# Absolute Truth

In Kyle's wiki, "Absolute Truth" is the load-bearing premise of [Dallin H. Oaks](/notes/dallin-h-oaks)'s BYU address Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks. Oaks opens with the claim "We believe in absolute truth, including the existence of God and the right and wrong established by His commandments," defining truth as "knowledge of things as they are, and as they were, and as they are to come" (D&C 93:24) and quoting [Joseph F. Smith](/notes/joseph-f-smith): "We are willing to receive all truth, from whatever source it may come; for truth will stand, truth will endure."

The concept matters because of the counterintuitive move it sets up: belief in absolute truth makes [Tolerance](/notes/tolerance) *harder*, not easier. Oaks contrasts it with [Moral Relativism](/notes/moral-relativism) — "the philosophy that each person is free to choose for himself what is right and wrong" — for which "almost anything goes." A relativist faces few occasions to be tolerant; a believer in absolute truth faces many, because the ideas and practices of others constantly confront his convictions. This is the basis for the [Truth and Tolerance](/notes/truth-and-tolerance) "two-sided coin": tolerance on one face, truth always on the other — modeled on Christ telling the woman taken in adultery "Neither do I condemn thee" *and* "Go, and sin no more."

**Context:** "Absolute truth" denotes moral and metaphysical realism — the view that some things are objectively, universally true or right independent of individual belief — standing in contrast to moral relativism and subjectivism.

## Where this appears

- Truth and Tolerance — Dallin H. Oaks — the foundational text; Oaks's first "movement" affirms absolute truth, then argues it makes tolerance more demanding than relativism does.
- [Truth and Tolerance](/notes/truth-and-tolerance) — the parent concept, which frames belief in absolute truth as what makes tolerance a "two-sided coin" rather than a cheap virtue.
