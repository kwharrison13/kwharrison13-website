# Christopher Alexander

Christopher Alexander is the conceptual spine Sam Gennawey uses to read Disneyland's spaces in *[Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city)*. Gennawey threads Walt Disney's vision through Alexander's "timeless way of building" and its "quality without a name," reading Disneyland's hub-and-spoke plan, Main Street, and pedestrian-first layout as instances of the principles Alexander articulated. The book describes Alexander as the Austrian-born architect, the first recipient of the American Institute of Architects' medal for research, who taught at UC Berkeley for thirty-eight years and influenced architecture, planning, object-oriented programming, and the [New Urbanism](/notes/new-urbanism) movement.

The single Alexander idea Kyle returns to is the "90/10" finding on human feeling: Alexander's roughly forty-year research showed that about 90% of human feeling is shared across people, with only ~10% idiosyncratic. Gennawey argues Walt's gift was designing for that shared 90% — the Plaza Hub "bridges every culture and speaks a universal language." Alexander's claims that "centers arise in space" and that "the simple social intercourse created when people rub shoulders in public is one of the most essential kinds of social 'glue' in society" are mapped directly onto the radial hub-and-spoke plan at the heart of the park. In the book's wider cast he sits alongside [Victor Gruen](/notes/victor-gruen)'s cellular city theory and the [New Urbanism](/notes/new-urbanism) community.

**Context:** Christopher Alexander (1936–2022) was an architect and design theorist best known for *A Pattern Language* and *The Timeless Way of Building*. His concept of "pattern languages" was widely influential beyond architecture, notably in software design.
