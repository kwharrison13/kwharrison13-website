# Apple

In Kyle's notes Apple shows up most substantively as the **central case study in the chips / vertical-integration thread**. [Chip War](/books/chip-war) frames it as the company that "designs the world's most advanced processors but can only have them fabricated by [TSMC](/notes/tsmc)" — so the "Designed by Apple in California. Assembled in China" engraving is "highly misleading" since the iPhone's most irreplaceable components "can only be made in Taiwan." The book's striking claim: as a *buyer* of chips, "Apple CEO Tim Cook has more influence on the industry than any Pentagon official today," now that the U.S. government buys ~2% of the chip market versus nearly all of it in the early 1960s. It also traces the lineage back to [Steve Jobs](/notes/steve-jobs)'s 1980 thinking on the software/hardware boundary.

The [Apple Earnings, An Interview with Jay Goldberg About Chips and Intel](/notes/apple-earnings-an-interview-with-jay-goldberg-about-chips-and-intel) long-read (Stratechery) extends this: [Jay Goldberg](/notes/jay-goldberg) on Apple's 5G iPhone strength in China (pent-up demand on a mature 5G network) and Apple's push to **re-integrate vertically down to the chip** (the A-series and M1). The contrast he draws is with [Qualcomm](/notes/qualcomm)'s "lowest common denominator" problem — one chip segmented across many customers — versus Apple optimizing its A-series "for exactly its own needs," a clean illustration of [Vertical Integration](/notes/vertical-integration). Apple also appears more glancingly in Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021 as a net-zero-by-2030 company and sustainability-diligence outreach target.

**Context:** Apple Inc. is the consumer-electronics and software company behind the iPhone, Mac, and its in-house Apple Silicon (A-series, M-series) processors; it is among the world's most valuable companies and designs its own chips while relying on TSMC to fabricate them.
