# Charles Francis Adams

Charles Francis Adams is the youngest son of [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) in James Traub's biography, and the book uses the father-son relationship to show Adams' intense pedagogical ambition. Adams took daily walks with the boy, listening to the four-year-old read La Fontaine's *Fables* in the original French; by the spring of 1812, several months shy of Charles's fifth birthday, the father was spending at least three hours a day with him, and Charles was reading French, German, and English — and, by year's end, from the Book of Common Prayer (Kyle tags these passages [Parenting](/notes/parenting) and [Education](/notes/education)). The biography notes this attention intensified as [Louisa Adams](/notes/louisa-adams) was "too distracted with grief even to attend to her little boy."

Charles Francis is also the custodian of his father's legacy: Adams told him near the end that his diary "was closed," placing it in Charles's hands "to do with it what I thought proper," while stating it had never been written for publication. Charles Francis was in turn the father of [Henry Adams](/notes/henry-adams), whose memoir supplies the biography's famous scene of John Quincy Adams silently walking his tantrum-throwing grandson the mile to school.

**Context:** Charles Francis Adams Sr. (1807–1886) was an American lawyer, politician, and diplomat — a Free Soil vice-presidential candidate and, as U.S. Minister to Britain during the Civil War, a key figure in keeping Britain neutral. He edited and published the papers of his father and grandfather.
