# Financial Independence

In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel) names independence his **top financial goal** — "cash is the oxygen of independence," and "being able to wake up one morning and change what you're doing, on your own terms, whenever you're ready, seems like the grandmother of all financial goals." Crucially, independence for Housel doesn't mean stopping work: "it means you only do the work you like with people you like at the times you want for as long as you want." He gets there through a continuously rising savings rate — virtually every dollar of a raise routed into an "independence fund" — and a lifestyle held below what he can afford, which doubles as an escape from "the psychological treadmill of keeping up with the Joneses." This connects directly to [Enough](/notes/enough): the person who never has enough keeps moving the finish line and can never actually buy independence.

The concept also surfaces in [Digital Minimalism](/books/digital-minimalism), where [Cal Newport](/notes/cal-newport) ties the Mr. Money Mustache / FIRE leisure philosophy — making and fixing things over passive consumption, value-first reasoning about money — to the book's case for high-quality leisure over engineered distraction. In Kyle's own June 2021 / June 2018-era reading captures, Financial Independence sits at the center of a cluster with [Retirement](/notes/retirement) and [Personal Finance](/notes/personal-finance): saved links on early retirement (FIRE), savings-rate strategies, and "things I regret not doing before retiring early at 34" map the practical end of the same idea.

**Context:** "Financial independence" is the state of having enough income from savings and investments to cover living expenses without needing active employment. It is the "FI" in the FIRE movement (Financial Independence, Retire Early), popularized by bloggers like Mr. Money Mustache, which emphasizes a high savings rate and frugality over high income.
