# Gospel Study

A theme Kyle tags (#[Gospel Study](/notes/gospel-study)) across his reading wherever deliberate, lived engagement with scripture and doctrine surfaces. In [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind), he attaches it to the epistemology of belief — science writer Christie Aschwanden's line that science "can't produce 'absolute certainty'" but is "a process of uncertainty reduction," reframing study (religious or scientific) as confidence-adjustment rather than proof. In [Feed My Sheep](/notes/feed-my-sheep), gospel study is reframed as *deliberate practice*: the "study like Elder Nelson" and "feet in cold water" passages cast [Russell M. Nelson](/notes/russell-m-nelson) as the model of practiced mastery, and "convert the missionary first" makes doctrine something that must be lived in the heart ([Religious Internalization](/notes/religious-internalization)) before it can be transmitted.

A third strand is generational transmission: in the [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) biography, Kyle tags the scenes where Adams and his son George read four chapters of the Bible to each other every morning before eight and discussed the meaning — and Adams's "extraordinary series of letters to George about the Bible" — as #[Gospel Study](/notes/gospel-study) paired with #[Parenting](/notes/parenting), scripture study as a family inheritance.

## Where this appears

- [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) — study as "uncertainty reduction," not absolute certainty
- [Feed My Sheep](/notes/feed-my-sheep) — gospel study as deliberate practice; "convert the missionary first," study like Elder Nelson
- [John Quincy Adams](/books/john-quincy-adams) — Adams's daily Bible reading with his son and his scriptural letters, study as parental transmission
