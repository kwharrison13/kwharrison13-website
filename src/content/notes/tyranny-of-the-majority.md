# Tyranny of the Majority

The dark twin of [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people): the same democratic principle that grants legitimacy to the collective will also lets a majority deprive a minority of its democratic rights "in the name of democracy." In Kyle's wiki the canonical case is the Mormon expulsions, which [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling) reads as exactly this failure mode — popular will overriding institutional authority until "the people, not Governor [Thomas Ford](/notes/thomas-ford), ruled western Illinois" ([Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet)). What makes "the voice of God, shall be the voice of the people" ([Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young), 1844) a sacred warrant in one breath becomes mob rule in the next.

The [Voice of the People](/notes/voice-of-the-people) page treats this as [Democracy](/notes/democracy)'s built-in vulnerability rather than an external attack: when majority opinion displaces restraint, the minority has no shelter. Kyle's note (drawn from [Brené Brown](/notes/bren-brown)'s [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness)) frames the social mechanism — group loyalty built on shared *belief* rather than shared *identity* makes dissent risky, so the majority's judgment hardens into righteousness. The structural antidote the sources keep pointing to is an [Educated Electorate](/notes/educated-electorate) and a media ecology that trains sustained reasoning ([Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death)) — the conditions that keep "wisdom of crowds" from curdling into "the crowds are wise."

**Context:** "Tyranny of the majority" is a phrase associated with Alexis de Tocqueville's *Democracy in America* (1835) and John Stuart Mill's *On Liberty* (1859), describing how democratic majorities can oppress minorities through both law and social pressure — a concern that motivated constitutional protections for minority rights.
