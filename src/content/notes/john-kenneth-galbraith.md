# John Kenneth Galbraith

Galbraith is the most-quoted outside voice in [Charlie Munger — The Great Financial Scandal of 2003](/notes/charlie-munger-the-great-financial-scandal-of-2003), where Munger threads three of his aphorisms through the satirical "Quant Tech" parable: "We all agree that pessimism is a mark of superior intellect," "Meetings are indispensable when you don't want to do anything," and "More die in the United States of too much food than of too little." The talk closes on a related #[Contrarian](/notes/contrarianism) note — "In any great organization it is far, far safer to be wrong with the majority than to be right alone" — which sits in the same vein of dry institutional skepticism Munger borrows from Galbraith.

**Context:** John Kenneth Galbraith (1908–2006) was a Canadian-American economist, Harvard professor, and public intellectual known for *The Affluent Society* and *The Great Crash, 1929*, and for a witty, aphoristic prose style of the kind Munger quotes here.
