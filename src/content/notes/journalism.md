# Journalism

Journalism recurs in Kyle's corpus mainly as a case study in broken business models and corrupted [Incentives](/notes/incentives). In [News in the Age of Abundance](/notes/news-in-the-age-of-abundance) the diagnosis is sharpest: the collapse of the local-newspaper business has corrupted journalism's incentives, because subscription and ad salesmen used to insulate journalists so they could "stick to the facts," and with that buffer gone, "a man cannot understand something if his salary depends on him not understanding it" (Upton Sinclair). The [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters) supply the operator's view of the same decline: Buffett argues local newspapers reign supreme in delivering community news that "only a local newspaper can" chronicle, but that "Journalism's First Principle should require that the media be scrupulous in deciding what" it prints — Kyle flags this as the "commoditization of journalism."

A second thread treats journalism as both cause and casualty of the [death of expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise). Tom Nichols argues that "in a world of constant information... journalism is now sometimes as much a contributor to the death of expertise as it is a defense against it" — because "in an age when everything is journalism, and everyone is a journalist, standards inevitably fall." In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book), journalism enters via Adler's *caveat lector* and the management of the news. The theme reappears in adjacent reads — [College Students: Focus On This](/notes/college-students-focus-on-this) notes the shift from newspapers to personalized feeds — and surprisingly in Kyle's investing notes: in the Dylan Reider call, equity analysts are compared to journalists, who must stay "super aware of what the narrative is from the management team each quarter."

**Context:** Journalism is the practice of gathering, verifying, and reporting news. Its twenty-first-century crisis — the collapse of print advertising revenue, the rise of free digital distribution, and an "everyone is a journalist" social-media environment — is a recurring subject of media criticism, including the Upton Sinclair and Warren Buffett arguments Kyle's sources invoke.
