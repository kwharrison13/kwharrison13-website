# Whatever you are, be a good one

One of the most-repeated refrains across Kyle's corpus — a **Lincoln-via-Munger maxim about committing to a path and mastering it**, however ordinary. The grounding text is [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s [Lesson on Elementary Worldly Wisdom](/notes/charlie-munger-a-lesson-on-elementary-worldly-wisdom): you may never become the best tennis player in the world, "however, if you want to become the best plumbing contractor in Bemidji, that is probably doable by two-thirds of you… given enough discipline." The point is the **slowly developed circle of competence** — people who could never win a chess tournament "can rise quite high in life by slowly developing a circle of competence." The revisited version pairs it with "the very first step towards success in any occupation is to become interested in it."

The refrain returns explicitly across at least four book pages, where Kyle's own notes flag it. [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) calls it "the Lincoln-via-Munger refrain, returning a fourth time across the corpus," extending it to the leadership analog of lifting your supporting players (Jordan with Rodman: "help them be their best") rather than forcing everyone to be Michael Jordan. [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) pairs it with [Don't Die With Your Music In You](/notes/don-t-die-with-your-music-in-you) to form "a complete instruction: pick something, commit, finish." [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child) applies it to interest discovery and committing to a path; [George F. Johnson and His Industrial Democracy](/books/george-f-johnson-and-his-industrial-democracy) grounds it as a pride-in-workmanship ethic ("Make sure that when it leaves your hands it is perfect"); and it appears in the concept lists of [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) and [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci). On the gospel side, General Conference- October 2021 attaches the tag to [Jeffrey R. Holland](/notes/jeffrey-r-holland)'s and [D. Todd Christofferson](/notes/d-todd-christofferson)'s teaching that God loves us as we are but "does not want to leave you just as you are."

**Context:** The phrase is widely attributed to Abraham Lincoln ("Whatever you are, be a good one"), though the attribution is not firmly documented; Charlie Munger popularized it in his talks on worldly wisdom and circle of competence.
