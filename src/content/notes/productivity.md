# Productivity

Labor productivity growth — output per hour of work — as the engine of national economic power, and the puzzle of its recent slowdown. The [McKinsey Global Institute report](/notes/rekindling-us-productivity-for-a-new-era) frames it as the central macro lever: productivity growth has powered US economic strength since WWII, adding 2.2% annually, but over the last 15 years it has averaged just **1.4%** — a paradoxical deceleration *during* a boom in digital technology. Regaining historical rates would add **$10 trillion to US GDP** ([GDP](/notes/gdp)). The report's structural finding is a split between **"frontier firms"** — which invest 2.6x more in technology and intangibles, attract more skilled [Talent](/books/talent), and "free their top thinkers" to outsize returns — and laggards that persist by serving different customers, workers, or geographies rather than being competed away. Geography and density matter: Seattle went from 16% more productive than other cities in 2007 to 38% by 2019, tying productivity to [City Building](/notes/city-building).

[The Build-Nothing Country](/notes/the-build-nothing-country) ([Noah Smith](/notes/noah-smith)) supplies the build-side complement: the constraint on raising real output isn't capital but *the ability to build*. "Money is not physical stuff… just because you earmark $5 billion for a subway in some Excel spreadsheet doesn't mean a physical train has been created." Broken permitting, land use, and NIMBY incentives (homeowners restricting local supply as a "costless subsidy") choke the housing, transit, and energy buildout that productivity gains depend on. Read together, the two pieces frame productivity as both a firm-level investment problem ([Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation)) and a national capacity-to-build problem.
