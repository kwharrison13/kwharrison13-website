# Private Equity

A [Capital Allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) concept that runs through Kyle's investing reading both as a model to learn from and a model to critique. [What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes) supplies the aspirational version — [Stephen A. Schwarzman](/notes/stephen-a-schwarzman)'s [Blackstone](/notes/blackstone) playbook of friendly deals, value created through ownership rather than price, and "Don't. Lose. Money." risk discipline. [The Messy Marketplace](/books/the-messy-marketplace) supplies the structural critique: a PE firm "has a fiduciary duty" to maximize return over a fixed fund life, which drives short-term-oriented moves — new executives, cost cuts, automation, product expansion — and [Brent Beshore](/notes/brent-beshore) contrasts that fixed-life schedule against [Permanent Capital](/notes/permanent-capital) and the buy-and-hold [Holding Company](/notes/holding-company) model, with return expectations of 20–35% for smaller PE, search funds, and individuals buying at 4–5x [EBITDA](/notes/ebitda).

The long-reads sharpen the critique into a thesis. [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds) argues that the wealthiest people are almost always business owners who held for decades or generations, never "the five-year flippers that private equity, venture capital, and MBA programs model around" — naming PE's five-year increment as the wrong unit of compounding. [We're Selling Entrepreneurship Short](/notes/we-re-selling-entrepreneurship-short) points back toward profitable, founder-controlled businesses that sit closer to traditional buyout/cash-flow logic than to blitzscaling venture. On the venture side, [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) notes PE, mutual, and hedge money crowding into late-stage rounds, blurring the line between PE and VC. Adjacent: [SMB](/notes/smb) roll-ups are framed as the "broader category" of PE strategies for buying up small businesses.

**Context:** Private equity is an asset class in which firms raise pooled funds (typically with a fixed ~10-year life) to acquire controlling or significant stakes in companies — most often via leveraged buyouts — improve or restructure them, and exit at a profit, in contrast to permanent-capital or buy-and-hold vehicles.
