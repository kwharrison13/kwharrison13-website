# GitLab

For Kyle, GitLab recurs as **the reference implementation of all-remote, radically transparent company culture**. In [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required) it surfaces as the structural answer to "pay for talent, not location" — the GitLab salary calculator is cited explicitly as the concrete tool that prices roles by level and skill rather than geography, with Kyle flagging "Gitlab salary calculator" on the relevant highlights. The Pave Diligence — 2021 notes echo this, pointing to GitLab as "the world's largest all-remote company, with more than 1,300 team members in more than 65 countries" and a model for remote-compensation best practices.

The richer thread is GitLab as a **handbook/transparency culture other companies copy**. [Joel Kwartler](/notes/joel-kwartler) joined [Sourcegraph](/notes/sourcegraph) partly drawn to its "GitLab-modeled handbook transparency" — the June 6th, 2022 — Joel Kwartler (Sourcegraph) meeting notes record that Sourcegraph "modeled their culture after GitLab where they have a ton of visibility," even copying GitLab's early handbook, and that Sourcegraph eventually hired a lot of former Helpscout and GitLab people ("the classic 'hard startups are easiest to hire for'"). The contrast Kwartler draws is with [Figma](/notes/figma), which "didn't really have any of that," leading to friction in how teams interact ("delineate the APIs between departments"). GitLab also appears in deal contexts as a data-hungry but unsophisticated buyer (Starburst - Diligence) and as a competitor in the dev-tool/security space (November 1st, 2021 — Garnet (Pre-Brief from Shardul Shah)).

**Context:** GitLab is a DevOps platform (web-based Git repository, CI/CD, and software-development lifecycle tooling) that went public in 2021. It is well known as a fully remote company with no central office, and for publishing its internal handbook and compensation calculator openly — a model frequently cited in discussions of distributed-work culture.
