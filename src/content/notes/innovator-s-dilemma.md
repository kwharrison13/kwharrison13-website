# Innovator's Dilemma

The concept-tag version of [Clayton Christensen](/notes/clayton-christensen)'s disruption theory (the book is [The Innovator's Dilemma](/books/the-innovator-s-dilemma)): the structural reason well-run incumbents fail to self-disrupt because the things that make them successful — listening to existing customers, protecting profitable lines — make cannibalizing those lines for a worse, cheaper, lower-margin new model rationally hard. Kyle applies the tag as a recurring lens across his library. In [The Everything Store](/books/the-everything-store), it is the framework Bezos's executives "devoured and raptly discussed" — the explicit rationale for the Kindle ("kill your own business") and for Barnes & Noble's failure to compete online. In [The Ride of a Lifetime](/books/the-ride-of-a-lifetime), Bob Iger frames it directly to his board: "Do you want to fall prey to the 'innovator's dilemma' or do you want to fight it?" — choosing to cannibalize profitable TV businesses for streaming. [Chip War](/books/chip-war) supplies the canonical departure case: [Morris Chang](/notes/morris-chang) leaving [Texas Instruments](/notes/texas-instruments) after TI "quietly bins" his foundry idea, and the model "walks away to find a better home" as TSMC.

The tag also marks where incumbents *can't* innovate and what that opens up. [Skunk Works](/books/skunk-works) reads it as opportunity for outsiders: "defense primes structurally can't run a low-cost, high-volume skunk works anymore; the opening exists for an outsider." In [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Kyle inverts it into a capital-allocation warning — "if you try to make forward thinking disruptive investments and fail, you won't be trusted with future capital to keep experimenting." [Todd McKinnon — Creating and Defining a New Market Category](/notes/todd-mckinnon-creating-and-defining-a-new-market-category) ties it to deliberately solving the dilemma: funding "risky, low-probability bets outside the normal budget," the way AWS-style disruptions get incubated inside incumbents. [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) threads it to [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger)'s VC critique ("not everyone should be Harvard, not everyone should be Sequoia"), and it connects to [Jobs to Be Done](/notes/jobs-to-be-done), the demand-side companion framing Kyle applies to venture.

**Context:** *The Innovator's Dilemma* (1997), by Harvard Business School professor Clayton Christensen, introduced "disruptive innovation" — the idea that incumbents lose to entrants offering initially-inferior, lower-margin products that improve until they capture the mainstream market. It became one of the most influential business strategy frameworks of its era.
