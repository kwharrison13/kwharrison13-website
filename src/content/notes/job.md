# Job

The biblical figure invoked in [The Broken Heart](/books/the-broken-heart) as a turning point in how suffering was understood. Where earlier Hebrew thought tied trouble to transgression, Job's "own true perspective introduced a new thought to the Hebrew mind: We can experience trouble not only because of transgression but because it is a natural, even essential, part of life." The book uses Job to support its larger, rehabilitative (rather than retributive) reading of the [Atonement](/notes/atonement) — that trials are part of the developmental process that changes our nature, not merely punishment to be paid off.

**Context:** Job is the protagonist of the Book of Job in the Hebrew Bible, a righteous man who endures catastrophic loss and whose story is the canonical Scriptural meditation on the problem of undeserved suffering.

## Where this appears

- [The Broken Heart](/books/the-broken-heart) — cited for the insight that suffering can be a natural, essential part of life rather than a consequence of sin.
