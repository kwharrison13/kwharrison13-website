# Freemium

A pricing model where a basic version of the product is given away for free and users are charged when they ask for more. [The Founders](/books/the-founders) traces the term's prehistory: when [PayPal](/notes/paypal) grappled with "the vexing questions of when to charge, how much to charge, and how to levy charges," there was no established playbook — the word *freemium* didn't exist until 2006, so the team "relied on instinct, improvisation, and iteration." The book quotes a blogger's split verdict on the model: "Freemium developers act just like cocaine dealers... they give the basic services for free and charge you when you ask for more," while conceding "the onset of the freemium model is probably the best thing that has ever happened to the world wide web."

[Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era) frames freemium as a structural response to channel saturation: while [CAC](/notes/customer-acquisition-cost) is up 50–65% over five years across B2B/B2C, freemium businesses saw it rise only ~25%. The book's load-bearing rule is the "aha moment" — "if you put the paywall before the aha moment, you kill your conversion rate." Free usage becomes the qualification signal, replacing the MQL/SQL with the **product qualified lead (PQL)**; users who convert from a freemium plan show ~10% better net retention than free-trial counterparts.

[SignEasy](/notes/signeasy) is a concrete case: it runs a free-trial-plus-paid-upgrade motion rather than true freemium, with high virality (1 of 10 "signers" becomes a paid user), and wants to add freemium specifically to "open the top of funnel."

**Context:** "Freemium" is a portmanteau of *free* and *premium*, popularized by venture capitalist Fred Wilson and a 2006 reader contest on his blog. It is the default monetization model for much of modern consumer and bottom-up SaaS software.
