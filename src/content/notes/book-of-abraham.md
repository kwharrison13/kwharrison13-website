# Book of Abraham

The Book of Abraham is a central case study in [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins), where [Grant H. Palmer](/notes/grant-h-palmer) reads its cosmology as reflecting early-1800s science rather than antiquity. Palmer leans on LDS scholar Klaus Hansen's observation that the progressive, cosmological aspect of [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s theology bears "remarkable resemblances" to Thomas Dick's *Philosophy of a Future State* — and argues that many of the book's astronomical and Newtonian concepts have since gone out of vogue and become "scientific relics." This grounds one of the book's load-bearing inversions, which Kyle flags in a note: ideas genuinely compatible with science should sound *increasingly modern, not ancient* over time; the Book of Abraham's cosmology aging poorly is itself treated as evidence that it was keyed to antebellum thought. As part of the [Pearl of Great Price](/notes/pearl-of-great-price), it sits alongside the [Book of Mormon](/notes/book-of-mormon) in Palmer's broader thesis that Joseph's scriptures reflect his nineteenth-century environment.

**Context:** The Book of Abraham is a text of LDS scripture, part of the Pearl of Great Price, that Joseph Smith said he translated from Egyptian papyri acquired in 1835. Its authenticity and translation method are long-running subjects of debate within and outside the LDS Church.
