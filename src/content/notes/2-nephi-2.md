# 2 Nephi 2

2 Nephi 2 — Lehi's blessing and counsel to his son Jacob — is the chapter Kyle's gospel study returns to as the Book of Mormon's foundational text on **agency, the Fall, and opposition**. Three verse-clusters do the heavy lifting across his notes. **2 Nephi 2:11** ("there is an opposition in all things") anchors his theology of trial; in [Pursue What Is Meaningful](/notes/pursue-what-is-meaningful) [David Perell](/notes/david-perell) pairs it with [Jordan Peterson](/notes/jordan-peterson)'s "life is suffering" as the most fundamental, irrefutable truth. **2 Nephi 2:25** ("Adam fell that men might be; and men are, that they might have joy") frames the Fall as "downward, yet forward" — setting man's feet "upon progression's highway" (Cowley & Whitney, quoted in June 22–28, 2020. Alma 17–22: "I Will Make an Instrument of Thee").

The chapter is, above all, Kyle's go-to agency proof-text. **2 Nephi 2:26–27** — redeemed from the Fall, men "have become free forever … to act for themselves and not to be acted upon," able to "choose liberty and eternal life … or to choose captivity and death" — recurs across his topical studies. In Obedience — Topical Study it grounds the claim that "life is built on agency: we choose liberty or captivity, but not the consequences." In Hope — Topical Study the same freedom ("free forever … to act") is called "empowering." In Teaching Repentance and Scripture Study — Topical Study it underwrites the idea that knowledge is an "accountability enhancer" — commitments enhance agency, and agency makes us accountable. The Lehonti Principle uses 2:27's "liberty and eternal life" vs. "captivity and death" as the verdict on every temptation, and Charity & Love draws on 2:30 ("none other object save … the everlasting welfare of your souls") as Lehi's expression of love.

**Context:** 2 Nephi 2 is a chapter in the Book of Mormon, a volume of scripture in the Latter-day Saint (Mormon) canon. It records the patriarch Lehi's deathbed teaching to his son Jacob and is among the most-cited LDS scriptural sources on moral agency, the necessity of opposition, and the redemptive purpose of the Fall.
