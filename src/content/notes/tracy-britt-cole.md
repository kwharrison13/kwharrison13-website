# Tracy Britt-Cole

In Kyle's reading of the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), Tracy Britt-Cole is the recurring case study for the **[Bifurcation of Investing](/notes/bifurcation-of-investing)** thesis — the idea that as funds grow they are forced to move upmarket, leaving behind a long tail of smaller opportunities they will no longer compete for. Kyle's note frames her departure from [Berkshire Hathaway](/notes/berkshire-hathaway) as the concrete illustration: "Bigger funds move up market exposing a long tail of businesses that they'll no longer compete for." He returns to the point repeatedly across the annual-letters notes — "the curse of size and scale; Tracy Britt Cole," "Tracy Britt Cole's opportunity" — treating her exit as evidence that even Berkshire's scale creates room for smaller acquirers in the small-and-medium-business tail.

**Context:** Tracy Britt Cole spent roughly a decade at Berkshire Hathaway, joining in 2009 as a financial assistant to [Warren Buffett](/notes/warren-buffett) and going on to chair several Berkshire subsidiaries (Benjamin Moore, Larson-Juhl, Oriental Trading, Pampered Chef). She left in 2020 to co-found Kanbrick, a long-term investment and operating partnership focused on building enduring mid-sized businesses — a move that maps directly onto the "long tail Berkshire can no longer chase" framing in Kyle's notes.
