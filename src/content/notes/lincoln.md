# Lincoln

This page is the 2012 [Steven Spielberg](/notes/steven-spielberg) film, not the president (see [Abraham Lincoln](/notes/abraham-lincoln) for the person). In the wiki it functions as one of Kyle's recurring movie-as-argument examples about the economics of creative work. He recounts that Spielberg "had almost been forced to release his film, Lincoln, as a mini-series instead of a full feature film because he couldn't get anyone to fund it! A movie made by Steven Spielberg, starring [Daniel Day-Lewis](/notes/daniel-day-lewis)!" — proof, in Kyle's telling, that "the studio model had pushed those unique stories into the periphery in favor of massive blockbuster potential."

The example appears in [The Existential Dread of Cognitive Dissonance](/essays/the-existential-dread-of-cognitive), where Kyle uses Hollywood's "business of blockbusters" — its tilt toward spin-offs, sequels, and reboots over unique stories — as an analogy for what venture capital is incentivized to build. It recurs in [The Hits Business](/essays/the-hits-business) as one of many films Kyle leans on to explain his thinking. The throughline: if even a Spielberg/Day-Lewis project nearly couldn't get funded as a feature, the hits-driven model systematically marginalizes original work.

**Context:** *Lincoln* (2012), directed by Steven Spielberg with Daniel Day-Lewis in the title role, dramatizes the final months of Abraham Lincoln's effort to pass the Thirteenth Amendment. Day-Lewis won the Academy Award for Best Actor for the performance.
