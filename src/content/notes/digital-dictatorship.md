# Digital Dictatorship

[Yuval Noah Harari](/notes/yuval-noah-harari)'s coinage in [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) for the political endpoint of merged infotech and biotech: a regime "in which all power is concentrated in the hands of a tiny elite while most people suffer not from exploitation but from something far worse — irrelevance." In Harari's framing, Big Data algorithms that can understand and predict humans better than they understand themselves dissolve the informational advantage that historically made centralized control inefficient — so the old practical case for distributing power (no single authority can process everything) no longer holds.

The concept is load-bearing for the book's argument about democracy. Harari states the stakes bluntly: "Democracy in its present form cannot survive the merger of biotech and infotech. Either democracy will successfully reinvent itself in a radically new form or humans will come to live in 'digital dictatorships.'" The term thus functions less as a prediction than as the failure mode against which Harari measures every proposed political reinvention.

**Context:** "Digital dictatorship" is a recurring term in Harari's later work (also developed in _Homo Deus_), describing how AI-driven mass surveillance and predictive data could enable authoritarian concentration of power more total than any 20th-century regime achieved.

## Where this appears

- [21 Lessons for the 21st Century](/books/21-lessons-for-the-21st-century) — Harari's name for the AI-enabled authoritarian endpoint; the failure mode that gates his argument about whether democracy can survive the infotech/biotech merger
