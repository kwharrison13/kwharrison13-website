# Jason Fried

Co-founder of 37signals (Basecamp) and, with [David Heinemeier Hansson](/notes/david-heinemeier-hansson), co-author of [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required) — the pre-COVID argument for distributed work that Kyle read in mid-2020s hindsight, finding most of its predictions now baseline. The book draws repeatedly on how 37signals itself operates: a roughly forty-hour week where employees freely distribute hours across the clock, whole-company in-person gatherings only about three times a year, and an aversion to constant face time on the theory that scarcity makes the rare meeting more valuable. Tools like Basecamp recur as the mechanism that makes remote accountability work — a "central online repository for tracking tasks and reporting progress" that creates an irrefutable record of what everyone is getting done and how long it's taking.

Fried surfaces again in [The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) as a venture-capital skeptic. [Sebastian Mallaby](/notes/sebastian-mallaby) cites Fried's 2019 declaration that venture capital "kills more businesses than it helps," because large VC war chests create pressure to spend before founders know how to spend wisely — captured in Fried's image of pouring "a whole fucking bucket of water" on a seed that only needed a little. The two threads are of a piece: a bootstrapped, capital-light, deliberately small company-building philosophy.

**Context:** Jason Fried is an American entrepreneur, co-founder and CEO of 37signals (formerly Basecamp), and co-author with David Heinemeier Hansson of *Getting Real*, *Rework*, *Remote*, and *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* — books that champion calm, profitable, bootstrapped software companies as an alternative to growth-at-all-costs.
