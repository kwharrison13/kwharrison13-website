# Pride cometh before the fall

A theme Kyle returns to repeatedly across his [Come, Follow Me](/notes/come-follow-me) study of the Book of Mormon, almost always tagged alongside [Pride](/notes/pride) and [The Pride Cycle](/notes/the-pride-cycle). It threads through the Nephite decline in Helaman 1–6 and Helaman 7–12, where it sits among the core themes of pride, sign-seeking, and humility. In Mormon 1–6 it pairs with [The Pride Cycle](/notes/the-pride-cycle), Godly Sorrow, and [Repentance](/notes/repentance) as the civilization-ending failure mode Mormon narrates.

Kyle also reads it at the personal scale in Alma 39–42 — [Alma the Younger](/notes/alma-the-younger)'s counsel to his son [Corianton](/notes/corianton) — where pride, the idle mind, and the call to "cross yourself" in [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery) are the path back, and where Kyle drew out the parenting insight that Alma spends only 14 verses on sin but 74 teaching about [Jesus Christ](/notes/jesus) first.

**Context:** The phrase echoes Proverbs 16:18 ("Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a fall"). In Book of Mormon theology it names the engine of "the pride cycle" — the repeating sequence of righteousness, prosperity, pride, destruction, humility, and repentance.
