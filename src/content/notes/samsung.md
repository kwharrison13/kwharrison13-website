# Samsung

Samsung appears in Kyle's wiki across two very different lenses. In the Cresta.ai - Diligence, Samsung Electronics America is a marquee customer-support logo: [Kostia Pertsovskyi](/notes/kostia-pertsovskyi) (Director, Go-to-Market — Strategy & Digital Transformation) describes a global rollout across 1,000+ support agents. COVID-era ecommerce growth and a D2C push made support a revenue driver (e.g., selling Samsung Care+), forcing them to move store-floor staff into call-center roles with heavy coaching needs. Samsung evaluated [Observe.ai](/notes/observe-ai) (chosen partly on its per-agent pricing model) and [Balto.ai](/notes/balto-ai) before re-evaluating Cresta; the diligence cites Samsung's measurable ROI (CSAT +20%, conversion +50%) and its praise for Cresta's non-black-box PII handling as evidence for the thesis — while also noting interaction-based pricing was a friction.

In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), Samsung is the case study in deliberate national-champion ascent. Lee Byung-chul built it "from a trader of dried fish into a tech company" via a three-strategy playbook: cultivate political relationships for favorable regulation and cheap capital; replicate Western/Japanese products at lower cost; and globalize relentlessly to learn from the best competitors. The book frames Samsung (with [Micron](/notes/micron)) as the low-cost DRAM producer that undercut Japan's overinvested chipmakers, and as one of the few firms — alongside [Intel](/notes/intel) and [TSMC](/notes/tsmc) — capable of fabricating the world's most advanced chips, with revenues equal to ~10% of South Korea's GDP.

**Context:** Samsung Group is a South Korean conglomerate; Samsung Electronics is its flagship, a global leader in memory chips (DRAM/NAND), smartphones, and consumer electronics, and one of the few semiconductor companies operating leading-edge logic foundries.
