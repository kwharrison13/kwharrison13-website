# Culture

Across Kyle's notes, culture appears as a competitive moat — something that outlasts a founder and is hard to duplicate. In the [Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters](/books/berkshire-hathaway-annual-letters), culture is named as one of Berkshire's three core advantages (alongside flexibility and "let opportunities thrive"). Buffett frames it as self-propagating ("Cultures self-propagate… You shape your houses and then they shape you") and durable enough to survive succession: "the special Berkshire culture is deeply ingrained throughout our subsidiaries, and these operations won't miss a beat when I die." Kyle's marginal note flags the theme directly — "Creating a [Culture](/notes/culture) that outlasts you" — and elsewhere ties it to running "at low cost / high quality" the way [Costco](/notes/costco) and [Amazon](/notes/amazon) do.

[What It Takes](/books/what-it-takes) (Stephen Schwarzman's memoir) treats culture as [Blackstone](/notes/blackstone)'s defining asset: "Blackstone is a remarkable success because of our culture" — meritocracy, excellence, openness, and intellectual integrity, hiring only people who share those beliefs. The concrete artifact is the open Monday-morning meeting where "all that mattered was the quality of their thinking," regardless of age or rank — a standing demonstration of transparency and equality. Schwarzman casts his long-tenured senior leaders as the culture's "most reliable guardians," echoing Buffett's succession framing.

**Context:** "Culture as competitive moat" is a recurring theme in business writing — the idea that durable advantage comes less from any single product than from norms and incentives that reproduce themselves and resist imitation.
