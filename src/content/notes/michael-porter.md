# Michael Porter

Porter appears across the wiki as the authority on competitive strategy and national competitiveness. In [No Apology](/books/no-apology), Mitt Romney leans on Porter to make an economic argument: he quotes Porter's claim that "National advantage is enhanced by stringent standards that are rapidly, efficiently, and consistently applied" (against the idea that standards drag on the economy), and cites *The Competitive Advantage of Nations* on education and training being "decisive in national competitive advantage." [Nathan Baschez](/notes/nathan-baschez) reaches for Porter from a different angle: he argues that Porter's "bargaining power of suppliers" reads very differently when the suppliers are workers — that decimating the labor movement cost workers compounding gains and a more equitable society, with disproportionate impact on Black Americans. Together the two references show Porter's framework being used both to defend national-competitiveness policy and to critique labor-power dynamics.

**Context:** Michael Porter is a Harvard Business School professor whose work on the Five Forces, competitive advantage, and *The Competitive Advantage of Nations* is foundational to modern competitive strategy.

## Where this appears

- [No Apology](/books/no-apology) — cited twice (stringent standards enhancing national advantage; education/training as decisive) as an authority in Romney's economic argument.
- [Nathan Baschez](/notes/nathan-baschez) — invokes Porter's "bargaining power of suppliers" to argue that weakening labor power cost workers compounding equity over time.
- [Invisible Companies](/notes/invisible-companies) — his barriers-to-entry framework is cited (alongside Jay Barney's) as one of the two classic explanations the piece argues can't account for "invisible companies."
