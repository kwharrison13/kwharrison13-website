# Andy Grove

Intel CEO whose paranoia is treated across Kyle's reading as the archetype of corporate strategic vigilance. In [Chip War](/books/chip-war), Grove "disrupts Intel himself" — recognizing the company's DRAM-memory business model was broken and abandoning it to bet everything on microprocessors, establishing Intel's stranglehold on PC chips. The book frames his ruthlessness as definitional Silicon Valley capitalism: 8 a.m. sharp workdays, public criticism of latecomers, and "constructive confrontation" between employees. His own summary — *"Only the paranoid survive"* — is repeatedly cited; the book argues it was paranoia, "more than innovation or expertise," that saved Intel. Late in life Grove turned that paranoia outward, warning that "abandoning today's 'commodity' manufacturing can lock you out of tomorrow's emerging industry," pointing to how the U.S. lost its lead in batteries after it stopped making consumer electronics; he proposed taxing the product of offshored labor and treating the resulting trade war "like other wars — fight to win." [Chip War](/books/chip-war) closes by saying America "is in desperate need of a new Andy Grove."

[Boom — Bubbles & The End of Stagnation](/books/boom-bubbles-and-the-end-of-stagnation) uses Grove to make a structural point: it was "no coincidence" that Intel's Grove wrote the canonical book on tech-company strategy once chips shifted from rewarding pure technological wizardry (the NASA era) to rewarding strategic brilliance (as fab costs rose) — nor a coincidence he called it *Only the Paranoid Survive*. It also draws on Michael Malone's "Intel trinity" to note that Grove's abrasive focus and Noyce's charisma were complementary, clashing personalities forced to collaborate. [The Start-Up of You](/books/the-start-up-of-you) opens with the same maxim, repurposing it as a career posture — framing "permanent beta" vigilance about one's own skills as the individual analogue of Grove's corporate paranoia.

**Context:** Andrew "Andy" Grove (1936–2016) was a Hungarian-born refugee who became Intel's third employee and its CEO from 1987 to 1998, presiding over its dominance of the PC-microprocessor era. His 1996 book *Only the Paranoid Survive* popularized the idea of the "strategic inflection point."
