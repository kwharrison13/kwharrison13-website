# Aggregation Theory

[Ben Thompson](/notes/ben-thompson)'s framework (from [Stratechery](/notes/stratechery)) used throughout the platform analysis in [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public). The load-bearing distinction is **platforms vs. aggregators**: as the book quotes Thompson, "Platforms deliver value to third parties that build on top of them, whereas aggregators are pure intermediaries." [Nadia Eghbal](/notes/nadia-eghbal) applies this to open source — GitHub as a platform third-party developers build on, and the "faceless publisher" dynamic of social platforms — to analyze how the relationship between platforms and their creators is reshaping the online world. The book draws on adjacent Thompson framings too: subscriptions as paying for "the regular delivery of well-defined value," and his observation (via the music industry) that software producers, like record labels selling plastic discs, "have never really figured out how to sell code itself."

**Context:** Aggregation Theory, coined by Ben Thompson on Stratechery, holds that the internet zeroed the cost of distribution and transactions, so the most valuable companies win by owning the demand-side user relationship and commoditizing suppliers — distinct from traditional platforms that create value for the third parties built atop them.
