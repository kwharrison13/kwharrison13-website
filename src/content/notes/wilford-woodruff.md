# Wilford Woodruff

Wilford Woodruff appears across Kyle's LDS reading in two main roles: as the fourth Church president who issued the 1890 Manifesto ending the public practice of plural marriage, and as the great diarist of nineteenth-century Mormonism. In [Saints: No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) his Manifesto is "the dramatic spine" of Kyle's highlights — Woodruff weighing the loss of the temples and their ordinances against holding the line on [Polygamy](/notes/polygamy) and choosing "to act for the temporal salvation of the Church." His theology of revelation is the live question Kyle probes in the margins: "inspiration *is* revelation… a man is not always required to say 'Thus saith the Lord,'" which sits directly on [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept) and [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation) — *why is Woodruff's medium of revelatory reception different from [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s?* and *can a commandment ever be irrevocable?* The same Manifesto line recurs in [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind): "the Lord will never permit me… to lead you astray," which the Hafens place alongside [Russell M. Nelson](/notes/russell-m-nelson)'s "give your leaders a little leeway" and [Dallin H. Oaks](/notes/dallin-h-oaks)'s "we don't believe in the infallibility of our leaders" — i.e., "won't lead you astray" does not mean the prophet always tells us exactly what to do.

As a diarist, Woodruff is, per [American Zion](/books/american-zion) and [Joseph Smith: Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling), the trek's "best chronicler" — he began his diary in 1835 and kept daily records for sixty years, producing thirty volumes and more than six thousand pages, "one of nineteenth-century Mormonism's most crucial records" (a [Journals](/notes/journals) exemplar; with [John Taylor](/notes/john-taylor) he preserved Joseph's 1839 teachings on revelation). [American Zion](/books/american-zion) also tracks his political role: describing the leaderless Nauvoo church as "sheep without a shepherd" after Joseph's death, his fury at the approaching U.S. army ("the United States had turned mob"), his vow to "go to prison and to Death" rather than forfeit divine law, and his hardball after the Manifesto — even threatening to deny dissenting apostles entry to the Salt Lake Temple dedication. In [Brigham Young: Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) he is the apostle [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) confided in about the Order of Enoch, trusting "only… you & me" to manage property righteously.

**Context:** Wilford Woodruff (1807–1898) was the fourth president of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (1889–1898). He is best known for the 1890 Manifesto (Official Declaration 1) that officially ended the Church's sanction of new plural marriages, and for his extensive personal journals, now a major primary source for Mormon history.
