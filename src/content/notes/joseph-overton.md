# Joseph Overton

Joseph Overton is the policy analyst who named the [Overton Window](/notes/overton-window) — the band of policy ideas politically "acceptable" at a given moment. He enters Kyle's notes through [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), which quotes Overton ("Why is it that so many good ideas don't get taken seriously?") and paraphrases his core insight: politicians who want to be reelected can't permit themselves viewpoints seen as too extreme, so to hold power they must keep their ideas within the margins of what's acceptable. [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman) turns this into the mechanism of utopian politics — to make a radical idea reasonable, you stretch the bounds of the radical and shift the whole window — dovetailing it with [Milton Friedman](/notes/milton-friedman)'s claim that ideas "lying around" during a crisis become the ones adopted.

**Context:** Joseph P. Overton (1960–2003) was a senior vice president at the free-market Mackinac Center for Public Policy in Michigan, where he developed the concept later named the "Overton Window" of politically feasible policy.

## Where this appears

- [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists) — [Rutger Bregman](/notes/rutger-bregman) quotes Overton and uses his window as the lever by which today's radical becomes tomorrow's common sense.
- [Overton Window](/notes/overton-window) — the concept page for the model Overton originated.
