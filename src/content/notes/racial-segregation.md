# Racial Segregation

In [Evicted](/books/evicted), Matthew Desmond treats racial segregation not as an accident of urbanization but as engineered social design with beneficiaries. Kyle's notes capture the book's central reframe: "Most Milwaukeeans believed their city was racially segregated because people preferred it that way. But the ghetto had always been more a product of social design than desire… a main feature of landed capital, a prime moneymaker for those who saw ripe opportunity in land scarcity, housing dilapidation, and racial segregation." The poor, Desmond writes, didn't crowd into slums because housing was cheap but "simply because they were allowed to be."

Segregation also compounds through ostensibly neutral processes. Eviction in Milwaukee is "gendered and racialized the way incarceration is" — "Poor black men were locked up. Poor black women were locked out" — so that even landlords applying "the same standards to everyone" reproduce inequality, because criminal and eviction screens fall hardest on African Americans. The book ties this to [Income Inequality](/notes/income-inequality) and warns that large-scale Public Housing risks "repeating the failures of the past… contributing to racial segregation and concentrated poverty." See also [City Building](/notes/city-building) on landlord screening distributing advantage block by block.

**Context:** Racial segregation is the separation of people into residential, social, or institutional spheres along racial lines. In American housing, it was actively produced through redlining, restrictive covenants, and discriminatory lending, not merely individual preference.
