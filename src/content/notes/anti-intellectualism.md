# Anti-intellectualism

In [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens), anti-intellectualism names the organizing tension of [Eugene England](/notes/eugene-england)'s life: a mid-twentieth-century Latter-day Saint culture that had retreated from its intellectual heyday into an orthodoxy of doctrine-over-theology and deference to institutional authority. The book sets England's "passion to translate church doctrines into a more organic, coherent framework for discipleship" against "a Mormon cultural commandment: Do not act in defiance of prevailing church norms of thought and behavior." England, the page notes, "had not questioned church doctrine or authority, but he had challenged" that cultural commandment — and paid for it in conflicts with figures like [Boyd K. Packer](/notes/boyd-k-packer) ("The Mantle Is Far, Far Greater than the Intellect") and [Bruce R. McConkie](/notes/bruce-r-mcconkie) (the "Seven Deadly Heresies" talk).

The book frames this as a fall from an earlier openness. It recalls that "[Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s commitment to the life of the mind, personally and institutionally, was absolute" — he studied German, Hebrew, and Egyptian, organized a School of the Prophets, and declared "Intelligence is the great object of our holy religion." Smith "bridled at orthodoxy tests," wanting "the liberty of believing as I please." England's project, as the wiki reads it, was to recover that founding intellectual confidence against a later institutional retreat — the central paradox of "personal autonomy and intellectual freedom contending against prophetic authority and institutional power."

**Context:** Anti-intellectualism is hostility toward or devaluation of intellectual life and expertise. Richard Hofstadter's *Anti-Intellectualism in American Life* (1963) is the classic study of the term in the American context; here it is applied specifically to mid-century Mormon religious culture.
