# Venture Returns

A recurring subject in Kyle's venture-investing essays: how returns are actually generated in venture capital, and whether the headline numbers reflect skill or structure. The load-bearing source is [Aswath Damodaran](/notes/aswath-damodaran)'s round-by-round breakdown of the [Instacart](/notes/instacart) IPO, which anchors The Value Chain of Capital - Research. Damodaran's framing — *"most successful VCs share more with successful traders than they do successful investors… they play the pricing game"* — reframes venture returns as a function of entry/exit timing and reflexive pricing (what the *next* investor will pay) rather than fundamental business judgment. Two threads Kyle extracts: **selection bias** (VC's "superstar" reputation is a survivorship artifact of the success stories that get written about) and the **winner-stays-winner flywheel** (a brand-name VC wins better terms *because* founders want them on the cap table to draw in other VCs — the structural seed of the essay's "back-scratch circle").

The Instacart arithmetic makes the dependency concrete: early-investor returns rest on roughly $1.8B of later, largely unprofitable capital — a company's "terminal value" is dependent on downstream, often money-losing rounds, so any round's "worth" is just what the next investor decides to pay. In The Puritans of Venture Capital - Research, venture returns appear alongside the [Power Law](/notes/power-law) as the distribution that splits venture into different games: a Cottage Keeper underwriting 5–10x out of a small fund and a Capital Agglomerator content with 2–3x out of the same-size check are operating on different return thresholds entirely.

**Context:** In venture capital the "power law" describes how a small number of investments (often one fund-returner) generate the overwhelming majority of a fund's returns, while most portfolio companies return little or nothing — making venture returns a fundamentally skewed, top-heavy distribution rather than a normal one.
