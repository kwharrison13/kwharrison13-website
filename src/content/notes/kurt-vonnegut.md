# Kurt Vonnegut

The novelist surfaces in Kyle's notes through two quotes he returns to repeatedly. The first is the **builders-vs-caretakers** line — "Another flaw in the human character is that everybody wants to build and nobody wants to do maintenance" — which Kyle uses as a counterweight to the creation bias in knowledge work. In [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public), it is the Vonnegut quote he "repeatedly connects to" the book's core tension that "creation is an intrinsic motivator, maintenance usually requires extrinsic motivation" (Kyle's own marginalia: *Connect to Kurt Vonnegut quote*). The same line appears in the June 6th, 2022 — Julie Chen (Retool) notes and in Historical Futurism - Research, where it points straight to [Maintenance](/notes/maintenance).

The second is the **automation-future** framing. [The AppetiZIRP](/notes/the-appetizirp) (Packy McCormick) casts Vonnegut as the pessimistic pole of the automation debate via his 1952 novel *Player Piano*: mechanization "creates widespread unemployment, unrest, and ennui," and "it was too bad for the human beings who got their dignity from their jobs." Historical Futurism - Research crystallizes the split — "Is automation going to lead to a depressing, meaningless future of work or a beautiful world of aristocratic abundance for all? It depends if you believe [Kurt Vonnegut](/notes/kurt-vonnegut) or [Isaac Asimov](/notes/isaac-asimov)" — making Vonnegut the dark counterpoint to Asimov's optimism.

**Context:** Kurt Vonnegut (1922–2007) was an American novelist known for satirical, science-fiction-inflected works including *Slaughterhouse-Five*, *Cat's Cradle*, and his debut *Player Piano* (1952), a dystopia about a society hollowed out by automation.
