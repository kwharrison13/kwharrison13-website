# Singularity

In Kyle's reading, the "singularity" surfaces along two linked threads. [The Man From The Future](/books/the-man-from-the-future) traces the term to [John von Neumann](/notes/john-von-neumann), who is credited with coining it in the context of accelerating technological change and the speculation that machine intelligence could outpace human comprehension. The idea then reappears as the central engine of [2054](/books/2054), the [Elliot Ackerman](/notes/elliot-ackerman) / [James Stavridis](/notes/james-stavridis) near-future novel, which dramatizes a "singularity race" — featuring [Ray Kurzweil](/notes/ray-kurzweil) as a character clinging to life to witness "bio-integrated quantum computing" fuse biological and technological evolution.

Across both sources, the singularity functions less as a fixed technical milestone than as a horizon: the point at which technological and biological evolution merge or where machine capability crosses a threshold beyond prediction. The novels treat it as a strategic and geopolitical stake, not just a futurist abstraction.

**Context:** The "technological singularity" is the hypothesized point at which artificial intelligence becomes capable of recursive self-improvement, triggering runaway growth beyond human control or comprehension. Von Neumann is often credited with an early framing of the idea; Ray Kurzweil popularized it in *The Singularity Is Near* (2005).
