# Boris Yeltsin

Russia's first post-Soviet president as portrayed in [The Invention of Russia](/books/the-invention-of-russia), where he is the load-bearing example of how fragile 1990s press freedom actually was. The book's sharpest line: "if Russia had freedom of speech in the 1990s, it was not because there was an inbuilt tradition or craving for it but because Yeltsin allowed it." That toleration rather than tradition framing is what makes the book's larger argument work — as one figure recalled, "If Yeltsin was the only guarantor of our freedom, then we would be finished sooner or later," foreshadowing the reversal under [Vladimir Putin](/notes/vladimir-putin). The book also notes Yeltsin's generational instinct: mistrustful of Gorbachev's reformers, he surrounded himself with people decades younger, a shift even more pronounced among those who came to control the media and the country's narrative. Tellingly, unlike Putin's later obsession with television, Yeltsin "rarely watched himself on television and simply turned off the channels he disliked."

**Context:** Boris Yeltsin (1931–2007) was President of Russia from 1991 to 1999, presiding over the breakup of the Soviet Union, the turbulent market reforms and privatizations of the 1990s, and ultimately handpicking Vladimir Putin as his successor.
