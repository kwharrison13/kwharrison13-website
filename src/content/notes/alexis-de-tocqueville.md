# Alexis de Tocqueville

The French political thinker and author of *Democracy in America* recurs across Kyle's corpus as the canonical observer of American civic and democratic character, deployed for several distinct arguments. As the seed of **citizenship through local self-interest**, [Evicted](/books/evicted) quotes his line that a man takes interest in the whole state only "if it is a question of taking a road past his property" — Matthew Desmond builds on it to argue that stable housing is the precondition for engaged citizenship ("only after we begin to see a street as our street…"). On **associational life**, the The Talent Vortex - Research notes pull his passage that "Americans of all ages, all conditions, all minds constantly unite" into a thousand kinds of associations — paired there with Franklin's [Junto](/notes/junto) as evidence for the collective-over-individual thesis.

He also supplies the recurring frame of **majority tyranny**: in [American Zion](/books/american-zion) and [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling), Tocqueville's "tyranny of the majority" names the Jacksonian-era democracy ([Andrew Jackson](/notes/andrew-jackson)) that trampled the early Latter-day Saints, "the classic dilemma of democratic society." And on **anti-expertise**, [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise) cites his 1835 observation that early Americans were not "enamored of experts," each appealing "only to the individual effort of his own understanding," while [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death) uses his notes on American pamphlet culture and the legal profession as "a privileged body in the scale of intellect." [2054](/books/2054) (Ackerman & Stavridis) closes the novel on his line from *Democracy in America* about the uncertain "issue of the struggle."

**Context:** Alexis de Tocqueville (1805–1859) was a French aristocrat, political scientist, and historian whose *Democracy in America* (1835/1840), written after touring the United States, remains a foundational analysis of democratic society, civic association, and equality.
