# Learned Helplessness

The psychological condition in which a person stops believing their efforts affect outcomes, and so either gives up or overcompensates. [Essentialism](/books/essentialism) uses the example of a child who struggles early with mathematics, tries repeatedly without improving, and eventually concludes nothing he does will matter. Greg McKeown observes the same pattern in organizations: when people believe their work doesn't matter, they either check out and stop trying, or — less obviously — become hyperactive "do-it-all"-ers who accept every opportunity and assignment. That second response only looks like belief in one's value; underneath, it is the conviction that one "has to do it all" rather than getting to choose, which McKeown reads as a smokescreen form of the same helplessness.

In [Hillbilly Elegy](/books/hillbilly-elegy), [J. D. Vance](/notes/j-d-vance) names the term directly to describe his own youth: the belief "that the choices I made had no effect on the outcomes in my life." The wiki note pairs it with the home message that countered the neighborhood's fatalism — "I received a different message at home. And that just might have saved me" — linking it to [Identity Formation](/notes/identity-formation) as the counterforce to inherited helplessness.

**Context:** "Learned helplessness" was coined by psychologist Martin Seligman from 1960s experiments showing that subjects conditioned to experience uncontrollable outcomes stopped trying to escape even when escape became possible. It is now a foundational concept in research on depression, motivation, and resilience.
