# Oliver Cowdery

Oliver Cowdery appears across Kyle's reading as [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s principal scribe — the man who took over full-time dictation of the Book of Mormon in April 1829. In Grant Palmer's [An Insider's View of Mormon Origins](/books/an-insider-s-view-of-mormon-origins), Cowdery is a key witness to Joseph's stone-based method: shortly after becoming scribe he "learned that just as the plates were not necessary for translation, other source documents could be read without being present," as when Joseph "translated" through a stone the answer to whether John the Revelator was on earth or in heaven (D&C 7). Palmer also uses Cowdery to date the environmental influences on early Mormon theology — Cowdery quoted lengthy excerpts from Thomas Dick's *Philosophy of a Future State* in the *Messenger and Advocate* in December 1836.

In [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling), Richard Bushman casts Cowdery (alongside [Sidney Rigdon](/notes/sidney-rigdon)) as an early associate whose later drift into republican "vox populi" rhetoric typified the church-vs-democracy fracture — a tension Bushman draws out via the observation that "the people" had no political standing in Mormon thought, since "in the Church, God was sovereign." Cowdery also rides along in the Enoch revelation, which "came while Oliver Cowdery and the missionaries to the Lamanites headed west to find a site for the City of Zion." In Kyle's gospel-study notes on Ether, Cowdery surfaces devotionally: his charge to the early apostles — "Never cease striving until you have seen God face to face" — frames the theme of seeing the face of God by revelation.

**Context:** Oliver Cowdery (1806–1850) was the second elder of the Latter-day Saint movement, Joseph Smith's chief Book of Mormon scribe, and one of the Three Witnesses to the gold plates. He left the church in 1838 after disputes but was rebaptized shortly before his death.
