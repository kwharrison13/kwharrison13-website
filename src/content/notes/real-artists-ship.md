# Real Artists Ship

"Real artists ship" is [Steve Jobs](/notes/steve-jobs)'s maxim that surfaces in Walter Isaacson's [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) as the explicit countermaxim to Leonardo's perfectionism. Isaacson frames it as the tension between two rules for creative work: Leonardo embodied "let the perfect be the enemy of the good" — he refused to let go of a painting (the *Mona Lisa* traveled with him for years) until it was perfect, often never delivering at all — while Jobs eventually embraced the opposite, that "sometimes you ought to deliver a product even when there are still improvements that could be made."

In the book this is not presented as a clean verdict for one side. Isaacson calls shipping "a good rule for daily life" but adds "there are times when it's nice to be like Leonardo and not let go of something until it's perfect." Jobs recurs throughout the biography as Isaacson's modern foil for Leonardo — also on the central-atrium / serendipitous-collision theory of ideas — so the "ship vs. perfect" pairing sits inside a larger vision-versus-execution thread that runs across the book.

**Context:** "Real artists ship" is widely attributed to Steve Jobs from the early-1980s Macintosh development era, used to insist that finishing and releasing a product is what separates real creators from perpetual tinkerers. Isaacson, who also wrote the authorized Jobs biography, repeatedly juxtaposes the two figures.
