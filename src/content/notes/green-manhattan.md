# Green Manhattan

The "density-is-green" argument — the counterintuitive claim that dense urban living (Manhattan as the archetype) is environmentally efficient relative to suburban sprawl, because shared infrastructure, walkability, and small living spaces cut per-capita energy use. In [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis), Kyle invokes it against sprawl's energy inefficiency, pairing it with the [Distributed Energy Model](/notes/distributed-energy-model). His marginal note ties it directly to his own [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) thinking: *"Connect to [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) and the New Yorker article from Cambridge about how green dense cities are — Green Manhattan."*

**Context:** "Green Manhattan" is the title of a 2004 New Yorker essay by David Owen, later expanded into the book *Green Metropolis*, arguing that Manhattan is one of the most environmentally efficient places in America precisely because of its extreme density.
