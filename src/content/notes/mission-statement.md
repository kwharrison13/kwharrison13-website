# Mission Statement

In [Essentialism](/books/essentialism), the mission statement is used as a foil for what [Greg McKeown](/notes/greg-mckeown) calls "essential intent." Kyle's note distills the book's line: "It is the concrete intent, not the words, that matter in a mission statement." McKeown maps statements onto a matrix of inspirational-vs-concrete and meaningful-vs-measurable. Vision and mission statements like "We want to change the world" sound inspirational but are so general they get ignored; vague values like "innovation," "leadership," and "teamwork" are too bland to inspire passion; short-term quarterly objectives ("increase profits 5 percent") are concrete enough to get attention but lack inspiration. An essential intent, by contrast, is both inspirational and concrete, both meaningful and measurable — "one decision that settles one thousand later decisions," like deciding to become a doctor instead of a lawyer. The book's "digital champion" example shows clear, essential intent expressed in a few syllables rather than three paragraphs.

**Context:** A mission statement is a formal summary of an organization's purpose and primary objectives, a staple of corporate strategy and management literature — and a frequent target of critiques (like McKeown's) that they collapse into generic, unmemorable language.
