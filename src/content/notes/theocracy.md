# Theocracy

Across Kyle's two histories of Mormonism, theocracy names the church's nineteenth-century fusion of ecclesiastical and political power — and the central reason it collided with the United States. In [American Zion](/books/american-zion), the Saints' "twin allegiances to theocracy and polygamy" are "deemed incongruous with American democracy," driving the federal conflict that at one point sent the army to Utah to quell what Washington read as an insurrection (four years before the Civil War). That legal struggle "would come to shape how American law still defines the limits of religious liberty today." The book also tracks theocracy's decline: the death of George Q. Cannon in 1901 "marked the passing of the old," his theocratic legacy "buried with him." And it lists theocracy among the doctrines the church later "forfeited" — alongside polygamy and the racial restriction — while still "proclaiming compliance with eternal laws that never bend."

In [Brigham Young: Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet), theocracy is the concrete political form [Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young) built in the Salt Lake Valley: "a theocratic kingdom resisting encroachments on its autonomy." The process of "colonizing the Great Basin, building a theocratic kingdom, and resisting the force of the U.S. government had fashioned a Mormon people." Young tolerated non-Mormons but warned them not to "blaspheme the God of Israel" — a posture the book situates within mainstream mid-century American attitudes toward religious minorities and blasphemy law.

**Context:** A theocracy is a system in which religious authority and civil government are merged, with rulers governing in the name of, or as agents of, a deity. The nineteenth-century Utah Territory under Brigham Young is a standard historical example, with church leadership exercising both spiritual and temporal authority.
