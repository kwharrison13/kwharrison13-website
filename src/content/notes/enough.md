# Enough

In [The Psychology of Money](/books/the-psychology-of-money), [Morgan Housel](/notes/morgan-housel) frames **"enough"** as the hardest financial skill: getting the goalpost to stop moving. The anchor is the [John Bogle](/notes/john-bogle) anecdote — at a billionaire's party, Kurt Vonnegut tells Joseph Heller that their hedge-fund-manager host made more in a single day than Heller earned from *Catch-22* over its whole history, and Heller replies, *"Yes, but I have something he will never have… enough."* Housel's gloss: "for a critical element of our society, including many of the wealthiest and most powerful among us, there seems to be no limit today on what enough entails."

The danger, in Housel's telling, "gets dangerous when the taste of having more — more money, more power, more prestige — increases ambition faster than satisfaction," driven by social comparison. He poses the Gupta/Madoff question: why would someone worth hundreds of millions risk everything for more? — and answers with the rule "there is no reason to risk what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need." The concept underwrites the book's broader argument about [Financial Independence](/notes/financial-independence) and lets [Compounding](/notes/compounding) actually run, since the person who never has enough keeps moving the finish line and selling early.
