# Oculus

Oculus is the VR company [Palmer Luckey](/notes/palmer-luckey) founded before [Anduril](/notes/anduril), and in Kyle's corpus it functions as the origin of Luckey's "Layer 2 naysayer" arc — the case study for conviction holding firm against social disapproval. In [Conviction-Led Contrarianism](/essays/conviction-led-contrarianism), Kyle frames Luckey as someone who "persevere[d] through" Layer 2 dependencies "first with Oculus because of his intense conviction in and passion for VR," then again with Anduril — despite investors saying VR "would never produce a large company." Oculus is the first instance of the pattern the whole Luckey thesis rests on.

The [Beliefs, Blockbusters, and Boyhood](/essays/beliefs-blockbusters-and-boyhood) essay supplies the biographical detail Kyle draws on: Luckey "was the founder of Oculus which he sold to Facebook for $2B," and was later fired from Facebook over a small political donation — an experience he calls "one of the most formative" of his life and the source of his chip-on-the-shoulder. Kyle reads the sale itself as conviction, not cash-out: Luckey said no to a $1B offer and sold only when Facebook also committed to investing roughly $1B a year into VR for ten years — "Palmer sold Oculus because of what that kind of investment would mean for the progress in VR."

**Context:** Oculus VR was a virtual-reality company founded by Palmer Luckey in 2012 (around the Oculus Rift headset, born from a Kickstarter campaign) and acquired by Facebook in 2014 for about $2 billion; it became the foundation of Meta's VR/Quest line.
