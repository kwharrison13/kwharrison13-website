# Self-Criticism

One of Kyle's most deliberately cultivated mental habits — he keeps a #[Self-Criticism](/notes/self-criticism) tag and built a whole section of his essay [Networked Conviction — Roam + Investing](/essays/networked-conviction-roam-investing) around the quotes that "help me understand the need for criticizing my own ideas." The defining formulation comes from [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) (citing Einstein): mental success owes to "curiosity, concentration, perseverance, and *self-criticism*," where self-criticism means "becoming good at destroying your own best-loved and hardest-won ideas" — a great gift. Munger's companion "iron prescription" runs through Kyle's notes too: "I'm not entitled to have an opinion unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people who are in opposition" (Books 2.0 - Research). Across the four Munger long-reads ([Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment), [Charlie Munger — Academic Economics](/notes/charlie-munger-academic-economics), and the others) this is the recurring throughline — staying open to changing your mind, "thinking in reverse" like [Charles Darwin](/notes/charles-darwin) to disprove your own best ideas.

The theme then recurs as a pattern Kyle finds in great minds generally. [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) abandoned a theory the moment an experiment disproved it — "one mark of a great mind is the willingness to change it." [How To Take Smart Notes](/books/how-to-take-smart-notes) frames Darwin as the model of disciplined self-criticism, writing down disconfirming arguments "without fail and at once," and pairs the idea with [Confirmation Bias](/notes/confirmation-bias). And [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart) applies it to [Sam Walton](/notes/sam-walton), who "cared about winning, not about being right" and wasn't afraid to destroy old beliefs when new evidence contradicted them — Kyle's framing question being "how often can you change your mind?"
