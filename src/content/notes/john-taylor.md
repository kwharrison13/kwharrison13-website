# John Taylor

John Taylor (1808–1887) was the third president of the LDS Church, and across Kyle's corpus he surfaces in two distinct registers. As a figure of conviction, he is the subject of [B.H. Roberts](/notes/b-h-roberts)'s reverent biography [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor), which casts him as "the embodiment of those qualities of mind and heart which most become a man" — a man over whose mind "the praise or censure of the world had little influence" where truth was concerned. In [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) he appears as one of the high-ranking leaders ([Brigham Young](/notes/brigham-young)'s "heavy-handed" later leadership bruised — alongside Orson Pratt, Orson Hyde, and Franklin Richards) and in [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) as a leader during the polygamy/Manifesto crisis whose son later found a purported revelation among his papers suggesting plural marriage would never be revoked.

His most generative appearance in the corpus is in [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion), where he is the architect of a distinctly economic theology. Taylor established Boards of Trade to coordinate LDS economic activity against the rise of monopolistic big business — a structure the authors note prefigures the central-coordination-plus-local-autonomy model of the Mondragon cooperatives. His charge was that financiering should serve "the poor, for the working man... act in the interest of the community, for the welfare of Zion," with the goal that no one go "crying for bread, or suffering for want of employment." He framed the [United Order](/notes/united-order) not as a fixed utopian state achieved by decree but as a continuum reached "line upon line" — "it is not articles and constitutions that make the united order, but the steady work of getting the united order into the hearts of the people." He also articulated a maximalist claim on truth ("Is there a true principle of science... of music... of government? It is ours, it is God's") and the conviction that the restored gospel exists "to introduce correct principles of every kind." In the gospel-study material he is quoted on teaching: "It is true intelligence for a man to take a subject that is mysterious and great in itself and to unfold and simplify it so that a child can understand" (Teaching Repentance).

**Context:** John Taylor was an English-born convert who survived the gunfire at Carthage Jail alongside [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) in 1844, led the LDS Church from 1880 until his death in 1887, and spent his final years in hiding ("on the underground") during the federal anti-polygamy raids.

## Where this appears

- [The Life of John Taylor](/books/the-life-of-john-taylor) — B.H. Roberts's biography; Taylor as a man of unbending conviction whom "the praise or censure of the world had little influence over."
- [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) — Taylor as economic theologian: Boards of Trade, full employment for all in the stake, and the [United Order](/notes/united-order) as a "line upon line" continuum rather than a fixed utopia.
- [Brigham Young — Pioneer Prophet](/books/brigham-young-pioneer-prophet) — one of the leaders whose feelings remained "bruised" by Brigham Young's heavy-handed later leadership.
- [Saints — No Unhallowed Hand](/books/saints-no-unhallowed-hand) — a leader through the polygamy crisis; the posthumous "1886 revelation" found among his papers.
- Teaching Repentance — quoted on "true intelligence" being the ability to simplify a great subject so a child can understand.
