# Eugene England

Eugene England is the subject of [Terryl Givens](/notes/terryl-givens)'s biography [Stretching The Heavens](/books/stretching-the-heavens), which Kyle read as the story of a Mormon thinker in tension with his own institution — "founder of Mormon studies, Dialogue editor, and disciple-in-exile." In Kyle's notes England emerges through several recurring threads: "learning to be righteous in the dark" (the instinct, echoing [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), that constant divine contact "moves towards the devil's plan" rather than the saint's); the "branch breaking from the tree" framing of sects that separate and lose connection to the broader Christian tradition; and an "ultimate universalism" recovered from [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s King Follett theosis and doctrine of eternal progression.

England held up [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) as his standard of orthodoxy and antidogmatism, while his conflicts with institutional authority run through the biography's cast — [Boyd K. Packer](/notes/boyd-k-packer)'s "The Mantle Is Far, Far Greater than the Intellect" and counsel to resign Dialogue, [Bruce R. McConkie](/notes/bruce-r-mcconkie)'s "Seven Deadly Heresies" public censure over God's progression, and [Leonard Arrington](/notes/leonard-arrington), who hired England and judged him blacklisted. He is cross-referenced in [The Faith of a Scientist](/books/the-faith-of-a-scientist) as a parallel LDS voice reconciling intellect and faith alongside Henry Eyring.

**Context:** Eugene England (1933–2001) was an American writer, literary scholar, and BYU English professor who co-founded _Dialogue: A Journal of Mormon Thought_ in 1966 and is widely regarded as a founder of the field of Mormon studies. His best-known essay is "Why the Church Is as True as the Gospel."
