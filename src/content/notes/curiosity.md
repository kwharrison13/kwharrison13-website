# Curiosity

A recurring virtue across Kyle's library — and one his sources treat with productive tension. In [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci) it is the master trait: Leonardo (like [Albert Einstein](/notes/albert-einstein)) never outgrew the childlike questions most people abandon by age ten, and Einstein's "I have no special talents… I am just passionately curious" frames curiosity as the engine of genius. [Range](/books/range) extends this to forecasting and innovation — Tetlock's best forecasters are "genuinely curious about… really everything," and "curious dilettantes" merging strands of public knowledge make connections at the cutting edge. [The Science of Learning](/notes/the-science-of-learning) names curiosity (with diligence) one of the core "habits of mind," alongside the three ingredients of learning: motivation, opportunity, and sustained attention.

But two sources complicate the picture. [Stop Asking Questions](/books/stop-asking-questions) warns that *naked* curiosity reads as neediness and lowers the quality of answers — "asking questions makes you seem needy… which reduces the quality of their answers" — so curiosity must be guided, not displayed. [Braving The Wilderness](/books/braving-the-wilderness) observes the social cost: in a fitting-in culture, "curiosity is seen as weakness and asking questions equates to antagonism rather than being valued as learning." The [Capital Allocator Mindset](/notes/capital-allocator-mindset) page sharpens the discipline further — the "dollar question" separates cheap *stated* curiosity from revealed priority, treating attention itself as capital to allocate.

**Context:** Curiosity is the disposition to seek out new information and experiences; psychology research links it to learning, memory, and well-being, and distinguishes "diversive" (novelty-seeking) from "epistemic" (knowledge-seeking) forms.
