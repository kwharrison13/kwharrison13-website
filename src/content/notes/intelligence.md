# Intelligence

Two distinct senses of "intelligence" meet on this page from opposite ends of Kyle's reading. In [The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog](/books/the-boy-who-was-raised-as-a-dog), Bruce Perry gives a neuroscientific definition: "Intelligence is basically faster information processing: a person requires fewer repetitions of an experience to make an association." This property is "largely genetically determined," so brighter children "can, in essence, do more with less" — letting them form associations from fewer patterned, repetitive experiences. It sits alongside [Memory](/notes/memory) (the brain as "an historical organ") in the book's account of how experience builds the brain.

In a doctrinal register, [The King Follett Sermon](/notes/the-king-follett-sermon) of [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) uses "intelligence" to mean the eternal, uncreated essence of the human mind: "the intelligence which man possesses is co-equal [co-eternal] with God himself." Smith argued that "God never had the power to create the spirit of man at all" — intelligence "had no beginning, neither will it have an end" — and that God, "because he was more intelligent, saw proper to institute laws whereby the rest could have a privilege to advance like himself." This underwrites the sermon's doctrine of [Exaltation](/notes/exaltation): weaker intelligences can be enlarged and "exalted with Himself."

**Context:** "Intelligence" carries different technical meanings by field — in cognitive science it spans processing speed, reasoning, and learning capacity; in Latter-day Saint theology, "intelligence(s)" denotes an eternal, uncreated aspect of the human spirit.
