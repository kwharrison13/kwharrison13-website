# David Eagleman

Neuroscientist cited in [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) in the book's debunking of learning "neuromyths." Specifically, against Myth 3 — that people have an optimal channel (visual, auditory, etc.) through which they learn — the book invokes Eagleman to argue the opposite: people use as many channels as they can access, and **learning is most effective when all the senses are combined** (audio, visual, touch). The note ties this to [Multiple Intelligences](/notes/multiple-intelligences) and adds Eagleman's point that people learn best when they have to teach someone else how to do something.

**Context:** David Eagleman is an American neuroscientist at Stanford, known for popular-science writing on the brain (*Incognito*, *Livewired*), the PBS series *The Brain*, and research on sensory substitution and brain plasticity.

## Where this appears

- [The Expertise Economy](/books/the-expertise-economy) — cited debunking the learning-styles "neuromyth," arguing multi-sensory learning (and teaching others) is most effective.
