# Carbon Accounting

Carbon accounting is the practice of measuring a company's greenhouse-gas footprint with the rigor of financial accounting — the "measure" layer that makes [Decarbonization](/notes/decarbonization) tractable by quantifying the emissions to be reduced. In the Watershed Climate Diligence — 2021, it's the first of [Watershed Climate](/notes/watershed-climate)'s three product modules (measure your footprint → build a reduction plan → buy [offsets](/notes/carbon-offsets) for the remainder), and the company is described as having "launched with the idea that carbon accounting should be just as detailed of a process as regular accounting." The diligence frames the [Greenhouse Gas Protocol](https://ghgprotocol.org/) as the field's global standard — "the climate equivalent of GAAP" — and notes that regulation is very likely to make carbon accounting mandatory, probably aligned with TCFD. The origin story: Watershed's founders spent two years at Stripe reconstructing the company's carbon footprint from thousands of spreadsheets and PDFs, then built a platform to automate that work.

The category was contested. The diligence lists [Persefoni](/notes/persefoni) and Emitwise as accounting-focused competitors (more aimed at heavy emitters like manufacturing), and in the November 1st, 2021 — Claudine Emeott (Salesforce Ventures) call, [Salesforce Ventures](/notes/salesforce-ventures) cites Watershed as "the most directly competitive solution out there" and notes an earlier carbon-accounting investment (Measurable, 2018) focused on real estate.

**Context:** Carbon accounting quantifies an organization's greenhouse-gas emissions, typically split into Scope 1 (direct), Scope 2 (purchased energy), and Scope 3 (value-chain) emissions under the Greenhouse Gas Protocol, increasingly required for corporate climate disclosure.
