# Rockefeller

In [Double Entry](/books/double-entry), Rockefeller appears as a case study in the practical power of accounting: he started out as a bookkeeper and attributed his vast wealth and the success of Standard Oil to his mastery of double-entry bookkeeping, especially cost accounting. The book uses him to argue that double-entry was not merely a clerical convention but a competitive instrument — a way of seeing costs precisely enough to dominate an industry.

**Context:** John D. Rockefeller (1839–1937) was the American industrialist who founded Standard Oil and became, by many measures, the wealthiest figure in modern history. His early training as a bookkeeper is frequently cited as the foundation of the relentless cost discipline that let Standard Oil out-compete and absorb rivals.

## Where this appears

- [Double Entry](/books/double-entry) — cited as a former bookkeeper who credited Standard Oil's success to mastery of double-entry cost accounting.
