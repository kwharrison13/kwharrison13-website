# Knowledge Commons

The framing, drawn from [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge), of online knowledge as a persistent, compounding pool that the whole field can draw on rather than a set of finished, privately-captured artifacts. The recurring claim is that "websites never die," so information persists and accumulates instead of freezing at publication — knowledge behaves as a living repository that requires continuous tending, not a snapshot. In this view publication is the *start* of responsibility, not the end, and "community is the thing that gives value to the commodity": a knowledge artifact derives value from ongoing engagement, not from being completed. The [Republic of Letters](/notes/republic-of-letters) is its historical antecedent, and the Cold War instinct of keeping work "in the public domain" to accelerate the whole field (von Neumann, per [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge)) is the same impulse at the level of a single contributor.

This is the "commons" sibling of [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) — distinct from open-source *software* (a licensing model); here the subject is shared learning, pedagogy, and expertise compounding in public.
