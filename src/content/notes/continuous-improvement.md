# Continuous Improvement

Across Kyle's notes, "Continuous Improvement" is the idea that **small, consistent, daily increments aggregate into dramatic results** — the discipline of evolution over revolution. In [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny), it appears under the Japanese word *kaizen*: "Continual improvement. Always finding something to work on, to make a little progress on… Revolution? Transformation? That's what amateurs chase. The pros are after evolution." Kyle ties this to [Compounding](/notes/compounding) ("everything you do is cumulative") and to the [SALY Principle](/notes/saly-principle) of the daily over the dramatic, attributing the frame to "Continuous Improvement" by President Topham.

The October 2021 General Conference — October 2021 notes ground the same idea in Michael A. Dunn's talk on "the aggregation of marginal gains" — the cycling example where improving every component by 1% yields a dramatic overall gain, and "if you improve something by 1% each day you will be 37x better after a year." The conference framing connects it to "micro-betterments," [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept), and the counsel to "reorient our patterns of improvement incrementally," especially when feeling overwhelmed — with the crucial caveat that for small gains to aggregate "there must be a consistent daily commitment," not sporadic effort.

**Context:** *Kaizen* and "aggregation of marginal gains" are well-known frameworks in management and high-performance sport — the latter popularized by Dave Brailsford's work with British Cycling — holding that compounding tiny, repeatable improvements outperforms chasing dramatic transformation.

## Where this appears

- [Discipline Is Destiny](/books/discipline-is-destiny) — frames continuous improvement as kaizen / evolution-over-revolution and links it to compounding and the SALY principle (credited to President Topham).
- General Conference — October 2021 — Michael A. Dunn's "aggregation of marginal gains" (the 1%-better cycling example) as the conference expression of incremental, daily improvement.
