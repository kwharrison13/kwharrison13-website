# Be Up and Doing

A principle of active obedience — that knowing is insufficient, one must do — anchored in [Joseph Smith — Rough Stone Rolling](/books/joseph-smith-rough-stone-rolling). The phrase comes from the angel Moroni's rebuke of [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith) near Cumorah: Joseph "had not been engaged enough in the work of the Lord," and "must be up and doing, and set myself about the things which God had commanded." Joseph called it "the severest chastisement that I have ever had in my life," then resolved, "I now know the course that I am to pursue, so all will be well." The biography pairs this with [Spencer W. Kimball](/notes/spencer-w-kimball)'s formulation "to know is not enough — one must do," and links it to the Nehemiah aphorism "arise and build," which the Church Presidency used to reframe the gathering to Zion from a fearful flight into a positive call to higher life and labor. Kyle's verbatim note on that passage reads simply: *"Be up and doing."*

Kyle's Helaman 1–6 study sharpens the definition: "to be up and doing is more than to be busy about whatever comes along. It is to be about a good work." The same Cumorah account is quoted again in his Alma 13–16 — Enter into the Rest of the Lord study, tying the principle to broader themes of initiative and being [Anxiously Engaged](/notes/anxiously-engaged).
