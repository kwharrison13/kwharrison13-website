# Alfred Adler

Alfred Adler is the psychologist whose "individual psychology" the entire dialogue of [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) expounds — the book is structured as a Plato-style exchange between a philosopher and a youth, with Adler's ideas as the doctrine. The highlights Kyle marked surface Adler's most distinctive moves: **teleology** over causality (we manufacture states like anxiety to serve present goals); the **denial of trauma** ("we are not determined by our experiences, but the meaning we give them is self-determining"); the **feeling of inferiority as a stimulant** to healthy striving rather than a disease (distinct from the paralyzing "inferiority complex" / "life-lie"); the **separation of tasks** as the cure for craving recognition; and the closing thesis that happiness is "the feeling of contribution." Adler is presented as "a psychology of courage" — unhappiness is reframed as a lack of "the courage to be happy."

In Kyle's corpus Adler also functions as a connective node in the self-help / influence lineage. [The Courage To Be Disliked](/books/the-courage-to-be-disliked) cites [Dale Carnegie](/notes/dale-carnegie) praising Adler as "a great psychologist who devoted his life to researching humans and their latent abilities," and notes that much of [Stephen Covey](/notes/stephen-covey)'s *7 Habits* "closely resembles Adler's ideas" — positioning Covey as a popularizer of Adlerian psychology. Kyle's own marginalia map Adlerian concepts onto outside vocabularies: inferiority/superiority as a parenting frame, reward/punishment critique as "righteous in the dark," and the "feeling of contribution" as civic virtue ("like putting away your shopping cart").

**Context:** Alfred Adler (1870–1937) was an Austrian psychotherapist who founded the school of individual psychology, breaking from Freud over the primacy of social interest and goal-orientation versus drive and the unconscious past.
