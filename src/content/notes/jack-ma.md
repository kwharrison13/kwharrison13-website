# Jack Ma

Founder of [Alibaba](/books/alibaba-the-house-that-jack-ma-built) and the central figure of [Duncan Clark](/notes/duncan-clark)'s biography in Kyle's library. The book profiles Ma as a self-described non-technical, often-underestimated leader who out-maneuvered larger rivals — "I am a crocodile in the Yangtze River" — and harvests his maxims throughout: that Alibaba survived its "1,001 mistakes" precisely because "we didn't have any money, we didn't have any technology, and we didn't have a plan"; that "consumption is done by entrepreneurship and the market economy," not government ([Entrepreneurship](/notes/entrepreneurship)); and the [capital-allocation](/notes/capital-allocation) discipline "raise money when we don't need it." His competitive playbook — out-positioning a stronger opponent (eBay) by choosing the home battlefield — is the book's recurring [Competition](/notes/competition) theme.

In [The Power Law](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future), Ma and Alibaba appear as proof that "Arthur Rock's financial model, not Communist Party planning, drove China's tech rise" — U.S. capital, legal structures, and talent were central to China's digital economy, and Goldman Sachs's early 17% Alibaba stake (sold for a "forgettable" 6.8x) is cited as "one of venture history's worst-timed exits." In the long-read [On the Nature of Long-Term Holds](/notes/on-the-nature-of-long-term-holds), Ma is tagged alongside [Dave Whorton](/notes/dave-whorton)'s evergreen / [100-year-business](/notes/100-year-business-plan) philosophy of long-horizon company building.

**Context:** Jack Ma (Ma Yun) is the Chinese entrepreneur who co-founded Alibaba Group in 1999, building it into one of the world's largest e-commerce and technology companies; a former English teacher, he became one of China's most prominent business figures before stepping back from public life around 2020.
