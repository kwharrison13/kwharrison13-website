# William Gibson

Science-fiction writer who recurs across Kyle's notes as a touchstone for thinking clearly about the future. He is the source of the line that opens [Remote — Office Not Required](/books/remote-office-not-required) — *"the future is already here, it's just not evenly distributed"* — used there as the epigraph framing async/remote work as a leading edge already visible in pockets. In Kyle's Historical Futurism - Research, Gibson appears both as a forecaster and as a theorist of forecasting: a [New Yorker profile](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2019/12/16/how-william-gibson-keeps-his-science-fiction-real) is quoted for the idea that reading science fiction is *"a kind of training"* — sci-fi traces the ramifications of a single postulated change, and readers co-create by judging the writer's plausibility, so they are *"a little less surprised by whatever does happen."*

His own track record is cited as evidence: Gibson's 1997 Wired piece *The Long Boom* is flagged (via his [@GreatDismal](https://twitter.com/GreatDismal/status/1532944750155681792?s=20) account) for predictions that landed — a new US–China cold war, Russia as a kleptocracy, a pandemic, and crime/terror-driven de-globalization — held up as "shockingly spot on." In Books 2.0 - Research he appears in his cyberpunk register: his 1984 novel Neuromancer is named as a seminal work featuring neural implants, interfaces, and hacking into cyberspace (alongside the *Johnny Mnemonic* adaptation of his short story).

**Context:** William Gibson (b. 1948) is an American-Canadian author who coined the term "cyberspace" and helped found the cyberpunk genre with *Neuromancer* (1984). The "future is already here, just not evenly distributed" remark is his best-known aphorism, repeated in many forms in interviews since the 1990s.
