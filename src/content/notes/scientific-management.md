# Scientific Management

In [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet), scientific management is a named case in the book's history of knowledge-producing institutions, where Frederick Winslow Taylor (1856–1915) is the "guru" of the movement his followers called scientific management. The book quotes Taylor's 1911 maxim — "In the past, the man has been first; in the future the system must be first" — which Kyle annotates as *systems thinking*. The thread continues through Frank and Lillian Gilbreth (with Lillian, not Frank, bringing the academic credentials to their motion-study partnership) and on to NASA director James Webb's *Space Age Management*, which heralded applying social-scientific technique to large-scale organization. It is one of Kyle's flagged [Systems Thinking](/notes/systems-thinking) touchpoints in the book.

**Context:** Scientific management (Taylorism), formalized in Frederick Taylor's *The Principles of Scientific Management* (1911), sought to optimize labor through time-and-motion study, standardized procedures, and management's control of work design. It deeply shaped 20th-century industrial organization and, through figures like the Gilbreths, the field of industrial engineering.
