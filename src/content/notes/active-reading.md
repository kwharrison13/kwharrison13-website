# Active Reading

Active reading is the central thesis Kyle draws out of [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) ([Mortimer Adler](/notes/mortimer-adler) & [Charles Van Doren](/notes/charles-van-doren)): reading as a "catcher in baseball" — a demanding, two-sided act that asks more of both the reader and the text, not passive consumption. In Kyle's notes it sits at the hub of a connected method for turning reading into retained, networked knowledge, paired with [Triangulation](/notes/triangulation) (reading several books on one subject against each other), [Progressive Summarization](/notes/progressive-summarization) (stating the unity of a whole book in a single sentence), and [Live the Library](/notes/live-the-library) ("reading what others have read"). The recurring prompt that operationalizes it is "[Therefore, what?](/notes/therefore-what)" — it is not enough to know; the discipline is interrogating what you read for action.

Kyle repeatedly overlays the technique with religious-text reading (scripture as something that has "done what Adler describes for millennia"), and ties it to relational-over-hierarchical thinking — ideas as networked rather than tree-structured, which links it to [Roam Research](/notes/roam-research) and his broader [Open Source Knowledge](/notes/open-source-knowledge) frame of a book that could "grow as you grow." It also threads into [Reinventing Knowledge — From Alexandria To The Internet](/books/reinventing-knowledge-from-alexandria-to-the-internet) via the same Republic-of-Letters lineage of how knowledge gets curated and transmitted.

**Context:** "Active reading" is a long-standing study and literacy practice — annotating, questioning, summarizing, and connecting text rather than passively reading — most famously codified in Adler and Van Doren's *How to Read a Book* (1940/1972).
