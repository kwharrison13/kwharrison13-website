# System of Record

In Kyle's notes, **system of record** is the data-backbone threshold that distinguishes lightweight *discovery* from full *diligence* in a [Procurement](/notes/procurement) process. The framing comes from [Nisha Swarup](/notes/nisha-swarup) in the September 13th, 2022 — Nisha Swarup call: at smaller customers like [Valon](/notes/valon) and [Netlify](/notes/netlify) the team was focused on discovery, because "diligence will require more system of record." Once a buying decision becomes cross-functional — a larger company (200+ vendors) with many stakeholders, finance/sales department heads, and BizOps people tasked with diligence — the pain shifts from finding options to aggregating all the data, and that's when a system of record is needed.

So in this corpus the term is less an abstract software category than a specific product-strategy boundary: discovery tools serve early/SMB buyers, but moving up-market into formal procurement diligence demands the deeper, persistent data layer of a system of record.

**Context:** In enterprise software, a "system of record" generally denotes the authoritative data store for a given domain (e.g., the HRIS for employees, the CRM for customers), as distinct from "systems of engagement" or workflow tools that sit on top of it.

## Where this appears

- September 13th, 2022 — Nisha Swarup — the origin: diligence at scale "will require more system of record," unlike lighter discovery; cross-functional procurement needs it.
- [Procurement](/notes/procurement) — larger-company procurement involves many stakeholders and needs a system of record to aggregate data.
- [Valon](/notes/valon) — cited (with [Netlify](/notes/netlify)) as a discovery-focused customer, since diligence would require more system of record.
- [Netlify](/notes/netlify) — same example: discovery rather than diligence, on the system-of-record reasoning.
