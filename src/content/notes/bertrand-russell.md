# Bertrand Russell

The philosopher recurs across four books in Kyle's library as a touchstone for rationality, skepticism toward dogma, and the limits of expertise. In [Amusing Ourselves To Death](/books/amusing-ourselves-to-death), Neil Postman invokes Russell's "immunity to eloquence" — the analytic reader's ability to separate the charm or tone of words from the logic of their argument. In [Brave New World Revisited](/books/brave-new-world-revisited), Aldous Huxley draws on Russell twice: his observation that "systems of dogma without empirical foundations, such as scholasticism, Marxism and fascism, have the advantage of producing a great deal of social coherence among their disciples," and his three-cause theory of historical change (economic change, political theory, and important individuals) against behaviorist scientism that explains the individual away.

In [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), Russell supplies the book's epigraph-grade warning — "even when the experts all agree, they may well be mistaken" — and his 1928 rules for when laypeople may overrule experts (the basis for Kyle's [Group Think](/notes/group-think) concept): when experts agree the opposite can't be held certain; when they disagree no opinion is certain for a non-expert; and when they find no sufficient grounds, the ordinary person should suspend judgment. In [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists), Rutger Bregman quotes him on leisure and civilization: "To be able to fill leisure intelligently is the last product of civilization."

**Context:** Bertrand Russell (1872–1970) was a British philosopher, logician, and essayist, co-author of *Principia Mathematica*, a leading figure in analytic philosophy, and winner of the 1950 Nobel Prize in Literature.
