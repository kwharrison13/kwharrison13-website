# Alma 32

Alma's sermon to the poor and humble Zoramites — the Book of Mormon's foundational text on faith, and one of the most-cited chapters across Kyle's gospel study. In the July 13–19, 2020 Come Follow Me study Kyle's notes "center entirely on [Alma 32](/notes/alma-32)": the Zoramites, cast out of their synagogues "because of the coarseness of their apparel," were poor in heart and so "in a preparation to hear the word" — a [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility) opens someone to new truth where [Pride](/notes/pride) makes it nearly impossible to accept. The chapter defines faith as "hoping for things not seen which are true" ([Alma 32](/notes/alma-32):21) and teaches the experiment of the word: "if ye can no more than desire to believe, let this desire work in you" ([Alma 32](/notes/alma-32):27). Faith is never standing still — it is "either growing or shrinking."

Across the topical studies, [Alma 32](/notes/alma-32) anchors two linked ideas. In Faith — Topical Study it draws the line between faith and [Perfect Knowledge](/notes/perfect-knowledge): "once you have a perfect knowledge, your faith is dormant" ([Alma 32](/notes/alma-32):34), and verse 19 carries "the responsibility of a perfect knowledge" — once you know, you must act. The "desire to believe" of [Alma 32](/notes/alma-32):27 recurs as the entry point to faith throughout the corpus, cross-cited in the close reading of Moroni's Promise ([Moroni 10](/notes/moroni-10)) and the faith-chain of [Moroni 7](/notes/moroni-7). It is the seed metaphor that lets even a flickering desire begin the work.

**Context:** Alma 32 is a chapter in the Book of Mormon in which the prophet Alma preaches to a group of impoverished Zoramites, using the metaphor of planting a seed to describe how faith grows through experimentation and nurture.
