# Gary Klein

In [Range](/books/range), psychologist Gary Klein is presented as a pioneer of the "naturalistic decision making" (NDM) model of expertise — an approach in which researchers observe expert performers in their natural working environment to learn how they make high-stakes decisions under time pressure. Klein showed that experts across many fields resemble chess masters in that they instinctively recognize familiar patterns. [David Epstein](/notes/david-epstein) uses Klein as the explicit counterpoint to [Daniel Kahneman](/notes/daniel-kahneman), whose "heuristics and biases" model found that experience often bred confidence without skill. The two psychologists, who were colleagues, co-authored a 2009 paper laying out their views and seeking common ground; they agreed that whether experience leads to expertise depends entirely on the domain — narrow, repetitive experience makes for better firefighters, chess, and poker players, but not better predictors of financial or political trends. This domain-dependence (kind vs. wicked learning environments) is central to the book's argument.

**Context:** Gary Klein is an American research psychologist known for developing the recognition-primed decision (RPD) model and for his foundational work in naturalistic decision making, drawn largely from studying firefighters, military commanders, and other professionals deciding under pressure.

## Where this appears

- [Range](/books/range) — Klein's NDM model of expert pattern recognition, set against Kahneman's heuristics-and-biases view, frames the book's thesis on when experience does and doesn't build skill.
