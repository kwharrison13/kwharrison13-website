# Alienation

Alienation is the central theme of [Franz Kafka](/notes/franz-kafka)'s [Metamorphosis](/books/metamorphosis), where it operates on two levels at once: Gregor Samsa's literal transformation into a "verminous" insect, and his social estrangement from the family he had defined himself by providing for. The wiki's reading ties his entire identity to that provider role — his pride that he could "provide a life like that in such a nice home for his sister and parents" — and tracks the family's drift from gratitude to revulsion to relief at his death. The apple lodged in Gregor's flesh, which no one dares remove, stands as "a visible reminder of his injury," a physical marker of his exclusion from the family circle even as his father reluctantly concedes he "could not be treated as an enemy."

The estrangement is also self-estrangement: Gregor questions his own humanity ("Was he an animal if music could captivate him so?") and ultimately wills his own disappearance, thinking "back of his family with emotion and love" while concluding he "must go away." The family's quick recovery and plan to move to a smaller flat completes the alienation — Gregor's death is registered as relief, not loss.

**Context:** Alienation — estrangement from one's work, society, or self — is a major theme of modernist literature and of social theory (notably Marx's concept of estranged labor). Kafka's *The Metamorphosis* (1915) is one of its canonical literary treatments.
