# Jane Gleeson-White

Author of [Double Entry](/books/double-entry), the history of [Accounting](/notes/accounting) that traces double-entry bookkeeping from [Luca Pacioli](/notes/luca-pacioli)'s 1494 treatise *Particularis de computis et scripturis* through the Renaissance, the rise of [Capitalism](/notes/capitalism) (via Sombart's claim that the two are inseparable, "like form and content"), and into the modern audit and the "expectations gap." The book's larger argument — the thread Kyle's note flags — is that financial statements "cannot be trusted to convey the true state of a business," and that measures like [GDP](/notes/gdp) (born of 1930s national accounting) and traditional accounts systematically fail to record externalities like [Climate Change](/notes/climate-change) and ecological debt. Kyle marks it a light-engagement read whose highlights stand on their own.

**Context:** Jane Gleeson-White is an Australian author and economics commentator. *Double Entry: How the Merchants of Venice Created Modern Finance* (2011) won and was shortlisted for several Australian non-fiction awards; she later wrote *Six Capitals* on the limits of accounting for natural and human capital.
