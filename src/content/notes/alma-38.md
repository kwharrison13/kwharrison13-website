# Alma 38

A Book of Mormon chapter — Alma's counsel to his son [Shiblon](/notes/shiblon), whom Alma praises for "steadiness and faithfulness" and for diligence among the Zoramites. The chapter opens by restating the [Central Theme of the Book of Mormon](/notes/central-theme-of-the-book-of-mormon) promise that "inasmuch as ye shall keep the commandments of God ye shall prosper in the land," and frames the salvific claim that "there is no other way or means whereby man can be saved, only in and through Christ" (Alma 38:9). Kyle's notes treat it as the source of two recurring threads: the charge to "look to the Lord your God" from one's youth, and the counsel to "use boldness, but not overbearance."

In Kyle's wiki, Alma 38:12 — "see that ye bridle all your passions, that ye may be filled with love" — is the single most-cited verse on [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery). Life's Gentle Helm builds an entire topical study around it, reading the bridle as a metaphor for guiding rather than imprisoning one's impulses, anchored by [Chad Harrison](/notes/chad-harrison)'s horse-training note that halter-breaking takes patient, repetitive work. The verse recurs in Teaching Repentance and is paired with Alma 39's "cross yourself in all these things" in the Alma 39–42 study. Humility — Topical Study draws on Alma 38:14 ("acknowledging our unworthiness before God at all times") as a model of spiritual submission.

**Context:** Alma 38 is a chapter in the Book of Mormon (scripture in the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints), the middle of three chapters in which the prophet Alma the Younger gives individual counsel to his sons Helaman, Shiblon, and Corianton.
