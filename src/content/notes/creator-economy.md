# Creator Economy

The shift from communities and firms toward individual creators as the epicenter of online social and economic systems. [Working in Public](/books/working-in-public) (by [Nadia Eghbal](/notes/nadia-eghbal)) frames it as the decline of "the firm as a principal agent of change" — newspapers, publishers, and talent agencies crumbling while individual creators rise — and connects it to the [Subscription Economy](/notes/subscription-economy), the model creators adopt as they shift from chasing reach to monetizing reader quality. In Kyle's sourcing call with [Zach Edwards](/notes/zach-edwards) (September 17th, 2021 — Zach Edwards (Clearstone Associates)), the creator economy is read as a symptom of a deeper drive: "the rise of entrepreneurship, and high employee turnover among millennials is all a result of a desire for autonomy."

Kyle's notes attach several people and ventures to the theme. [Catherine Yeo](/notes/catherine-yeo) is a creator-economy writer and longtime Wikipedia editor whose "Wikipedia's Struggle with Creators" is the source of his "notability is inherently subjective" quote; [Bradley Miles](/notes/bradley-miles) is building crypto-native creator infrastructure (FanCoin, letting creators issue their own fan currencies), and a daily-log entry captures a similar pitch positioning Roll as "social token infrastructure for the creator economy" across 50M+ creators. There's also a skeptical thread: [The AppetiZIRP](/notes/the-appetizirp) (Packy McCormick) cites the creator economy as a quintessential "ZIRP Phenomenon" — an appetizer of the cheap-money era that will "look quaint" once automation and UBI reshape what people do with their time.

**Context:** "Creator economy" is the common label for the ecosystem of independent creators (writers, video makers, streamers, podcasters) who monetize audiences directly through platforms like Substack, YouTube, Patreon, and TikTok, rather than through traditional media firms.
