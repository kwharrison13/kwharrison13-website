# Reality Distortion Field

The Reality Distortion Field is, in Kyle's notes, the idea that sufficiently committed storytelling can *bend* reality rather than merely describe it — a thread that bridges innovation, memetics, and the [Steve Jobs](/notes/steve-jobs) mythology. The canonical definition Kyle pulls (via [Walter Isaacson](/notes/walter-isaacson), through Robert Friedland / Andy Hertzfeld) is "a confounding mélange of a charismatic rhetorical style, indomitable will, and eagerness to bend any fact to fit the purpose at hand." In The Meme Economy - Research it is the exemplar of memes being "read **and** write" — not just communication but mechanisms of execution — sitting alongside [Silicon Valley Bank](/notes/silicon-valley-bank) (the first social-media-induced bank run) as a case where what people decide to believe dictates reality. Isaacson's framing that "innovation requires a reality distortion field" makes it central to the overreach-as-virtue thesis in [Leonardo Da Vinci](/books/leonardo-da-vinci).

[The Enchanted Notebook](/notes/the-enchanted-notebook) supplies the constructive twin: [Ray Kurzweil](/notes/ray-kurzweil)'s practice of describing the future "in the present tense, as if it already exists" — storytelling as a tool that *builds* reality. Kyle reads the two together — the distortion field and the enchanted notebook — as the same insight that "innovation is half storytelling," with [F. Scott Fitzgerald](/notes/f-scott-fitzgerald)'s "two opposed ideas at once" (reality matters / belief shapes reality) as the governing frame.

**Context:** "Reality distortion field" was coined by Apple engineer Bud Tribble in 1981 to describe Steve Jobs's ability to convince himself and others that the impossible was achievable; Walter Isaacson's *Steve Jobs* biography popularized the phrase.
