# Intel

Intel is the central recurring company across the second half of [Chip War](/books/chip-war) — the vertically integrated U.S. chipmaker that designed and manufactured its own silicon, founded by Noyce and Moore (the name standing for "Integrated Electronics"). The book's defining Intel story is [Andy Grove](/notes/andy-grove) "disrupting Intel himself": recognizing that the DRAM-memory business the firm was founded on was broken, he abandoned it to bet everything on microprocessors, establishing Intel's stranglehold on PC chips — *"more than innovation or expertise, it was his paranoia that saved Intel."* [Chip War](/books/chip-war) also documents Intel's manufacturing discipline ("copy exactly," replicating one proven process across every fab) and its two great strategic misses: it turned down the iPhone contract under CEO Otellini (an economics MBA, not an engineer) because the forecast price/volume looked unattractive — *"the volume was 100× what anyone thought"* — and so never won a foothold in mobile. The book contrasts Intel's integrated model against [TSMC](/notes/tsmc)'s pure-play foundry, using TSMC's volume efficiencies to explain Intel's later foundry failure and decline, and closes by saying America "is in desperate need of a new Andy Grove."

The [Jay Goldberg interview](/notes/apple-earnings-an-interview-with-jay-goldberg-about-chips-and-intel) updates the worry into the 2020s. [Jay Goldberg](/notes/jay-goldberg) frames Intel's structural risk as full-fab economics — a leading-edge fab costs ~$15B and *has to stay full*, a problem as PCs flatline and [Apple](/notes/apple)'s M1 threatens to shrink the market further. But he argues Intel's deeper problem is **cultural**: immense engineering talent, yet a company that "cannot do customer service" and has spent decades telling others "how it's going to be" rather than accepting it is no longer in control. Goldberg — a self-described finance person — argues a chip company like Intel "should be run by engineers," not finance leaders, and is skeptical a new CEO can turn it around (while noting Intel has reinvented itself before, from memory to processors). The whole conversation runs on the [Clayton M. Christensen](/notes/clayton-christensen) integrate-then-modularize cycle and the [fabless](/notes/fabless) economics that made Intel's owned-fab model an increasing liability.

**Context:** Intel Corporation, founded 1968 by Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore, was the dominant designer-manufacturer of x86 microprocessors through the PC era. It later ceded process-technology leadership to TSMC and Samsung, missed the smartphone transition, and as of the early 2020s was attempting a turnaround built partly around opening its own contract-foundry business.
