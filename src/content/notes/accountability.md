# Accountability

Across Kyle's corpus, "accountability" carries two related threads. In the **secular/institutional** sense, it is the loop that closes a system of trust: in [The Death of Expertise](/books/the-death-of-expertise), it appears both as Tetlock's proposed reform — "experts maintaining a record of how often they were right or wrong" — and as the democratic [accountability loop](/notes/educated-electorate) that "only closes when voters can evaluate expertise." [Wisdom From Walmart](/notes/wisdom-from-walmart) gives the operational version: Sam Walton "believed in pushing responsibility and authority down" to the front lines, so that the person stocking the shelves owns the customer outcome.

In the **gospel/personal** sense, accountability is a function of knowledge and covenant. In [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) it is paired with [Agency](/notes/agency) — "choosing between two principled alternatives (two 'goods')... is essential to our spiritual maturity" — and Kyle ties it to [Camden Harrison](/notes/camden-harrison)'s reflection on "the level of accountability we have for what people ultimately decide to do." In Come Follow Me study it becomes "the accountability of knowledge": once you have gained a testimony you are held to a higher standard, and to "fall into sin again is worse for you than the incremental sin before you had repented" (June 29–July 5, 2020. Alma 23–29, July 13–19, 2020. Alma 32–35). The companionship-inventory practice applies the principle relationally: agreeing on a solution and establishing accountability, because "without an outcome there's no accountability."
