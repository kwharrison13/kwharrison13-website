# White Flight

In [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis), white flight names the abandonment of central cities — the exodus of people, companies, and stores that hollowed out places like Newark and tipped them into "steep decline." Richard Florida frames it through his own boyhood haunting: "What was causing people, companies, and stores to abandon Newark? Why had the city exploded into racial turmoil and entered into such a steep decline? Why had the factory where my father worked closed down?" That "original urban crisis" — racial turmoil, deindustrialization, and the flight of capital and residents from the urban core — is the historical backdrop against which Florida sets the *new* urban crisis of inequality and unaffordability. The concept sits alongside [suburban sprawl](/notes/suburban-sprawl) and [gentrification](/notes/gentrification) in the book's vocabulary of how cities lose and recover their centers.

**Context:** "White flight" is the standard sociological term for the mid-20th-century migration of white residents out of racially diversifying American urban centers into suburbs, driven by a mix of housing policy, racial anxiety, and disinvestment, and a major cause of the postwar decline of cities like Detroit and Newark.

## Where this appears

- [The New Urban Crisis](/books/the-new-urban-crisis) — invoked in Florida's account of Newark's collapse, paired with suburban sprawl and gentrification as forces that emptied the urban core.
