# Tree of Life

In Kyle's [Plan of Salvation](/notes/plan-of-salvation) topical study, Lehi's vision of the Tree of Life (1 Nephi 8, 11–12, 15) is read not just as a parable about righteousness but as a **map of our whole existence** — the full arc of the [Plan of Salvation](/notes/plan-of-salvation). The study decodes the vision element by element: the dark and dreary waste is premortal life, which combined with God's mercy becomes the large and spacious field (earth); the rod of iron is the word of God, guidance that "whoso would hearken unto the word of God, and would hold fast unto it, they would never perish" (1 Nephi 15:24); and the mists of darkness are the temptations of the devil.

The central claim of the reading is that **celestial glory springs up only from the roots of the tree — the love of God**. The pride of the great and spacious building stands "in the air, high above the earth," having no foundation (1 Nephi 8:26), set against the tree whose roots are grounded in divine love. The "awful gulf" that separates the wicked from the tree of life is the justice of God dividing the wicked from the righteous (1 Nephi 15:28, 30). For Kyle the vision functions as a single integrated image of spiritual, physical, and eternal life.

**Context:** The Tree of Life is a recurring symbol across scripture; in Latter-day Saint study Lehi's dream in 1 Nephi 8 — with its iron rod, mists of darkness, great and spacious building, and fruit representing the love of God — is among the most-taught passages in the Book of Mormon.
