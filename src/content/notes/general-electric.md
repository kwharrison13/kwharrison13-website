# General Electric

General Electric is the primary subject company of [The Man Who Broke Capitalism](/books/the-man-who-broke-capitalism) (David Gelles) and the institutional setting for "Welchism." Under [Jack Welch](/notes/jack-welch) (CEO 1981–2001), GE grew from $14 billion to $600 billion — the most valuable company in the world — but, per Gelles, via three "dark arts": downsizing, dealmaking, and [Financialization](/notes/financialization). The book recounts how GE, founded by Thomas Edison and once "admired for quality engineering," became a conglomerate "addicted to short-term profits"; how Welch's "campaign against loyalty" ended the implicit promise of lifetime employment; how GE made nearly 1,000 acquisitions for ~$130 billion ("fix it, close it, or sell it"); and how GE Capital — "essentially a giant unregulated bank" — grew to 40% of revenues and 60% of profits. GE became the template other industrials (John Deere, Caterpillar, [Hewlett-Packard](/notes/hewlett-packard)) copied in building finance arms that earned more from lending than from products.

GE's reach in Kyle's notes extends through its alumni: in [Flying Blind](/books/flying-blind), Welch protégés (Harry Stonecipher, McNerney, [Dennis Muilenburg](/notes/dennis-muilenburg)) carried the GE playbook into [Boeing](/notes/boeing) via the 1997 [McDonnell Douglas](/notes/mcdonnell-douglas) reverse takeover, trading engineering culture for buybacks with fatal results. [Jeff Immelt](/notes/jeff-immelt) (CEO 2001–2017) succeeds Welch as a central figure in GE's "decline narrative." GE also surfaces with a different valence in [Poor Charlie's Almanack](/books/poor-charlie-s-almanack) and the [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) long-read, where Munger cites "demanding places, like athletic teams and General Electric" as institutions that discard chronic excuse-givers.

**Context:** General Electric, founded in 1892 through a merger involving Thomas Edison's electric companies, was for a century a flagship American industrial conglomerate. After peaking under Welch, it spent the 2010s–2020s unwinding, eventually splitting into separate aerospace, energy, and healthcare companies.
