# Chris Hadfield

Chris Hadfield surfaces in Kyle's notes as an exemplar of **preparing for the worst as a way to stay calm and act well under pressure**. In the [Charlie Munger — The Psychology of Human Misjudgment](/notes/charlie-munger-the-psychology-of-human-misjudgment) long-read, Hadfield's astronaut-training lesson is quoted directly: the habit of "play-acting grim scenarios day in and day out" is "actually weirdly uplifting," because "anticipating problems and figuring out how to solve them is actually the opposite of worrying: it's productive." Kyle files this as the antidote to anxious rumination — turning fear into rehearsal.

He reappears in [The Self-Driven Child](/books/the-self-driven-child), where Kyle's note on a passage about visualizing and thinking through pros, cons, and a "Plan B" reads: "Visualize your activities; connect to Chris Hadfield in The Profile." The connective thread across both references is Hadfield as a model of deliberate mental preparation and visualization in the face of high-stakes uncertainty.

**Context:** Chris Hadfield is a retired Canadian astronaut and former commander of the International Space Station, widely known for popular writing and talks on how astronaut training reframes fear and risk — themes captured in his book *An Astronaut's Guide to Life on Earth*.
