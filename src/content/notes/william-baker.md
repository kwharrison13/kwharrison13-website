# William Baker

William ("Bill") Baker is one of the small set of figures [The Idea Factory](/books/the-idea-factory) follows to tell the story of Bell Labs — listed alongside Mervin Kelly, Jim Fisk, William Shockley, Claude Shannon, and John Pierce. A chemist who joined the Labs just before World War II, Baker described the Labs' work as having "such specific requirements that ordinary raw materials had an agonizing time meeting them" — the solution being "to literally create new types of matter." He rose to head Bell Labs' research division and saw materials science as "the grand alliance of engineering and science."

The book paints Baker as a man "thrifty with everything but words" — a mesmerizing but deliberately opaque speaker who could talk for ten minutes and leave listeners with no idea what he'd said. His defining habit, in the book, was information-gathering: every day at lunch he would sit with the first person he spotted in the cafeteria, glassblower or metallurgist alike, and "gently interview" them about their work and lives — "at the end of any conversation, you would then realize that he would know everything about you but you would know absolutely nothing about him." Kyle's note on the page ties this exact trait to [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley)'s same ability to extract information from any conversational partner. Baker also embodied a belief that "science rests on a foundation of inquiry rather than certainty," demolishing a presenter who claimed something was "completely understood."

**Context:** William O. Baker (1915–2005) was an American chemist who became president of Bell Telephone Laboratories (1973–1979) and advised several US presidents on science and intelligence matters.
