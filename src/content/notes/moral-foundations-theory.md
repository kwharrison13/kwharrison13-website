# Moral Foundations Theory

The central framework of [The Righteous Mind](/books/the-righteous-mind), developed by [Jonathan Haidt](/notes/jonathan-haidt) to argue that "there's more to morality than harm and fairness." The theory holds six moral foundations — Care/harm, Fairness/cheating, Liberty/oppression, Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity — and its load-bearing political claim in the book is that "liberals have a three-foundation morality, whereas conservatives use all six." Liberal moral matrices rest on Care, Liberty, and Fairness (and liberals will often trade away fairness-as-proportionality when it conflicts with compassion or fighting oppression), while conservatives also draw on the three "binding" foundations of Loyalty, Authority, and Sanctity. Haidt notes that even shared foundations split: on the left, fairness tends to mean equality; on the right, proportionality — people rewarded in proportion to what they contribute.

The book uses the theory to reframe political disagreement as a clash of "alternative moral worlds" rather than one side seeing clearly and the other voting against its interests — rural and working-class voters, in this reading, are "voting for their moral interests," not being duped. Haidt observes that when he presents the three binding foundations to liberal audiences, many "actively reject these concerns as immoral" (loyalty as the basis of racism, authority as oppression, sanctity as "religious mumbo-jumbo"), which the book treats as the core empathy gap to be bridged. His advice — "if you want to understand another group, follow the sacredness" — flows directly from the framework. The theory pairs with Haidt's social-intuitionist "elephant and rider" model elsewhere in the book.

**Context:** Moral Foundations Theory is a social-psychology framework, primarily associated with Jonathan Haidt and Jesse Graham, proposing that human moral judgments rest on several innate, modular foundations and that political groups differ systematically in how much weight they give each one.
