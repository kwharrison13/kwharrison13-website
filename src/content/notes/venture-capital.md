# Venture Capital

Venture capital is one of the most heavily-cross-linked concepts in Kyle's corpus — the financing of high-risk, high-tech startups by intermediaries who use domain expertise to chase outsized returns. Two histories anchor the page. [VC — An American History](/books/vc) traces the industry's origins to the Boston-based American Research and Development Corporation (ARD, 1946) and the limited-partnership ([LP](/notes/lp)) structure of Draper, Gaither & Anderson (1959), arguing that VCs exist precisely because they "maintain informational advantages in the selection and governance of startup investments," and that exceptional payoffs are "sporadic and infrequent, concentrated in specific firms and time periods" — the [Power Law](/notes/power-law) claim. [The Power Law — Venture Capital & the Making of the New Future](/books/the-power-law-venture-capital-and-the-making-of-the-new-future) historicizes the same industry from Davis & Rock through a16z, Tiger, and the unicorn era, but carries Kyle's more cynical standout note: "Venture capital isn't a long-term bet, it's often a long-term charade of passing the bag."

The concept also organizes Kyle's own investing essays. In What Is An Investor - Research, VC sits as one bucket among many investor types, paired with the "we are not investors / we are value-creators" framing of [Tom Perkins](/notes/tom-perkins) and [Ho Nam](/notes/ho-nam), and with debates (via [Del Johnson](/notes/del-johnson) and [Noah Smith](/notes/noah-smith)) over whether the industry's recent underperformance is structural or just a temporary end to easy money. The page links out to Kyle's framing concepts — [The Productization of Venture Capital](/essays/productization), The Value Chain of Capital - Research, and [Knightian Uncertainty](/notes/knightian-uncertainty) — and pulls in the many firms tagged across the corpus, including [CapitalG](/notes/capitalg), [First Round Capital](/notes/first-round-capital), [Lux Capital](/notes/lux-capital), [Atomic Ventures](/notes/atomic-ventures), [Contrary](/notes/contrary), [Long Journey Ventures](/notes/long-journey-ventures), and [Norwest](/notes/norwest).

**Context:** Venture capital is a form of private-equity financing in which investors provide capital to early-stage, high-growth-potential startups in exchange for equity, typically organized as limited partnerships with a fixed fund life and returns driven by a small number of outsized exits.
