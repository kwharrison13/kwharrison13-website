# Chiasmus

In Kyle's [Come, Follow Me](/notes/come-follow-me) study of Alma 36–Alma 38, chiasmus is the literary structure organizing Alma's account of his own conversion to his son [Helaman](/notes/helaman). The notes describe it as "an inverted parallel structure" that descends from "the pains of a damned soul" down to the turning point — Alma's cry, "O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me" — and then ascends symmetrically back out to "joy as exceeding as was my pain." The crisis and the conversion sit at the center, with the descent and ascent mirroring each other on either side.

The study treats Alma 36 as the standout Book-of-Mormon example of this form, placing it alongside the chapter's central promise ("inasmuch as ye shall keep the commandments of God ye shall prosper in the land") and the themes of [Repentance](/notes/repentance), [Faith](/notes/faith), and [Self-Mastery](/notes/self-mastery).

**Context:** Chiasmus is an ancient Hebrew and Near-Eastern poetic device in which ideas are stated and then repeated in reverse order (an A-B-C-C-B-A pattern), focusing emphasis on the central element. Its presence in the Book of Mormon, especially Alma 36, is frequently cited in LDS scholarship as evidence of the text's Hebraic literary roots.
