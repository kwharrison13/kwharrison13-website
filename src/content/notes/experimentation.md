# Experimentation

In [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era), experimentation is one of the three core ingredients of a PLG strategy ("data, an experimentation-driven mindset, and the ability to think about your product as a sales channel"). It's framed in [The Lean Startup](/books/the-lean-startup)'s spirit: release an [MVP](/notes/minimum-viable-product) and evolve it, and — crucially — *test the negative hypothesis* rather than bending data to fit a predefined conclusion, "to make sure that we're getting the most accurate information possible." The book is candid about the cost: experimentation "is, by its nature, risky because you don't know the outcomes ahead of time. It's all about learning from what works, and — just as importantly — what doesn't. You're not always going to get the 'right' answer, but you will always learn something."

[Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) supplies the same instinct in a city-building register: Disneyland's monorail and PeopleMover were live durability tests for systems Walt wanted in EPCOT — "use what you already run as the testbed." But the source also marks the hard limit on experimentation, via Ward Kimball's counter-voice: **"you can't experiment with people's lives."** Walt's utopia required near-total control precisely because real residents couldn't be treated as test subjects. So across the two sources experimentation is both the engine of learning (PLG, Lean Startup) and a method with an ethical boundary when the subjects are people, not products.

## Where this appears

- [Product Led Growth — The End User Era](/books/product-led-growth-the-end-user-era) — experimentation as a core PLG ingredient: ship an MVP, test the negative hypothesis, learn from what fails.
- [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city) — Disneyland's transit systems as live testbeds for EPCOT, bounded by "you can't experiment with people's lives."
