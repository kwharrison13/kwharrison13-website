# Triangulation

The discipline of building a thesis from multiple, cross-checking sources rather than trusting any single one. In [How To Read a Book](/books/how-to-read-a-book) Kyle tags it against Adler's "first rule of reading history" — *"it is necessary to read more than one account of the history of an event or period if we want to understand it"* — and again against the syntopical-reading rules and the biography rule (*"read as many biographies of him as you can find, including his own"*). It pairs with [Progressive Summarization](/notes/progressive-summarization) and the "live the library" frame as the actual method of multi-source reading.

In [Talent (Book)](/books/talent), triangulation becomes an evaluation discipline: Tyler Cowen and Daniel Gross's *"In which areas might this work? Might this not work? When does this work or not work?"* — what they call **looking for the cross-sectional variation**. Kyle tags this verbatim as "Triangulation": if you don't understand when and where a claim is wrong, you don't understand the claim. The same move appears in [Equity Research](/notes/equity-research) (thesis-building from multiple sources, against the disclosure regime public markets supply) and in the Open-Source Knowledge - Research notes, where Kyle frames it as *"What do you have to believe?"* / *"What gives you the right to believe?"* — a single Glassdoor review or a team in sales-mode isn't enough; you need corroborating angles because "you don't know what you don't know."
