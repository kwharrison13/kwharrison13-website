—

Everyone in tech knows a [[Mormon]]. Whether it’s the clean cut guy knocking back Diet Coke at the office happy hour or the engineering manager who is not only incredible at her job, but you’re shocked to find out she also has five kids. Outside of tech, your cultural exposure to [[Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints|Mormons]] might be limited to the white shirts and black name tags you’ve seen on the street, or social clips of [[Mitt Romney]] debates (being *[very](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=N0IWe11RWOM)*[ right about Russia](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=N0IWe11RWOM) in 2012, even though he was mocked for it). Surprisingly, Mormons are not only a surprisingly present force in the tech industry, but Utah also has an undeniable tech-forward mandate as well. Just last year, Utah [broke ground](https://www.ans.org/news/2025-09-25/article-7408/valar-atomics-breaks-ground-in-utah/) on what could be the [first](https://x.com/isaiah_p_taylor/status/1987931991929417840) fully deployed small modular nuclear reactor in the US and [kicked off](https://x.com/ADoricko/status/1990464237853114716) “the largest cloud seeding project in modern American history.”

All of that may come as a surprise to anyone who sees Mormons as an odd, yet simple offshoot of Protestant [[Christianity]]. But the reality runs much deeper than that. **The heritage of success in business and technology is tied not only to Latter-Day Saint history, but the religion’s very theology**. From Bryan Johnson (*Don’t Die*) to [[Clayton Christensen]] (*[[Innovator's Dilemma|Innovator’s Dilemma]]*) or Keith Johnson (the head of [[Sequoia]]’s family office), and across the founding stories of companies like [[Adobe]], The Trade Desk, JetBlue, Stance Socks, Vivint, Ancestry, and countless others — the spirit of the quintessential American religion has left its mark.

# An American Moses

**America is a nation built on the dual concepts of restoration and reinvention**. Making what is old into something also new. Americans reinvigorated and perfected little-r republicanism, little-d democracy, and inalienable rights.

American religious life is no different. While we transplanted Old World religion, we also made way for the reinvigoration of American prophetic voices. Where the ancient Moses had tablets, as a member of the [[The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints|Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints]], I believe that the American Moses had a pen.

In 1831, [[Joseph Smith]], under a prophetic mantle, took up that pen and [wrote](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/58?lang=eng) words that fell from the lips of God, as far as we’re concerned. In what he wrote, you find articulated a fundamental ethos that rings true in the heart of every so-called Mormon: “Men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause.”

While we may not [like](https://newsroom.churchofjesuschrist.org/style-guide) being called Mormons, members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints have ultimately felt that anxious pursuit driving us forward. It’s how we became, arguably, the most influential American-made religion, spanning millions of members across [150](https://www.thechurchnews.com/members/2025/06/23/30-nations-most-stakes-church-of-jesus-christ-of-latter-day-saints/) nations. Almost any entry over the last 200-or-so years of that history could justify its own book.

# Put Your Shoulder To The Wheel

Chased from a half dozen towns, murdered and persecuted along the way, we built a city out of a swamp in [Nauvoo, Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nauvoo,_Illinois). At its peak, it reached a population of 12,000, [rivaling](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/americanexperience/films/mormons/) [[Chicago]] at the time. After our first prophet was murdered, our second one, [[Brigham Young]], led 148 fearless pioneers across a [1,300-mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mormon_Trail) trek to [[Salt Lake|Salt Lake City]]. Then, we turned around and spent the next twenty years helping [65,000](https://history.churchofjesuschrist.org/blog/mormon-pioneer-emigration-facts) other people join us there. We spent over [$50 million](https://www.in2013dollars.com/us/inflation/1880?amount=1600000) — adjusted for inflation — helping poorer European immigrants make the journey, spreading out across the west, not just Utah, but also Idaho, Arizona, and California. And, these frontiersmen succeeded: the first millionaire in California, Samual Brannan, [was](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Brannan) a Latter-Day Saint businessman literally selling picks and shovels to gold miners (digging in gold mines discovered by the [Mormon Battalion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mormon_Battalion)).

We imported industries wholesale to Utah to reinforce our self-reliance. From iron to textiles, we saw physical toil as a spiritual commandment. Our own scriptures [reinforce](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/29?lang=eng) that every commandment from God is spiritual; never temporal. **We didn’t build industry to get rich, we did it to build the [[Kingdom of God]], our own home-grown Zion**. In fact, [arguably](https://www.company-histories.com/Zions-Cooperative-Mercantile-Institution-Company-History.html) the first full-line department store in the US was the Latter-Day Saints’ Zion’s Cooperative Mercantile Institution (ZCMI) founded in Salt Lake City in 1868 that pooled industrial output and local buying power into one enterprise.

Out of a barren desert, my ancestors built [Deseret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_of_Deseret) (the name we wanted for what is now “Utah” before the feds forced us to abandon the name), a [[Book of Mormon]] word that means honeybee — the perfect mascot for a hard-working, industrious hive.

![Source: Wikimedia Commons; The boundaries of the provisional State of Deseret (orange with black outline) as proposed in 1849. Modern state boundaries underlaid for reference.](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-118b3dd6.png)

By the 1860s, out-of-state travelers [said](https://community.utah.gov/overland-travelers-early-visitors-and-the-coming-of-the-railroad/) Utah looked more akin to established Eastern towns like Chicago or [[New York City|New York]], rather than the western outposts they were expecting. Brick homes, wide streets, mills, stores, schools. In fact, at a time when [20%](https://nces.ed.gov/naal/lit_history.asp) of the US population was illiterate, only [2.5%](https://www2.census.gov/library/publications/decennial/1910/bulletins/demographics/359-population-ut-composition-and-characteristics.pdf) of Utah had the same disadvantage. Our economy was well-rounded and strong, resisting the swings of the mining boom towns that then dotted the American West. Utah was built for broad stability, not quick fortunes.

# Adding Investment To Industry

![](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-e36a29f7.png)

The first generation of Latter-Day Saint entrepreneurialism after Utah’s settlement revolved around industrial categories: iron, sugar processing, utilities, and railroads. All were capital-intensive endeavors. When the Union Pacific and the Central Pacific railroads finally met, connecting the east and west coasts, they did so in [Promonotory, Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Promontory,_Utah). The arrival of the railroad in 1869 brought cheap products from the east, crowding out local industry. The Church was left carrying millions in debt with industries that were much more exposed to competition.

In the late 19th century and early 20th century, Utahans, determined to create heaven on Earth, continued to build everything from Church-owned enterprises, across media, insurance, and construction, to adjacent projects in the state. Founded in 1940, [Hill Air Force Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_Air_Force_Base) became a major Air Force base with 22,000 personnel during World War II, offering critical maintenance. [Thiokol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiokol), founded in 1929, manufactured everything from rubber to missile propulsion systems, including Minuteman ICBMs and the airbags on the Mars Pathfinder. [Geneva Steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geneva_Steel), founded in 1941, was at one point producing [60%](https://web.archive.org/web/20130105052820/http://www.timpharley.com/default.asp?page=info) of the steel in the Western US.

The same tenacity that powered my ancestors through spiritual and physical obstacles in the quest to find our American Zion prepared us to tackle financial obstacles at the turn of the century. Weathering the Great [[Depression]] and World War II redoubled our focus on self-reliance. One old pioneer motto came in handy: “use it up, wear it out, make do, or do without.” The Church created the [Church Security Plan](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/history/topics/great-depression?lang=eng) in 1936 to supplement government relief, provide food, jobs, and resources, and encourage self-reliance. By the 1960s the Church was solvent, but not wealthy. Then, [N. Eldon Tanner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathan_Eldon_Tanner) helped introduce modern financial discipline into the hearts of every Latter-Day Saint.

Tanner was an oilman and politician in Canada, but was chosen as a modern-day Apostle in [1962](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathan_Eldon_Tanner). That was an unusual call at the time, given most Apostles came from academic and legal backgrounds. Upon becoming a senior leader of the Church, he [was](https://rsc.byu.edu/firm-foundation/n-eldon-tanner-church-administration) “thrust into financial affairs.” He took the raw fundamentals of self-reliance and grit among the Latter-Day Saints and supercharged it with corporate-style accounting, an aversion to debt, and a diversified investment portfolio.

Tanner pushed the Church towards an additional golden rule: “We do not spend money we do not have.” From debt-ridden to conservative spending to building up long-term reserves, the Church’s financial picture changed dramatically. Over the next 60 years, the Church built on a foundation of industriousness and financial discipline [following](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/ot/gen/41?lang=eng) [scripture](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/25?lang=eng). The [call](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/109?lang=eng) from God was to “organize [ourselves] and prepare every needful thing.” So we did.

By the 2020s, the Church had built a net worth of [$265 billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finances_of_the_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints). That includes 290,000 acres of ranch land and citrus in Florida, one of the largest cattle ranches in the country, billions in real estate, a [$55 billion](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/top-10-holdings-mormon-churchs-214737974.html) equity portfolio, and on and on.

The entire purpose of the Church’s aggregation of financial assets had nothing to do with amassing wealth. The late president of the Church, [Russell M. Nelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_M._Nelson), may have been the head of a $265 billion organization. But he had a net worth of a few million as a former [renowned](https://attheu.utah.edu/facultystaff/honoring-medical-trailblazer-and-global-faith-leader-russell-m-nelson/) heart surgeon, and was earning a [$120,000](https://www.deseret.com/2017/1/10/20603771/mormonleaks-dumps-four-new-documents-about-lds-church/) annual salary working full-time for the church, all while wearing a [thirty dollar](https://nauvoo.supply/products/silver-timex-casio-watch-like-president-nelson-wears) Timex Casio wristwatch and vacuuming his own house.

![Source: Facebook](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-5eb6c1c3.png)

So what, then, does the Church do with the $265 billion empire it has built? **The leaders of the Church will be the first to [reemphasize](https://youtu.be/mpm3O1raENw?t=1578) the mission: “help people learn about and live the teachings of [[Jesus]] Christ, to share that message with the world, to strengthen and unite families, and to care for the poor and the needy.”** The Church spent [$1.45 billion](https://www.sltrib.com/religion/2025/03/25/lds-church-vowed-up-its/) in 2024 alone on humanitarian aid and welfare efforts across over 3,000 projects in 192 countries. Beyond physical needs, the Church invests in supporting its members’ spiritual needs. That includes access to over [200](https://churchofjesuschristtemples.org/statistics/) temples. Not just casual chapels, but sacred sanctuaries we believe are [literally](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/gs/temple-house-of-the-lord?lang=eng) “the House of the Lord.”

![Source: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-e149bae7.png)

We built industries, amassed financial assets, and gathered a reserve that can support millions of people spiritually and physically. But we didn’t stop at shaping the physical world. The digital world has been just as fertile a playing field for Latter-Day Saint grit.

# “The Glory of God Is Intelligence”

In February 2024, [[Sam Altman]] [said something](https://x.com/kwharrison13/status/1753443950105698667) that made the ears of every Latter-Day Saint perk up: “I grew up implicitly thinking that intelligence was this really special human thing and kind of somewhat magical. And I now think that it’s sort of a fundamental property of matter.”

Latter-Day Saint [scripture](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/dc-testament/dc/93?lang=eng) teaches that all matter is eternal and can be neither created, nor destroyed. It can simply be organized, right [in line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_mass) with the law of conservation of mass. Where religion is often framed as being at odds with science, my religion offers a fundamental cosmology that has remained the same since at least 1833 (if not since, you know… forever).

Far from a congregation of Luddites, Latter-Day Saints have embraced science as frequently as possible. One of the most noteworthy entries in Utah’s technological history came from the University of Utah’s [computer graphics lab](https://ohiostate.pressbooks.pub/graphicshistory/chapter/4-3-university-of-utah/) in the 1960s and 1970s. Our own home-grown Traitorous Eight, this group yielded an insanely dense pool of technology titans from [Ed Catmull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Catmull) (founder of Pixar) and [Jim Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_H._Clark) (founder of Silicon Graphics and NetScape) to [John Warnock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Warnock) (founder of Adobe) and [Alan Kay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Kay) (one of the most influential technologists to come out of [[Xerox PARC]]).

They may not all have been “Mormon” ([[Alan Kay]] and Jim Clark weren’t), but their technical training sprouted from an institutional environment fostered in Utah where, at the time, [70%](https://www.uen.org/utah_history_encyclopedia/c/CHURCH_OF_JESUS_CHRIST_OF_LATTER-DAY_SAINTS.shtml) of the population was LDS. That environment also enabled adjacent technology history to grow up around it. In 1969, in large part because of that graphics lab, [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET) (a fundamental precursor to the internet) set up its fourth node at the University of Utah! (The first three were all in California.)

![Source: MIT Advanced Network Architecture Group](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-5b15fed6.png)

One graphics lab alumni, Alan Ashton, developed [WordPerfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordPerfect) in 1979, which eventually became the dominant word processor in the 1980s before [[Microsoft]] Word came on the scene. That same Utah environment birthed Novell, an [early pioneer](https://research.contrary.com/deep-dive/identity-crisis) in networking founded in [1980](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novell), reaching 70% of enterprises at its peak, and [Cirrus Logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cirrus_Logic), the first fabless semiconductor company before [[Nvidia]] made it cool.

Most of that technical prowess in Utah over the second half of the 20th century grew out of a comparative advantage. Members of the Church were disproportionately literate, multilingual (almost half of Latter-Day Saints serve on a two-year mission, often learning a second language), globally comfortable, industrious, and thrifty. But every aspect of that character grew out of our heritage.

# Our Heritage

Members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints make up just [2%](https://www.pewresearch.org/religious-landscape-study/religious-tradition/latter-day-saint-mormon/) of the US population, but we’ve taken seriously Christ’s [admonition](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/study/scriptures/nt/matt/5?lang=eng&id=p13#p13) to be the “salt of the earth.” Preserving something uniquely American that is more rare these days. Derided as un-American pariahs in our early years, the Church has steeled itself around what we see as fundamental American principles: fairness, kindness, service, unrelenting optimism, and a commitment to the American ideal.

In a great piece called [The Most American Religion](https://web.archive.org/web/20250204083259/https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2021/01/the-most-american-religion/617263/) by [[McKay Coppins]], himself a member of the Church, he makes the argument that Latter-Day Saints didn’t become this “Norman Rockwellian ideal by accident.”

> “We taught ourselves to play the part over a centuries-long audition for full acceptance into American life. That we finally succeeded just as the country was on the brink of an identity crisis is one of the core ironies of modern [[Mormonism]].”

There’s an [apocryphal story](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Horse_Prophecy) among members of the Church about a prophecy that, one day, the US Constitution would hang by a thread, “only to be saved by a ‘white horse’ from God’s true Church.” Coppins explains why this story resonates so much with Latter-Day Saints: “It appeals to the Mormons’ faith in America—and to their conviction that they have a role to play in its preservation.”

While the American ideal that the culture of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints was built around felt fundamentally American over its first two centuries, it’s become increasingly true that that American identity is much more rare than it used to be. Where we anchor to kindness, there is often suspicion. Where we seek for unity, we’re surrounded by division. While we strive for optimism, there is an unending wave of pessimism about the world around us. That optimism was on full display last year as we dealt with [the loss](https://apnews.com/article/mormon-president-russell-m-nelson-obituary-d23fed7544364adf8453613fa761981d) of our Prophet, and reacted to a [shooter](https://www.thefp.com/p/terror-at-the-meetinghouse) in one of our chapels on the same weekend. How did we respond? We set up a [giving campaign](https://www.givesendgo.com/helptheSanfordfamily) for the family of the shooter who was killed.

As our late [[Prophet]], [[Russell M. Nelson|President Nelson]], once [said](https://web.archive.org/web/20250204083259/https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/archive/2021/01/the-most-american-religion/617263/): “We exist to make life better for people.” Our hope is that [our heritage](https://www.churchofjesuschrist.org/bc/content/shared/content/english/pdf/language-materials/35448_eng.pdf) is clear from the way we live our lives. **We work hard to build things and impact people as positively as we can. And we want that impact to be as long-lasting as possible; ideally forever.**

In the first book of the science fiction series, the [Expanse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leviathan_Wakes), written by a non-Mormon author, there is a critical side plot where, in the year [2350](https://www.reddit.com/r/TheExpanse/comments/cdk9dq/what_year_is_the_events_of_the_expanse_set_in/), “the Mormons” have built the [LDSS Nauvoo](https://expanse.fandom.com/wiki/Nauvoo_%28Books%29); an interstellar ship running the length of 6 of the longest aircraft carriers in the world. A “portable Eden” built for a 100-year voyage to Tau Ceti.

![](/images/anxiously-engaged-in-a-good-cause-d5f81585.png)

In a world of raunchy musicals like *The Book of Mormon* and other “cultural artifacts” like the reality TV show, *The Secret Lives of Mormon Wives*, a more true to life portrayal of the Latter-Day Saint character would be calling out the grit, self-reliance, and commitment that it would take to self-finance an intergalactic ship. Something I have no doubt we could roll up our sleeves and do if God asked. **The LDSS Nauvoo represents an embrace of, if my co-religionists will allow me, a techno-Mormon future. And the utopia we’ve been tasked with creating on Earth is just as much a techno-utopia as it is a spiritual utopia**.
