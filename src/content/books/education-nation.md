## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
[Milton Chen](/notes/milton-chen), former executive director of the George Lucas Educational Foundation ([Edutopia](/notes/edutopia)), argues that American education's most innovative practices already exist — at the edges of the system — and the task is to bring those "pockets of educational innovation" to the center. When the multiple edges converge and occupy the center, "an Education Nation will be born."

- **Innovation is recombination.** Chen's working definition: "take the best elements of what has been, integrate diverse sources of knowledge and talent, and create a breakthrough that hasn't been imagined before." Change agents don't adopt new practices wholesale — they adapt them to local needs and often improve the imported idea.
- **The testing obsession measures the elephant instead of feeding it.** "When we want the elephant to grow, we feed the elephant. We don't weigh the elephant." Assessment's primary purpose should be ongoing classroom-level feedback to teachers and learners, not high-stakes ranking. American curricula also try to "jam too many topics" — fifty to sixty science topics a year vs. nine in Japan — at the expense of depth.
- **Engagement is the whole game.** "If your students are engaged, you can teach them anything. If they're not engaged, you can be [Socrates](/notes/socrates), and not teach them anything." Give students choice and voice — connect learning to what they enjoy (arts, sports) — and they work harder.
- **The sports analogy is the indictment.** We teach athletics through performance, watching others perform, and self-observation (videotape analysis), yet settle for science and history taught through rote memorization. "If we could get half as exercised about scholastics as sports, our schools would rapidly improve."
- **Skate to where the puck is going.** Chen's recurring frame for [Project-Based Learning](/notes/project-based-learning), one-to-one computing ("the digital civil right of every student"), and treating education as an investment rather than a cost. "The best way to predict the future is to invent it" ([Alan Kay](/notes/alan-kay)).

## Connections

<!-- interconnections -->
- **[Project-Based Learning](/notes/project-based-learning)** — Chen's central reform; he argues PBL should *supplant* the old curriculum, not be implanted within it, and that memorization-based assessment frustrates it ("you perform to what is measured").
- **[Multiple Intelligences](/notes/multiple-intelligences) / [Howard Gardner](/notes/howard-gardner)** — the intrapersonal and interpersonal intelligences are reframed here as twenty-first-century capacities.
- **[Growth Mindset](/notes/growth-mindset)** — "teaching children to appreciate their brains motivates them to learn and expend greater effort."
- **[John Dewey](/notes/john-dewey)** — quoted on the "great waste" of children unable to connect in-school and out-of-school experience.
