## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
Read in 2021 and tagged #[Faith](/notes/faith) #[History of the Church](/notes/history-of-the-church) #[Family History](/notes/family-history). The highlights cluster around a handful of obsessions — not the events of the trek so much as the *interior* problems of building an institution out of fallible people. Six threads run through what Kyle marked:

1. **"Righteous in the dark."** The most-repeated Kyle note in the whole book. Brigham frames the trek as a proving ground — "an opportunity for every man to prove himself, to know whether he will pray and remember his God, without being asked to do it every day." Who you are when you arrive matters as much as how you get there (Judges 7). Character is what you do unprompted, unwatched, untold. See [Obedience](/notes/obedience), [Spirit of Humility](/notes/spirit-of-humility).

2. **Prosperity as the real threat.** When gold is discovered in California, Brigham's response is counterintuitive: "If we were to go to San Francisco and dig up chunks of gold, it would ruin us." His "worst fear" is not persecution but success — that the Saints "will get rich… forget God… wax fat, and kick themselves out of the Church." Kyle's gloss: prosperity makes it *easy to think you don't need to call on God*. This is the connective tissue to [Working Toward Zion](/books/working-toward-zion) and [Prosperity Gospel](/notes/prosperity-gospel) — the retrenchment, anti-extravagance, and cooperative economics that follow are an institutional immune response to wealth. "Seek ye *first* the kingdom."

3. **Fallible men, infallible work.** The book is unusually candid about leaders' imperfection, and Kyle marked nearly every instance. "Can a prophet be mistaken? I will acknowledge that all the time." "Man having the priesthood may be fallible; I do not pretend to be infallible." Yet "the work… emanated from God. He is its author." The resolution Kyle reaches for: *believe certain keystone truths that let you make the right decisions* — "cut the thread that ties you to the prophet of God and sink yourself to hell." See [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation), [Revelation](/notes/revelation).

4. **The Manifesto and the temporal–spiritual calculus.** The dramatic spine of Kyle's highlights. Woodruff weighs the loss of the temples (and the ordinances for the living and dead they house) against holding the line on plural marriage, and chooses "to act for the temporal salvation of the Church." His theology of revelation is the live question: "inspiration *is* revelation… a man is not always required to say 'Thus saith the Lord.'" Kyle's recurring probe in the margins — *why is Woodruff's medium of revelatory reception different from Joseph Smith's?* and *can we ever say a commandment will never be revoked?* — sits right on [Line upon line, precept upon precept](/notes/line-upon-line-precept-upon-precept) and [Living Revelation](/notes/living-revelation). Lorena Larsen's Abraham-and-Isaac dark night and B. H. Roberts's inability to raise his hand to sustain it are the human cost.

5. **Authority to decide vs. authority to execute.** A governance theme Kyle pulled out explicitly. The Quorum of the Twelve acts unanimously "or not at all"; "it is not in the power of two of them to rip it up"; reorganizing the First Presidency was "as much experimentation as anything." Brigham forgives Parley but flags a structural need for clarification. Kyle connects it to Henry B. Eyring studying decision-making bodies as an academic and finding the Quorum genuinely abnormal. See [Institution Building](/notes/institution-building), [Stewardship](/notes/stewardship).

6. **Women's agency, record-keeping, and "home literature."** Eliza R. Snow ("to do good — to bring into requisition every capacity… for saving souls"), the discipline of keeping careful minutes (Kyle: *note taking*), Susa Young Gates writing before six a.m. with three children underfoot, and the haunting divorce dream — "He did not understand then. But he does now." The counsel to "never write a line… not calculated to help and benefit this kingdom" reads as a personal note to a writer. See [Stewardship](/notes/stewardship).
