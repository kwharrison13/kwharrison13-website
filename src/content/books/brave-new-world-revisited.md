## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
The marginalia read this 1958 book of essays as a running argument with Huxley — more disagreement than agreement. Five threads:

1. **Huxley's demographic pessimism was wrong.** The "Age of Over-population" prophecy is marked simply "Wrong," and the claim that the world's poor grew worse off between 1907 and 1957 draws flat disbelief: "I feel like this can't possibly be true." The over-population panic that anchors much of the argument did not hold.

2. **Growth doesn't cause authoritarianism — its byproducts do.** The longest note pushes back hard: authoritarian states are born from problems, not from growth directly; growth causes some of those problems but is more often the path to the solutions that *avoid* authoritarianism. "Population growth doesn't cause societal collapse, but fear of population growth certainly can." Champions of growth aren't trying to strain the system — they're trying to revolutionize past it.

3. **The educated electorate as the structural defense.** The single most repeated tag — across Jefferson, Mill, and the suggestibility chapters — is that democracy's survival rests on an informed, reason-responsive citizenry. The defense that pre-dates and outlasts whatever specific authoritarian shape the present takes.

4. **Behaviorist determinism rejected.** The "crime and punishment" tag recurs across Huxley's own takedown of [B.F. Skinner](/notes/b-f-skinner): the genetic and individual contribution to behavior can't be wished away by environmental scientism. The freedom-and-responsibility question Huxley keeps circling.

5. **Resisting conditioning.** "Become unprogrammable," against the Social Ethic's "dynamic conformity"; "The Borg," on Huxley's line that men can only build an organization, never an organism, and that trying yields despotism. Plus the body-keeps-score note on conditioning learned under stress that can't be undone.

The throughline: Huxley got the alarm right but the diagnosis wrong. The interesting work is sorting which warnings still hold from which were artifacts of 1950s demographic anxiety.
