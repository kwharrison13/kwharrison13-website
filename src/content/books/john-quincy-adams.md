## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
- **The solitary politician.** Adams was constitutionally unsuited for the warmth politics demands — happiest alone, bearish with friends, withdrawn from his own wife and children — yet he outlasted every contemporary. His compensations were discipline, principle, and inexhaustible drudgery.
- **Education as moral formation.** John and [Abigail Adams](/notes/abigail-adams) believed intellect taught without morality was pointless. The curriculum they imposed on JQA — classics, Scripture, ancient history as a *lens* — shaped his entire public philosophy. He recreated it with his own children, with mixed results.
- **Foreign policy realism before it had a name.** Adams distinguished sharply between American interests and universal goods, and was deeply skeptical of America's capacity to do good abroad. He is the fountainhead of realist foreign policy thought.
- **The Macbeth Policy.** Adams believed a candidate who solicits support has already corrupted himself. He would "stand for" office but not "run for" it — a principle admirably suited to the Washington era but increasingly anachronistic by 1824.
- **Leadership against constituent wishes.** Adams took as a first principle that a leader's duty is to defend constituents' interests *against their inclinations* when necessary. He paid for this with his presidency.
- **Journals as the man's truest voice.** His memoirs — written for himself alone, late at night by candlelight — are one of the great works of American political literature, though Adams himself dismissed them as pedestrian.
- **A great man in the wrong place, at the wrong time, with the right motives.** George Dangerfield's verdict: his ideas were bold and forward-looking at a moment when most Americans wanted liberty, not power. He was simultaneously behind his time and ahead of it.
- **The House years as vindication.** No other president has gone on to serve in the House. The antislavery cause gave Adams the heroic purpose he always craved — and had been born too late to find in the Revolution.
