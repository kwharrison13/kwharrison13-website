## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
Read in 2014 on [Darwin Harrison](/notes/darwin-harrison)'s recommendation, and captured with a section-title on nearly every highlight — the titles are the marginalia here, and they cluster into a few insistent threads:

1. **Not whether you work, but what you work *for*.** "The whole emphasis in the holy writ is not on whether one works or not, but what one works for." Labor in Zion is real labor — just not labor for money or for the self. The recurring target is the "first I'll get rich, *then* I'll help the Church" bargain, which Nibley calls hokum.

2. **Consecration is a present, non-negotiable obligation — not a future one.** "What is there to stop me from observing and keeping the law of consecration at this very day?" The repeated move is to strip away every rationalization (compromise, "easier payments," seeking-the-kingdom-*first*-then-riches) and insist the covenant is binding now.

3. **Goods of first intent vs. goods of second intent.** Education, art, knowledge, and the life of the mind are ends in themselves ("goods of first intent"); money, degrees, and gadgets are only instruments ("second intent") — and a culture dies when it lets the instruments become their own excuse for being. BYU's drift "from humanities to business and law" is the running case study.

4. **Zion / Utopia as a real, buildable order, not a fantasy.** Zion is "a code word denoting a very real thing: any community in which the celestial order prevails." The long closing survey of the historical utopians (Lycurgus, Solon, More, the 19th-century American communities) argues they were the most practical, successful people of their day — and that money and private property are the one insuperable obstacle they all named.

5. **Only the individual can repent — alone, against the crowd.** "You do not have to wait for the group to change... only an individual can repent." The lemmings-to-the-sea image recurs against the one righteous person standing against the stream.

## Highlights

- Zion is a code word denoting a very real thing. Zion is any community in which the celestial order prevails. Zion is "the pure in heart" (D&C 97:21), but Zion is also a real city or any number of real cities. it is a constant; it is unchaining. There are Zions among all the worlds, and there are Zions that come and go. Zion is a constant in time and place—it belongs to the order of the eternities. We're not making Zion here, but we're preparing the ground to receive it. As the Lord says, "My people must be tried in all things, that they may be prepared to receive the glory that I have for them, even the glory of Zion; and he that will not bear chastisement is not worthy of my kingdom" (D&C 136:31).
