## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
[Sheri Dew](/notes/sheri-l-dew)'s authorized biography of [Gordon B. Hinckley](/notes/gordon-b-hinckley), read for the texture of how one man fused relentless work with faith over a sixty-year institutional career. The highlights cluster around a handful of durable threads:

1. **"Get on your knees and ask for the Lord's help, then get on your feet and go to work."** This is the load-bearing maxim of the whole book — it recurs verbatim three times (his father's terse missionary letter, "forget yourself and go to work," is the same idea). The marginal note pairs it with [Darwin Harrison](/notes/darwin-harrison)'s version: *pray as if everything depended on God, work as if everything depended on you.* Faith and effort are not in tension; they are the same motion.

2. **"Things will work out."** Hinckley's personal motto, and the emotional spine of the book. An attitude of abundance over scarcity, accentuating the positive, refusing to dwell on the dark side — "if you want to die at an early age, dwell on the negative." Marjorie's "things always worked out somehow" is the household version.

3. **Parent by principles, not rules; lead by example, not preaching.** The most heavily-marked theme. "There are never enough rules to tell you what to do in every situation" — so put a few principles in place and trust children to measure against them. Trust over surveillance ("never say no if I could possibly say yes"). The testimony that lands is the one lived, not narrated.

4. **Marriage as partnership and ballast.** Marjorie is co-protagonist, not backdrop — her humor ("the only way to get through life was to laugh your way through it") was the thing that got the family through every crisis. "She is a companion and an equal." She chose, knowingly, to be second to the Lord in his life — "and that was okay."

5. **Operational discipline: decide, don't re-litigate.** Meetings start and end on time; grasp the essence fast; once a decision is made, don't look back ("we have already covered that ground… now let us move forward"). Phone the person rather than call another meeting. See [running a meeting](/notes/running-a-meeting), [Operational Skills](/notes/operational-skills), [efficiency](/notes/efficiency).

6. **Lead by encouragement, not criticism.** The Komatsu story — "he didn't come into my area and tell me all about the weaknesses that I already knew about" — against the "spirit of [criticism](/notes/criticism)": *restrain your tongues; it is so much nobler to speak constructively.*

7. **"This is the Lord's church, the Lord's problem — not mine."** The delegation of ultimate responsibility that frees a leader to simply work the problem. Kyle's note ties it to [Mosiah 26](/notes/mosiah-26): *"This is my church."* Revelation as iterative process, not lightning bolt — the smaller-temples idea "developed over many years and a variety of experiences."

8. **People over programs.** "People are more important than programs and efficiency." The recurring [Elder Klebingat](/notes/elder-klebingat) note — *never allow this to become more of a program than a ministry* — marks the same nerve four times.

9. **Insatiable, integrated learning.** A polymath who "remembers what he has read and distills that which he wishes to retain." Breadth as a leadership asset: he can talk fruit trees, finials, anatomy, and law enforcement with the specialists. Echoes Kyle's [Charlie Munger](/notes/charlie-munger) note (and Munger's worldly-wisdom latticework).
