## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
[Richard Florida](/notes/richard-florida)'s reckoning with the dark side of his own creative-class thesis, read as a city-builder's field manual. Several threads recur across the marginalia:

1. **The clustering paradox as an investment thesis.** The same force that drives growth — talented people clustering in superstar cities — also drives inequality and prices everyone else out. Where Florida sees a policy problem, the notes repeatedly see an arbitrage: the gulf between $64,800-an-acre superstar land and $6,700-an-acre small-metro land is "a huge buying opportunity… if you have a formula / strategy for increasing the value of that land." Distressed, crumbling suburbs and dead malls get read the same way — as [Alternative Real Estate](/notes/alternative-real-estate) to reinvent rather than write off.

2. **Micro-cities over both sprawl and mega-cities.** The throughline answer to Florida's crisis: "Instead of [suburban sprawl](/notes/suburban-sprawl) or [mega cities](/notes/mega-cities) of inequality, we need [micro cities](/notes/micro-cities) deliberately designed." Even small towns should have a bustling downtown / main-street core. This ties the book directly into the [The New City](/notes/the-new-city) / [Prosperable](/notes/prosperable) project and the [EPCOT](/notes/epcot) hub-and-spoke idea from [Walt Disney and the Promise of Progress City](/books/walt-disney-and-the-promise-of-progress-city).

3. **Remote work dissolves the proximity constraint.** Florida's data says the affluent return to the urban core for three reasons — high-paying jobs, short commutes, and amenities. [Remote Work](/notes/remote-work) removes the first two, leaving amenities as the only question — and in a [COVID](/notes/covid) world those amenities (backyards, more space, the outdoors) point away from the dense core. See [COVID pushes us towards something like Prosperable](/notes/covid-pushes-us-towards-something-like-prosperable).

4. **Purpose-driven community and employment.** A recurring "what if": select residents and workers by *character* and *commitment* rather than income — cheaper rent and store prices in exchange for stewardship and loyalty, higher pay in exchange for buy-in (the old "company families" idea, [ESOP](/notes/esop)s, [WeWork](/notes/wework)-style community curation).

5. **Redistribution as growth, not drag.** The notes lean hard into Florida's most counterintuitive finding — that redistribution (up to and including [Universal Basic Income](/notes/universal-basic-income) via a negative income tax) *raises* growth rather than lowering it. Explicitly linked to [Utopia For Realists](/books/utopia-for-realists). Paired with his "do both" stance: people-based *and* place-based anti-poverty approaches.

6. **City-building is an untrained discipline.** The note that lands hardest: unlike medicine, law, or engineering, there is "very little systematic training to equip mayors and city-builders." That gap is the animating premise behind the whole [City Building](/notes/city-building) interest — including the open question of why solutions so rarely scale (Ben & Jerry's, microfinance) and how to get real dollars and intangible ideas to the cities that are losing.

### Florida's policy agenda

- Reform zoning and building codes, as well as tax policies, to ensure that the [clustering force](/notes/clustering) works to the benefit of all.
- Invest in the infrastructure needed to spur density and clustering and limit costly and inefficient sprawl — in other words, don't make it easier to spread out.
- Build more affordable rental housing in central locations.
  > **Kyle:** *Can you determine approval of rental applications by character over income? Why can't a landlord interview applicants based on character and offer lower rent in exchange for more support and responsible living? How does WeWork select applicants to live and how do they ensure 'community-minded' living?*
- Expand the middle class by turning low-wage service jobs into family-supporting work.
  > **Kyle:** *Think about 'purpose-driven employment.' What if we had shops and stores and restaurants where we select employees based on character, agree to pay them more in exchange for more commitment (e.g. like ESOPs)?*
- Tackle concentrated poverty head-on by investing in people and places — better learning opportunities in places like libraries.
- Engage in a global effort to build stronger, more prosperous cities in rapidly urbanizing parts of the emerging world.
- Empower communities and enable local leaders to strengthen their own economies and cope with the challenges of the New Urban Crisis — e.g. sharing best practices.
