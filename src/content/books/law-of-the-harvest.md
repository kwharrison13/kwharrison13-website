## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
[David G. Stewart](/notes/david-g-stewart)'s data-driven critique of LDS missionary work — recommended by [Erik Hansen](/notes/erik-hansen) — argues that the Church baptizes far more than it retains, and that the gap is a methodology problem, not an inevitability of growth. Read with heavy annotation; the highlights cluster around a handful of threads:

1. **Retention is the real metric, not baptisms.** The book's central provocation: "When individuals are baptized but do not attend church, do not identify themselves as members of the Church, and do not believe or live the teachings of the Church, has the Church grown?" With roughly two-thirds of members inactive and a lost-address file likely near 2 million, raw baptism counts mislead. The scriptural standard is fruit that *remains* (John 15:16); Ammon's converts achieved 100% retention, and the British Isles sustained near-100% for half a century — so poor retention is "no more a necessary part of the growth of the Church than sickness is a prerequisite to health."

2. **High-demand faiths grow faster, not slower.** Strictness correlates with growth, not attrition. [Seventh-Day Adventists](/notes/seventh-day-adventists) and [Jehovah's Witnesses](/notes/jehovah-s-witnesses) impose commitments as demanding as the [LDS Church](/notes/church-of-jesus-christ-of-latter-day-saints)'s, retain far better, and grow 8–11% annually. "Faiths that require little offer little and fail to mobilize the commitment and dedication that are prerequisites for sustained rapid growth." [Joseph Smith](/notes/joseph-smith)'s formulation anchors it: "A religion that does not require the sacrifice of all things never has power sufficient to produce the faith necessary to lead unto life and salvation."

3. **Build habits before deadlines.** The fix for attrition is pre-baptismal preparation: four consecutive weeks of gospel habits (daily [Book of Mormon](/notes/book-of-mormon) study, church attendance, Sabbath, prayer, [Word of Wisdom](/notes/word-of-wisdom)) *before* the font, not a baptismal date imposed on top. "Time is not a threat to true conversion." [Aristotle](/notes/aristotle) and [Jim Rohn](/notes/jim-rohn) supply the engine — "We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act but a habit" — and sociology supplies the clock: three to four weeks of repetition to form a habit. This is the through-line to [Atomic Habits](/books/atomic-habits) and the activities-not-outcomes logic of [Measure What Matters](/books/measure-what-matters).

4. **Finding is the bottleneck, so just open your mouth.** Effectiveness separates great missionaries from poor ones in their *finding* program, not their teaching program. "The selection process is to occur in the heart of the hearer and not in the mind of the sharer" — reach everyone, let God sort acceptance. The practical unit is one gospel conversation ("OYM") per day; the barrier is fear (73% of surveyed members), and what nonmembers actually value is righteous example, not articulate explanation (only 2% cited that). "The world is not 'fished out': we simply aren't doing much fishing."

5. **Measure honestly; candor beats complacency.** "It is impossible to achieve meaningful improvement without awareness of present reality." A social-acceptability reporting bias filters out the very information most useful for improvement — "all is well in Zion" — and selective reporting fuels the false belief that the Church is growing rapidly. The posture Stewart wants is the one [Faith is Not Blind](/books/faith-is-not-blind) models and [Radical Candor](/notes/radical-candor) names: "Those whose faith is grounded in a true testimony of our Savior Jesus Christ and His restored Gospel will welcome data and objective analysis." Echoes [How Will You Measure Your Life](/books/how-will-you-measure-your-life) — optimize the metric that matters, not the one that flatters.

6. **Over-programming crowds out the core mission.** [Boyd K. Packer](/notes/boyd-k-packer)'s "team of doctors" critique runs through the back half: every spiritual ailment gets encapsulated as a program with pages of instructions, until local leaders are "conditioned to hold back until programmed" and individual revelation is "in jeopardy." Peripheral activity consumes member time while member-missionary outreach goes undone — "any child can take all of the toys out of the closet… it takes wisdom to know which ones not to take out." Leadership here is execution and example ([Drucker](/notes/peter-drucker): "Popularity is not leadership. Results are."), and outreach leadership is a distinct skill from pastoral leadership.
