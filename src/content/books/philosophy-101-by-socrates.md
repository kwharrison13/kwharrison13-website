## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
[Peter Kreeft](/notes/peter-kreeft)'s introduction to philosophy teaches the discipline through the example of [Socrates](/notes/socrates) — primarily the figure of the [Apology](/notes/apology-of-socrates) and the trial that ended his life. The highlighted quotes all circle one idea: that integrity and virtue are worth more than self-preservation, reputation, or money.

- **Being right is more costly than being wrong.** "People will often forgive you for being wrong where they are right; they will seldom forgive you for being right when you are wrong." The Socratic stance toward truth-telling carries a social price.
- **The good cannot be harmed by the wicked.** Socrates' claim that "no evil can happen to a good man" reframes death and persecution as injuries the persecutor inflicts on himself, not the victim — virtue is its own protection.
- **Virtue over money, as a standard for one's children.** His parting request — to "punish my sons" if they care for money or anything before virtue — turns his own indictment into a model of parenting (#[Parenting](/notes/parenting)): the thing to fear is not poverty but misplaced values.

## Highlights

- "People will often forgive you for being wrong where they are right; they will seldom forgive you for being right when you are wrong."
- "No evil can happen to a good man… living or dead. Be sure of this, that if you put me to death, being such as I am, you will not hurt me so much as yourselves…for I think the eternal law forbids a better man to be hurt by a worse."
- "One thing I ask… punish my sons, gentlemen, when they grow up; give them this same pain I gave you, if you think they care for money or anything before virtue." #[Parenting](/notes/parenting)
