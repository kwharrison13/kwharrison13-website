## Key Takeaways

<!-- key-takeaways -->
The marginalia returns again and again to persuasion through humility. Franklin's verdict that being "disputatious" was "a very bad habit" reads as a precursor to [Stephen Covey](/notes/stephen-covey)'s "seek first to understand, then to be understood" — winning the argument costs you the goodwill. That thread runs straight to the [Constitutional Convention](/notes/constitutional-convention), where Franklin's willingness to doubt his own judgment ("the older I grow, the more apt I am to doubt my own judgment") is treated as the load-bearing virtue of the whole enterprise. [Compromise](/notes/compromise) is read here not as a failure of nerve but as a moral stance: "Compromisers may not make great heroes, but they do make great democracies."

Self-improvement shows up as deliberate practice rather than aspiration — the method by which Franklin taught himself to write (read an essay, take notes, rebuild it from scratch, compare) is flagged as the actual mechanism behind the prose, and the [Thirteen Virtues](/notes/thirteen-virtues) as a tracked, one-bed-at-a-time system rather than a moral pose.

A recurring political note is the inversion of merit and birth. Franklin's America asks "not, What is he? but, What can he do?" — and the observation is that the country spent centuries making sure birth couldn't guarantee success, and now spends its time making sure birth can't guarantee failure. The defense of [Frugality](/notes/frugality) and [Thrift](/notes/thrift) is similarly contrarian: thrift can only be belittled in times of luxury, and the romantic ([D.H. Lawrence](/notes/d-h-lawrence)) and academic ([Weber](/notes/max-weber)) attacks on Franklin land on the Poor Richard persona, not the man — a distinction the book itself insists on.

- Franklin's 13 virtues:
    - [Temperance](/notes/temperance). Eat not to dullness; drink not to elevation.
    - Silence. Speak not but what may benefit others or yourself; avoid trifling conversation.
    - Order. Let all your things have their places; let each part of your business have its time.
    - Resolution. Resolve to perform what you ought; perform without fail what you resolve.
    - [Frugality](/notes/frugality). Make no expense but to do good to others or yourself; i.e., waste nothing.
    - Industry. Lose no time; be always employ'd in something useful; cut off all unnecessary actions.
    - Sincerity. Use no hurtful deceit; think innocently and justly, and, if you speak, speak accordingly.
    - [Justice](/notes/justice). Wrong none by doing injuries, or omitting the benefits that are your duty.
    - Moderation. Avoid extremes; forbear resenting injuries so much as you think they deserve.
    - Cleanliness. Tolerate no uncleanliness in body, cloaths, or habitation.
    - Tranquillity. Be not disturbed at trifles, or at accidents common or unavoidable.
    - Chastity. Rarely use venery but for health or offspring, never to dullness, weakness, or the injury of your own or another's peace or reputation.
    - Humility. Imitate Jesus and [Socrates](/notes/socrates).
